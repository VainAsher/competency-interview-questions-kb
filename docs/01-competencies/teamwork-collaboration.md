# Executive Summary

This is the Tier-2 deep-dive for the teamwork and collaboration competency family, the child of `competencies-foundation`. Where the foundation document establishes that "working with others" is one of eight recurring competency families and maps it across frameworks, this document goes down a level: it records the **full published level descriptors** for the competency in four named frameworks, assembles a bank of 51 practice questions and probes with per-question provenance, and sets out coaching and assessor material tied back to those descriptors.

The evidence is unusually good for this competency, because all four frameworks publish their descriptors openly and at multiple levels of seniority. The UK Civil Service defines **Working Together** as "Form effective partnerships and relationships with people both internally and externally, from a range of diverse backgrounds, sharing information, resources and support", and publishes distinct behaviour examples at six grade bands from AA/AO to Director General [1]. EPSO's 2023 Competency Framework defines **Working together** as "Demonstrates cross-functional cooperation and fosters diversity and inclusion. Cooperates well by seeking compromise, sharing knowledge and assisting others", decomposed into five observable anchors [5]. The College of Policing's 2024 Competency and Values Framework carries **We collaborate** at three levels of increasing complexity [12]. NHS recruitment frames the same territory as the value "working together for patients" rather than as a competency [10].

The single most consequential finding for candidates is structural: every one of these frameworks describes teamwork in the **first person singular** or as things "you" do. The Civil Service descriptors are phrased "examples of working together … are when you: proactively contribute…, get to know your colleagues…, ask for help when needed" [1]; the police CVF descriptors are phrased "I work cooperatively with others to get things done" [12]. This is the documented basis for the most common coaching warning about teamwork answers — that a "we" narrative gives the assessor no individual behaviour to score. Document-level confidence is **Medium**: the framework descriptors, the 1–7 scoring scale and the assessor guidance rest on official documents and would merit High, but the question bank's composed entries and the pitfall-to-descriptor mappings are this knowledge base's reasoned synthesis.

# Key Takeaways

- The Civil Service **Working Together** behaviour is published with separate example behaviours at six grade bands (AA/AO, EO, HEO/SEO, Grade 7/6, Deputy Director, Director/DG) — a candidate can read the exact level they are being assessed against before the interview. *(documented)* [1]
- Across all four frameworks the descriptors are written as individual actions ("you: ask for help when needed"; "I work cooperatively with others") — not as team outcomes. *(documented)* [1] [12]
- Teamwork descriptors at every level include an **inclusion and anti-discrimination** strand: the Civil Service lists "understand that bullying, harassment and discrimination are unacceptable" from AA/AO grade upward [1], and EPSO's anchors include "Embraces diversity and fosters an environment of inclusion" [5]. Answers that show only task cooperation miss a scored strand. *(documented)*
- Teamwork descriptors **shift outward with seniority**: junior levels emphasise contributing and asking for help; senior levels emphasise networks, partnerships across organisational boundaries, and cultural climate. Both the Civil Service ladder [1] and the police CVF's three levels [12] show this pattern. *(documented)*
- **Conflict handling is inside the teamwork competency, not adjacent to it.** EPSO's anchors explicitly require "Discusses differences of opinion openly with the individuals concerned … and strives for compromise in situations of conflict" [5]; the Civil Service Grade 7/6 descriptor includes "challenge assumptions while being willing to compromise if beneficial to progress" [1]. *(documented)*
- Where a published scoring scale exists, the bar is concrete: the Crown Office and Procurator Fiscal Service publishes a 7-point Success Profiles scale and states that candidates "must demonstrate the minimum essential criteria for all roles, this includes scoring 4 or more in all behaviours" [4]. *(documented)*
- Private-sector and graduate-scheme practice converges on the same content without the framework vocabulary; targetjobs publishes eleven teamwork interview questions and warns against implying "that your efforts were the sole reason for your team being able to achieve the collective goal" [23]. *(documented)*
- Prepare **three** teamwork examples, not one: a peer-collaboration example, a cross-boundary/stakeholder example, and a disagreement-resolution example. The published descriptors sample all three areas, and interviewers commonly probe across them. *(guidance)*

# Purpose

This document answers, for a candidate: *"What exactly is an employer looking for when they ask a teamwork question, what will they actually ask, and how do I build answers that hit the published descriptors rather than sounding pleasant and generic?"* Teamwork is the competency candidates most often assume they can answer without preparation, and therefore the one where prepared candidates gain most ground.

For an interviewer or assessor it answers: *"What do the published frameworks say this competency consists of at each level, what question set fairly samples it, and what separates a scoring answer from a non-scoring one?"*

It deliberately assumes the reader has met the basics elsewhere. `competencies-foundation` covers what competency interviewing is and why structured methods are used; `techniques-foundation` covers STAR and its variants in full. This document does not restate either.

# Scope

**Covered:** the teamwork/collaboration competency family in depth — how it is named, defined and levelled in the UK Civil Service Success Profiles Behaviours [1], the EPSO Competency Framework [5], NHS values-based recruitment [10] [11], and the College of Policing CVF [12] [13]; how private-sector and graduate-scheme recruiters treat it [23] [24]; a 51-entry practice question bank grouped by level and sub-theme with per-question provenance; evidence-selection and worked-answer coaching; published positive and negative indicators and scoring guidance; teamwork-specific pitfalls tied back to cited descriptors; practice drills; and the fairness obligations that bear on this competency specifically.

**Not covered:** the general case for structured interviewing and the eight-family taxonomy (see `competencies-foundation`); full treatment of STAR, CARL, SOAR and answer-structuring technique (see `techniques-foundation`); leading a team, motivating and developing others, which sit in the leadership family (see `leadership-management`) even though the frameworks sometimes place related descriptors under teamwork; persuasion and stakeholder communication as a competency in its own right (see `communication-influencing`); group exercises and assessment-centre team simulations as a *format* (see the Formats category). Sector frameworks beyond the four named above — teaching, social work, armed forces — are out of scope.

# Definitions

- **Working Together** — the UK Civil Service Success Profiles behaviour: "Form effective partnerships and relationships with people both internally and externally, from a range of diverse backgrounds, sharing information, resources and support" [1]. Note the scope is deliberately wider than "your team" — it includes external partnerships.
- **Working together (EPSO)** — the fifth of EPSO's eight general competencies: "Demonstrates cross-functional cooperation and fosters diversity and inclusion. Cooperates well by seeking compromise, sharing knowledge and assisting others" [5].
- **We collaborate** — one of the six competencies in the College of Policing CVF (2024), described as delivering effective policing "by working with others — not just with the colleagues we see daily, but across our whole force, with other organisations and with the public" [12].
- **Anchor** — in the EPSO framework, an observable element of a general competency used to recognise and assess it [5] [6]. Anchors are the closest thing any of these frameworks publishes to a marking key.
- **Example behavioural indicator** — the College of Policing's term for the illustrative behaviours listed under each competency level; the guidance stresses these "are examples and are not intended as a comprehensive list" and that the CVF "should not be used as a simple checklist" [12] [13].
- **Values-based interview (VBI)** — per the Health Education England VBR Framework, "a type of structured interview which is particularly focused on understanding a candidate's values", where "it is the probing questions, designed as part of a VBI, that are used to provide insight into a candidate's values" [11].
- **Cross-boundary collaboration** — this document's shorthand for the strand of the competency dealing with people outside your own team: other departments, partner organisations, suppliers, communities. An editorial term, used because all four frameworks contain this strand under different wording.

## UK Civil Service: the Working Together behaviour, level by level

Working Together is one of nine Civil Service Behaviours assessed under Success Profiles; recruiting managers select a subset for each role, and candidates "will not be asked to demonstrate all Civil Service Behaviours for one role" [1]. Behaviours "are specific to the grade level of the job role", and GOV.UK publishes example behaviours at six bands [1]. The guidance also states there is "no expectation that you need to demonstrate every part of each example to be successful", and recommends the STAR method for candidates' own examples [1].

The published examples of working together at each level [1]:

| Grade band | Example behaviours — "examples of working together … are when you:" |
|---|---|
| AA and AO | "proactively contribute to the work of the whole team and remain open to taking on new and different roles"; "get to know your colleagues and build supportive relationships"; "listen to alternative perspectives and needs, responding sensitively and checking understanding where necessary"; "ask for help when needed and support others when the opportunity arises"; "are aware of the need to consider your own wellbeing and that of your colleagues"; "understand that bullying, harassment and discrimination are unacceptable" |
| EO | "develop a range of contacts outside own team and identify opportunities to share knowledge, information and learning"; "show genuine interest when listening to others"; "contribute to an inclusive working environment where all opinions and challenges are listened to"; "ensure it is clear that bullying, harassment and discrimination are unacceptable"; "offer support and help to colleagues when in need"; "change ways of working to aid cooperation within and between teams in order to achieve results" |
| HEO and SEO | "encourage joined up team work within your own team and across other groups"; "establish professional relationships with a range of stakeholders"; "collaborate with these to share information, resources and support"; "invest time to develop a common focus and genuine positive team spirit where colleagues feel valued and respect one another"; "put in place support for the wellbeing of individuals within the team"; "make it clear to all team members that bullying, harassment and discrimination are unacceptable"; "actively seek and consider input of people from diverse backgrounds and perspectives" |
| Grade 7 and Grade 6 | "actively build and maintain a network of colleagues and contacts to achieve progress on shared objectives"; "challenge assumptions while being willing to compromise if beneficial to progress"; "build strong interpersonal relationships and show genuine care for colleagues"; "ensure consideration and support for the wellbeing of yourself and individuals throughout the team"; "understand the varying needs of the team to ensure they are supported and their experiences are utilised"; "create an inclusive working environment where all opinions and challenges are taken into account and bullying, harassment and discrimination are unacceptable"; "remain available and approachable to all colleagues and be receptive to new ideas" |
| Deputy Director | "proactively create, maintain and promote a strong network of contacts across the organisation and externally"; "embed an inclusive culture of creating positive and supportive teams who consider the diverse needs and feelings of other colleagues"; "ensure consideration and support for the wellbeing of all individuals across the organisation"; "set out clear expectations that bullying, harassment, and discrimination are unacceptable"; "encourage and establish mechanisms to share knowledge and resources across boundaries to support the business"; "encourage teams to engage with a variety of delivery partners and stakeholders, listen to and act on their feedback" |
| Director and Director General | "drive an inclusive and collaborative working culture which values diversity and encourages, openness, approachability and sensitivity"; "ensure the wellbeing of all individuals is valued across the organisation"; "confront issues and challenge assumptions at the highest levels, no matter how uncomfortable, in an assertive yet constructive way"; "create a culture where bullying, harassment and discrimination are unacceptable"; "build a strong diverse network of professional relationships across the highest levels of government and with Non-Executive Directors"; "act for the wider good of the Civil Service by improving governance at board level" |

Behaviours can be assessed through application forms with written examples, Civil Service judgement tests, interviews, assessment centres and job-related simulations including group exercises and role-play [1]. The Civil Service Careers site confirms behaviours sit alongside strengths, ability, technical and experience elements in a role's Success Profile, with the mix stated in the job advert [3].

## EU institutions: EPSO's Working together competency

EPSO's Competency Framework "defines the 8 general competencies that EU officials are expected to demonstrate throughout their career", each composed of "various observable elements, called anchors" [5]. Working together is competency 5 of 8 [5]. Its definition and full anchor set [5]:

> **Working together** — "Demonstrates cross-functional cooperation and fosters diversity and inclusion. Cooperates well by seeking compromise, sharing knowledge and assisting others."
>
> Anchors:
>
> - "Encourages cooperation between teams and identifies themselves with the common goals and interests."
> - "Discusses differences of opinion openly with the individuals concerned, understands other points of view, and strives for compromise in situations of conflict."
> - "Embraces diversity and fosters an environment of inclusion, strives to treat all others equally and with fairness."
> - "Offers assistance and help to others."
> - "Proactively shares knowledge for the benefit and learning of others on an ongoing basis."

The competency is assessed through the tests specified for each competition. EPSO's competency-based interview (CBI) "is designed to evaluate a number of selected general competencies, depending on the competition and grade", with candidates "interviewed for about 10 minutes per competency" and questions "structured to allow the assessors to evaluate the candidate's selected general competencies" [7]. EPSO also runs a situational variant, the SCBI, "an interview that aims to assess general competencies by asking the candidate how he/she would react in a specific situation" [8]. Which competencies are tested in any given competition is fixed by the Notice of Competition, which EPSO describes as "the only official document that contains detailed information about the actual tests used by EPSO for a given competition" [9].

The ten-minutes-per-competency figure [7] is materially useful: a candidate facing a CBI that includes Working together should expect roughly ten minutes on it — enough for one example plus sustained probing, not a single rehearsed paragraph.

## NHS: teamwork as a value, not a competency

NHS recruitment in England frames this territory as the NHS Constitution value **"Working together for patients"** — "Patients come first in everything we do. We fully involve patients, staff, families, carers, communities, and professionals inside and outside the NHS" [10]. It is the first of six values, alongside respect and dignity, commitment to quality of care, compassion, improving lives, and everyone counts [10].

Assessment of these values is governed by the Health Education England Values Based Recruitment Framework, which defines a values-based interview as "a type of structured interview which is particularly focused on understanding a candidate's values", notes that a VBI "may appear similar to other types of interviews in that the format will involve asking candidates to provide examples of behaviour they have previously demonstrated or to explain how they would respond to a particular situation", and identifies the distinguishing feature as the probing: "it is the probing questions, designed as part of a VBI, that are used to provide insight into a candidate's values or what they consider to be important" [11]. The framework's national core requirements include conducting "an individual, face-to-face structured interview to include an assessment of values" [11], and it lists structured interviews, multiple mini interviews, selection centres and situational judgement tests as the methods the evidence supports [11]. NHS Employers describes values-based recruitment as "an approach to recruitment which will help you find people whose personal values match with your organisation", delivered through pre-screening, values-based interviewing techniques including role play and written scenario responses, and assessment centres [21].

The practical implication for candidates is that the NHS variant of a teamwork question is likely to be probed toward *why* you acted, not only what you did — the probe is where the value is read [11].

## Policing: We collaborate in the CVF (2024)

The College of Policing's Competency and Values Framework consists of "three values" and "six competencies", with each competency described "at three levels, which reflect increasing complexity of behaviours"; the levels "are not assigned to specific ranks" and apply to competencies only, not values [12] [13]. The 2016 version's competency clusters "have been removed for simplicity" in the 2024 edition [13].

**We collaborate** is described thus [12]:

> "We can only deliver effective policing by working with others — not just with the colleagues we see daily, but across our whole force, with other organisations and with the public. We take time to know our stakeholders and build sustainable and strong partnerships with them so we can make the most appropriate contribution to shared objectives. Often, our job means we need to establish effective ways of working with new people beyond our usual teams, including sometimes at great speed and under pressure."

Its example behaviours by level [12]:

| Level | Example behaviours (first person, as published) |
|---|---|
| Level 1 (practitioners) | "I learn about stakeholders in other teams, organisations and the community so I can work with them effectively."; "I work to get to know others and build rapport so that we can achieve shared goals."; "I work flexibly with people across different levels, teams and backgrounds."; "I work cooperatively with others to get things done, willingly giving help and support to colleagues."; "I actively seek to engage with and learn from a diverse group to improve the work I do." |
| Level 2 (supervisors/managers) | "I create a work environment that combines diverse styles, backgrounds and experiences to get better results."; "I build strong relationships across a broad range of stakeholders, including colleagues, partners and communities, and use these connections to accomplish goals."; "I work with partners to decide clear roles appropriate to each partner's remit."; "I include others in my thinking and understand their priorities to make collaboration more effective."; "I help create joined-up solutions across organisational and geographical boundaries"; "I manage long-term partnerships, sharing information and building trust to find the best solutions." |
| Level 3 (senior managers/executive) | "I build strong partnerships by finding common ground with others, acknowledging their different priorities and negotiating effectively with them."; "I am politically astute and understand how national policy and politics affects our partners."; "I use my knowledge of our partners' and stakeholders' interests and concerns to anticipate conflict before it happens."; "I support stakeholder relationships to enable things to get done by the most appropriate partners."; "I create a culture and environment in which partnership working flourishes and creates tangible benefits for all." |

The CVF guidance is explicit about interview method: "it is good practice to ask about examples of past behaviour, probing for evidence. It is also sometimes helpful to ask about how candidates would deal with hypothetical future situations. However, most of the interview should focus on past behaviour" [13].

## Private sector and graduate schemes

There is no single published private-sector framework, but the practitioner literature is consistent. targetjobs states that competency-based teamwork questions "typically ask you for an example of when you have worked in a team in the past to discover whether you have good teamworking skills", that answers "should be structured by using the STAR method", and that recruiters assess collaboration, relationship-building, compromise, communication and emotional intelligence [23]. Teamwork also appears on targetjobs' list of competencies graduate employers commonly assess, alongside communication, problem-solving and leadership [24]. Skills Development Scotland's My World of Work maps the question "Describe a time you collaborated successfully with a team." directly to the teamwork competency [27].

The volume pressure behind this is documented: Institute of Student Employers survey data records applications running at "140 per vacancy" in recent years compared with 38 two decades ago, rising to 290 per position in retail, FMCG and tourism [22]. That volume is why graduate employers use structured, criterion-scored assessment rather than conversation — and it is why a generic teamwork answer, which is what most applicants give, is a poor differentiator.

## How the competency escalates with seniority

Both frameworks that publish a full ladder show the same escalation, and the direction is documented rather than inferred. In the Civil Service the AA/AO descriptor is bounded by the candidate's own team ("contribute to the work of the whole team", "get to know your colleagues"); by EO it reaches outside ("develop a range of contacts outside own team"); by HEO/SEO it includes stakeholders and team climate ("establish professional relationships with a range of stakeholders", "invest time to develop a common focus and genuine positive team spirit"); by Grade 7/6 it becomes network-building plus principled disagreement ("actively build and maintain a network"; "challenge assumptions while being willing to compromise"); at Deputy Director it becomes system design ("encourage and establish mechanisms to share knowledge and resources across boundaries"); and at Director/DG it becomes culture ("drive an inclusive and collaborative working culture") [1].

The police CVF shows the identical arc across three levels: Level 1 is learning about stakeholders and building rapport; Level 2 is building a work environment, deciding partner roles and managing long-term partnerships; Level 3 is negotiating with partners, political astuteness, anticipating conflict and creating "a culture and environment in which partnership working flourishes" [12].

This matters for evidence selection: an example that would score well at one level can under-reach at another. The Civil Service guidance is nonetheless permissive about completeness — "There is no expectation that you need to demonstrate every part of each example to be successful" [1].

## How this competency is assessed

- **Civil Service.** Behaviours are assessed through application forms with written examples, Civil Service judgement tests, personality tests, interviews, assessment centres, and job-related simulations including presentations, in-tray exercises, written analysis, group exercises, role-play and oral briefings [1]. Recruiting managers select which behaviours a role assesses [1].
- **EPSO.** Approximately ten minutes of interview per competency in the CBI, with questions structured to let assessors evaluate the selected competencies [7]; a situational variant (SCBI) asks how the candidate would react in a specified situation [8]; the tests used are fixed by the Notice of Competition [9].
- **NHS.** An individual, face-to-face structured interview including an assessment of values is a national core requirement, and may be delivered as a multiple mini interview, within a selection centre, or alongside a situational judgement test [11].
- **Policing.** Recruitment and promotion interviews should ask about examples of past behaviour, probing for evidence, with "most of the interview" focused on past behaviour, though hypothetical questions are sometimes used [13].
- **Private sector / graduate schemes.** Competency-based teamwork questions ask for a past example, structured with STAR [23].

5. How do you help build good team spirit? *(sourced: University of Oxford Careers Service, Example interview questions)* [25]

## Follow-up probes assessors use

Probes are where values-based and competency interviews actually discriminate between candidates — the HEE framework identifies the probing questions as the mechanism by which a values-based interview reads a candidate's values [11]. Expect several of these on top of any question above.

1. What was your specific role in that, as distinct from the team's? *(composed)*
2. Who disagreed with you, and what exactly did you say to them? *(composed)*
3. What would your colleagues in that team say about how you worked with them? *(composed)*
4. Why did you choose to handle it that way rather than escalating? *(composed)*
5. What did the other person want, and how did you find that out? *(composed)*
6. What would you do differently if the same situation arose tomorrow? *(composed)*
7. How did you know the collaboration had worked? *(composed)*

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact. It points back to the cited descriptors above.**

## Selecting teamwork evidence

Three tests, in order:

1. **Does the example let you speak in the first person?** The descriptors are individual actions — "you: ask for help when needed" [1], "I work cooperatively with others" [12]. If your best story is one where the team did something impressive but your own contribution was diffuse, it is the wrong story regardless of how good the outcome was.
2. **Does it reach the right level?** Match the example's scope to the published level you are being assessed at. A story about covering a colleague's tasks evidences the AA/AO strand "support others when the opportunity arises" [1] but under-reaches a Grade 7 assessment, where the descriptor asks for network-building and principled compromise [1]. Conversely, a stakeholder-negotiation story told at AO level may sound implausible.
3. **Does it evidence more than one strand?** The strongest examples touch cooperation *and* one of: knowledge-sharing, disagreement-handling, or inclusion. A story with a moment of genuine friction in it usually outscores a frictionless one, because it lets you evidence the compromise anchor [5] as well as the cooperation anchor.

**Build three examples, not one.** The bank above shows interviewers sampling across peer collaboration, cross-boundary work, and disagreement. Preparing one example for each covers the territory; preparing one example total means re-telling it three times, which reads as thin.

## Worked STAR answer skeleton

Question: *"Tell me about a time when you had a disagreement with another team member. How was it resolved?"* [23]

The STAR structure is Situation, Task, Action, Result — "the situation you had to deal with", "the task you were given to do", "the action you took", "what happened as a result of your action and what you learned" [26]. The Civil Service explicitly recommends it for behaviour examples [1]. Keep S and T short; A and R carry the score.

- **Situation (2 sentences).** Two of us on a four-person project were assigned overlapping analysis work ahead of a board paper. My colleague wanted to run the modelling their way, which would have missed our deadline by roughly a week.
- **Task (1 sentence).** I needed us to agree an approach within two days without damaging a working relationship I would depend on for the rest of the year.
- **Action (the bulk — 5 or 6 sentences, all "I").** I asked for twenty minutes with them privately rather than raising it in the team meeting. I opened by asking what was driving their preference, and learned their method mattered because a previous version had been challenged on methodology. I said plainly that I disagreed about timing and explained the board deadline. I proposed a split: their method on the two figures most likely to be challenged, my faster approach on the rest, with the methodology difference flagged in a footnote. I asked them to sanity-check my sections and offered to draft the footnote myself. We agreed in that meeting and I confirmed it in writing to the whole team.
- **Result (2 sentences, with a number and a learning).** The paper went to the board on time and the methodology footnote pre-empted the one question we had expected. I now open disagreements by asking what is behind the other person's position before stating mine — it has cut the number of these that need escalating.

**Why this scores.** It maps directly onto published wording: "Discusses differences of opinion openly with the individuals concerned, understands other points of view, and strives for compromise in situations of conflict" [5], and "challenge assumptions while being willing to compromise if beneficial to progress" [1]. The private conversation, the question about their reasoning, the explicit disagreement, the compromise, and the relationship surviving are each a distinct scoreable action, not one action described five ways.

## Answering the opinion questions

"In your opinion, what makes a team work well together?" [23] has no Situation to describe. Treat it as a chance to state two or three principles and immediately evidence each with a half-sentence from your own experience — principle plus miniature example. A pure abstraction ("trust and communication") gives an assessor nothing to score against behavioural descriptors. Similarly, "How do you work best: in a team or by yourself?" [23] is not a trap requiring you to claim you only ever love teams; a credible answer names both, with the conditions under which you seek collaboration.

## For NHS and values-based interviews

Because the probing is where values are read [11], carry your *reasoning* into the answer, not just your actions: say why patients, colleagues or service users made the choice the right one. The value being sampled is "Working together for patients" — patients come first, and the involvement extends to "professionals inside and outside the NHS" [10]. An answer about smooth internal teamwork that never reaches the patient misses the value's stated object.

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## Published scoring guidance

The Crown Office and Procurator Fiscal Service publishes an operational Success Profiles scoring scale: applications "are scored using the 7 point scale", running from 1 (Not Demonstrated) to 7 (Outstanding Demonstration), with a score of 4 described as "Adequate positive evidence and any negative evidence would not cause concern" [4]. On thresholds, candidates "must demonstrate the minimum essential criteria for all roles, this includes scoring 4 or more in all behaviours", although "a candidate can score a single score of 3 where scores for all other behaviours are high" [4]. Interview benchmarks may be set "where there is a job and business need" and are "detailed in the application pack and invite to interview letter" [4].

Two things follow directly from that published wording. First, scoring is per-behaviour, so a strong overall impression does not rescue a weak Working Together answer. Second, the phrase "any negative evidence would not cause concern" [4] establishes that assessors record *negative* evidence, not only the absence of positive evidence — which is why a story in which the candidate handled a colleague dismissively can score below a story with a smaller achievement.

## Positive indicators

The published descriptors function as the positive-indicator lists. Assessors listening to a teamwork answer are matching against, among others: "proactively contribute", "listen to alternative perspectives … checking understanding", "ask for help when needed and support others" [1]; "Offers assistance and help to others", "Proactively shares knowledge for the benefit and learning of others on an ongoing basis", "strives for compromise in situations of conflict" [5]; and "I work cooperatively with others to get things done, willingly giving help and support to colleagues", "I include others in my thinking and understand their priorities" [12].

## Negative indicators and the limits of checklists

The College of Policing is explicit that its example behaviours "are examples and are not intended as a comprehensive list of all behaviours under the competency", that "there will be many more ways of demonstrating the behaviour", and that consequently "the CVF itself should not be used as a simple checklist, whether as part of selection or assessment" [12] [13]. Assessors "should retain flexibility and be open to diverse ways of demonstrating the behaviours, as long as they are in line with the definition of the competency" [13]. Assessment "should take account of individual circumstances, the specific requirements of a role, and the competency description (not just the behavioural examples)" [13].

On the negative side, the NHS VBR framework's supporting resources include a behaviour framework providing "examples of behaviours which do and do not align with the values of the NHS Constitution" [21] — that is, published negative indicators exist in the NHS system, though the framework document itself does not enumerate teamwork-specific ones [11].

## Question design and interview conduct

For interviewers designing a teamwork assessment, the documented guidance is convergent:

- **Ask about past behaviour, and probe.** "It is good practice to ask about examples of past behaviour, probing for evidence"; hypotheticals are "sometimes helpful" but "most of the interview should focus on past behaviour" [13].
- **Use consistent questions.** CIPD describes structured interviews as "asking a predefined set of questions, in the same order, to all candidates", with responses "scored using consistent criteria against a set of pre-agreed criteria for each question", and advises panels to "ask all candidates the same questions in the same order and score responses according to objective criteria" [20].
- **Prepare questions and use more than one interviewer.** ACAS: "it's a good idea to prepare a set of questions to ask at the interview. Questions must not discriminate"; "ask each applicant the same questions where possible. This is to ensure you're treating applicants in the same way"; "If possible, each interview should be carried out by more than 1 person to reduce the risk of discriminating" [19].
- **Allow enough time per competency.** EPSO allocates roughly ten minutes per competency in its CBI [7] — a useful benchmark for how much airtime a properly probed competency needs.
- **Select the competencies deliberately.** Civil Service recruiting managers choose which behaviours a role assesses, and candidates are not asked to demonstrate all nine [1].

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact. Each pitfall is tied to the descriptor it violates.**

- **The "we" answer.** Narrating the team's achievement without isolating your own actions is the defining failure mode of this competency, and it is a failure precisely because every framework writes the descriptors in the singular: "examples of working together … are when **you**: proactively contribute…, ask for help when needed" [1]; "**I** work cooperatively with others to get things done" [12]. An assessor scoring against those lines has nothing to mark. targetjobs makes the inverse point too: do not imply "that your efforts were the sole reason for your team being able to achieve the collective goal", because an example "that downplays your teammates' contributions or that doesn't explicitly demonstrate cooperation will not bode well with the employer" [23]. The target is the narrow band between the two — visible individual action, inside visible cooperation.
- **The frictionless story.** An example where everyone got on and the work went well cannot evidence the compromise strand, which is published as a core part of the competency: "strives for compromise in situations of conflict" [5]; "challenge assumptions while being willing to compromise if beneficial to progress" [1]. Choose an example containing at least one moment where something had to be negotiated.
- **Treating teamwork as internal only.** Both the Civil Service definition ("people both internally and externally") [1] and the police CVF ("not just with the colleagues we see daily, but across our whole force, with other organisations and with the public") [12] extend the competency beyond your own team. Candidates who only offer within-team stories leave the cross-boundary strand unevidenced.
- **Ignoring the inclusion strand.** Inclusion and anti-discrimination appear in the published descriptors at every Civil Service level [1] and in EPSO's anchors [5], yet most candidate answers never touch them. Evidencing "actively seek and consider input of people from diverse backgrounds and perspectives" [1] is available scoring that is routinely left on the table.
- **Answering the leadership question instead.** "If you were the team leader, how would you ensure your team members stay motivated?" [23] sits in a teamwork question set but samples leadership behaviour. Conversely, giving a "I took charge and directed everyone" answer to a plain teamwork question can read as failing to cooperate. Read which competency the employer named in the advert — Civil Service adverts name the behaviours assessed [1] and EPSO's Notice of Competition fixes the competencies tested [9].
- **Under- or over-reaching the level.** Because descriptors are grade-specific [1] and the CVF's three levels reflect "increasing complexity of behaviours" [12], an example pitched at the wrong altitude scores poorly even when the story is true and good. Check the published level before choosing.
- **Stopping at the Action.** The R in STAR is "what happened as a result of your action and what you learned" [26]; an answer that describes cooperation but never says what it produced omits half of what the structure exists to deliver.
- **For interviewers: improvising the follow-ups.** Probes are the mechanism through which values and depth are read [11] [13], but unplanned probes vary between candidates and undercut the comparability that structured interviewing exists to create [19] [20]. Draft a standard probe set — the seven in the bank above are a starting point — and apply it consistently.
- **For interviewers: using the descriptor lists as a checklist.** Explicitly warned against: the CVF "should not be used as a simple checklist", and the behavioural indicators are "not the totality of the competency" [13]. A candidate who evidences the definition through behaviour not on the list should score.

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.**

1. **Build the three-example set (60 minutes).** Write one example each for: peer collaboration; working across a boundary with a partner or another team; and a disagreement you resolved. For each, write the Action section only, in the first person, in six sentences. If you cannot fill six sentences with "I" statements, the example is too diffuse — pick another.
2. **Descriptor self-scoring (30 minutes).** Take the published Working Together descriptor for the exact grade you are applying to [1] — or the CVF level [12], or the EPSO anchor set [5]. Read your Action section against each bullet and mark hit/miss. Rewrite to hit at least three. Do not manufacture actions you did not take; look for real actions you omitted because they felt too small to mention.
3. **The friction audit (15 minutes).** Underline the sentence in each example where something was genuinely contested. If any example has no such sentence, it will not evidence the compromise anchor [5] — replace it.
4. **Probe survival drill (pairs, 20 minutes).** Have a partner ask you any question from the bank, then hit you with all seven follow-up probes in sequence without letting you re-tell the story. Most unprepared answers collapse at probe 2 ("Who disagreed with you, and what exactly did you say to them?") because the original answer summarised rather than recounted.
5. **Timed answer practice (3 × 10 minutes).** Deliver one sourced question aloud in under three minutes. Then deliver the same answer stretched to fill ten minutes of questioning, which is roughly EPSO's per-competency allocation [7] — the stretch exposes whether you have detail behind the summary.
6. **Pronoun count (5 minutes).** Transcribe or record one answer and count "I" versus "we". This is a crude proxy but a fast one: if "we" dominates the Action section, rewrite it against the singular descriptors [1] [12].
7. **Interviewer drill (pairs, 40 minutes).** Choose one framework level. Write three teamwork questions and agree the positive indicators you will score against *before* interviewing, per structured practice [20]. Interview two people with identical questions and probes, score independently, then compare. Divergence in scores usually traces to indicators that were never operationalised.

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — every claim cited. Signposting only — not legal advice.**

- **Reasonable adjustments are engaged by this competency in a specific way.** Equality Act 2010 s.20 sets out the duty to make adjustments, comprising three requirements: taking reasonable steps to avoid substantial disadvantage caused by a provision, criterion or practice; addressing disadvantage arising from physical features; and providing auxiliary aids where needed to avoid substantial disadvantage — and those subject to the duty may not require a disabled person to bear the cost of compliance [15]. This bites hardest where teamwork is assessed through **group exercises and role-play**, which the Civil Service lists among the methods used to assess behaviours [1]. GOV.UK guidance confirms that candidates can request adjustments to the recruitment process [18].
- **The College of Policing addresses this directly.** Its framework states that the CVF "should be used in ways that allow for differing abilities. This includes those that stem from disabilities or neurodiversity", that assessments "should allow for candidates to demonstrate strengths in different areas", and that "reasonable adjustments should always be made for people who have disabilities that impact on their ability to demonstrate the competencies. This is in line with equality legislation" [12]. This is unusually explicit framework-level acknowledgement that a behavioural competency can be demonstrated in more than one way.
- **A live fairness risk specific to teamwork.** Because teamwork descriptors reward observable social behaviour — rapport-building, "showing genuine interest when listening" [1] — assessments of this competency can disadvantage neurodivergent candidates and candidates whose cultural norms around self-presentation differ, unless assessors score against the *definition* rather than a stylistic checklist. The framework guidance anticipates exactly this: assessors "should retain flexibility and be open to diverse ways of demonstrating the behaviours, as long as they are in line with the definition of the competency" [13]. *(The obligation and the guidance are cited; the inference that teamwork is a higher-risk competency for this effect is this document's reasoned view.)*
- **Protected characteristics.** Equality Act 2010 s.4 lists the nine protected characteristics against which discrimination in recruitment is unlawful [14]. ACAS operationalises this for interviews: questions must not discriminate; ask each applicant the same questions where possible; use more than one interviewer where possible to reduce the risk of discriminating; and by law employers must ask anyone coming to an interview to say if they need reasonable adjustments to attend [19].
- **EU market.** Council Directive 2000/78/EC establishes a general framework for equal treatment in employment and occupation, expressly covering conditions for access to employment including selection criteria and recruitment conditions, prohibiting direct and indirect discrimination on grounds of religion or belief, disability, age or sexual orientation, and imposing reasonable-accommodation obligations for disabled persons [16].
- **Candidate data.** Interview notes and competency scores about identifiable candidates are personal data; in the EU their processing falls under Regulation (EU) 2016/679 [17]. Assessors' written comments on a candidate's teamwork — which can be unusually personal in character — are disclosable to the data subject on a subject access request.

# Risks & Caveats

- **Descriptors change.** The GOV.UK Behaviours guidance carries an "Updated 29 January 2025" stamp [1] and the police CVF was revised in 2024, with forces given until 1 May 2025 to transition [12]. Verify the live descriptor before high-stakes use; review is due 2027-07-20.
- **The 7-point scale is departmental, not universal.** The scoring scale and the "4 or more in all behaviours" threshold quoted here are published by COPFS [4], one organisation applying Success Profiles. Other departments publish variants and some publish none. Do not present the 7-point scale as a Civil Service-wide standard; check the application pack, which COPFS itself says is where benchmarks are detailed [4].
- **The five-strand structure is synthesis.** The strand table in Core Reference is this knowledge base's organising device. Every cell is cited, but the framework publishers do not describe their competencies as having five strands.
- **Composed questions are illustrative.** Thirty-one of the 51 question-bank entries were written for this knowledge base in the style of the cited descriptors. They are not employer questions and must never be represented as such.
- **Sourced questions evidence what is published, not what a given employer asks.** The targetjobs and Oxford question sets [23] [25] show what careers services publish as typical teamwork questions; no inference about any named employer's actual question set is supported.
- **Sector coverage is partial.** Four frameworks are covered in depth. Teaching, social work, the armed forces, financial-services conduct frameworks and individual corporate competency dictionaries are not.
- **The NHS evidence has a recency gap.** The HEE Values Based Recruitment Framework cited here dates from March 2016 [11] and is hosted on the legacy HEE site following HEE's merger into NHS England. It remains the substantive published framework, but readers should check NHS Employers [21] for current practice.
- **EPSO's model is in flux.** The competency framework cited is the 2023 version [5] [6], introduced with EPSO's new competition model. Which competencies a competition tests is settled only by its Notice of Competition [9].

# Open Questions

- **Are there published Civil Service negative indicators for Working Together?** The COPFS scale references "negative evidence" [4] without enumerating it for specific behaviours. Departmental assessor packs likely contain negative-indicator lists; a stable public URL has not been found. Resolution: FOI-published assessor guidance or a departmental recruitment pack.
- **What does the NHS behaviour framework say about "working together for patients" specifically?** NHS Employers references a supporting behaviour framework with examples of aligned and non-aligned behaviours [21], but the per-value indicator lists were not located at a citable URL. Resolution: locate the current NHS England VBR resource pack.
- **How are group exercises scored against teamwork descriptors?** The Civil Service lists group exercises among behaviour-assessment methods [1], but no published marking scheme for a group exercise against Working Together was found. This may be deliberate (test security). Belongs to the Formats category.
- **Do EPSO CBI assessors score anchors individually?** The anchors are published [5] and interview time per competency is published [7], but whether assessors mark anchor-by-anchor or holistically per competency is not stated in any source located.
- **Is there evidence on adjustment practice for teamwork assessment specifically?** The obligation is clear [15] and the policing framework acknowledges it [12], but published data on what adjustments are actually granted for group exercises and role-play was not found.
