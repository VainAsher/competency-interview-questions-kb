# Executive Summary

This is the Tier-2 deep-dive for the resilience and adaptability competency family, the child of **competencies-foundation**. Where the foundation document placed this family in a mapping table, this document goes to the level below: the exact framework wording that defines it, the published behavioural indicators an assessor scores against, a bank of 34 practice questions with provenance, and coaching on the hardest problem this family presents — choosing a genuine failure or pressure example that demonstrates recovery without damaging your candidacy.

The evidence picture is unusually fragmented for this family, and that fragmentation is itself a finding. The UK Civil Service does **not** have a Behaviour called "Resilience"; resilience appears instead as a named *Strength* — "Resilient: You have inner composure, recover quickly from setbacks and learn from them" [3] — while adaptability is split across the Strength "Adaptable" [3] and the Behaviour "Changing and Improving" [2]. EPSO's pre-2023 assessment-centre model *did* assess a competency called Resilience, confirmed in a published EPSO SCBI assignment [7]; the 2023 Competency Framework replaced it with **Self-management**, whose anchors carry the resilience content explicitly — "Responds calmly to obstacles", "Applies strategies to help prevent stress and setbacks", "Is adaptable concerning changing work conditions" [4]. The College of Policing's 2024 CVF distributes the same material across "We are emotionally aware" and "We take ownership", and is notable for framing resilience as a *wellbeing* matter rather than a stoicism test [9]. Candidates who prepare for a competency called "resilience" and then meet a question scored against one of these differently-named criteria will mis-target their evidence.

Document-level confidence is **Medium**. The framework definitions, behavioural indicators and the COPFS 1–7 scoring scale rest on official primary sources and would individually merit High. Two things pull the rating down: the legacy EPSO "Resilience" definition is attested by a named practitioner training source quoting EPSO guidelines rather than by a document this pipeline opened directly [27], and a substantial part of the private-sector/graduate-scheme picture rests on careers-service question lists, which evidence *what is published as typical* rather than what any named employer asks. Under the confidence rubric's hard floor, that combination caps the document at Medium.

# Key Takeaways

- The UK Civil Service has no Behaviour named "Resilience". It is a **Strength** — "You have inner composure, recover quickly from setbacks and learn from them" — and "Adaptable" is a separate Strength: "Your effectiveness is not affected by change." *(documented)* [3]
- Strengths are assessed differently from Behaviours: GOV.UK explicitly tells candidates "Do not rehearse your answers - because we are looking for your initial response." Preparing a polished STAR story for a strengths question can work against you. *(documented)* [3]
- EPSO's 2023 Competency Framework folded resilience into **Self-management**, whose five anchors include responding calmly to obstacles, distancing oneself from frustration, and applying strategies to prevent stress and setbacks — including "asking for help or support when necessary". Help-seeking is scored as evidence *for* the competency, not against it. *(documented)* [4]
- EPSO's earlier model assessed a standalone competency called Resilience; a published EPSO assignment for competitions EPSO/AD/365-370/19 lists it among the six competencies the interview assessed. Older preparation material describing that competency is not fabricated — it is superseded. *(documented)* [7]
- The College of Policing 2024 CVF frames this family around wellbeing and support-seeking: Level 1 of "We are emotionally aware" includes "I seek out support to help me perform effectively" and "I recognise that some situations might affect my ability to deal with stress and pressure". *(documented)* [9]
- Where a published scoring scale exists, the bar is evidence-density, not drama: COPFS scores Success Profiles evidence 1–7, with 4 "Acceptable Demonstration" as the normal minimum across behaviours. *(documented)* [11]
- Questions about pressure, failure and coping sit close to health and disability. Equality Act 2010 s.60 restricts asking about health or disability *before* offering work, subject to listed exceptions — a legal constraint on interviewers, and a shield candidates should know they have. *(documented)* [14]
- The strongest failure answers are genuinely costly and end in a changed practice, not a rescued outcome. Choosing a "failure" that was secretly a success is the most common self-inflicted wound in this family. *(guidance)*

# Purpose

This document answers, for candidates: *"Which competency is actually being scored when I am asked about pressure, change or failure — and how much genuine failure is it safe to disclose?"* For interviewers and HR: *"What do the published frameworks say this competency is, what indicators can I defensibly score against, and where does probing about pressure and coping become a legal risk?"*

It assumes the reader has the shared vocabulary from **competencies-foundation** (competency families, structured interviewing, STAR) and does not restate it.

# Scope

**Covered:** the named framework criteria that carry resilience and adaptability content in UK and EU public-sector frameworks and in graduate/private-sector practice; the distinction between resilience-as-Behaviour and resilience-as-Strength in Success Profiles; the EPSO 2023 Self-management and Learning-as-a-skill anchors and the superseded pre-2023 Resilience competency; College of Policing CVF 2024 indicators; NHS values-based recruitment as it touches pressure; a 34-question bank across handling setbacks, adapting to change, sustained pressure and follow-up probes; evidence-selection coaching for failure examples; one worked STAR skeleton; assessor-side scoring evidence; and the Equality Act constraints specific to health-adjacent questioning.

**Not covered:** general STAR/CARL technique (see **techniques-foundation**); stress in the sense of assessment-centre stress exercises and interview formats (see the Formats category); clinical or occupational-health assessment; performance management of existing staff; and the other seven competency families, each of which has its own Tier-2 document. Sector-specific resilience requirements for uniformed and emergency roles are touched on only via the published CVF wording.

# Definitions

Terms used with a narrowed meaning in this document. General terms live in `GLOSSARY.md`.

- **Strength (Success Profiles sense)** — "the things that we do regularly, do well and that motivate us"; Civil Service strengths are not defined by job grade [3]. Contrast with a Behaviour, which is graded and evidenced by prepared examples.
- **Anchor (EPSO sense)** — an observable element of a general competency; the 2023 framework decomposes each of the eight competencies into anchors used to recognise and assess it [4].
- **Self-management (EPSO 2023)** — the competency that carries the resilience content in the current EPSO framework: "Organises own tasks, demonstrates responsibility and a constructive attitude at all times" [4].
- **Resilience (EPSO, pre-2023)** — the superseded standalone competency, assessed in the earlier assessment-centre model [7]. Described in practitioner training material quoting EPSO's guidelines governing general competitions as remaining effective under a heavy workload, handling organisational frustrations positively and adapting to a changing work environment [27].
- **Recovery arc** — this document's term (coaching, not a framework term) for the structure of a strong setback answer: the setback is real, the response is deliberate, and the ending is a durable change in how the candidate works.

# Framework Context

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## UK Civil Service: split across Behaviours and Strengths

Success Profiles can draw on five elements — Behaviours, Strengths, Ability, Experience and Technical — with the mix depending on the role [1]. Resilience and adaptability are unusual in this family because they sit primarily in the **Strengths** element rather than the Behaviours element.

The Strengths guidance names, among others [3]:

- **Resilient** — "You have inner composure, recover quickly from setbacks and learn from them."
- **Adaptable** — "You can adapt to variations in work or environment. Your effectiveness is not affected by change. You are flexible and versatile. You act as an advocate for change."
- **Courageous** — "You are an innovator who tries new approaches. You push yourself to work outside your comfort zone."
- **Improver** — "You look for better ways of doing things and enjoy coming up with new and original ideas."

Strengths are described as things done regularly, done well and that motivate the person; they are not defined by job grade [3]. Crucially for preparation, GOV.UK instructs candidates: "Do not rehearse your answers - because we are looking for your initial response", advising instead that candidates reflect on the qualities in the job advert [3].

On the Behaviours side, the closest named Behaviour is **Changing and Improving**: "Seek out opportunities to create effective change and suggest innovative ideas for improvement. Review ways of working, including seeking and providing feedback" [2]. Its published level examples carry the adaptability content explicitly. At AA/AO grade, examples include to "respond in an effective and appropriate manner when emergencies arise"; at EO grade, to "take a positive, open approach to the possibility of change and encourage others to do the same" and to "identify and act on the effects changes are having on your role and that of the team"; at HEO/SEO, to "clearly explain the reasons for change to colleagues and how to implement them, supporting individuals with different needs to adapt to change" [2].

Setback-resistance appears under a different Behaviour again. **Delivering at Pace** — "Take responsibility for delivering timely and quality results with focus and drive" — lists at EO grade the example "have a positive and focused attitude to achieving outcomes, despite any setbacks", alongside "ensure that colleagues are supported where tasks are challenging" [2]. At Director and Director General grades, Changing and Improving expects leaders to "create a culture of innovation, flexibility and responsiveness enabling departments to swiftly respond to change" [2].

## EU institutions: EPSO Self-management, and the superseded Resilience competency

The current EPSO Competency Framework defines eight general competencies, each decomposed into observable anchors [4]. Resilience content sits in **Self-management** — "Organises own tasks, demonstrates responsibility and a constructive attitude at all times" — whose five anchors are [4]:

- "Organises own work effectively."
- "Takes responsibility for own results and demonstrates commitment to achieve their goals."
- "Responds calmly to obstacles and is able to distance themselves from frustration and negativity."
- "Applies strategies to help prevent stress and setbacks (e.g. asking for help or support when necessary)."
- "Is adaptable concerning changing work conditions and responds to fluctuating circumstances in a positive manner."

The learning-from-failure content sits in **Learning as a skill** — "Actively manages own learning, growth and self-improvement" — whose anchors include "Responds positively to feedback and to constructive criticism", "Seizes every opportunity to learn, even from failure", and "Identifies opportunities to apply lessons learned and improve their performance" [4]. Change-driving sits in **Intrapreneurship**: "Drives change, inspires and empowers others in implementing change", with the anchor "Takes on new challenges and initiates change, takes the lead when required" [4].

Before the 2023 framework, EPSO assessed a standalone competency called **Resilience**. This is documented in EPSO's own published assignment for open competitions EPSO/AD/365-370/19, which states that the Situational Competency Based Interview "is designed to assess the following competencies: Analysis & Problem Solving, Learning & Development, Prioritising & Organising, Resilience, Working with Others, and Leadership" [7]. Named practitioner training material quoting EPSO's guidelines governing general competitions renders the definition as remaining effective under a heavy workload, handling organisational frustrations positively and adapting to a changing work environment [27]. Candidates should treat that definition as historical: for any live competition, the Notice of Competition is "the only official document that contains detailed information about the actual tests used by EPSO for a given competition" [8].

Format matters for how much evidence is expected. In the competency-based interview, "Candidates are interviewed for about 10 minutes per competency" [5]. The situational variant assesses "general competencies by asking the candidate how he/she would react in a specific situation" [6] — a hypothetical framing, unlike the past-behaviour framing of most UK practice.

## Policing: College of Policing CVF (2024)

The CVF was introduced in 2016 and updated in 2024 to reflect the new Code of Ethics; the 2024 edition retains six competencies at three levels plus three values, having removed the 2016 "clusters" for simplicity [10]. The College states that the refreshed behaviours reflect a workforce where "wellbeing, resilience and protection of others are promoted" [10]. Forces had until May 2025 to transition from the 2016 version [10].

Resilience content sits chiefly in **"We are emotionally aware"**. The framework states that "Understanding ourselves means that we can improve our own resilience and therefore cope more effectively during challenging and emotionally charged situations" [9]. Level 1 indicators include [9]:

- "I seek to understand and manage my own emotions to remain effective, even under pressure."
- "I seek out support to help me perform effectively."
- "I recognise that some situations might affect my ability to deal with stress and pressure."

Level 2 moves the competency outward to others: "I provide others with confidence by staying focused and composed in stressful situations"; "I monitor the wellbeing of others, taking responsibility to ensure appropriate support is provided to help colleagues stay at their best, without stigma or judgement"; and "I recognise when my colleagues are affected by the demands, stresses and traumas that can exist in policing, and support them however I can" [9]. Level 3 expects leaders to "champion behaviours and ways of working that promote wellbeing throughout my organisation" [9].

The persistence-and-ownership content sits in **"We take ownership"**, whose Level 1 indicators include "I approach tasks with enthusiasm and a positive attitude, adapting quickly from one situation to the next" and "I respond to demanding and tough situations, demonstrating commitment to tackling the issue" [9]. Level 3 includes "I promote a culture that responds to mistakes with learning rather than with blame" [9].

## NHS: values-based recruitment

NHS recruitment in England sits within the NHS Constitution values, which include "commitment to quality of care" and "compassion", and the Constitution commits staff to respond with "humanity and kindness to each person's pain, distress, anxiety or need" [12]. Health Education England's Values Based Recruitment Framework (March 2016) is the programme-level document; stakeholder evidence recorded in it includes the view that "simulation/scenario-based assessment or group work can really reveal how individuals display their values in a high pressure setting or when working to a common goal" [13]. The VBR Framework does not publish a standard question set, so the pressure-related NHS questions in this document's bank are composed rather than sourced.

## Private sector and graduate schemes

There is no single private-sector framework. Prospects lists resilience, adaptability and flexibility among the "key competencies regularly sought after by employers", alongside commercial awareness, communication, conflict resolution, decisiveness, independence, leadership, problem solving, organisation and teamwork [26]. The University of Oxford Careers Service groups its published example questions under headings that include "Self-management and resilience" [25]. EY's published interview guidance frames the setback probe directly, advising candidates to be ready for "When it didn't go so well, how did you handle that and what happened?", and states that "your mindset is as important as your skillset" [28].

# Core Reference

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## The single most consequential fact: Behaviour or Strength?

For UK Civil Service applications, whether resilience is being assessed as a Behaviour or a Strength changes the correct preparation strategy, and the two published instructions pull in opposite directions.

Behaviours are assessed through written application examples, judgement tests, interviews, assessment centres and job simulations, and candidates may be asked to give examples of when they have demonstrated a particular behaviour [2] — that is, prepared, evidenced past examples. Strengths, by contrast, are assessed through interviews, video interviews, situational tests, simulations or personality assessments, and the guidance states there are no correct or incorrect answers; candidates are told not to rehearse answers because assessors are "looking for your initial response", and to prepare by reflecting on personal strengths and preferred working styles [3].

Because "Resilient" and "Adaptable" are named Strengths [3] while "Changing and Improving" is a Behaviour [2], a single advert can require both a rehearsed change example *and* an unrehearsed reaction to a resilience prompt. The advert names which elements are assessed [1] [2].

## What "evidence" means at each level

The published level examples show the competency escalating from self-regulation, to team support, to organisational culture. This progression is explicit in all three detailed frameworks:

- **Civil Service, Changing and Improving:** AA/AO "learn new procedures and help colleagues to do the same"; EO "help others to understand changes and the reasons they are being put in place"; HEO/SEO "supporting individuals with different needs to adapt to change"; Deputy Director "manage change effectively and respond promptly to critical events"; Director "create a culture of innovation, flexibility and responsiveness" [2].
- **College of Policing, We are emotionally aware:** L1 manage own emotions "to remain effective, even under pressure"; L2 "provide others with confidence by staying focused and composed in stressful situations"; L3 "champion behaviours and ways of working that promote wellbeing throughout my organisation" [9].
- **College of Policing, We take ownership:** L1 "I respond to demanding and tough situations"; L2 "I take personal responsibility for seeing events through to a satisfactory conclusion and dealing with any problems both promptly and openly"; L3 "I promote a culture that responds to mistakes with learning rather than with blame" [9].

## Help-seeking is scored positively

Three independent frameworks explicitly credit asking for support, which contradicts a widespread candidate assumption that resilience means coping alone:

- EPSO Self-management anchor: "Applies strategies to help prevent stress and setbacks (e.g. asking for help or support when necessary)" [4].
- College of Policing Level 1: "I seek out support to help me perform effectively" [9].
- Civil Service Working Together at EO grade: "offer support and help to colleagues when in need, including consideration of your own and their wellbeing" [2].

## Learning is part of the criterion, not a nicety

The Civil Service Strength definition of Resilient ends "…and learn from them" [3]. The EPSO Learning-as-a-skill anchors include "Seizes every opportunity to learn, even from failure" and "Identifies opportunities to apply lessons learned and improve their performance" [4]. The CVF expects a culture responding to mistakes "with learning rather than with blame" [9]. An answer that describes recovery but no changed practice omits a documented element of the criterion.

## How the CVF is meant to be used

The College is explicit that the behavioural indicators are examples, not a checklist: "The list of behavioural indicators is not the totality of the competency", and the CVF "should not be used as a simple checklist, whether as part of selection or assessment, or professional development review" [10]. Assessors are told to "retain flexibility and be open to diverse ways of demonstrating the behaviours, as long as they are in line with the definition of the competency" [10]. On interview design, the College states it is "good practice to ask about examples of past behaviour, probing for evidence", that hypothetical questions are sometimes helpful, but that "most of the interview should focus on past behaviour" [10].

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact. It points back to the citations above and introduces no new facts.**

## First, work out which criterion is being scored

Before choosing an example, decide which of three things the question is testing, because the winning evidence differs:

- **A Behaviour (prepared example expected).** If the advert names Changing and Improving [2], bring a rehearsed, structured change example.
- **A Strength (unrehearsed response expected).** If the advert names Strengths, GOV.UK tells you not to rehearse [3]. Prepare by knowing your own patterns — what energises you, how you actually react to disruption — not by memorising a script.
- **A hypothetical (EPSO SCBI).** The situational interview asks how you *would* react [6]. Past-example answers under-serve it; reason aloud through the situation instead.

## Choosing the failure example — the hard part

This family punishes evasion more than it punishes failure. Three selection tests, in order:

1. **Was it genuinely costly?** A "failure" where the project succeeded anyway is not a failure, and assessors hear the dodge. The criterion asks you to "recover quickly from setbacks and learn from them" [3] — a costless setback evidences neither.
2. **Was the cause within your reach?** Blaming a reorganisation or a colleague gives the assessor no behaviour of yours to score. The CVF Level 1 indicator is "I take responsibility for my own actions and decisions" [9].
3. **Did it change how you work?** The learning element is documented, not decorative: "Seizes every opportunity to learn, even from failure" and "Identifies opportunities to apply lessons learned and improve their performance" [4].

Safe territory that satisfies all three: a misjudged estimate, a stakeholder you failed to consult, an escalation you left too late, a piece of work rejected on quality. Territory to avoid: failures involving dishonesty or safeguarding — these engage a different competency family, treated in **integrity-values-ethics** — and anything you are still visibly angry about, since "distance themselves from frustration and negativity" is itself an anchor [4].

**On disclosing health or personal circumstances.** You are not required to explain *why* a period was hard. If a genuine example involves illness, bereavement or a disability, you can describe the pressure and your response without disclosing the underlying cause — and an interviewer's freedom to probe that cause is legally constrained (see Legal & Fairness Considerations, and s.60 at [14]).

## Worked STAR skeleton

Composed question, "Sustained pressure" number 6: *"Describe a time you recognised you were at risk of not coping, and what you did about it."* Target criteria: EPSO Self-management anchors on responding calmly and applying strategies to prevent stress and setbacks [4]; CVF Level 1 "I recognise that some situations might affect my ability to deal with stress and pressure" and "I seek out support to help me perform effectively" [9].

- **Situation (2 sentences).** Two colleagues left within a month; I absorbed their casework on top of a fixed statutory deadline. Volume roughly doubled with no change to the deadline.
- **Task (1 sentence).** I had to protect the statutory deadline without letting the routine casework quietly fail — and without burning out four weeks into a six-month gap.
- **Action (the bulk).** I spent an hour listing everything I now owned and marked what was genuinely time-critical versus what merely felt urgent. I took that list to my manager the same week rather than waiting for a scheduled one-to-one, and asked explicitly for two things: agreement on what would slip, and temporary cover for the routine queue. I proposed the trade-off myself rather than presenting a problem. I set a weekly checkpoint so slippage was visible early. When a stakeholder pushed back on a deferred item, I explained the trade-off and the date it would be picked up, rather than quietly re-absorbing it.
- **Result and learning.** The statutory deadline was met; two routine items slipped by three weeks, agreed and communicated in advance rather than discovered. The change I kept: I now flag capacity risk at the point I notice it, not at the point it becomes a missed deadline — and I bring a proposed trade-off with the flag.

Why this scores: it evidences the *recognition* anchor before the coping one, treats asking for support as a deliberate strategy (which EPSO names explicitly [4] and the CVF rewards [9]), keeps the ending on a durable behavioural change [3], and never asks the assessor to accept a rescued outcome as evidence of resilience.

## Calibrating the ending

Do not end on "and everything worked out". End on what is different now. Where the framework is Civil Service Strengths, keep it lighter and more reflective — the guidance asks for your initial response, not a case study [3]. Where it is EPSO Self-management, name the strategy you applied, because "applies strategies" is the anchor's own verb [4].

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## Published scoring: the COPFS 1–7 scale

The most explicitly published Success Profiles scoring scale traced for this document is used by the Crown Office and Procurator Fiscal Service, which rates evidence 1–7 [11]:

| Score | Rating | Definition [11] |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Not Demonstrated | "No positive evidence and/ or substantial negative evidence" |
| 2 | Minimal Demonstration | "Limited positive evidence and/ or mainly negative evidence" |
| 3 | Moderate Demonstration | "Moderate positive evidence but some negative evidence" |
| 4 | Acceptable Demonstration | "Adequate positive evidence and any negative evidence would not cause concern" |
| 5 | Good Demonstration | "Substantial positive evidence of the behaviour" |
| 6 | Strong Demonstration | "Substantial positive evidence; includes some evidence of exceeding expectations" |
| 7 | Outstanding Demonstration | "The evidence provided wholly exceeds expectation at this level" |

COPFS states the minimum requirement is a score of 4 across behaviours, though a single 3 can be tolerated where other scores are high, and that additional benchmarks may apply — such as a 5 on lead behaviours or a combined score threshold — communicated in the application pack [11]. COPFS notes strengths are not currently used in its process [11]. The 1–7 scale itself is central Cabinet Office practice, but these benchmarks are COPFS's own — see Risks & Caveats.

The practical reading for both audiences: the scale rewards *evidence density* and penalises negative evidence. A dramatic story with thin personal action scores lower than an undramatic one with dense, specific action.

## What assessors are told to look for

- **EPSO.** Assessment is against published anchors [4]; in the CBI, "Candidates are interviewed for about 10 minutes per competency" [5], so a competency is evidenced in a bounded window with follow-up probing. Which competencies apply is set by the Notice of Competition, "the only official document that contains detailed information about the actual tests used" [8]. In the pre-2023 model, the SCBI assignment told candidates in advance exactly which six competencies were assessed, Resilience among them [7].
- **College of Policing.** Assessors must "retain flexibility and be open to diverse ways of demonstrating the behaviours", and the indicator list "is not the totality of the competency"; the CVF "should not be used as a simple checklist" [10]. Candidates should be "clearly told which competencies and values are being assessed", and assessors should receive "clear guidance and training … so they know how candidates are expected to deal with the exercise" [10]. Most of the interview "should focus on past behaviour" [10].
- **Civil Service.** Behaviours are assessed across written examples, tests, interviews, assessment centres and job simulations [2]; the level examples in the published guidance are the reference points for what counts at grade [2].
- **Structured method.** CIPD guidance describes structured interviewing as a predefined set of questions asked in the same order to all candidates, scored against consistent, pre-agreed criteria [20], and the ranking of structured interviews among selection methods is supported in the revised meta-analytic estimates [29].

## A design caution specific to this family

The College of Policing's own framing places resilience next to wellbeing — the 2024 update reflects a workforce where "wellbeing, resilience and protection of others are promoted" [10], and the competency credits recognising when situations "might affect my ability to deal with stress and pressure" [9]. An interview design that rewards only stoicism scores against the published criterion, not with it.

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact.**

- **Preparing a Behaviour answer for a Strengths question.** The most specific pitfall in this family: rehearsing a polished resilience story when GOV.UK has told candidates "Do not rehearse your answers" for Strengths [3]. Check which Success Profile elements the advert names [1].
- **The fake failure.** Presenting a success wearing a failure costume ("I'm a perfectionist"; "the project was late but we delivered brilliantly"). It fails the documented criterion, which requires an actual setback to recover from and learn from [3].
- **The blame answer.** Attributing the setback entirely to a reorganisation, a manager or a supplier. It leaves nothing of yours to score against "I take responsibility for my own actions and decisions" [9], and can read as the opposite of "distance themselves from frustration and negativity" [4].
- **Ending at recovery.** Stopping once the crisis passed, with no changed practice. This omits an element the frameworks state explicitly [3] [4] [9].
- **Treating help-seeking as weakness.** Answers built on "I just worked through the night alone" contradict anchors that name asking for support as a strategy [4] and an indicator that credits seeking support [9].
- **Volunteering health information you were not asked for.** Understandable in a question about a hard period, but it moves the conversation into territory the interviewer is legally constrained from exploring [14]. Describe the pressure and your response; the cause is yours to withhold.
- **Answering a hypothetical with a past example, or the reverse.** EPSO's SCBI asks how you *would* react [6]; the CVF says most of a police interview should focus on past behaviour [10]. Mismatching wastes the window.
- **Single-register examples.** Using only a deadline-crunch story when the criterion spans setbacks, change and sustained load. The mapping table above shows the sub-themes assessors sample across.
- **For interviewers: probing "why was that so hard for you?"** A natural follow-up that drifts toward health and disability. Probe the *behaviour* — what the candidate did, what changed — not the underlying cause [14].
- **For interviewers: rewarding drama over evidence.** Scales like the COPFS 1–7 reward evidence quality, not story severity [11]; and the CVF warns against checklist-scoring while demanding openness to "diverse ways of demonstrating the behaviours" [10].
- **For interviewers: designing a stoicism test.** Scoring down a candidate who describes escalating or seeking support inverts the published criteria [4] [9] [10].

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.**

1. **The three-failure audit (45 minutes).** Write down three genuine setbacks from the last three years. Score each against the three selection tests in Model Answer Guidance — genuinely costly, cause within your reach, changed how you work. Discard any scoring below 2 out of 3. Most people find their instinctive first choice fails test 1.
2. **Anchor self-scoring (30 minutes).** Take your best pressure example and score it line by line against the five EPSO Self-management anchors [4]. Mark which anchors your current telling evidences. Rewrite the Action section so it evidences at least three — particularly "applies strategies", which most drafts leave implicit.
3. **Level-matching drill (20 minutes).** Read the Changing and Improving examples at the grade above and below your target [2]. Retell your change example twice — once at each level. The difference should be whether you changed *your* work, helped *others* adapt, or shaped the *culture*. Match the target grade.
4. **The unrehearsed run (15 minutes, with a partner).** For strengths practice, have a partner ask three of the Adapting-to-change questions cold, with no preparation time, and answer in 60 seconds each. This deliberately trains the mode GOV.UK asks for [3]. Notice which answers come easily — those are your real strengths.
5. **Probe survival (20 minutes, pairs).** Answer one setback question, then have your partner ask all nine Follow-up probes from the Question Bank in sequence. Most answers collapse at "What did it cost?" or "Has that change stuck?". Rebuild the answer so both have honest replies.
6. **Interviewer drill: fair probing (30 minutes, pairs).** Draft four probes for a pressure question. Then check each against the s.60 constraint [14]: does it ask about *behaviour* or about *health*? Rewrite any that stray. Agree the scoring criteria before the mock, per structured practice [20], and score against the COPFS descriptors [11].
7. **The wellbeing register (15 minutes).** Write two sentences on how you notice you are approaching overload, and two on what you do about it. This is the raw material for the CVF Level 1 recognition indicator [9] and the EPSO prevention anchor [4], and almost nobody prepares it.

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — every claim cited. Signposting only — not legal advice.**

This family sits closer to protected characteristics than any other except integrity. Questions about pressure, coping and failure can shade into questions about health, disability and mental health, which are legally constrained.

## UK: Equality Act 2010, section 60 — health and disability enquiries

Section 60 restricts an employer from asking about a job applicant's health or disability before offering work or including the applicant in a pool of successful candidates [14]. The restriction is not absolute; s.60 sets out permitted purposes, including establishing whether the applicant will be able to comply with a requirement to undergo an assessment, determining whether the applicant can carry out a function intrinsic to the work (taking account of reasonable adjustments), monitoring diversity in the range of applicants, taking positive action in relation to disabled persons, and where having a particular disability is an occupational requirement that is a proportionate means of achieving a legitimate aim; national-security vetting is also addressed [14]. Contravention is enforceable as an unlawful act under the enforcement provisions the section references [14].

Practical implication for interviewers: a question such as *"Tell me about a time you worked under pressure"* is fine; *"Have you ever been signed off with stress?"* or *"Why did you find that period so difficult — is there a health issue?"* is not a behaviour probe and is the kind of health enquiry s.60 restricts [14]. Probe the actions and the outcome, not the cause.

Practical implication for candidates: you are not obliged to explain the personal cause of a difficult period, and an interviewer who presses for it is on contested ground [14].

## UK: reasonable adjustments

The duty to make reasonable adjustments arises under s.20 of the Act [15], and disability is one of the nine protected characteristics listed at s.4 [16]. GOV.UK guidance to employers states adjustments must be made where reasonable, giving examples such as allowing "wheelchair users to have their interview on the ground floor" and "candidates to complete a written test using a computer", and notes an employer "can ask if a candidate needs an adjustment to the recruitment process to allow them to be considered for the job" [17]. ACAS states that "By law you must ask anyone coming to an interview to tell you if they need reasonable adjustments so they can attend", offering examples including extra time for written tests and providing a sign language interpreter [19].

The College of Policing applies this to its own framework, stating that the CVF "should be used in ways which allow for differing abilities, including those which stem from disabilities or neurodiversity", that assessments "should allow for different expression of the competencies", that "Reasonable adjustments should be provided for those who need them under the Equality Act (2010)", and that accommodations can also be made for circumstances such as pregnancy or breastfeeding, or an injury or illness making the assessment harder to complete [10].

**Why this matters specifically here:** a competency assessed partly through composure and pace can disadvantage candidates whose disability affects processing speed, fatigue or anxiety. The adjustment duty is the corrective, and the CVF's own guidance anticipates it [10] [15].

## Consistency and non-discrimination

ACAS advises asking each applicant the same questions where possible "to ensure you're treating applicants in the same way", states that "Questions must not discriminate", and expects interviewers to understand discrimination law [19]. CIPD's structured-interview description — same questions, same order, consistent scoring criteria — is itself a fairness control [20].

## EU

Council Directive 2000/78/EC establishes a general framework for equal treatment in employment and occupation, expressly covering conditions for access to employment including selection criteria and recruitment conditions, prohibits direct and indirect discrimination on grounds including disability, and imposes reasonable-accommodation obligations in respect of disabled persons [18]. EPSO's selection procedures operate within this framework, and the Notice of Competition governs the tests used in any given competition [8].

# Risks & Caveats

- **The 1–7 scale is government-wide; the COPFS benchmarks are not.** Cabinet Office guidance states that "Experience, technical skills and behaviours should be scored using the 7 point scale. Strengths should be scored using the 4 point scale" [30], so the scale can be presented to a candidate as *the* Civil Service scale. Three qualifications survive: that central pack dates from 2020, and no refreshed central scoring publication was found after the January 2025 behaviour-guide refresh; the seven descriptor labels reproduced above come from COPFS [11], still the only located source publishing them; and the benchmarks are COPFS's own — other departments set their own, and COPFS itself notes strengths are not currently used in its process [11].
- **The legacy EPSO Resilience definition is second-hand.** That Resilience was an assessed competency is confirmed by EPSO's own published assignment [7]. The *wording* of its definition — heavy workload, organisational frustrations, changing work environment — comes from a named practitioner training source quoting EPSO's competition guidelines [27]; attempts to retrieve the underlying EPSO guidelines or a EUR-Lex competition notice directly were unsuccessful during this document's research window, as EUR-Lex blocked automated retrieval. Treat that definition as indicative and historical.
- **Frameworks in transition.** The CVF 2024 replaced the 2016 version, with forces given until May 2025 to transition, and policing professional profiles were still being updated on a rolling basis [10]. A force or College assessment may still reference older material; the College advises checking which version you are being assessed against [10]. Similarly, EPSO's 2023 framework superseded the model under which most published EPSO preparation material was written [4] [7].
- **Careers-site question lists are practitioner evidence.** The sourced questions from targetjobs, Prospects, My World of Work, Oxford and the National Careers Service [21] [23] [24] [25] [26] evidence what is *published as typical*, not what any named employer asks in a given round.
- **NHS coverage is thin by necessity.** The HEE VBR Framework (March 2016) documents the programme and stakeholder views but publishes no standard question set [13]; every NHS-flavoured pressure question in this bank is therefore composed. The 2016 date also makes it the oldest primary source here.
- **Strengths guidance and rehearsal advice can conflict with good preparation.** "Do not rehearse" [3] is the published instruction, but candidates with anxiety or communication-affecting disabilities may be disadvantaged by an unrehearsed format; the adjustment route [15] [17] is the mitigation, not more rehearsal.
- **The Oxford question list is dated.** Its published example questions carry a May 2016 date [25]; they remain representative of careers-service practice but are the oldest practitioner source cited.

# Open Questions

- **Is there a government-wide published Success Profiles rating scale? — resolved 2026-07-20.** Yes. Cabinet Office guidance directs that behaviours, experience and technical skills be scored on the 7-point scale and strengths on the 4-point scale [30]; the same sentence appears in three independent grade-tier packs, so the scale is central. Two sub-questions remain open: no central publication of the seven descriptor *labels* was located (COPFS [11] is still the only source for those), and no published descriptors for the 4-point strengths scale were found at all. Shared with **delivering-results-planning** and the UK frameworks documents.
- **Can the pre-2023 EPSO "Resilience" definition be sourced primarily?** The competency's existence is documented [7]; its official definition is not, in anything this pipeline could open. A EUR-Lex Notice of Competition from roughly 2016–2022, retrieved by a method EUR-Lex does not block, would resolve it.
- **What replaced Resilience in EPSO scoring practice, not just in naming?** The 2023 framework maps the content onto Self-management and Learning as a skill [4], but whether assessors weight it comparably is not publicly documented.
- **Do any NHS trusts publish values-based question sets touching pressure?** If a named trust publishes its VBR question bank, the NHS coverage here could move from composed to sourced [13].
- **How should interviewers handle volunteered health information?** No guidance was found telling interviewers what to do when a candidate *volunteers* health information in answer to a pressure question — a real and common situation that s.60 does not directly address [14].
