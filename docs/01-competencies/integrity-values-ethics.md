# Executive Summary

This is the Tier-2 deep-dive for the integrity, values and ethics competency family, the child of `competencies-foundation`. That parent document noted a genuine oddity in its mapping table: the Civil Service has no *Behaviour* called "integrity", because its values live in a separate instrument — the Civil Service Code [1]. This document takes that observation as its starting point and works out what follows for candidates and interviewers. Integrity is rarely assessed as a standalone competency with its own level descriptors; it is assessed as a **cross-cutting expectation** that surfaces inside other competencies, inside values-based interview questions, and inside the codes of conduct that govern the employer.

The evidence layer establishes what is actually documented. The Civil Service Code defines four core values — integrity, honesty, objectivity and impartiality — with short, quotable definitions, and gives civil servants a route to raise concerns without penalty [1]. The Seven Principles of Public Life (the Nolan Principles) apply across the wider public sector, including local government, police, courts and health [3]. The NHS recruits explicitly against the values of the NHS Constitution through Values Based Recruitment, a national programme with a published framework [5] [6]. Policing has gone furthest in operationalising this: the 2024 Competency and Values Framework places three core values — courage, respect and empathy, and public service — at the centre of the framework, mapped directly to the 2024 Code of Ethics, and states plainly that candidates "should demonstrate how they apply the three values in their work" [7] [8]. EU officials are bound by legally binding ethics obligations in Title II of the Staff Regulations, covering loyalty, conflicts of interest, outside activities and a duty to report suspected illegal activity [13]. In the private sector and on graduate schemes the ethical-dilemma question is a documented staple [17] [18].

Document-level confidence is **Medium**. The framework facts, value definitions, statutory obligations and the published 1–7 Success Profiles scoring scale [9] rest on official primary sources and would individually merit High. However, the organising claim of this document — that these scattered instruments constitute one coherent, preparable interview family, and the four-way question grouping used in the Question Bank — is this knowledge base's reasoned synthesis, and a portion of the sourced questions comes from a single NHS trust's published toolkit [10] and from careers-sector publishers [17] [18] rather than from national assessor guidance. Under the confidence rubric, a document whose central organising device is synthesis is Medium.

# Key Takeaways

- The Civil Service Code defines four core values with quotable definitions: integrity is "putting the obligations of public service above your own personal interests", honesty is "being truthful and open", objectivity is "basing your advice and decisions on rigorous analysis of the evidence", and impartiality is "acting solely according to the merits of the case and serving equally well governments of different political persuasions". *(documented)* [1]
- Integrity is not one of the nine Civil Service Behaviours; it appears *inside* behaviour descriptors — for example, Communicating and Influencing is defined as communicating "purpose and direction with clarity, integrity and enthusiasm", and at Director level the descriptors require communicating "with honesty, integrity, impartiality and objectivity". *(documented)* [2]
- The Seven Principles of Public Life apply far beyond Whitehall — to elected and appointed officials, the Civil Service, local government, police, courts, NDPBs and health, education and social care, plus others delivering public services. *(documented)* [3]
- Policing is the clearest published case of values being formally assessed at interview: the 2024 CVF puts courage, respect and empathy, and public service at the centre of the framework, and its guidance states that during recruitment interviews "candidates should demonstrate how they apply the three values in their work". *(documented)* [7]
- NHS Values Based Recruitment "attracts and selects students, trainees or employees on the basis that their individual values and behaviours align with the values of the NHS Constitution", and the HEE framework rates unstructured interviews as unsuitable for the purpose. *(documented)* [6]
- EU staff obligations are legal, not aspirational: officials must act "solely with the interests of the Union in mind" (Art. 11), must not deal with matters in which they have a personal interest impairing independence (Art. 11a), and must report suspected illegal activity "without delay" and in writing, with protection from prejudicial effects where they "acted reasonably and honestly" (Arts. 22a, 22c). *(documented)* [13]
- Published scoring is unforgiving of vagueness: the COPFS Success Profiles scale awards 1 for "No positive evidence and/ or substantial negative evidence demonstrated" and typically requires 4 or above across behaviours. A moralising answer with no action evidences nothing. *(documented)* [9]
- The single most common failure mode on this family is stating a value instead of evidencing a decision — the answer that says "I believe in honesty" but never reaches an action the assessor can score. *(guidance)*
- Ethical-dilemma questions do not require a dramatic example: targetjobs advises that a relatively commonplace dilemma is fine and that what matters is the quality of your analysis, warning specifically against generic platitudes. *(documented)* [17]

# Purpose

This document answers two questions that the parent foundation deliberately left open.

For candidates: *"If integrity isn't a named competency with published level descriptors, how do I actually prepare for it — and what does a strong answer to an ethical-dilemma or speaking-up question contain?"* The honest answer is that preparation for this family looks different from preparation for teamwork or leadership. You are not matching a level descriptor; you are demonstrating a *decision process* against a values instrument that the employer has usually published. This document identifies those instruments, extracts what they actually say, and shows how to build answers that reach the action an assessor can score.

For interviewers and HR practitioners: *"What is the documented basis for assessing values at interview, what question forms are actually published, and where are the fairness traps?"* Values-based questioning carries a specific risk that competency questioning does not — the slide from assessing *conduct* into assessing *belief*, which is where discrimination exposure lives. That risk is treated in Legal & Fairness Considerations.

# Definitions

- **Integrity (Civil Service usage)** — "putting the obligations of public service above your own personal interests" [1]. Narrower and more precise than the everyday sense of "being a good person": it is specifically about the priority ordering when public duty and personal interest conflict.
- **Honesty (Civil Service usage)** — "being truthful and open" [1]. Note that openness is included; withholding is a honesty failure, not merely a communication one.
- **Objectivity (Civil Service usage)** — "basing your advice and decisions on rigorous analysis of the evidence" [1].
- **Impartiality (Civil Service usage)** — "acting solely according to the merits of the case and serving equally well governments of different political persuasions" [1].
- **Values-based recruitment (VBR)** — "an approach which attracts and selects students, trainees or employees on the basis that their individual values and behaviours align with the values of the NHS Constitution" [6]. Used in this document as the generic term for values-aligned selection, with the NHS meaning cited where the NHS programme is meant.
- **Value (CVF usage)** — "beliefs which are important to an individual and which guide and motivate particular behaviours and actions", distinguished in the CVF from competencies, which are "behaviours (knowledge, skills or abilities) related to effective job performance" [7].
- **Qualifying disclosure (UK law)** — a disclosure of information which, "in the reasonable belief of the worker making the disclosure, is made in the public interest" and tends to show one of the categories of relevant failure listed in s.43B of the Employment Rights Act 1996 [11].
- **Conflict of interest (EU staff usage)** — a matter in which an official has, "directly or indirectly, any personal interest such as to impair his independence, and, in particular, family and financial interests" (Art. 11a) [13].
- **Ethical dilemma** — used in this document in the interview sense: a situation in which two defensible obligations conflict, so that any course of action sacrifices something legitimate. A situation where one option is simply wrong is a *compliance test*, not a dilemma; the distinction matters for example selection (see Model Answer Guidance).

# Framework Context

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## UK Civil Service: values sit outside the Behaviours

The Civil Service Code sets out the four core values — integrity, honesty, objectivity and impartiality — and derives its management framework from Part 1 of the Constitutional Reform and Governance Act 2010 [1]. This is the structural fact that shapes preparation: the values are in the Code, while the assessed Behaviours are in Success Profiles [2]. A candidate looking for an "Integrity" behaviour to prepare against will not find one.

What they will find is integrity distributed through the behaviour descriptors. Communicating and Influencing is defined as "Communicate purpose and direction with clarity, integrity and enthusiasm. Respect the needs, responses and opinions of others" [2]. The grade-level descriptors carry the values explicitly: at AA/AO level candidates are expected to "remain honest and truthful when explaining opinions"; at Grade 6/7 to "remain open-minded and impartial in discussions"; and at Director level to "demonstrate and promote communicating with honesty, integrity, impartiality and objectivity" [2]. Making Effective Decisions — "Use evidence and knowledge to support accurate, expert decisions and advice. Carefully consider alternative options, implications and risks of decisions" — is the behaviour that most directly carries the objectivity value [2]. Several grade descriptors also reference creating an environment where colleagues feel safe to challenge decisions [2], which is the speaking-up theme appearing inside a behaviour rather than as one.

The Code also documents the route for raising concerns: staff should contact their line manager or a nominated officer, and may escalate to the Civil Service Commission if unsatisfied; the Code states that "If you believe that you are being required to act in a way which conflicts with this Code, your department or agency must consider your concern, and make sure that you are not penalised for raising it" [1]. Separately, the Civil Service Commission regulates recruitment itself: the Constitutional Reform and Governance Act 2010 requires appointments to be made on merit on the basis of fair and open competition, and the Commission publishes Recruitment Principles that departments must follow [4].

## The Nolan Principles

The Seven Principles of Public Life were first published on 31 May 1995 by the Committee on Standards in Public Life [3]. They apply to holders of public office, a category the Committee defines broadly: elected and appointed officials, the Civil Service, local government, police, courts, non-departmental public bodies and health, education and social care services, plus people in other sectors delivering public services [3]. The seven, in the published wording, are [3]:

| Principle | Published definition |
|---|---|
| Selflessness | "Holders of public office should act solely in terms of the public interest." |
| Integrity | "Holders of public office must avoid placing themselves under any obligation to people or organisations that might try inappropriately to influence them in their work." |
| Objectivity | "Holders of public office must act and take decisions impartially, fairly and on merit, using the best evidence and without discrimination or bias." |
| Accountability | "Holders of public office are accountable to the public for their decisions and actions and must submit themselves to the scrutiny necessary to ensure this." |
| Openness | "Holders of public office should act and take decisions in an open and transparent manner." |
| Honesty | "Holders of public office should be truthful." |
| Leadership | "Holders of public office should exhibit these principles in their own behaviour and treat others with respect." |

Note that the Nolan definition of *integrity* is narrower and more specific than the colloquial sense: it is about avoiding obligations that could inappropriately influence you [3]. This is a conflicts-of-interest definition, which is why the Question Bank treats conflicts of interest as a distinct sub-theme.

## NHS: values-based recruitment

NHS recruitment in England sits within the values of the NHS Constitution [5]. Health Education England's Values Based Recruitment Framework defines VBR as "an approach which attracts and selects students, trainees or employees on the basis that their individual values and behaviours align with the values of the NHS Constitution", framed as "enhancing existing processes to ensure that we recruit the right workforce not only with the right skills and in the right numbers, but with the right values" [6]. The framework records that the programme arose in part from the report of the Mid Staffordshire NHS Foundation Trust Public Inquiry (Francis, 2013), which "emphasised the importance of staff values and behaviours for the level of care and patient experience" [6], and that HEE's Mandate required selection into all new NHS-funded training posts to incorporate testing of values-based recruitment [6].

On method, the framework is explicit and useful to both audiences. It defines structured interviewing as "a selection method where an assessor asks the candidate a set of questions that are specified in advance and mapped to the selection criteria", and distinguishes situational interviews (future-oriented, scenario-based, requiring "the development of dilemmas that employees are likely to encounter in the role, and the development of scoring guides in advance") from behavioural interviews (competency-based, past-oriented) [6]. It states that "criterion-related validity is highest for interviews that are structured, ask relevant and standardised questions based on thorough role analysis, and utilise a panel of interviewers trained in best practice interview techniques and using validated scoring criteria" [6]. On unstructured interviews it is blunt: "The evidence suggests they lack reliability and validity and therefore are not suitable for VBR" [6]. The framework also records honestly that "research into VBR is still in its infancy" with "very little empirical evidence regarding the long term impact of VBR" [6].

At trust level this becomes concrete question sets. Gloucestershire Hospitals NHS Foundation Trust's published recruiting-managers toolkit maps questions to its values and instructs managers to "choose one question for each value and one question for diversity and inclusion from the examples below to ask all candidates at interview", supplying each question with "Things to look out for" and "Negative indicators" [10]. Questions from that toolkit are used, with attribution, in the Question Bank below.

## Policing: the 2024 CVF and Code of Ethics

Policing provides the most fully worked published example of values being embedded in an assessment framework. The College of Policing's Competency and Values Framework "describes the behaviours required by police officers and staff to be effective in their roles and uphold the Code of Ethics for policing" [7]. The 2024 edition consists of six competencies at three levels plus "three defined and measurable core values", with values "at the centre of the CVF" applying to all roles; levels apply to competencies only, not values [7]. The three values are courage, respect and empathy, and public service, and they map directly onto the three ethical policing principles of the 2024 Code of Ethics, under the strapline "Doing the right things, in the right way, for the right reasons" [7] [8]. The published commitments are [7] [8]:

| Value / ethical policing principle | "As policing professionals, we commit to:" |
|---|---|
| Courage | "making, communicating and being accountable for decisions, and standing against anything that could bring our profession into disrepute." |
| Respect and empathy | "encouraging, listening to and understanding the views of others, and seeking to recognise and respond to the physical, mental and emotional challenges that we and other people may face." |
| Public service | "working in the public interest, fostering public trust and confidence, and taking pride in providing an excellent service to the public." |

The 2024 update was made specifically "to include the principles underpinning the new Code of Ethics", with the values-related behaviours updated to reflect them; the guidance describes the CVF's purpose as making "general application of the Code of Ethics a reality" and embedding it "into all of our people processes" [7]. Note the courage commitment includes "standing against anything that could bring our profession into disrepute" [7] [8] — an explicit speaking-up obligation inside a value definition.

## EU institutions: ethics as legal obligation

For EU officials the ethics layer is statutory. Title II of the Staff Regulations sets out that an official "shall carry out his duties and conduct himself solely with the interests of the Union in mind", shall "neither seek nor take instructions from any government, authority, organisation or person outside his institution", and shall carry out duties "objectively, impartially and in keeping with his duty of loyalty to the Union" (Art. 11) [13]. The same article restricts accepting "any honour, decoration, favour, gift or payment of any kind whatever" from outside sources without permission, and requires candidates to declare "any actual or potential conflict of interest" using a specific form *before recruitment* [13]. Article 12 adds that an official "shall refrain from any action or behaviour which might reflect adversely upon his position", and Article 12b requires prior permission from the Appointing Authority for outside activities, paid or unpaid [13].

The EPSO Competency Framework, which governs what is actually assessed at selection, does not name integrity as one of its eight general competencies; as the parent document notes, it is visible in the anchors rather than as a heading [14]. The practical consequence for EPSO candidates is that ethics questions arrive inside competency questions — most often within Working together and Decision-making and getting results — rather than in a dedicated ethics block.

## The documented duty to speak up

Four of the frameworks in scope contain an explicit obligation to report or challenge, and the wording differs in ways that matter for answering questions.

**UK Civil Service.** The Code provides that where a civil servant believes they are being required to act in conflict with the Code, the department "must consider your concern, and make sure that you are not penalised for raising it", with escalation to the Civil Service Commission available if the response is unsatisfactory [1].

**UK statute (all workers).** Section 43B of the Employment Rights Act 1996 defines a qualifying disclosure as "any disclosure of information which, in the reasonable belief of the worker making the disclosure, is made in the public interest and tends to show one or more of the following" — that a criminal offence has been, is being or is likely to be committed; that a person has failed, is failing or is likely to fail to comply with any legal obligation; that a miscarriage of justice has occurred, is occurring or is likely to occur; that the health or safety of any individual has been, is being or is likely to be endangered; that sexual harassment has occurred, is occurring or is likely to occur; that the environment has been, is being or is likely to be damaged; or that information tending to show any of the preceding matters "has been, is being or is likely to be deliberately concealed" [11]. Two elements of that test are worth a candidate's attention: the belief must be *reasonable*, and the disclosure must be *in the public interest* — neither requires the worker to have been correct. The sexual-harassment category was added with effect from 6 April 2026 [11].

**EU officials.** Article 22a of the Staff Regulations obliges any official who becomes aware of "facts which give rise to a presumption of the existence of possible illegal activity, including fraud or corruption" or of "conduct relating to the discharge of professional duties which may constitute a serious failure to comply with the obligations of officials" to inform, "without delay", their immediate superior, Director-General, Secretary-General or the European Anti-Fraud Office (OLAF) directly; the information "shall be given in writing" [13]. The protection is conditional: an official "shall not suffer any prejudicial effects on the part of the institution as a result of having communicated the information … provided that he acted reasonably and honestly" (Art. 22a(3)) [13]. Article 22b extends protection to further disclosure to the President of the Commission, Court of Auditors, Council, European Parliament or the European Ombudsman, subject to the official honestly and reasonably believing the information "substantially true" and having first disclosed to OLAF or their institution and allowed a set period for action [13]. Article 22c requires each institution to put in place a confidential complaints procedure for officials treated adversely after fulfilling those obligations [13].

**Policing.** The courage value commits policing professionals to "standing against anything that could bring our profession into disrepute" [7] [8], and the CVF guidance records that the Code of Ethics is supported by a Code of Practice for Ethical Policing with "specific sections on challenging unprofessional behaviour and recognising and responding to misconduct" [7].

The common structure across all four is worth naming, because it is what a strong answer mirrors: a *reasonable belief* threshold rather than a certainty threshold, a *first-line internal route*, an *escalation route* if the first line fails, and a *protection* conditional on good faith.

## What "impartiality" and "conflict of interest" mean operationally

The frameworks converge on the same operational test but express it differently. The Civil Service formulation is behavioural — "acting solely according to the merits of the case and serving equally well governments of different political persuasions" [1]. The Nolan formulation is preventive — office holders "must avoid placing themselves under any obligation to people or organisations that might try inappropriately to influence them in their work" [3], with the objectivity principle adding that decisions must be taken "impartially, fairly and on merit, using the best evidence and without discrimination or bias" [3]. The EU formulation is procedural and mandatory: an official must not "deal with a matter in which, directly or indirectly, he has any personal interest such as to impair his independence, and, in particular, family and financial interests", and where such a matter falls to them they "shall immediately inform the Appointing Authority", which "may in particular relieve the official from responsibility in this matter" (Art. 11a) [13]. Article 11a(3) further bars officials from keeping or acquiring interests in undertakings subject to their institution's authority "of such kind or magnitude as might impair his independence" [13].

The procedural point is the one most candidates miss: in the EU framework, and in most corporate conflict-of-interest policies modelled on it, the required action on identifying a conflict is *declaration and recusal*, not a private judgement that you can remain objective. An answer that concludes "I decided I could be fair about it" fails the documented standard even if the decision was in fact fair.

## The ethical-dilemma question in private-sector and graduate recruitment

Outside the public sector the family typically surfaces as a single well-known question rather than a framework. targetjobs lists "Give an example of a time when you faced an ethical dilemma" among the competency questions graduate employers commonly ask [18], and publishes a dedicated treatment of it as a "tricky graduate interview question" [17]. That article documents what recruiters are assessing: transparency in your dealings with others, whether your values align with the organisation's stated principles, how well you articulate your ethical framework and its effect on your behaviour, and your thoughtfulness when navigating difficult decisions [17]. On example selection it advises that the specific scenario matters less than the quality of your analysis, that commonplace workplace dilemmas are perfectly acceptable, and that candidates should not fabricate situations because interviewers detect dishonesty [17]. Its warnings are specific: do not confess to serious wrongdoing, and avoid generic platitudes such as "honesty is the best policy" offered without demonstrating any actual complexity [17].

The professional-body position on method applies here as it does elsewhere: CIPD guidance holds that structured interviews predict job performance better than unstructured ones, and that situational and past-behavioural questions are the best kinds of interview question, with consistent questions and objective scoring criteria making responses comparable and reducing the influence of personal bias [16].

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact. It points back to the evidence above and adds no new facts.**

## Selecting the example

- **Pick a dilemma, not a crime.** A situation where one option was plainly wrong tests only whether you know the rules; it gives you nothing to analyse. The examples that score are those where two legitimate obligations conflicted. This aligns with what targetjobs documents recruiters as assessing — the articulation of your ethical framework and your thoughtfulness in navigating difficulty [17] — rather than your ability to identify obvious misconduct.
- **Commonplace beats dramatic.** targetjobs states that a relatively commonplace dilemma is fine, and warns against fabrication because interviewers detect it [17]. A modest, true example you can probe deeply outperforms a borrowed one you cannot.
- **Do not confess.** The same source warns against confessing to serious wrongdoing [17]. An example in which you did the wrong thing and learned from it is only usable if the wrong thing was minor and the learning is concrete.
- **Check the employer's own instrument first.** Every framework in scope publishes its values: the four Civil Service Code values [1], the seven Nolan Principles [3], the NHS Constitution values [5], the three policing values [7] [8], or the Staff Regulations obligations [13]. Select an example that evidences a value the employer has actually published, and use their vocabulary.

## Structuring the answer

Use STAR — Situation, Task, Action, Result, where the Result includes "what you learned from" it [19] — but weight it differently from other competency families. For integrity questions the scored substance sits in a step that STAR does not name explicitly: the **reasoning between Task and Action**. Assessors on this family are listening for *how you decided*, not merely what you did. In practice this means expanding the Action into three visible moves: what you noticed, how you weighed it and who you involved, and what you then did.

**Worked skeleton — composed question, "Describe a time you raised a concern that you knew would be unwelcome":**

- **Situation (brief).** During a routine reconciliation I found that a figure being reported upward had been prepared on an assumption I could not evidence. The reporting deadline was two days away and the team was already under pressure.
- **Task (brief).** I was responsible for signing off the underlying data, so the accuracy of what was reported was mine to stand behind.
- **Action — what I noticed.** I checked my own work first, to be sure the discrepancy was real and not my error, and wrote down precisely what I could and could not evidence. *(This mirrors the reasonable-belief threshold in the documented tests — s.43B requires a reasonable belief [11], and Staff Regulations protection is conditional on having "acted reasonably and honestly" [13]. Checking first is what makes a belief reasonable.)*
- **Action — how I weighed it and who I involved.** I considered saying nothing until after the deadline and decided against it, because the figure would by then have been relied on. I raised it with my line manager the same morning rather than in the group channel, so the person who prepared the figure was not exposed publicly before the facts were established. *(First-line internal route, which is the pattern the Civil Service Code [1] and Article 22a [13] both prescribe.)*
- **Action — escalation.** When the initial response was that it was too late to change, I put the specific discrepancy in writing with the evidence attached and asked for a decision to be recorded either way. *(Escalation with a written record — Article 22a requires the information to be given in writing [13].)*
- **Result.** The figure was corrected before submission; the reconciliation step was moved earlier in the cycle so the check happened with time to act. What I learned was to raise the *evidence gap* rather than an accusation — describing what I could not verify, rather than asserting someone was wrong, kept the conversation about the number.

Note what this skeleton does *not* do: it never states a value. It never says "I believe in honesty." The integrity is visible entirely in the sequence of decisions, which is what the published scoring scale can actually award marks for [9].

## Adapting the same example across frameworks

One well-built example usually serves several frameworks with a change of emphasis rather than a change of story. Foregrounding the written record and the escalation serves EU Article 22a expectations [13]. Foregrounding "I checked my own work first" and the evidence gap serves Civil Service objectivity — "basing your advice and decisions on rigorous analysis of the evidence" [1] — and Making Effective Decisions [2]. Foregrounding the decision to protect a colleague from public exposure while still raising the issue serves the policing pairing of courage with respect and empathy [7] [8]. Foregrounding the effect on the person relying on the figure serves NHS values-based framing [5] [6].

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

- **Values are explicitly in scope at interview in policing.** The CVF guidance states that "Values now play an important part in recruitment, assessment and selection" and that "During recruitment interviews and assessments for selection, candidates should demonstrate how they apply the three values in their work, as well as how they demonstrate the competencies" [7]. On method it advises that "it is good practice to ask about examples of past behaviour, probing for evidence", that hypothetical future situations are sometimes helpful, but that "most of the interview should focus on past behaviour" [7].
- **Indicators are not a checklist.** The CVF guidance is unusually direct about this, and it constrains how assessors should score: the example behavioural indicators "are just that — they are indicators of the sorts of behaviours that could demonstrate the competency", "the list of behavioural indicators is not the totality of the competency", and "the CVF itself should not be used as a simple checklist, whether as part of selection or assessment". Assessors "should retain flexibility and be open to diverse ways of demonstrating the behaviours, as long as they are in line with the definition" [7].
- **Published scoring scale.** COPFS publishes the 7-point Success Profiles scale used to assess behaviours: 1 "Not Demonstrated — No positive evidence and/ or substantial negative evidence demonstrated"; 2 "Minimal Demonstration — Limited positive evidence and/ or mainly negative evidence demonstrated"; 3 "Moderate Demonstration — Moderate positive evidence but some negative evidence demonstrated"; 4 "Acceptable Demonstration — Adequate positive evidence and any negative evidence would not cause concern"; 5 "Good Demonstration — Substantial positive evidence of the behaviour"; 6 "Strong Demonstration — Substantial positive evidence; includes some evidence of exceeding expectations at this level"; 7 "Outstanding Demonstration — The evidence provided wholly exceeds expectation at this level" [9]. The standard minimum is a score of 4 or above in all behaviours, with the noted exception that "a candidate can score a single score of 3 where scores for all other behaviours are high" [9]. The recurring word across bands is *evidence* — the scale rewards demonstrated behaviour, not stated conviction.
- **Values questions come with negative indicators.** The Gloucestershire toolkit supplies each values question with both "Things to look out for" and "Negative indicators", and instructs managers to choose one question per value and ask it of all candidates [10]. Published negative indicators in that toolkit include "Lack of accountability", "Failing to constructively challenge", "Failure to consult with others" and "Failure to support staff development" [10] — note that *failing to challenge* is scored negatively, not neutrally.
- **Structured design is the documented standard for values assessment.** The HEE framework holds that criterion-related validity is highest for structured interviews using standardised questions based on role analysis, panels trained in best-practice technique, and validated scoring criteria — and that unstructured interviews "lack reliability and validity and therefore are not suitable for VBR" [6]. Where situational (hypothetical) questions are used, the framework specifies developing both the dilemmas and "scoring guides in advance for trained assessors" [6].
- **Consistency obligations.** ACAS advises that "When interviewing it's a good idea to ask each applicant the same questions where possible", that "Questions must not discriminate", that "If possible, each interview should be carried out by more than 1 person to reduce the risk of discriminating", that recordings or notes taken in interviews must be stored "securely and confidentially" following data protection rules, and that decisions should be checked against "how each applicant met each point in the job description and person specification" [15]. CIPD adds that consistent questions and objective scoring criteria make responses comparable and reduce the influence of personal bias [16].
- **Selection integrity is itself regulated in the Civil Service.** The Constitutional Reform and Governance Act 2010 requires appointments to be made on merit on the basis of fair and open competition, and the Civil Service Commission publishes Recruitment Principles departments must follow, acting as an independent regulator separate from government [4].

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact.**

## For candidates

- **Moralising without an action.** The commonest failure on this family: an answer that asserts a value and never reaches a decision. Against the published scale this is band 1–2 territory — "No positive evidence" or "Limited positive evidence" [9] — because there is no behaviour to score. targetjobs names the same failure from the recruiter side, warning against platitudes like "honesty is the best policy" offered without demonstrating complexity [17].
- **Choosing a non-dilemma.** Presenting a situation where one option was obviously wrong removes the analysis the question exists to elicit. It also tends to produce a self-congratulatory answer, which reads poorly against indicators that reward consulting others [10].
- **Concluding "I decided I could be objective."** Where a personal interest exists, the documented standard is declaration and, where appropriate, being relieved of the matter — Article 11a requires the official to "immediately inform the Appointing Authority", which "may in particular relieve the official from responsibility in this matter" [13]. Self-certified objectivity fails that standard however sound the eventual decision.
- **Claiming certainty you did not have.** Candidates often overstate how sure they were, believing it strengthens the answer. It weakens it: the documented tests are reasonable-belief tests [11] [13], and describing how you tested an uncertain belief is stronger evidence than claiming you simply knew.
- **Going nuclear as the first move.** An answer in which the first action is external escalation ignores the first-line internal route that the Civil Service Code [1] and Article 22b's sequencing [13] both prescribe. Article 22b conditions protection on having previously disclosed to OLAF or the institution and allowed time for action [13].
- **Silence framed as loyalty.** Where a framework treats failing to challenge as a negative indicator [10], and where the policing courage value commits professionals to "standing against anything that could bring our profession into disrepute" [7] [8], an example whose resolution is "I said nothing to protect the team" scores against you.
- **Reciting the values list.** Being able to name the four Civil Service values [1] or the seven Nolan Principles [3] is table stakes, not evidence. The scale rewards demonstrated behaviour [9].
- **Fabrication.** targetjobs warns directly that interviewers detect invented scenarios [17]; probes of the kind published in the NHS toolkit — who was involved, what was the outcome, how did it make you feel [10] — are precisely what invented examples fail.

## For interviewers

- **Assessing belief rather than conduct.** Values questions can slide into probing personal, religious or political conviction. This is both a validity problem and a discrimination exposure — religion or belief is a protected characteristic under s.4 of the Equality Act 2010 [12], and ACAS is explicit that questions must not discriminate [15]. Assess what the candidate *did*, not what they believe.
- **Using indicators as a tick-list.** Directly contrary to the CVF guidance, which states the indicators "are just that", are "not the totality of the competency", and that the framework "should not be used as a simple checklist" [7].
- **Improvising values questions per candidate.** Undermines comparability and legal defensibility; ACAS advises the same questions where possible [15], and the NHS toolkit instructs managers to choose set questions "to ask all candidates at interview" [10].
- **Running values assessment through unstructured conversation.** The HEE framework rates unstructured interviews as "not suitable for VBR" on reliability and validity grounds [6].
- **Using hypothetical dilemmas without prepared scoring guides.** Where situational questions are used, the framework specifies developing dilemmas *and* "scoring guides in advance for trained assessors" [6]; without them, scoring reduces to whether the assessor liked the answer.
- **Over-reading a single value.** The CVF cautions that assessment "should allow for people to show strengths in some areas and need for development in others" [7].

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.**

1. **Instrument mapping (20 minutes).** Identify which values instrument governs the employer you are applying to — Civil Service Code [1], Nolan Principles [3], NHS Constitution [5], policing CVF and Code of Ethics [7] [8], EU Staff Regulations [13], or a published corporate code. Write out its values in its own words. Every subsequent exercise uses that vocabulary.
2. **Dilemma inventory (45 minutes).** List six situations from the last three years in which two legitimate obligations conflicted. For each, write one line on what was sacrificed. Discard any where one option was simply wrong — those are compliance stories, not dilemmas. You are looking for two or three usable examples, not six.
3. **The reasoning-expansion drill (30 minutes).** Take your best example and write the Action in three separate paragraphs: what you noticed, how you weighed it and who you involved, what you then did. Most candidates write only the third. Read it back and check that a reader could reconstruct your decision without you present.
4. **Probe survival (pairs, 20 minutes).** Give your answer, then have your partner ask the five published probes in sequence — what did you learn, what would you do different next time, how did it make you feel, who was involved, what was the outcome [10] — followed by "what would have had to be different for you to have made the opposite decision?" Any probe you cannot answer in two sentences marks a part of the example you have not actually thought through.
5. **Self-score against the published scale (20 minutes).** Score your own answer 1–7 against the COPFS descriptors [9], then justify the score in one sentence using the descriptor's own words. If you cannot get past "Moderate positive evidence but some negative evidence demonstrated" (3), the gap is almost always a missing action rather than a missing value.
6. **The value-free rewrite (15 minutes).** Rewrite your answer with every abstract value word removed — no "honesty", "integrity", "the right thing", "morally". If the answer still evidences integrity, it is strong. If it collapses, you were asserting rather than demonstrating.
7. **Interviewer drill (pairs, 40 minutes).** Design a values question for one published value, write "things to look out for" and "negative indicators" for it *before* interviewing, in the style of the published toolkit [10], then interview two people with the identical question and compare scores. Divergence reveals criteria that were never really agreed.

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — every claim cited. Signposting only — not legal advice.**

- **Religion and belief are protected characteristics.** Section 4 of the Equality Act 2010 lists the nine protected characteristics: age, disability, gender reassignment, marriage and civil partnership, pregnancy and maternity, race, religion or belief, sex and sexual orientation [12]. This is the specific fairness risk of values-based questioning: a question that probes the *source* of a candidate's values, rather than their conduct at work, moves towards territory the Act protects. ACAS states that interview questions "must not discriminate" and that interviewers should understand discrimination law before interviewing [15].
- **Consistency and panel composition as fairness controls.** ACAS advises asking each applicant the same questions where possible, using more than one interviewer where possible "to reduce the risk of discriminating", and checking that decisions are based on how each applicant met each point in the job description and person specification [15]. These controls matter more, not less, for values questions, because values answers are more open to subjective reading than task-based ones.
- **Reasonable adjustments apply to values assessment.** The CVF guidance states that the framework "should be used in ways which allow for differing abilities, including those which stem from disabilities or neurodiversity", that assessments "should allow for different expression of the competencies", and that "Reasonable adjustments should be provided for those who need them under the Equality Act (2010)"; it also notes accommodations for pregnancy, breastfeeding, injury or illness [7]. This is directly relevant here: questions about how something "made you feel" [10] may disadvantage some neurodivergent candidates if scored naively.
- **Whistleblowing protection is a statutory backdrop to speaking-up answers.** Section 43B of the Employment Rights Act 1996 sets the qualifying-disclosure test — reasonable belief, public interest, and one of the listed categories of relevant failure [11]. Candidates should be aware that describing a past disclosure in an interview may touch on matters still confidential; describe your conduct without disclosing the underlying confidential information.
- **EU officials' obligations and protections are statutory.** Reporting obligations sit in Article 22a, extended disclosure protection in Article 22b, and a required confidential complaints procedure in Article 22c of the Staff Regulations [13]. Protection is conditional on having acted reasonably and honestly (Art. 22a(3)) [13]. Article 12a additionally protects both victims of and honest witnesses to psychological or sexual harassment from prejudicial effects [13].
- **Interview records are personal data.** ACAS advises that recordings or notes taken in interviews must be stored securely and confidentially, following data protection rules [15]. Notes on a values or ethics question may capture unusually sensitive material about a candidate's past conduct or a third party's; the retention case for such notes should be considered deliberately rather than by default.
- **Selection integrity itself is regulated in the Civil Service.** Appointments must be on merit on the basis of fair and open competition under the Constitutional Reform and Governance Act 2010, regulated by the independent Civil Service Commission through its Recruitment Principles [4].

# Risks & Caveats

- **The four-theme grouping is editorial.** Ethical dilemmas, speaking up, impartiality/conflicts and values fit are this knowledge base's organising device for a family that no cited framework subdivides in that way. Employers assess against their own published instrument.
- **The trust-level question set is one trust, not the NHS.** The sourced questions attributed to Gloucestershire Hospitals NHS Foundation Trust [10] evidence how one NHS employer has published values questions against its own local values — not a national NHS question bank. Other trusts phrase questions differently and map them to different value labels. Treat them as documented examples of the form, not as questions any given NHS employer will ask.
- **The 1–7 scale is central; the descriptors and benchmark are not.** Cabinet Office guidance states that "Experience, technical skills and behaviours should be scored using the 7 point scale. Strengths should be scored using the 4 point scale" [20], so the scale itself can be assumed across the Civil Service. Three qualifications hold: that central pack dates from 2020, and no refreshed central scoring publication was found after the January 2025 behaviour-guide refresh; the seven descriptor labels quoted above are published by COPFS [9], still the only located source for them; and the "4 or above" benchmark is COPFS's own, with COPFS itself noting that additional benchmarks may be set for specific roles and detailed in the application pack [9].
- **The evidence base for VBR is thin by its own account.** The HEE framework states that "research into VBR is still in its infancy" with "very little empirical evidence regarding the long term impact of VBR", and referenced a longitudinal study then underway [6]. The framework itself dates from March 2016 and predates NHS England's absorption of Health Education England; its URL sits on the legacy HEE domain and may move.
- **Frameworks in this document are recent but moving.** The policing CVF and Code of Ethics date from 2024, and the CVF guidance records that the 2016 CVF remained available until May 2025 and that professional profiles were still being updated through April 2025 [7]. Candidates should confirm which CVF version an assessment uses — the guidance explicitly tells candidates to check [7].
- **The whistleblowing category list changed recently.** The sexual-harassment category in s.43B took effect on 6 April 2026 [11]; older guidance and commentary will not reflect it.
- **EU sources were partially unverifiable at the time of writing.** The consolidated Staff Regulations were opened and quoted directly [13], but EUR-Lex rate-limited further automated retrieval during research. Directive (EU) 2019/1937 on the protection of persons who report breaches of Union law is therefore listed under Further Reading rather than cited, because it could not be opened and verified in line with this knowledge base's sourcing rule. Council Directive 2000/78/EC, cited in the parent document, was likewise not re-verified here and is not cited.
- **Careers-sector sources evidence the form, not any employer's practice.** targetjobs material [17] [18] documents what is published as typical of graduate recruitment; it is not evidence that any named employer asks these questions.
- **No published private-sector integrity framework was located.** Unlike the public-sector cases, the private-sector treatment here rests on careers-sector publishers rather than on named corporate competency dictionaries. This is a real weakness in the evidence base for the graduate-scheme audience and is recorded in Open Questions.

# Open Questions

- **Are there published national assessor guides for values scoring? — partly resolved 2026-07-20.** Central Cabinet Office guidance does mandate the 7-point scale for behaviours, experience and technical skills, and the 4-point scale for strengths [20]; COPFS [9] is a departmental publication *of* that central scale, and remains the only located source for the seven descriptor labels. What is still open is values-specific: the Civil Service treats values as a separate instrument from the Behaviours, and no central, citable assessor guide for scoring values-adjacent evidence has been found.
- **Did the HEE VBR longitudinal study report?** The framework describes a three-year study commissioned by the Department of Health that commenced April 2015 and was due to report in October 2019 [6]. Locating the published findings would materially change the confidence rating attachable to VBR's effectiveness claims.
- **Named corporate codes of conduct used in selection.** Several large employers publish codes of conduct and values statements; whether any publish the *interview questions or scoring criteria* used to assess them has not been established. This would strengthen the private-sector layer considerably.
- **CVF 2024 behavioural indicators for the three values.** This document cites the CVF guidance [7] and the Code of Ethics overview [8]; the full CVF document containing the value-level behavioural indicators was not separately retrieved, as College of Policing HTML pages return 403 to automated fetchers. Obtaining those indicators would let this document give candidates precise, published anchors for the policing values.
- **EU whistleblowing directive as it applies to candidates.** Directive (EU) 2019/1937 could not be verified in this session; its interaction with the Staff Regulations obligations [13] for EU-institution candidates should be established in the EU frameworks deep-dive.
- **Does EPSO probe ethics explicitly?** The EPSO Competency Framework has no integrity heading [14]. Whether SCBI or CBI assignments in practice include ethical dilemmas is not established from published EPSO materials.
