# Executive Summary

This is the Tier-2 deep-dive for the customer-service and stakeholder-management competency family — the family that the parent document `competencies-foundation` maps to the Civil Service behaviour **Managing a Quality Service**, to parts of the EPSO general competencies, to NHS patient-focus values, and to the "customer-facing skills" label common in graduate recruitment. Where the parent stays broad, this document goes to the level of published level descriptors, a 38-question bank with per-question provenance, and answer guidance tied to those descriptors.

The central documented finding is that this competency is **not** primarily about being nice to an angry customer. The UK Civil Service defines Managing a Quality Service as "Deliver service objectives with professional excellence, expertise and efficiency, taking account of diverse customer needs" [1], and its published level descriptors escalate from handling individual customers at AA/AO and EO grades to designing, reviewing and benchmarking whole services at HEO/SEO and Grade 7/6 [1]. NHS values-based recruitment approaches the same territory from the patient side, and published NHS trust question banks ask not just about caring for individuals but about seeking dialogue with service users to improve the services they receive [7]. In EPSO's framework the territory is distributed rather than named: service and stakeholder behaviour appears inside the anchors for Communication, Working together and Information management rather than as a standalone competency [3].

Document-level confidence is **Medium**. The UK level descriptors, the EPSO anchors, the NHS values-based recruitment method and the two published scoring scales are official or institutional sources and are quoted directly. However, the private-sector and graduate-scheme portion of the question corpus rests on careers-site question lists, which evidence *what is published as typical* rather than what any named employer asks; and the "one competency, four sub-themes" organisation of the question bank is this knowledge base's own synthesis. Under the confidence rubric, that mix is Medium.

# Key Takeaways

- The Civil Service defines the behaviour as delivering service objectives "with professional excellence, expertise and efficiency, taking account of diverse customer needs" — efficiency and cost-effectiveness sit inside the definition, not outside it. *(documented)* [1]
- The published level descriptors shift the unit of analysis with grade: at EO the candidate must "deliver good customer service which balances quality and cost effectiveness"; at HEO/SEO they must "establish ways to find and respond to feedback from customers"; at Grade 7/6 they must "establish how the business area compares to industry best practice". *(documented)* [1]
- Consequently, a story that resolves one complaint beautifully and stops there under-evidences the behaviour at every level above EO. Candidates should carry the answer through to what changed in the service. *(guidance)*
- EPSO has no standalone customer or service competency; the behaviour is evidenced through anchors such as tailoring the message "to respond to the needs of the person or persons with whom they are communicating" and explaining needs "clearly to colleagues, service providers and stakeholders". *(documented)* [3]
- NHS values-based interviewing is a *structured* interview with predetermined questions and predetermined scoring criteria, in which the probing questions — not the headline question — are designed to reveal the candidate's values. *(documented)* [6]
- Two published scoring scales are available to calibrate against: a 1–7 Success Profiles scale where 4 is "adequate positive evidence" and candidates must normally score 4 or more in all behaviours [8], and a 1–5 competency evaluation scheme in which 3 means positive and negative evidence in similar amounts [15]. *(documented)*
- Interviewers should ask each applicant the same questions where possible, use more than one interviewer, and base decisions on the job description and person specification — ACAS guidance that also reduces discrimination risk. *(documented)* [13]
- "Difficult customer" questions are the most published sub-theme in private-sector lists, but the highest-scoring answers in framework terms are usually the service-improvement ones. Prepare both. *(guidance)*

# Scope

# Definitions

- **Managing a Quality Service** — the Civil Service Success Profiles behaviour defined as "Deliver service objectives with professional excellence, expertise and efficiency, taking account of diverse customer needs" [1].
- **Customer** — in the Civil Service descriptors the term is used broadly, internally as well as externally, appearing alongside "colleagues, stakeholders and delivery partners" in the same level descriptors [1]. In this document, "customer" means the recipient of the service, whether an external paying customer, a citizen, a patient or an internal business unit.
- **Stakeholder** — a party with an interest in, or influence over, the service who is not necessarily its recipient. The Civil Service descriptors distinguish stakeholders from customers, requiring candidates at HEO/SEO to "work with stakeholders to set priorities, objectives and timescales" while separately meeting "the customers' needs" [1].
- **Values-based interview (VBI)** — per the HEE Values Based Recruitment Framework, "a type of structured interview which is particularly focused on understanding a candidate's values", in which it is "the probing questions, designed as part of a VBI, that are used to provide insight into a candidate's values" [6].
- **Anchor** — in the EPSO Competency Framework, an observable element of a general competency; the framework "defines the 8 general competencies that EU officials are expected to demonstrate throughout their career", each "composed of various observable elements, called anchors" [3].

# Framework Context

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## UK Civil Service: Managing a Quality Service

Managing a Quality Service is one of the nine Civil Service Behaviours within Success Profiles [1] [2]. Its definition is: "Deliver service objectives with professional excellence, expertise and efficiency, taking account of diverse customer needs" [1]. Behaviours are assessed across multiple stages of recruitment — application forms, tests, interviews, assessment centres and job-related simulations — and recruiting managers select the behaviours relevant to a specific role rather than requiring all nine [1].

Two other behaviours carry the stakeholder load. **Working Together** is defined as "Form effective partnerships and relationships with people both internally and externally, from a range of diverse backgrounds, sharing information, resources and support" [1]. **Communicating and Influencing** is defined as "Communicate purpose and direction with clarity, integrity and enthusiasm. Respect the needs, responses and opinions of others" [1]. A candidate asked about a difficult stakeholder in a Civil Service interview is therefore usually being scored against one of these three named behaviours, and the advert states which.

## EU institutions: where service and stakeholder behaviour sits in EPSO's framework

The EPSO Competency Framework in use since 2023 defines eight general competencies — critical thinking, analysing and creative problem-solving; decision-making and getting results; information management; self-management; working together; learning as a skill; communication; and intrapreneurship — each composed of observable anchors [3]. **None of the eight is a customer-service or stakeholder-management competency by name.**

The behaviour is instead distributed across anchors [3]:

- Communication: "Tailors the message to respond to the needs of the person or persons with whom they are communicating."
- Communication: "Takes into account the point of view of others."
- Communication: "Communicates in ways that generate buy-in and create a win-win situation for the parties involved."
- Working together: "Discusses differences of opinion openly with the individuals concerned, understands other points of view, and strives for compromise in situations of conflict."
- Working together: "Offers assistance and help to others."
- Information management: "Indicates and explains technological needs clearly to colleagues, service providers and stakeholders."
- Intrapreneurship: "Acts as a role model and an ambassador for the department."

In an EPSO competency-based interview, candidates are interviewed "for about 10 minutes per competency", with questions structured to let assessors evaluate the selected general competencies for that competition and grade [4].

## NHS: patient focus through values-based recruitment

NHS recruitment in England sits within the NHS Constitution values, which include "working together for patients", "commitment to quality of care", "compassion" and "improving lives" [5]. The HEE Values Based Recruitment Framework expects a structured interview including an assessment of values, and defines structured interviews as ones which "ask a standardised set of predetermined questions so that each candidate receives the same, or very similar, questions", which "are scored against predetermined scoring criteria which are the same for all candidates", and which "interview a candidate on an individual 1:1 basis" [6].

Individual trusts publish their own question banks against their core values. Wirral University Teaching Hospital NHS Foundation Trust publishes a bank against values including "Caring for Everyone", "Respect for all", "Embracing Teamwork" and "Committed to Improvement", instructing recruiters to ask "a minimum of two questions from the bank of values based questions below, during interviews" [7]. Notably, its "Committed to Improvement" section asks candidates to describe seeking "a dialogue with a service user and their family to improve the services they receive" [7] — the NHS analogue of the Civil Service's HEO/SEO feedback-mechanism requirement.

# Core Reference

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## The published level descriptors (Civil Service, Managing a Quality Service)

These are the most granular published criteria available for this competency in either market, and they are the backbone of this document. Quoted from GOV.UK [1].

**AA/AO grade.** The candidate is expected to: "gain a clear understanding of customers' needs and expectations"; "plan, organise and manage your own time to deliver a high quality service"; "act to prevent problems by identifying issues, reporting them and providing solutions"; "keep customers and all colleagues up to date with progress"; and "show customers where to access relevant information and support" [1].

**EO grade.** The candidate is expected to: "work with customers to understand their needs and expectations"; "create clear plans and set priorities which meet the needs of both customer and business"; "clearly explain to customers what can be done"; "keep colleagues and stakeholders fully informed of plans, possibilities and progress"; "identify common problems that affect service, report them and find possible solutions"; and "deliver good customer service which balances quality and cost effectiveness" [1].

**HEO/SEO grade.** The candidate is expected to: "develop, implement, maintain and review systems and services to ensure delivery of professional excellence"; "work with stakeholders to set priorities, objectives and timescales"; "successfully deliver high quality outcomes that meet the customers' needs and gives value for money"; "identify risks and resolve issues efficiently"; "involve a diverse range of colleagues, stakeholders and delivery partners in developing suggestions"; and "establish ways to find and respond to feedback from customers about the services provided" [1].

**Grade 7/6.** The candidate is expected to: "demonstrate positive customer service by understanding the complexity and diversity of customer needs"; "deliver a high quality, efficient and cost effective service by considering a broad range of methods"; "ensure full consideration of new technologies, accessibility and costings"; "make clear, practical and manageable plans for service delivery"; "ensure adherence to legal, regulatory and security requirements in service delivery"; "proactively manage risks and identify solutions"; "establish how the business area compares to industry best practice"; and "create regular opportunities for colleagues, stakeholders, delivery partners and customers to improve service" [1].

At Deputy Director level the emphasis moves to clarifying customer requirements, using insight for high-quality delivery, collaborating with partners and breaking complex aims into plans; at Director and Director General level it moves to ensuring delivery across government, maintaining an evolving understanding of customers, negotiating service levels and ensuring stakeholder clarity on customer impacts [1].

Three structural observations follow directly from the quoted text. First, **cost and efficiency are inside the competency** from EO upwards ("balances quality and cost effectiveness"; "value for money"; "cost effective service") [1]. Second, **feedback machinery becomes an explicit requirement at HEO/SEO** ("establish ways to find and respond to feedback") [1]. Third, **accessibility and diversity of need are explicit at Grade 7/6** ("new technologies, accessibility and costings"; "complexity and diversity of customer needs") [1], which links this competency directly to the reasonable-adjustment duty discussed under Legal & Fairness below [10].

## What the published question corpus actually contains

The published question sets divide unevenly. Private-sector and careers-service lists cluster heavily on the individual difficult-customer encounter: Indeed's UK customer-service article asks candidates to "Describe a time you dealt with a difficult customer and how you resolved the issue" and to "Describe a time you helped a customer who had spoken to multiple agents without getting a solution" [18]; My World of Work lists "Describe a time you had to handle a customer's difficult request" [17]; targetjobs lists "When have you previously delivered excellent customer service?" [16].

Institutional question banks reach further up the descriptor ladder. The Wirral trust bank includes questions on making "an improvement in your work area, role or service", on seeking "a dialogue with a service user and their family to improve the services they receive", and on what happened "when you've been given feedback on services by a service user, around what they'd like to see change" [7]. That asymmetry — careers sites publish the encounter, institutions publish the improvement loop — is the single most useful fact in this document for a candidate deciding what to prepare.

## Why structured questioning is used here

CIPD guidance states that structured interviews are better at predicting job performance than unstructured interviews, that situational and past-behavioural questions are the best kinds of interview questions, and that consistent questions with objective scoring criteria make responses comparable and reduce the influence of personal bias [14]. The academic re-analysis by Sackett, Zhang, Berry and Lievens found structured interviews to be the top-ranked selection procedure for predicting job performance after correcting for systematic overcorrection for restriction of range [20].

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact.**

## Choosing which example to use

The descriptors above [1] give a mechanical selection test. Read the grade or seniority of the role, then pick an example whose *unit of analysis* matches:

- **Entry level (AA/AO and EO analogues; retail, contact centre, first graduate role):** an individual customer encounter is appropriate, but the answer must still show understanding of the customer's need, a plan, clear explanation of what could be done, and keeping people updated [1].
- **Mid level (HEO/SEO analogues; team lead, consultant, analyst):** an individual encounter is *not enough*. The example should involve stakeholders, priorities and timescales, and ideally a feedback mechanism [1].
- **Senior level (Grade 7/6 and above):** the example should be about a service, not a transaction — plans, risks, legal and regulatory adherence, benchmarking, and opportunities for others to improve the service [1].

For EU-institution interviews, remember there is no service competency to name; select the example that best evidences the *Communication* or *Working together* anchors, and use language that maps onto them — tailoring the message to the person's needs, taking others' points of view into account, striving for compromise [3]. With roughly ten minutes per competency [4], one well-developed example per competency is the right amount of material.

## Worked STAR skeleton

Question: *"Give me an example of a time you set up a way of collecting feedback from the people who use your service, and what you changed as a result."* (composed question 7 under Service improvement).

- **Situation (brief).** "I ran the enquiries inbox for a 200-person department. We were closing tickets quickly, but the same three questions kept coming back every month."
- **Task (brief).** "I wanted to know whether we were actually solving anything, so I proposed a short post-resolution survey and a monthly review of ticket categories."
- **Action (the bulk of the answer).** "I agreed the two survey questions with our team lead so it took under 20 seconds to answer. I categorised six months of past tickets and found 41% clustered into three topics. I took that to the service owner and the two teams whose processes generated the queries — I framed it in their terms, as avoidable work landing on them, not as a complaint about them. We agreed I would draft self-service guidance for the top three topics and they would review it."
- **Result.** "Survey response rate was 34%; satisfaction with resolutions rose from 3.4 to 4.1 out of 5 over the following quarter, and tickets in those three categories fell by roughly a third, which freed about half a day a week across the team. We kept the monthly category review as a standing item."
- **Reflection (optional, one sentence).** "What I would do differently is agree the measure with the stakeholders *before* building the survey — I had to redo the categories once."

Why this skeleton scores: it evidences "establish ways to find and respond to feedback from customers about the services provided", "involve a diverse range of colleagues, stakeholders and delivery partners in developing suggestions" and "develop, implement, maintain and review systems and services", all HEO/SEO-level descriptor language [1]. It also carries the EPSO Communication anchor about generating buy-in and creating a win-win [3], because the stakeholder framing is explicit in the Action. The STAR structure itself follows the National Careers Service formulation of Situation, Task, Action and Result including what you learned [19].

## Answering the difficult-customer question well

The published difficult-customer questions [17] [18] are easy to answer adequately and hard to answer distinctively. Three moves lift the answer against the cited descriptors:

- **Separate the emotion from the problem, out loud.** Say what you did to understand the need before you say what you fixed — the descriptors at every level begin with understanding customers' needs and expectations [1].
- **Say what you could and could not do.** The EO descriptor asks candidates to "clearly explain to customers what can be done" [1]; a candidate who describes setting an honest boundary usually gives more scoreable evidence than one who implies the customer got everything they asked for.
- **Finish above the transaction.** Add one sentence on whether the cause was systemic and what you did about it. This is the bridge from an AA/AO-level story to an HEO/SEO-level one [1].

## Interviewer-side guidance

Decide before the interview which behaviour a question is scoring — a difficult-stakeholder question can legitimately sit under Working Together, Communicating and Influencing or Managing a Quality Service [1], and the criteria differ. Agree the probes in advance too, since in values-based practice the probes carry the diagnostic weight [6].

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## How answers are scored

Two published scales are available.

The Crown Office and Procurator Fiscal Service publishes the Success Profiles scoring approach it uses: applications and interviews are "scored using the 7 point scale", running from "Not Demonstrated" (1) to "Outstanding Demonstration" (7), where scores 1–3 indicate negative or limited evidence and 4 represents "adequate positive evidence" [8]. On benchmarks, its guidance states that "candidates must demonstrate the minimum essential criteria for all roles, this scoring 4 or more in all behaviours", while noting that a candidate can score a single 3 where scores for all other behaviours are high [8]. Assessors look for positive evidence of behaviours demonstrated through actions and activities, and the board uses probing questions to understand the situation, the actions taken and the outcomes [8].

The University of Oxford Careers Service reproduces a five-point competency evaluation scheme, attributed to Civil Service Resourcing best-practice recruitment guidance, in which 5 is "demonstrates positive evidence of a high level of competence with no substantial negative evidence", 4 is "demonstrates positive evidence of competence with little negative evidence", 3 is "demonstrates similar amounts of positive evidence and negative evidence of competence", 2 is "demonstrates some positive evidence of competence, but with significant negative evidence", and 1 is "demonstrates little or no positive evidence of competence, along with considerable negative evidence" [15].

Both scales are **evidence-balance** scales, not impression scales. The practical consequence for this competency is that a story containing a strong resolution *and* an unaddressed admission — for example, that the same failure kept recurring — can score at the midpoint rather than high, because negative evidence is weighed, not ignored.

## What assessors are listening for

- **Behaviour matched to anchors, not opinions.** The EPSO framework decomposes each competency into observable anchors [3]; assessors listen for described behaviour matching those anchors.
- **The probes, not the headline.** In values-based interviewing, the probing questions are the instrument designed to provide insight into the candidate's values [6].
- **Consistency across candidates.** HEE requires structured interviews scored "against predetermined scoring criteria which are the same for all candidates" [6]; ACAS advises preparing questions in advance, asking each applicant the same questions where possible, using more than one interviewer to reduce the risk of discriminating, and checking that decisions are based on how each applicant met each point in the job description and person specification [13].
- **Selected behaviours only.** Recruiting managers choose which behaviours a role is assessed against rather than requiring all nine, so the advert tells candidates what will be scored [1].
- **Time budget.** In EPSO competency-based interviews, candidates are interviewed for about ten minutes per competency [4], which bounds how much detail an answer can carry.

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact.**

- **Resolving the complaint but showing no service-improvement thinking.** The most common failure on this competency. It under-evidences everything above EO level: the HEO/SEO descriptor requires establishing ways to find and respond to feedback and reviewing systems and services [1], and a story that ends at "the customer left happy" simply contains no evidence for it. On an evidence-balance scale [8] [15] this is not so much a low score as a capped one.
- **Ignoring cost entirely.** Candidates often describe heroic effort for one customer. But "balances quality and cost effectiveness" is in the EO descriptor, and "value for money" and "cost effective service" appear at HEO/SEO and Grade 7/6 [1]. An answer where the candidate spent unbounded time on one case, with no acknowledgement of the trade-off, invites a negative-evidence mark.
- **Treating "stakeholder" as "person who was annoying".** The framework language is about partnerships and relationships, and about setting priorities, objectives and timescales with stakeholders [1]. An answer that frames the stakeholder as an obstacle to be managed around, rather than a party to be worked with, runs against the EPSO Working together anchor on discussing differences openly and striving for compromise [3].
- **The rescued-by-someone-else story.** Assessors score your actions; the COPFS guidance is explicit that they look for evidence of behaviours demonstrated through actions and activities [8]. Escalating is legitimate, but the answer needs your judgement in it.
- **Hypothetical answers to past-behaviour questions.** Several published questions in the bank above are deliberately future-framed ("How would you work with your team to…" [7]) and others are past-framed. Answer in the tense asked; an "I would…" answer to a "tell me about a time…" question provides no behavioural evidence.
- **Forgetting diversity of need.** Grade 7/6 requires "understanding the complexity and diversity of customer needs" and consideration of accessibility [1]; NHS trust banks ask directly about respect for cultural and individual differences [7]. An answer that treats all customers as identical misses available marks.
- **For interviewers: scoring a service question against a communication rubric.** If the advert names the behaviour, score that behaviour [1]; drifting criteria reintroduce the comparability problems that structured interviewing exists to solve [14].
- **For interviewers: improvising probes.** ACAS advises the same questions where possible for consistency and fairness [13], and HEE requires the same predetermined criteria for all candidates [6]. Probes should be planned, not invented per candidate.

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.**

1. **The ladder drill (45 minutes).** Take one real customer story. Write it three times: once as an AA/AO-level answer, once at HEO/SEO, once at Grade 7/6, using the quoted descriptors [1] as your checklist. Most people discover their story only reaches the first rung — that tells you what evidence you need to go and find.
2. **Self-score on a published scale (30 minutes).** Record a three-minute answer to Difficult customers question 2 [18]. Play it back and score yourself 1–5 on the evaluation scheme reproduced by Oxford [15], writing down each piece of positive evidence *and* each piece of negative evidence. If you cannot list at least three positive items, the answer is not yet a 4.
3. **The probe gauntlet (20 minutes, pairs).** Have a partner ask you one Service improvement question, then all five sourced follow-up probes in sequence [7] [15]. Answers that survive the probes are the ones worth taking to interview.
4. **Anchor translation for EPSO (20 minutes).** Take your best stakeholder story and rewrite the Action section so it evidences at least three named EPSO anchors [3] — for example tailoring the message to the person's needs, taking others' points of view into account, and striving for compromise. Say the anchor language aloud; it should sound natural, not recited.
5. **Cost sentence (10 minutes).** For each of your prepared service examples, write one sentence stating what the solution cost in time, money or opportunity, and why it was proportionate. Rehearse until it is a natural part of the Result.
6. **Interviewer drill (45 minutes, pairs).** Choose three questions from the bank, decide in advance which behaviour each scores [1], write the positive and negative indicators you will look for, agree three probes, then run a mock and compare scores. Disagreement between the two of you usually means the indicators were too vague — rewrite them.

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — every claim cited. Signposting only — not legal advice.**

- **Accessibility is part of the competency, not just of the interview.** The Grade 7/6 descriptor requires "full consideration of new technologies, accessibility and costings" in service delivery [1]. In the UK this connects to the Equality Act 2010, Part 5 of which covers work [9] and section 20 of which sets out the duty to make reasonable adjustments [10]. Candidates describing service design should expect accessibility to be a legitimate probe; interviewers should not treat it as an optional extra.
- **Adjustments in the interview itself.** ACAS states that by law employers must ask anyone coming to an interview to tell them if they need reasonable adjustments so they can attend [13]. This matters disproportionately for this competency because customer-service assessment often includes role-plays and simulations, which can disadvantage some disabled candidates without adjustment.
- **Consistency as a fairness control.** ACAS advises preparing questions in advance, asking each applicant the same questions where possible, and using more than one interviewer to reduce the risk of discriminating [13]; CIPD notes that consistent questions and objective scoring criteria reduce the influence of personal bias [14]. HEE builds the same control into values-based recruitment through predetermined questions and criteria [6].
- **EU equal treatment.** Council Directive 2000/78/EC establishes a general framework for equal treatment in employment and occupation, expressly covering conditions for access to employment including selection criteria and recruitment conditions, and imposes reasonable-accommodation obligations in respect of disabled persons [11].
- **Candidate data.** Interview notes and scores about identifiable candidates are personal data; in the EU their processing falls under Regulation (EU) 2016/679 (GDPR) [12]. Where a customer-service assessment involves recording role-plays, the recording is candidate personal data and should be handled accordingly.
- **A competency-specific caution.** Questions about "cultural fit with our customers", or about how customers might react to a candidate, risk importing third-party prejudice into a selection decision. ACAS guidance that decisions must rest on how each applicant met the job description and person specification [13] is the safer footing.

# Risks & Caveats

- **UK-weighted evidence.** The strongest published criteria for this competency are British. EPSO has no named customer or stakeholder competency [3], so the EU material in this document is necessarily inferential in its *organisation*, even though every anchor quoted is verbatim.
- **A single trust's question bank.** The NHS question corpus here is drawn from one trust's published bank [7], chosen because it is publicly available and unusually complete. Other trusts phrase questions against their own values; treat these as representative of published NHS practice, not as a national standard. A second trust bank (NHS Blood and Transplant) was identified in search results but its host did not resolve at the time of verification and it is therefore not cited.
- **Careers-site questions evidence what is published, not what is asked.** The targetjobs, My World of Work and Indeed questions [16] [17] [18] show what is published as typical; none is attributable to a named employer's actual process.
- **Scoring scales are departmental, not universal.** The 1–7 scale is published by COPFS for its own recruitment [8], and the 1–5 scheme is reproduced by a university careers service from Civil Service Resourcing guidance [15]. Other departments and employers use other scales. Use them to understand the *logic* of evidence-balance scoring, not to predict a specific employer's mark scheme.
- **Level descriptors are periodically revised.** The Success Profiles behaviours are maintained on GOV.UK [2] and the descriptors quoted here were verified on 2026-07-20; the HEE Values Based Recruitment Framework dates from March 2016 [6] and HEE has since been absorbed into NHS England, so its status should be re-checked before high-stakes use. Review is due 2027-07-20.
- **Private-sector depth is thin.** Consulting, financial-services and technology employers demonstrably ask client and stakeholder questions, but the specific published sources located for this document were general careers sites rather than named corporate frameworks. See Open Questions.

# Open Questions

- **Named private-sector competency dictionaries.** No publicly available, named consultancy or bank competency dictionary covering client service was located during verification; two large-firm careers pages were checked and carried no example questions. Obtaining one would raise the private-sector portion of this document from Medium towards High confidence.
- **Current status of the HEE Values Based Recruitment Framework under NHS England.** Whether the March 2016 framework [6] remains the operative national guidance after HEE's merger into NHS England could not be confirmed from a primary source; a current NHS England statement would resolve it.
- **A second NHS trust question bank.** The NHS Blood and Transplant values-based question document appeared in search results but its host did not resolve during verification. Corroborating the Wirral bank [7] against a second published trust bank would strengthen the NHS section.
- **EPSO situational interview treatment of service scenarios.** Whether EPSO's situational competency-based interview assignments include service or stakeholder scenarios, and how they are scored, was not verified here and belongs to the EU frameworks deep-dive.
- **Role-play and simulation criteria.** Customer-service selection frequently uses role-plays, and the Civil Service uses job-related simulations to assess behaviours [1]; published marking criteria for customer-service role-plays were not located and would be a valuable addition.
