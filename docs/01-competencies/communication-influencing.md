# Executive Summary

This is the Tier-2 deep-dive for the communication and influencing competency family. Its parent, **competencies-foundation**, established that this family recurs across frameworks under different names; this document takes that one row of the mapping table and opens it up. The central evidence is that the two most fully published frameworks in scope define the competency in a way that is *not* about eloquence. The UK Civil Service behaviour **Communicating and Influencing** is defined as "Communicate purpose and direction with clarity, integrity and enthusiasm. Respect the needs, responses and opinions of others" [2] — the second sentence is half the definition, and it is about the audience, not the speaker. The EPSO general competency **Communication** is defined as "Conveys information and opinions clearly, generates buy-in by facilitating interaction and engaging effectively with others", and three of its five published anchors concern the *other party*: tailoring the message, taking account of others' points of view, and creating a win-win [6].

The practical consequence, which drives the whole coaching layer below, is that a candidate who describes only what they said has evidenced perhaps a third of the competency. The published descriptors reward a chain: read the audience, choose the channel and register deliberately, adapt when it is not landing, and land a measurable outcome. This document reproduces the Civil Service level descriptors from AA/AO up to Director General [2] and the EPSO anchors [6], shows where the escalation between levels actually happens, and provides 42 practice questions and probes in ten groups, each labelled *(sourced …)* or *(composed)*.

Document-level confidence is **Medium**. The framework definitions, level descriptors, EPSO anchors, the College of Policing indicators and the 1–7 scoring scale rest on official primary documents and would individually merit High. The document is rated Medium because two of its organising devices — the four-stage "escalation" reading of the level descriptors, and the grouping of the question bank into levels and sub-themes — are this knowledge base's reasoned synthesis rather than anything the frameworks publish, and because published *communication-specific* question sets are thin: only eight of the 42 entries could be sourced to a named publisher, the rest are composed in the style of the cited frameworks.

# Key Takeaways

- The Civil Service defines Communicating and Influencing as communicating "purpose and direction with clarity, integrity and enthusiasm" *and* respecting "the needs, responses and opinions of others" — audience-awareness is written into the definition, not an optional extra. *(documented)* [2]
- EPSO's Communication competency has five published anchors; only the first is about clarity of expression. The other four cover tailoring the message, using "convincing arguments and solid reasoning", taking account of others' viewpoints, and generating buy-in and "a win-win situation for the parties involved" [6]. Influencing is not a separate EPSO competency — it sits inside Communication. *(documented)*
- The Civil Service level descriptors escalate in a readable pattern: AA/AO is about plain language and being checkable; EO adds channel choice and "challenging conversations"; HEO/SEO adds style-switching "to maximise understanding and impact"; Grade 7/6 adds building trust, making complex issues accessible and delivering difficult messages; Deputy Director and above add negotiation and external partner influence [2]. *(documented descriptors; the staged reading is guidance)*
- Where scoring scales are published, the bar is explicit: the Crown Office and Procurator Fiscal Service publishes a 1–7 Success Profiles scale in which 4 is "Adequate positive evidence", 5 is "Substantial positive evidence of the behaviour", and candidates must normally score 4 or more in all behaviours [4]. *(documented)*
- Written communication is separately assessable and frequently under-prepared. The Civil Service AA/AO descriptor explicitly requires "plain and simple language, being careful to check written work for errors" [2], EPSO's first anchor covers conveying information clearly "verbally as well as in writing" [6], and a published question asks specifically about written influence [19]. *(documented)*
- Other UK frameworks carry the same competency without a "communication" label: the College of Policing CVF distributes it across values and competencies — "I tailor my communication to be appropriate and respectful to my audience" sits under Public Service, and influencing and negotiating sit inside the collaboration competency [10]. NHS values-based recruitment reaches it through the 6Cs, in which communication is one of the six [12]. *(documented)*
- For graduate schemes and private-sector roles the same behaviours are assessed under looser labels and often inside other exercises. Employers publish the expectation directly: EY tells candidates to "build a rapport with your interviewers" and to answer the question actually asked without getting side-tracked [22]. *(documented)*
- Prepare **three** distinct examples, not one: explaining something complex to a non-expert, influencing someone who could say no to you, and a piece of written communication that changed a decision. *(guidance)*
- The most common failure is describing the message but not the audience adaptation or the outcome — an answer that cannot evidence the second half of the Civil Service definition [2] or four of EPSO's five anchors [6]. *(guidance)*

# Purpose

This document answers, for a candidate: *"What exactly is being scored when an interviewer asks me about communication or influencing, how does that change with the seniority of the role, and what does an answer that scores 5 rather than 3 contain?"* The parent document teaches signal-reading across eight families; this one assumes the family has already been identified and goes to depth — the actual level descriptors, the actual anchors, and enough practice questions to drill against.

For an interviewer or HR practitioner it answers: *"What do the published frameworks say this competency consists of, at what level should I pitch questions for this grade, and what indicators can I defensibly score against?"* It is deliberately usable in both directions, because the published descriptors are the same document for both parties — candidates are explicitly pointed to them by the Civil Service Careers guidance [3].

# Definitions

- **Communicating and Influencing** — the Civil Service Success Profiles behaviour: "Communicate purpose and direction with clarity, integrity and enthusiasm. Respect the needs, responses and opinions of others" [2]. Note that UK Civil Service practice treats communicating and influencing as a *single* behaviour, not two.
- **Communication (EPSO)** — the EPSO general competency: "Conveys information and opinions clearly, generates buy-in by facilitating interaction and engaging effectively with others" [6]. Influencing is subsumed within it.
- **Anchor** — in the EPSO framework, an observable element of a general competency used to recognise and assess it; the framework leaflet publishes all eight competencies with their anchors [5] [6].
- **Behavioural indicator** — in the College of Policing CVF, an example behaviour written in the first person ("I tailor my communication…") that indicates the competency or value; the CVF guidance is explicit that indicators "should never be used as a simple checklist" [10] [11].
- **Influencing without authority** — this knowledge base's term for persuading someone who does not report to you and can decline. It is not a framework term, but it is the situation most influencing questions describe, and it is the distinction that separates this family from **leadership-management**.
- **Register** — the level of formality, technicality and vocabulary chosen for a particular audience. Used here as the concrete lever behind the descriptors' repeated demand to adapt "styles" and "methods" to the audience [2].

# Framework Context

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## UK Civil Service: Communicating and Influencing

Success Profiles is the recruitment framework used within the UK Civil Service, drawing on five elements — Behaviours, Strengths, Ability, Experience and Technical — with the mix depending on the role [1]. Communicating and Influencing is one of the nine named Behaviours, defined as: "Communicate purpose and direction with clarity, integrity and enthusiasm. Respect the needs, responses and opinions of others" [2].

Civil Service Careers guidance tells candidates that Behaviours "are actions and activities people do which result in effective performance", that they "are specific to the grade or level of the role", and that recruiting managers "will choose the behaviours best suited to a job role" — a candidate will not be asked to demonstrate all nine for one role [3]. The same guidance directs candidates to choose examples that "clearly demonstrate the behaviour", that "will allow you to explain in some detail what you personally did", and that "had positive results", while noting explicitly that "less successful examples can be used if you can demonstrate the lessons learned and how errors would be avoided in future" [3].

Behaviours are not assessed only at interview. The published guidance lists application forms with written examples, Civil Service judgement tests, personality tests, interviews, assessment centres, and job-related simulations including presentations, in-tray exercises, written analysis, group exercises, role-play and oral briefings [2]. Several of those simulation types — presentation, written analysis, oral briefing, role-play — assess communication *directly by observation* rather than by asking about a past example. Candidates may also be asked how they would behave in a given situation, through situational judgement tests or situational interview questions [3].

## EU institutions: EPSO's Communication competency

EPSO's Competency Framework "defines the competencies that are considered essential to perform effectively in any given job, by the EU institutions and bodies that EPSO serves" [5]. The framework in use since 2023 defines eight general competencies, each decomposed into observable anchors; Communication is the seventh [6].

EPSO assesses selected general competencies at interview in two published formats. The **competency-based interview (CBI)** evaluates "selected general competencies, depending on the competition and grade the candidate has applied for", with the interview lasting approximately ten minutes per competency assessed and questions "structured to allow the assessors to evaluate the candidate's selected general competencies" [7]. The **situational competency-based interview (SCBI)** instead assesses general competencies "by asking the candidate how he/she would react in a specific situation", with sample materials published in English and French for both AST and AD categories [8]. Which competencies are assessed, and by which test, is set out in the Notice of Competition for each competition [7].

## UK policing: the Competency and Values Framework

The College of Policing CVF has no competency called "communication". Instead the behaviours are distributed across its values and competencies. The framework comprises six competencies and three core values reflecting the Code of Ethics principles, and "Each competency has three levels, with three being the most complex. These levels are not assigned to specific ranks"; levels apply to competencies only, not values [11]. Level 1 broadly relates to practitioners, Level 2 to supervisors and managers and Level 3 to senior managers and executives, though the guidance stresses that "levels can be flexibly applied to specific professional profiles as required" [11].

Communication behaviours appear as first-person indicators throughout: under **Public Service**, "I tailor my communication to be appropriate and respectful to my audience"; under **Transparency**, "I am clear and comprehensive when communicating with others" and "I am consistent and truthful in my communications"; and at Level 1 of the emotional-awareness competency, "I communicate in clear and simple language so that I can be easily understood by others" [10]. Influencing appears explicitly at senior level: "I understand internal and external politics and I am able to wield influence effectively, tailoring my actions to achieve the impact needed", and "I ensure that all perspectives inform decision making and communicate the reasons behind decisions in a way that is clear and compelling" [10]. Negotiation is placed inside collaboration: the framework states that partnership working requires the service to "negotiate new ways of providing services together", and that "we need to influence and negotiate in order to achieve outcomes for everyone" [10].

## NHS: communication as a value, not a competency

NHS recruitment in England sits within the six values of the NHS Constitution — working together for patients; respect and dignity; commitment to quality of care; compassion; improving lives; and everyone counts [9]. Communication is not among them. It enters through the **6Cs**: the Health Education England Values Based Recruitment Framework records that the core values and behaviours behind the strategy are "care, compassion, competence, communication, courage and commitment", and that these "are clearly linked to the values of the NHS Constitution" [12]. NHS Employers describes values-based recruitment as a method to "find people whose personal values match with your organisation", delivered through pre-screening assessments, "values based interviewing techniques (role play, written responses to scenarios)" and assessment-centre approaches [18]. Role-play and written scenario responses assess communication behaviours by observation.

## Private sector and graduate schemes

There is no single published private-sector framework, but the expectation is stated openly by employers and careers services. EY's published interview guidance tells candidates to pay "keen attention to the question being asked of you and ensure you fully answer it without getting" side-tracked, and to "build a rapport with your interviewers by showing an interest in their roles or" divisions — that is, communication is assessed *during* the interview as well as through its content [22]. targetjobs, whose competency guidance is written for graduate applicants, publishes two communication-family questions among its example set and recommends the STAR technique with "most of your response" devoted to the Action [20]. The University of Oxford Careers Service publishes a competency question set with a dedicated **Communication** heading, indicating that graduate recruiters ask about written influence, audience adaptation and presentation as distinct sub-themes [19].

# Core Reference

## The Civil Service level descriptors, grade by grade

The published guidance sets out Communicating and Influencing at each grade band. Verbatim fragments are quoted; the remainder summarises the published descriptor [2].

**AA/AO.** Put forward your views "in a clear, constructive and considerate manner". Use appropriate communication methods for each person with consideration for individual needs. Use "plain and simple language, being careful to check written work for errors". Consider the impact of language on different stakeholder groups. "Remain honest and truthful when explaining opinions" [2].

**EO.** "Communicate clearly and concisely both orally and in writing." Select appropriate channels, considering the audience and value for money. "Interact with others in an enthusiastic way." Listen to and value different ideas and viewpoints. Handle challenging conversations with confidence and sensitivity [2].

**HEO/SEO.** "Communicate in a straightforward, honest and engaging manner — choosing appropriate styles to maximise understanding and impact." Encourage varied communication methods including digital resources. Ensure communications consider individual needs and have a clear purpose. Show positivity and enthusiasm while respecting diverse interests [2].

**Grade 7/6.** "Communicate with others in a clear, honest and enthusiastic way in order to build trust." Explain complex issues accessibly. Deliver difficult messages with clarity and sensitivity. Monitor the effectiveness of communications and improve them [2].

**Deputy Director.** Implement communication strategies, ensuring effectiveness and consideration of individual needs. Embed culture using varied communication methods efficiently. "Communicate in a straightforward, honest and truthful way with consideration for the impact of the method used." Communicate with conviction during negotiations. Influence external partners successfully [2].

**Director / Director General.** "Demonstrate and promote communicating with honesty, integrity, impartiality and objectivity." Ensure the infrastructure supports varied, cost-effective methods. Develop a culture that considers individual communication needs. "Communicate purpose and direction with respect, clarity and enthusiasm." Use influence positively across the Civil Service [2].

> **Reading the escalation (synthesis, not a framework claim).** The descriptors above are documented [2]; the following four-stage summary is this knowledge base's reading of them, offered as a navigation aid and not as a framework statement. Stage 1 (AA/AO–EO) is *be clear and be checkable* — plain language, error-free writing, the right channel. Stage 2 (EO–HEO/SEO) is *adapt deliberately* — style-switching to maximise impact, and handling challenging conversations. Stage 3 (Grade 7/6) is *build trust and simplify complexity* — making complex issues accessible, delivering difficult messages, and measuring whether the communication worked. Stage 4 (Deputy Director and above) is *influence beyond your own authority* — negotiation, external partners, and setting communication strategy and culture.

Two features of the published set are worth naming because candidates routinely miss them. First, **written communication is explicitly in scope at every level**, from checking work for errors at AA/AO to communicating "clearly and concisely both orally and in writing" at EO [2]. Second, **honesty is part of this competency**, not a separate integrity behaviour: "remain honest and truthful when explaining opinions" at AA/AO, "honest and engaging" at HEO/SEO, and "honesty, integrity, impartiality and objectivity" at Director level [2]. An influencing example that relies on selective disclosure is scoring against itself.

## The EPSO Communication anchors

EPSO's leaflet publishes the competency and all five of its anchors verbatim [6]:

> **Communication.** "Conveys information and opinions clearly, generates buy-in by facilitating interaction and engaging effectively with others."
>
> Anchors:
>
> - "Conveys information and opinions clearly and concisely (verbally as well as in writing)."
> - "Tailors the message to respond to the needs of the person or persons with whom they are communicating."
> - "Uses convincing arguments and solid reasoning to put the message across."
> - "Takes into account the point of view of others."
> - "Communicates in ways that generate buy-in and create a win-win situation for the parties involved."

The anchors are the closest thing either framework publishes to a scoring checklist, and their distribution is instructive: one anchor covers expression, one covers argument quality, and three concern the counterparty — tailoring, taking account of their view, and creating mutual benefit [6]. Because the CBI allots roughly ten minutes per competency [7], a candidate answering a Communication question has time to evidence several anchors within a single example.

Three observations follow from the table, each resting on the cited cells. **Audience adaptation is the only element every framework in scope states explicitly** [2] [6] [10]. **Persuasion is framed as reasoned, not rhetorical** — EPSO requires "solid reasoning" [6] and the CVF requires reasons communicated compellingly [10]; neither rewards charisma as such. And **delivering bad news is a distinctively UK Civil Service emphasis**, appearing at EO and Grade 7/6 [2] with no direct EPSO anchor equivalent [6] — which matters for candidates preparing across both markets.

## What the frameworks do not settle

Neither the Civil Service guidance [2] nor the EPSO leaflet [6] publishes worked example answers, model responses, or the interview questions themselves. The Notice of Competition is the operative document for any given EPSO competition [7], and Civil Service question sets are set per campaign by the recruiting manager [3]. Published *communication-specific* questions therefore come from careers services and practitioner publishers [19] [20], which evidence what is typically asked, not what a named employer will ask.

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact. It points back to the evidence above and adds no new facts.**

## Selecting the right example

Three tests, each derived from a documented requirement:

1. **Was there a real audience gap?** The descriptors reward adaptation — "choosing appropriate styles to maximise understanding and impact" [2], "tailors the message to respond to the needs of the person" [6]. If your audience already understood, already agreed, and had no competing interest, the example gives you nothing to adapt to.
2. **Could they have said no?** For influencing questions specifically, an example where you instructed a direct report is a *leadership* example. This competency lives in the space where you had no authority and had to generate buy-in [6].
3. **Do you know the outcome?** The Civil Service guidance asks for examples that "had positive results", while explicitly permitting "less successful examples … if you can demonstrate the lessons learned" [3]. Grade 7/6 goes further and requires monitoring effectiveness and improving communications [2] — so an example where you *measured* whether it worked is disproportionately valuable at senior levels.

A candidate should hold three prepared examples: **explain** (complex thing, non-expert audience), **influence** (someone who could refuse) and **write** (a document that changed something). The ten sub-headings above are variations these three can usually cover.

## Worked STAR skeleton

Applying STAR — Situation, Task, Action, Result [21] — to composed question 2 in *Influencing without authority* ("a time you persuaded someone who had the power to say no to you"). Note the proportions: Situation and Task are two sentences; Action carries the answer, as targetjobs advises devoting "most of your response" to it [20].

- **Situation (brief).** "Our team needed the finance function to move a monthly reporting deadline. The finance lead owned that deadline and had refused a similar request the year before."
- **Task (brief).** "I had six weeks to get agreement, with no authority over finance and no escalation route I wanted to use."
- **Action (the substance — evidence the anchors).** "I started by asking rather than proposing: I booked twenty minutes to understand why the deadline existed, and learned it was driven by an audit dependency, not preference — *takes into account the point of view of others* [6]. That changed my argument entirely. I rebuilt the case around audit risk rather than our convenience, and quantified it: moving two days earlier gave them four extra days of buffer before the audit window — *convincing arguments and solid reasoning* [6]. I sent a one-page note ahead of the meeting written in their terms, with the audit implication in the first paragraph, because I knew she read papers before meetings and disliked being pitched at live — *tailors the message* [6], and *choosing appropriate styles to maximise understanding and impact* [2]. In the meeting I led with what she would gain, offered a single-month trial with an agreed rollback, and was explicit that if it created problems we would revert — *generate buy-in and create a win-win situation* [6]. When she raised a resourcing objection I had not anticipated, I did not argue it; I took it away and came back with our team absorbing the reconciliation step."
- **Result (measurable, plus effectiveness check).** "She agreed to the trial and after one month made it permanent. The audit buffer went from two days to six. I asked her afterwards what had actually moved her — she said the one-pager, not the meeting — and I have written to that pattern with her ever since." *(The closing sentence evidences monitoring effectiveness and improving communications, the Grade 7/6 descriptor [2].)*

## Calibrating to the level

Read the advert. If the role is EO, the descriptor asks for clarity, channel choice and handling a challenging conversation [2] — a well-told explaining example scores well. If it is Grade 7/6, that same explaining example will score adequately but not strongly, because the descriptor has moved on to building trust, making complexity accessible, delivering difficult messages and monitoring effectiveness [2]. Senior candidates should lead with negotiation and external influence [2]. For EPSO, run your example against the five anchors beforehand and make sure at least three are visibly evidenced in the Action [6]; with roughly ten minutes per competency [7] there is time to do this, but only if the structure is prepared.

## Adapting to the format

Communication is frequently assessed by observation rather than by question — presentations, written analysis, oral briefings, group exercises and role-play are all named in the Civil Service guidance [2], and NHS values-based recruitment uses role-play and written scenario responses [18]. In those exercises the competency is demonstrated live, so preparation shifts from example-selection to rehearsal. For situational formats — the EPSO SCBI asks "how he/she would react in a specific situation" [8] — answer in the future conditional but keep the same anchor discipline: say who the audience is, what you would need to understand about them first, and what outcome you would be aiming for.

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

- **What a published scoring scale looks like.** The Crown Office and Procurator Fiscal Service publishes the Success Profiles scale it applies to applications and interviews: 1–2 "No positive evidence and/or substantial negative evidence"; 3 "Moderate positive evidence but some negative evidence"; 4 "Adequate positive evidence and any negative evidence would not cause concern"; 5 "Substantial positive evidence of the behaviour"; 6 "Substantial positive evidence; includes some evidence of exceeding expectations"; 7 "The evidence provided wholly exceeds expectation at this level" [4]. The standard benchmark is that candidates must "demonstrate the minimum essential criteria for all roles, this includes scoring 4 or more in all behaviours", though one behaviour may score 3 if all others are high [4].
- **Evidence, not assertion, is what is scored.** The same guidance directs candidates to provide concrete examples, ideally using the STAR model, and states that the selection panel will ask "probing questions to ensure they fully understand the situation" [4]. Every point on the scale is worded in terms of *evidence provided* [4] — which is why the follow-up probes in the question bank matter as much as the opening question.
- **Behaviours are grade-specific.** Recruiting managers choose the behaviours suited to the role, and Behaviours "are specific to the grade or level of the role" [3]. An assessor scores against the descriptor for *that* grade [2], not against a general impression of how well the candidate speaks.
- **What EPSO assessors are structured to do.** CBI questions are "structured to allow the assessors to evaluate the candidate's selected general competencies", with approximately ten minutes per competency, and the competencies assessed depend on the competition and grade [7]. The anchors are the published observable elements against which a competency is recognised [5] [6].
- **Indicators are not checklists.** The College of Policing is explicit that its example behavioural indicators "are just that — they are indicators of the sorts of behaviours that could demonstrate the competency", that "It would be impossible to list every possible expression of the competency or value", and that "The CVF behaviours should never be used as a simple checklist"; the context of the individual's role should always be considered [11]. Assessors match substance, not keywords.
- **Consistency is the assessor's obligation.** ACAS advises that it is "a good idea to prepare a set of questions to ask at the interview", and that "When interviewing it's a good idea to ask each applicant the same questions where possible. This is to ensure you're treating applicants in the same way" [16]. CIPD describes the structured method as "asking a predefined set of questions, in the same order, to all candidates" with responses "scored using consistent criteria", noting that structured interviews "ask all candidates the same questions in the same order and score responses according to the objective criteria. This makes responses comparable" [17].

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact. Each pitfall names the documented criterion it fails.**

- **Describing the message but not the audience.** The single most common failure in this family. An answer that says what you communicated, in what format, and how well it went — but never says who the audience was, what they already knew, what they cared about, or how that shaped your choices — cannot evidence the second sentence of the Civil Service definition ("Respect the needs, responses and opinions of others" [2]) or the EPSO anchor "Tailors the message to respond to the needs of the person or persons with whom they are communicating" [6]. On a 1–7 scale that is the difference between "Moderate positive evidence" and "Substantial positive evidence of the behaviour" [4]. Fix: name the audience and one specific thing you knew about them, before describing what you did.
- **No outcome, or an outcome you cannot evidence.** Stopping at "and I presented it" leaves the R of STAR empty [21]. It also forecloses the Grade 7/6 requirement to monitor effectiveness and improve communications [2]. Fix: state what changed, and if you can, how you knew.
- **Confusing influencing with instructing.** Answering an influencing question with an example where you told a direct report what to do puts you in the leadership family, not this one; the competency is about generating buy-in [6]. Fix: choose someone who could have refused.
- **Charisma as the answer.** Both frameworks reward reasoning: "convincing arguments and solid reasoning" [6] and, in policing, reasons communicated "in a way that is clear and compelling" [10]. An answer whose mechanism is "I'm good with people" or "I built rapport" describes a trait, not a behaviour. Fix: state the actual argument you made and why it worked on that person.
- **Ignoring written communication entirely.** Written work is in the descriptors at every Civil Service level [2] and in EPSO's first anchor [6], and there is a published question specifically about written influence [19]. Candidates who prepare only spoken examples are unprepared for a documented sub-theme. Fix: prepare one written example.
- **Winning by concealment.** "I didn't mention the downside" fails the honesty requirements written *into* this competency — "remain honest and truthful when explaining opinions" [2] — not into a separate one. Fix: if your example turned on managing what people knew, pick a different example.
- **One example stretched across all ten sub-themes.** Explaining, persuading, negotiating, delivering bad news and writing are separately described in the frameworks [2] [6] [10]; a single story stretched across all of them fits none of them well.
- **For interviewers: scoring delivery instead of the descriptor.** A fluent candidate describing a trivial communication challenge should not outscore a hesitant candidate describing a genuinely difficult audience adaptation — the descriptor is what is scored [2], against the grade [3]. This is also where accent, fluency and disability-related differences in delivery leak into scores; see *Legal & Fairness Considerations*.
- **For interviewers: treating indicator lists as checklists.** Explicitly warned against by the College of Policing [11]; the same logic applies to the EPSO anchors [6], which are observable elements, not boxes.
- **For interviewers: improvising different questions per candidate.** Undermines comparability and fairness — ACAS advises the same questions where possible [16] and CIPD ties comparability to consistent questions and objective criteria [17].

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.** Drills a reader can complete this week.

1. **Anchor audit (30 minutes).** Write out your best influencing example in full. Then take EPSO's five anchors [6] and mark, line by line, which sentence evidences which anchor. Most first drafts evidence one or two. Rewrite the Action section until at least three are visible, and specifically until "Tailors the message" and "Takes into account the point of view of others" both appear.
2. **Level-shift drill (20 minutes).** Take one explaining example. Write a two-minute version pitched at the EO descriptor (clear and concise, appropriate channel, handled a challenging conversation) and a two-minute version pitched at the Grade 7/6 descriptor (built trust, made complexity accessible, monitored whether it worked) [2]. Notice how much of the second version you had to invent — those gaps show where you need a better example.
3. **Self-score against the published scale (20 minutes).** Record yourself answering three questions from the bank above, then score yourself 1–7 using the COPFS definitions [4]. Be strict about the boundary between 3 ("Moderate positive evidence but some negative evidence") and 5 ("Substantial positive evidence of the behaviour") [4]. Anything you score below 4 is a benchmark failure under that scheme [4].
4. **Probe survival (pairs, 20 minutes).** Give your example to a partner, who then asks only the three follow-up probes in the final question-bank group — including "How did you know your message had landed?" Answers that collapse under probing usually collapse because the Result was asserted rather than evidenced [4].
5. **Written-influence exercise (45 minutes).** Take a decision you want made and write the one-page note that would make it. Then cut it by half without losing the argument. This drills the AA/AO requirement for "plain and simple language" [2] and the EPSO anchor for conveying information "clearly and concisely (verbally as well as in writing)" [6].
6. **Audience-inversion drill (15 minutes).** Take any prepared example and re-tell it entirely from the other person's point of view: what they wanted, what they feared, what would have made them say no. If you cannot narrate it from their side, you have an example about yourself, not about communication [6].
7. **Interviewer drill (pairs, 30 minutes).** Pick a grade band, write three questions pitched at that band's descriptor [2], agree the scoring criteria *before* the mock interview [17], run it, then compare scores. Where you disagree, check whether you were scoring the descriptor or the delivery.

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — every claim cited. Signposting only, not legal advice.**

Communication competencies carry a fairness risk that most other competencies do not: the thing being assessed — how a person speaks and writes — overlaps with characteristics that are legally protected. Fluency, accent, processing speed, eye contact, turn-taking and written expression can all be affected by disability, neurodivergence, or by the candidate working in a second language. Assessing "communication" without care therefore risks assessing something else.

- **UK — reasonable adjustments.** Section 20 of the Equality Act 2010 imposes the duty to make adjustments, comprising three requirements: taking reasonable steps to avoid the substantial disadvantage caused by a provision, criterion or practice; addressing disadvantage arising from physical features; and providing auxiliary aids where their absence would put a disabled person at a substantial disadvantage [13]. An interview question set, and the way a communication exercise is run, are a "provision, criterion or practice".
- **UK — the employer must ask.** ACAS states that employers must by law ask candidates coming to an interview about accommodations they need in order to attend — for example wheelchair access, extra time for tests, or a sign-language interpreter [16]. ACAS also states plainly that "Questions must not discriminate", advises having more than one interviewer present, and advises checking that interviewers understand discrimination law beforehand; questions based on protected characteristics are prohibited [16].
- **UK — Civil Service practice.** The Civil Service candidate guidance tells candidates that if they have any worries or concerns about the way an assessment will affect them they should contact the vacancy holder, and that "Every effort will be made to make adjustments to make things work for you" [3].
- **EU — equal treatment.** Council Directive 2000/78/EC establishes a general framework for equal treatment in employment and occupation and applies "to all persons, as regards both the public and private sectors, including public bodies"; Article 3(1)(a) brings within scope "conditions for access to employment, to self-employment or to occupation, including selection criteria and recruitment conditions" [14]. Selection criteria — including a communication competency and how it is scored — sit squarely inside that scope.
- **Candidate data.** Interview notes, recorded answers, scores and written exercise submissions about identifiable candidates are personal data; in the EU their processing falls under Regulation (EU) 2016/679, which requires processing to be lawful, fair and transparent [15]. Recorded or transcribed communication exercises are a particularly rich form of candidate personal data and should be retained and shared accordingly.
- **Practical implication for this competency (guidance following from the above).** Score the *outcome and adaptation* described in the example, not the candidate's live delivery, unless live delivery is a documented, job-relevant requirement assessed through a named exercise [2] and adjustments have been offered [16].

# Risks & Caveats

- **The four-stage escalation reading is synthesis.** The level descriptors are documented verbatim [2]; the "clear → adapt → trust → influence" summary is this knowledge base's reading and could be drawn differently. Always work from the descriptor for the grade in the advert, not from the summary.
- **Only eight of 42 entries are sourced.** Published question sets specific to communication and influencing are thin: this document could source four questions and two probes to a single university careers service [19] and two questions to a practitioner publisher [20]. The remaining 34 are composed in the style of the cited frameworks. They are realistic, but they are not evidence of what any employer asks.
- **The Oxford question set is dated.** The University of Oxford Careers Service example questions PDF is dated May 2016 [19]. Its communication questions remain consistent with the current framework descriptors, but it is a decade old at the time of writing.
- **The 1–7 scale is departmental, not universal.** The scale and benchmark quoted are published by COPFS [4]. Success Profiles scoring practice varies between departments and campaigns; the central GOV.UK guidance does not publish a single mandatory scale [1] [2]. Do not assume the same numbers apply to another department's campaign.
- **EPSO competition detail lives in the Notice of Competition.** Which competencies are assessed and by which test is set per competition [7] [8]; the competency framework itself [5] [6] does not settle it.
- **The CVF is distributed, and the 2016 indicators are the detailed source.** The 2024 CVF guidance sets out the structure — six competencies, three levels, and the caution against checklist use [11] — while the detailed first-person indicators quoted here come from the 2016 framework document [10]. Readers working in policing should check whether their force uses refreshed indicator wording.
- **NHS coverage is deliberately shallow.** Communication reaches NHS recruitment through the 6Cs [12] and values-based interviewing methods [18] rather than through a named competency; this document signposts rather than treats it. The planned NHS framework documents should go further.
- **Private-sector evidence is the weakest link in this document.** The private-sector and graduate-scheme material rests on one employer's published interview guidance [22], one practitioner publisher [20] and university careers services [19] [23]. No named corporate competency dictionary was obtained. This is the main reason the document is rated Medium rather than High.

# Open Questions

- **Are there published Civil Service assessor marking notes for Communicating and Influencing specifically?** The COPFS scale [4] is generic across behaviours. Behaviour-specific assessor guidance — what distinguishes a 5 from a 6 on *this* behaviour — has not been located at a stable public URL.
- **Does EPSO publish sample CBI questions for Communication?** Sample SCBI materials are published [8]; an equivalent published Communication CBI question, rather than the anchors alone [6], would let this bank source a genuinely EU question rather than composing in EPSO's style.
- **What does refreshed College of Policing CVF indicator wording say?** The 2024 guidance [11] describes the structure; whether the first-person indicators quoted from the 2016 document [10] have been reworded in a current published indicator set is unresolved.
- **Named private-sector competency dictionaries.** A publicly documented corporate framework — a bank, consultancy or large graduate employer publishing its communication competency and its levels — would materially raise this document's confidence and is the single highest-value gap.
- **Is there evidence on accent and fluency bias in structured communication scoring?** The fairness section reasons from the general obligations [13] [14] [16]; peer-reviewed evidence quantifying bias in communication-competency scoring specifically was not searched in this pass and would strengthen the guidance.
