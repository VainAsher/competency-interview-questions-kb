# Executive Summary

This is the foundation document for the Competencies category. It explains what competency-based (also called behavioural or structured) interviewing is, summarises the published evidence for why employers across the UK and EU use it, and organises the competencies that recur across major frameworks into eight recurring **competency families**: working with others; communication and influencing; leadership and management; problem-solving and analysis; delivering results; resilience and adaptability; integrity and values; and customer and stakeholder focus. It then maps those families onto three named public frameworks — the UK Civil Service Success Profiles Behaviours [2], the EPSO general competencies used for EU-institution selection [4], and the NHS values [6] — and onto the competency labels most commonly assessed in private-sector and graduate-scheme recruitment [15].

The document serves both audiences of this knowledge base. Candidates learn how to recognise which competency a question is really testing from its wording, so preparation effort lands where it counts. Interviewers and HR practitioners see how the major frameworks define and group competencies, and why professional-body and academic evidence favours structured, criterion-scored interviewing over unstructured conversation [12] [18].

Document-level confidence is **Medium**. The framework definitions, competency lists and validity evidence rest on official and academic sources and would individually merit High confidence; however, the eight-family taxonomy and the cross-framework mapping table are this knowledge base's own reasoned synthesis (clearly labelled as such), and a document whose central organising device is synthesis is rated Medium under the confidence rubric.

# Key Takeaways

- Competency-based interviews ask candidates for real past examples ("Tell me about a time when…") and score the answers against pre-agreed criteria; the CIPD describes structured interviews as asking a predefined set of questions, in the same order, to all candidates, with responses scored against consistent criteria. *(documented)* [12]
- The evidence case is strong: in the most recent large re-analysis of selection-method validity, structured interviews emerged as the top-ranked selection procedure for predicting job performance. *(documented)* [18] [19]
- The UK Civil Service assesses nine named Behaviours (e.g. Working Together, Communicating and Influencing, Delivering at Pace) as part of Success Profiles [1] [2]; EPSO assesses eight general competencies (e.g. Working Together, Communication, Decision-Making and Getting Results) defined in its 2023 Competency Framework [3] [4]. *(documented)*
- The same underlying families recur across public-sector, EU and graduate-scheme frameworks under different names — a candidate who prepares one strong example per family can adapt it across employers. *(guidance)*
- Question wording signals the competency being tested: stems such as "worked in a team", "persuade/explain", "led", "difficult decision", "tight deadline", "setback/pressure" each point to a different family. *(guidance, illustrated with sourced questions below)*
- Interviewers should ask each applicant the same questions where possible and base decisions on the job description and person specification — this is ACAS guidance and also reduces discrimination risk under the Equality Act 2010. *(documented)* [7] [11]
- Graduate-scheme competition is intense — ISE data reported an average of 140 applications per graduate job in 2024 — which is one reason large employers rely on structured, criterion-scored assessment at scale. *(documented)* [17]
- The eight-family taxonomy used in this knowledge base is an editorial organising device, not an official standard; always check the named framework an employer actually uses. *(guidance)*

# Purpose

This document answers two questions. For candidates: *"What competencies do UK and EU employers actually assess in interviews, and how do I tell which one a question is testing?"* For interviewers and HR: *"What are the recognised competency families, how do the major frameworks define them, and what does good evidence-based practice look like when mapping questions to competencies?"* It is the parent document for the per-competency deep-dives in this category, which take each family to depth; this document deliberately stays broad.

# Scope

**Covered:** the definition and evidence base of competency-based/structured interviewing; eight recurring competency families; mapping of those families onto the UK Civil Service Success Profiles Behaviours, the EPSO general competencies, NHS values (briefly), and common private-sector/graduate-scheme competency labels; how question wording signals the competency assessed; a starter question bank spanning the families; and the legal and fairness essentials for UK and EU markets.

**Not covered (deliberate exclusions):** per-competency depth — level descriptors, grade-by-grade expectations and extended model answers belong to the child documents (teamwork-collaboration, communication-influencing, leadership-management, problem-solving-decision-making, delivering-results-planning, resilience-adaptability, integrity-values-ethics, customer-stakeholder-service, all planned); detailed treatment of interview techniques such as STAR/CARL (see techniques-foundation); full framework guides (see uk-frameworks-foundation and eu-frameworks-foundation); and interview formats such as panels, video and assessment centres (see formats-foundation). Sector-specific frameworks (e.g. policing, teaching) are out of scope here.

# Definitions

- **Competency / behaviour** — in the Civil Service Success Profiles framework, Behaviours are "the actions and activities that people do which result in effective performance in a job" [2]. "Competency" is the more common generic term for the same idea in private-sector and EU usage [3] [15].
- **Competency-based (behavioural) interview** — an interview in which candidates draw on examples from their own experience to demonstrate particular competencies; targetjobs describes it as one where "recruiters will ask questions where you will need to draw on examples from your life so far to demonstrate times when you have employed particular competencies" [15].
- **Structured interview** — per CIPD guidance, an interview using a predefined set of questions, asked in the same order to all candidates, with responses scored using consistent criteria against pre-agreed criteria for each question [12].
- **Anchor** — in the EPSO Competency Framework, an observable element of a general competency used to recognise and assess it [4].
- **Competency family** — this knowledge base's term for a cluster of closely related competencies that recur across frameworks under different names. An editorial grouping, not an official framework term.
- **STAR** — Situation, Task, Action, Result: a widely recommended structure for competency answers [13]. Treated fully in techniques-foundation.

# Framework Context

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## UK Civil Service: Success Profiles Behaviours

Success Profiles are the recruitment framework used within the UK Civil Service. A Success Profile can draw on five elements — Behaviours, Strengths, Ability, Experience and Technical — with the mix depending on the role [1]. The Behaviours element defines nine named behaviours [2]:

1. Seeing the Big Picture
2. Changing and Improving
3. Making Effective Decisions
4. Leadership
5. Communicating and Influencing
6. Working Together
7. Developing Self and Others
8. Managing a Quality Service
9. Delivering at Pace

Behaviours are assessed in several ways across the recruitment process, including written examples at application, interviews, and job-related simulations; candidates may be asked to give examples of when they have demonstrated a particular behaviour [2].

## EU institutions: EPSO general competencies

The European Personnel Selection Office (EPSO) selects staff for the EU institutions against a Competency Framework that "defines the competencies that are considered essential to perform effectively in any given job" [3]. The framework in use since 2023 defines eight general competencies, each broken into observable anchors [4]:

1. Critical thinking, analysing & creative problem-solving
2. Decision-making & getting results
3. Information management (digital and data literacy)
4. Self-management
5. Working together
6. Learning as a skill
7. Communication
8. Intrapreneurship

For any given competition, the Notice of Competition published in the Official Journal is the only official document containing detailed information about the actual tests used [5].

## NHS (brief)

NHS recruitment in England sits within the values of the NHS Constitution: working together for patients; respect and dignity; commitment to quality of care; compassion; improving lives; and everyone counts [6]. Values-based recruitment against these is treated in the planned NHS framework documents; they are included here because they show the same families appearing as *values* rather than *competencies*.

## Private sector and graduate schemes

There is no single private-sector framework, but the competency labels used in graduate and professional recruitment are highly convergent: targetjobs lists communication, teamwork, problem-solving, leadership, time management, planning and organisation, customer-facing skills, influencing, decision making, motivation, adaptability, commercial awareness and creativity among the competencies graduate employers commonly assess [15]. The scale of graduate recruitment pushes employers towards structured methods: Institute of Student Employers survey data reported by Prospects Luminate found the average employer received 140 applications per graduate job in 2024, a 59% increase on the prior year, across a survey of 145 employers [17].

# Core Reference

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## Why employers use competency-based, structured interviews

The professional-body position is clear: CIPD guidance states that structured interviews are better at predicting job performance than unstructured interviews, and that situational and past-behavioural questions are the best kinds of interview questions; consistent questions and objective scoring criteria make responses comparable and reduce the influence of personal bias [12].

The academic evidence supports this. Sackett, Zhang, Berry and Lievens (2022) re-analysed the meta-analytic validity estimates for personnel-selection methods, correcting a systematic overcorrection for range restriction; in their revised estimates, structured interviews emerged as the top-ranked selection procedure, even though absolute validity values for most methods came down from earlier estimates [18]. Their 2023 follow-up draws out the applied implications for selection-system design, including the trade-off between validity and diversity when combining predictors [19].

ACAS guidance to employers reinforces the fairness rationale: prepare questions in advance, ask each applicant the same questions where possible to ensure applicants are treated the same way, use more than one interviewer to reduce the risk of discriminating, and base decisions on how each applicant met each point in the job description and person specification [11].

## The eight competency families and how frameworks name them

The framework entries in each row are documented [2] [4] [6] [15]; the *grouping* of them into a family is reasoned synthesis, and cells marked "—" mean the framework has no close named equivalent.

| Competency family | Civil Service Behaviours [2] | EPSO general competencies [4] | NHS values [6] | Common graduate-scheme labels [15] |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Working with others | Working Together | Working together | Working together for patients | Teamwork |
| Communication & influencing | Communicating and Influencing | Communication | — | Communication; influencing |
| Leadership & management | Leadership; Developing Self and Others | Intrapreneurship | — | Leadership; motivation; delegation |
| Problem-solving & analysis | Making Effective Decisions; Seeing the Big Picture | Critical thinking, analysing & creative problem-solving | — | Problem-solving; decision making; creativity; commercial awareness |
| Delivering results | Delivering at Pace; Managing a Quality Service | Decision-making & getting results | Commitment to quality of care | Time management; planning and organisation |
| Resilience & adaptability | Changing and Improving | Self-management; Learning as a skill | — | Adaptability |
| Integrity & values | — (values sit in the Civil Service Code, outside the Behaviours list) | — (partially visible in anchors, e.g. treating others "equally and with fairness") | Respect and dignity; compassion; everyone counts | Motivation; values fit |
| Customer & stakeholder focus | Managing a Quality Service | — | Working together for patients; improving lives | Customer-facing skills |

Documented definitions anchoring the table:

- Civil Service **Working Together**: "Form effective partnerships and relationships with people both internally and externally, from a range of diverse backgrounds, sharing information, resources and support" [2].
- Civil Service **Communicating and Influencing**: "Communicate purpose and direction with clarity, integrity and enthusiasm. Respect the needs, responses and opinions of others" [2].
- EPSO **Working together**: "Demonstrates cross-functional cooperation and fosters diversity and inclusion. Cooperates well by seeking compromise, sharing knowledge and assisting others" [4].
- EPSO **Communication**: "Conveys information and opinions clearly, generates buy-in by facilitating interaction and engaging effectively with others" [4].
- EPSO **Critical thinking, analysing & creative problem-solving**: "Thinks holistically and translates complex information into creative and innovative solutions" [4].
- EPSO **Decision-making and getting results**: "Takes decisions in a shifting environment, translates them into action and works towards tangible results" [4].
- EPSO **Self-management**: "Organises own tasks, demonstrates responsibility and a constructive attitude at all times", with anchors including responding calmly to obstacles and adapting to changing work conditions [4].
- EPSO **Intrapreneurship**: "Drives change, inspires and empowers others in implementing change and offers guidance at all levels" [4].

## How question wording signals the competency

Competency questions characteristically open with stems that invite a specific past example — targetjobs and Skills Development Scotland both present competency interviewing as asking how you have used specific skills in past situations [15] [16], and the National Careers Service presents the STAR structure against exactly such prompts (e.g. "Tell me about a time when you have shown leadership skills.") [13]. Published question sets pair recognisable wording cues with the competency assessed: for example, My World of Work maps "Describe a time you collaborated successfully with a team." to teamwork, "Name a time you had to make a challenging decision in the workplace." to decisiveness, and "Can you tell us about a time you had to work under pressure?" to resilience [16]; targetjobs maps "Can you give me an example of when you worked in a team?" to teamwork and "Tell me about a time when you took on a leadership role" to leadership [15]. The signal-reading skill this implies — keyword in the stem → family → prepared example — is developed in the coaching sections below.

# Question Bank

A starter bank spanning the eight families. Provenance per entry: *(sourced …)* questions are quoted from the named published source; *(composed)* questions were written for this knowledge base in the style of the cited frameworks and are not attributed to any employer.

## Working with others

1. Tell me about a time you have worked as part of a team? *(sourced: National Careers Service, The STAR method)* [13]
2. Describe a time you collaborated successfully with a team. *(sourced: My World of Work, Skills Development Scotland)* [16]
3. Describe a situation where you had to build a working relationship with a colleague whose approach was very different from yours. *(composed)*

## Communication & influencing

1. Have you ever had to explain a topic to somebody with limited understanding? *(sourced: targetjobs)* [15]
2. Describe a time you had to persuade a sceptical colleague or stakeholder to accept your recommendation. *(composed)*

## Leadership & management

1. Tell me about a time when you have shown leadership skills. *(sourced: National Careers Service, The STAR method)* [13]
2. Tell me about a time when you took on a leadership role. *(sourced: targetjobs)* [15]
3. Could you tell us about a time you led a complex project? How did you handle it? *(sourced: My World of Work, Skills Development Scotland)* [16]

## Problem-solving & analysis

1. Give me an example of when you faced a problem at work. How did you handle it? *(sourced: National Careers Service, The STAR method)* [13]
2. Name a time you had to make a challenging decision in the workplace. *(sourced: My World of Work, Skills Development Scotland)* [16]
3. Walk me through a time you had to reach a decision with incomplete information. What did you weigh up? *(composed)*

## Delivering results

1. Give an example of a time when you showed initiative. *(sourced: targetjobs)* [15]
2. Tell me about a time you delivered a piece of work to a tight deadline while other priorities competed for your attention. *(composed)*

## Resilience & adaptability

1. Can you tell us about a time you had to work under pressure? *(sourced: My World of Work, Skills Development Scotland)* [16]
2. Give an example of a time when you handled a major crisis. *(sourced: targetjobs)* [15]
3. Tell me about a time a project changed direction late on. How did you adapt your plans? *(composed)*

## Integrity & values

1. Tell me about a time you were asked to do something you believed was wrong. What did you do? *(composed)*
2. Describe a situation where you saw behaviour that fell short of your organisation's values. How did you respond? *(composed)*

## Customer & stakeholder focus

1. Describe a time you had to handle a customer's difficult request. *(sourced: My World of Work, Skills Development Scotland)* [16]
2. Describe a time you improved a service or product based on feedback from a customer or service user. *(composed)*

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact.**

- **Structure every example with STAR.** The National Careers Service defines the structure: the Situation you had to deal with, the Task you were given, the Action you took, and the Result — what happened and what you learned [13]. Keep Situation and Task brief; the scored substance is almost always in Action and Result.
- **Prepare one strong, recent example per family, not per question.** Because the same eight families recur across frameworks (see the mapping table above), a bank of six to eight well-rehearsed examples can be adapted to most competency interviews. Choose examples where *you* acted — "I" not "we" — since assessors score your behaviour, not your team's.
- **Match the example's altitude to the role.** A team-leading example serves the leadership family; a peer-persuasion example serves communication and influencing. Read the framework the employer names in the advert (Civil Service adverts name the Behaviours assessed [2]; EPSO notices specify the competencies tested [5]) and select examples that reach the level described.
- **Signal-read the question before answering.** Identify the keyword in the stem (team / explain / led / decision / deadline / pressure / customer), place it in a family, and retrieve the matching prepared example. If a question could fit two families (e.g. "led a project under pressure"), let the interviewer's framing decide which to foreground.
- **Worked skeleton (communication & influencing, composed question 2 above):** Situation — a stakeholder resisted a process change; Task — you needed their sign-off by month-end; Action — you sought their objections first, quantified the benefit in their terms, and offered a pilot; Result — sign-off achieved, pilot adopted. This mirrors the documented definition of Communicating and Influencing — clarity of purpose while respecting "the needs, responses and opinions of others" [2] — and the EPSO Communication anchors on tailoring the message and using convincing arguments [4].
- **End on the Result and, where natural, the learning.** The NCS formulation explicitly includes "what you learned" in the R [13]; a sentence of reflection also serves frameworks that assess learning (EPSO's Learning as a skill [4]).

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

- **What is scored.** In the Civil Service, Behaviours are assessed through written examples, tests, interviews and job simulations; candidates may be asked to give examples of when they have demonstrated a particular behaviour [2]. In EPSO competitions, competencies from the Competency Framework are assessed through the tests specified in the Notice of Competition, which is the only official document detailing the tests for a given competition [5].
- **How scoring is structured.** CIPD describes the structured method: a predefined set of questions asked in the same order to all candidates, with responses scored using consistent criteria against pre-agreed criteria for each question; CIPD also reports evidence that scoring rubrics improve fairness of outcomes [12].
- **Consistency and panel composition.** ACAS advises employers to prepare questions in advance, ask each applicant the same questions where possible so applicants are treated in the same way, and use more than one interviewer to reduce the risk of discriminating; selection decisions should be based on how each applicant met each point in the job description and person specification [11].
- **Observable anchors.** The EPSO framework shows what "evidence" means to an assessor: each competency is decomposed into observable anchors — e.g. for Working together, "Discusses differences of opinion openly with the individuals concerned … and strives for compromise in situations of conflict" [4]. Assessors listen for behaviour matching the anchors, not for opinions or hypotheticals.
- **Why the method is trusted.** The evidence ranking matters to assessors as well as candidates: structured interviews emerged as the top-ranked selection procedure in the revised meta-analytic estimates [18], and the applied literature discusses combining them with other predictors in system design [19].

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact.**

- **Answering the wrong family.** Giving a teamwork story to a leadership question wastes the scoring opportunity: assessors score against the criteria for the competency asked (see the structured-scoring practice documented by CIPD [12]). Signal-read first.
- **"We" answers.** Describing the team's achievement without isolating your own actions gives the assessor no behaviour to score against anchors of the kind EPSO publishes [4].
- **Hypotheticals instead of examples.** Competency questions ask for what you *did*; frameworks explicitly ask candidates for examples of when they demonstrated the behaviour [2]. "I would…" answers fail that test unless the interview is deliberately situational.
- **One mega-example for everything.** Reusing a single story across all questions undercuts breadth; the family taxonomy exists precisely because employers sample across distinct behaviours [2] [4].
- **Result-free stories.** Stopping at Action drops the R that the STAR structure and assessors expect [13].
- **For interviewers: improvising questions per candidate.** Varying questions between candidates undermines both comparability and legal defensibility — ACAS advises the same questions where possible and warns that questions must not discriminate [11].
- **For interviewers: labelling a question with one competency and scoring another.** If the advert names the Behaviour assessed [2], score that Behaviour; drifting criteria reintroduces the unstructured-interview weaknesses documented by CIPD [12].

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.**

1. **Build the example bank (60–90 minutes).** Draw the eight-family table and write one candidate example per row from your last two to three years. Mark rows where your example is weak — those are your preparation priorities.
2. **Signal-reading drill (15 minutes).** Take the 19 questions in the Question Bank above in random order and, before answering, name the family each belongs to. Check yourself against the sub-headings.
3. **Timed STAR practice (3 × 10 minutes).** Answer one sourced question aloud in under three minutes using STAR [13]. Record yourself; check that Action and Result took at least two-thirds of the time.
4. **Anchor self-scoring (30 minutes).** Take your Working-with-others example and score it against the published EPSO Working together anchors [4]: did you show compromise-seeking, knowledge-sharing, assisting others? Rewrite the Action section to evidence at least two anchors.
5. **Interviewer drill (pairs, 30 minutes).** Draft three questions for one family, agree scoring criteria *before* the mock interview (per CIPD structured practice [12]), interview your partner, then compare the scores you each expected. Disagreements reveal vague criteria.

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — every claim cited. Signposting only — not legal advice.**

- **UK — Equality Act 2010.** Part 5 of the Act covers work, including recruitment [7]. Section 4 lists nine protected characteristics: age, disability, gender reassignment, marriage and civil partnership, pregnancy and maternity, race, religion or belief, sex and sexual orientation [8]. ACAS guidance operationalises this for interviews: questions must not discriminate, and employers must ask anyone coming to an interview to say if they need reasonable adjustments to attend — for example wheelchair access, extra time for tests, or a sign-language interpreter [11].
- **EU — equal treatment in selection.** Council Directive 2000/78/EC establishes a general framework for equal treatment in employment and occupation, expressly covering "conditions for access to employment … including selection criteria and recruitment conditions", and prohibits direct and indirect discrimination on grounds of religion or belief, disability, age or sexual orientation, with reasonable-accommodation obligations for disabled persons [9].
- **Candidate data.** Interview notes, scores and application records about identifiable candidates are personal data; in the EU their processing falls under Regulation (EU) 2016/679 (GDPR) [10]. Interviewers should assume candidates may exercise data-subject rights over interview records.
- **Fairness by design.** The structured method itself is a fairness control: consistent questions and criterion-based scoring make responses comparable and reduce the scope for bias, per CIPD [12] and ACAS [11].

# Risks & Caveats

- **The family taxonomy is editorial.** The eight families are a synthesis for navigation and preparation; employers assess against their *own* named framework, and mapping cells marked "—" show real gaps. Always defer to the employer's published framework.
- **Frameworks change.** The EPSO framework in this document dates from 2023 [4], and EPSO has been through an organisational transformation; the Success Profiles guidance is periodically updated on GOV.UK [1]. Verify against the live pages before high-stakes use; review is due 2027-07-19.
- **Validity estimates are contested in magnitude.** Sackett et al. revised headline validity numbers downwards relative to older meta-analyses [18] [19]; older sources quoting higher coefficients are still widely circulated. The *ranking* of structured interviews is robust in the cited work; specific coefficients should be quoted from [18] only.
- **Careers-site question lists are practitioner sources.** The sourced questions from targetjobs and My World of Work [15] [16] evidence *what is published as typical*, not what any specific employer asks.
- **ISE figures are reported second-hand.** The 140-applications-per-job figure is ISE survey data reported via Prospects Luminate by an ISE author [17]; the underlying ISE report is member-gated and was not independently inspected.

# Open Questions

- **Private-sector frameworks by name.** This document evidences graduate-scheme competency labels via careers sources [15]; obtaining named, publicly documented corporate frameworks (e.g. published bank or consultancy competency dictionaries) would strengthen the mapping table. Candidate for the per-competency child documents.
- **NHS values-based recruitment mechanics.** How NHS values [6] translate into interview questions and scoring is only signposted here; the planned NHS/UK-frameworks documents should cover values-based recruitment guidance from NHS Employers.
- **Strengths vs competency mix in graduate selection.** ISE members-only data likely quantifies the strengths-based shift in graduate recruitment; a public, citable figure has not yet been found.
- **Civil Service assessor scoring scales.** Published grade-level rating scales for Behaviours (e.g. 1–7 scales in departmental guidance) exist in departmental candidate packs; verifying a stable official URL is left to the uk-frameworks deep-dive.
