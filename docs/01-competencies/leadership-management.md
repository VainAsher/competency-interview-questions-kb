# Executive Summary

This is the Tier-2 deep-dive for the leadership and management competency family, the child of **competencies-foundation**. Where the foundation document places leadership in a map of eight families, this document goes to the level that actually decides interview outcomes: the *published level descriptors*. The UK Civil Service defines a single Leadership behaviour and then writes it out separately for six grade bands, from Administrative Assistant to Director General — and the wording changes fundamentally as the grades rise, from "take personal accountability for your role" to "present a clear and focused strategy with direction for the future" [2]. A candidate who answers a Grade 7 Leadership question with an Executive Officer-level example has not given a weak answer; they have given an answer scored against the wrong descriptor.

The evidence layer spans four public frameworks and the graduate market. EPSO's position is the most easily misunderstood and is worth stating precisely: under the pre-2023 competition model, Leadership was one of eight general competencies, defined as "Manage, develop and motivate people to achieve results", and the General Rules marked it explicitly **"(for administrators only)"** — it was not assessed for assistants [5]. Competition notices confirm this in practice [6]. The 2023 Competency Framework that replaced it has **no** competency called Leadership; the leadership content now sits inside **Intrapreneurship**, defined as "Drives change, inspires and empowers others in implementing change and offers guidance at all levels" [3]. The 2024 College of Policing CVF removed the 2016 clusters and now carries six competencies at three levels, of which **We support and inspire** and **We collaborate** hold the leadership material — and the CVF states plainly that supporting and inspiring "is not restricted to those who are in formal or senior management positions" [8]. NHS England's 2024 framework for board members adds a governance-grade set of six leadership domains intended to be built into board recruitment [10].

That last point drives the document's central distinction: **leadership-without-title questions** (asked of graduates and non-managers) are not junior versions of people-management questions — they are a different evidence problem, and they are the ones most graduate applicants will actually face. Document confidence is **Medium**: the framework definitions, descriptors and scoring scales are official and would rate High on their own, but the three-tier question grouping is this knowledge base's synthesis and the private-sector strand rests on named careers sources rather than published corporate frameworks.

# Key Takeaways

- The Civil Service Leadership behaviour is one behaviour with six *different* published descriptor sets by grade band; the level, not the topic, is what most often mismatches an answer to a scoring criterion. *(documented)* [2]
- Civil Service Leadership is explicitly a values-and-inclusion behaviour, not just a delivery one: every grade band's descriptor contains diversity, inclusion or fairness language, and from HEO/SEO upward it requires managing "conflict, misconduct and non-inclusive behaviour". *(documented)* [2]
- EPSO assessed **Leadership** as a general competency "for administrators only" under the pre-2023 model — AST candidates were not scored on it — and the 2023 framework has since folded that content into **Intrapreneurship**. *(documented)* [3] [5] [6]
- The 2024 police CVF states that supporting and inspiring "is not restricted to those who are in formal or senior management positions" — the clearest official statement that leadership is assessed without a job title. *(documented)* [8]
- Published scoring is granular where it exists: COPFS scores Success Profiles behaviours on a 1–7 scale requiring 4 or more in all behaviours [11], while EPSO competitions have used a pass mark of 3/10 per competency and 40/80 overall, with roughly ten minutes of interview per competency [4] [6].
- Graduate employers ask leadership questions of candidates who have never managed anyone; targetjobs advises that you do not need to be the overall leader or a society president to evidence the competency. *(documented)* [21]
- Prepare **three** leadership examples at different altitudes — peer-influence, first-line management, cross-boundary/strategic — rather than one story retold; the frameworks sample different behaviours at different levels. *(guidance)*
- Assessors score your behaviour, not your team's outcome: targetjobs advises concentrating on personal contribution, using "I" rather than "we". *(documented)* [21]

# Purpose

This document answers, for candidates: *"What exactly is being scored when an interviewer asks me a leadership question, and how do I pitch my example at the right level?"* And for interviewers and HR: *"What do the published frameworks actually say about leadership at each level, what indicators and scales exist, and how do I design questions that separate leading-without-authority from people-management evidence?"*

It exists because leadership is the competency where the gap between the question as asked ("tell me about a time you showed leadership") and the criterion as scored (a grade-specific descriptor) is widest. It deliberately goes deeper than **competencies-foundation**, which introduces the family and maps it across frameworks; that mapping is not repeated here.

# Scope

**Covered:** the Civil Service Leadership behaviour and its six published grade-band descriptors; the Civil Service Developing Self and Others behaviour where it overlaps; the EPSO Leadership competency (legacy, administrators only) and its successor Intrapreneurship; the College of Policing CVF (2024) competencies **We support and inspire** and **We collaborate** across levels 1–3; NHS England's Leadership Competency Framework for board members (2024); graduate-scheme and private-sector leadership questioning; a 40-question bank across four groupings; answer guidance with a worked STAR skeleton; published assessor indicators and scoring scales; and the legal and fairness constraints specific to leadership questioning.

**Not covered:** the competency-family taxonomy and cross-framework mapping (see **competencies-foundation**); STAR/CARL technique in depth (see **techniques-foundation**); teamwork as a peer competency (see **teamwork-collaboration**); influencing and persuasion as a communication competency (see **communication-influencing**); decision-making under uncertainty (see **problem-solving-decision-making**); and full framework guides for Success Profiles, EPSO or the CVF. Leadership *assessment centre* exercises (group exercises, role-plays) are named only where a cited framework describes them. This document is not legal advice.

# Definitions

- **Leadership (Civil Service Behaviour)** — "Show pride and passion for public service. Create and engage others in delivering a shared vision. Value difference, diversity and inclusion, ensuring fairness and opportunity for all" [2].
- **Leadership (EPSO, legacy general competency)** — "Manage, develop and motivate people to achieve results", listed in the General rules governing open competitions and marked "(for administrators only)" [5].
- **Intrapreneurship (EPSO, 2023 framework)** — "Drives change, inspires and empowers others in implementing change and offers guidance at all levels"; the competency that now carries EPSO's leadership content [3].
- **We support and inspire (police CVF, 2024)** — one of six CVF competencies, concerned with applying organisational values "to provide inspiration and clarity to others" and creating "the right climate for people to get the job done to the best of their abilities" [8].
- **Grade band (Success Profiles)** — the published groupings against which each Civil Service behaviour is separately described: AA/AO; EO; HEO/SEO; Grade 7/Grade 6; Deputy Director; Director/Director General [2].
- **CVF level** — one of three levels of increasing behavioural complexity applied to each police competency; levels "are not assigned to specific ranks" [9].
- **Leading without authority** — this knowledge base's term for demonstrating leadership behaviours over people who do not report to you. Not an official framework term, though the CVF makes the underlying point explicitly [8].

# Framework Context

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## UK Civil Service: the Leadership behaviour by grade band

Leadership is one of the nine Civil Service Behaviours within Success Profiles [1] [2]. Its headline definition is given above. What makes it a Tier-2 topic is that GOV.UK publishes a distinct descriptor for each grade band [2]:

| Grade band | Representative published descriptor wording [2] |
|---|---|
| AA / AO | "show enthusiasm for your work and take personal accountability for your role"; "act in a fair, inclusive and respectful way when dealing with others"; "demonstrate consideration of the wider consequences of own actions" |
| EO | "show pride and passion for your work and positive, inclusive engagement with your team"; "proactively role model and promote an inclusive workplace, promptly dealing with inappropriate language and behaviours when they arise"; "give praise and credit to colleagues and stakeholders where appropriate" |
| HEO / SEO | "ensure colleagues and stakeholders have a clear understanding of objectives, activities and time-frames"; "recognise and praise the achievements of others to drive positivity within the team"; "effectively manage conflict, misconduct and non-inclusive behaviour" |
| Grade 7 / Grade 6 | "welcome and respond to views and challenges from others, despite any conflicting pressures to ignore or give in to them"; "stand by, promote or defend your own and your team's actions and decisions where needed"; "inspire and motivate teams to be fully engaged in their work and dedicated to their role" |
| Deputy Director | "remain visible and approachable to all colleagues and stakeholders"; "embed a culture of inclusion and equal opportunity for all"; "work to influence the strategy, direction and culture to increase effectiveness" |
| Director / Director General | "present a clear and focused strategy with direction for the future"; "act in ways that are highly visible, inspirational and credible at the most senior levels across and outside of the Civil Service"; "engage positively in debate and resolve ambiguities, gaining the best possible outcomes for the department" |

Three properties of this progression are documented rather than inferred. First, inclusion language is present at **every** band, from "act in a fair, inclusive and respectful way" at AA/AO to "create an inclusive environment where diversity is valued" at Director [2]. Second, the obligation to *act on* poor behaviour appears from EO ("promptly dealing with inappropriate language and behaviours when they arise - including any instances of discrimination or misconduct") and hardens at HEO/SEO ("effectively manage conflict, misconduct and non-inclusive behaviour") [2]. Third, the descriptors at AA/AO and EO contain no requirement to manage anyone — they describe accountability, role-modelling and giving credit [2]. Leadership is assessed below management grades.

Behaviours are assessed across the process through application forms, Civil Service judgement tests, personality tests, interviews, assessment centres and job-related simulations including presentations, in-tray exercises, written analyses, group exercises, role-play and oral briefings [2].

## EU institutions: EPSO's Leadership competency and its successor

Under the competition model in force before 2023, the *General rules governing open competitions* listed eight general competencies, the eighth being "**Leadership** (for administrators only): Manage, develop and motivate people to achieve results" [5]. The parenthetical is the framework's own: leadership was assessed for AD (administrator) candidates and not for AST (assistant) candidates [5]. Competition notices operationalised this — the notice for EPSO/AD/389/21, an AD 6 administrator competition, lists all eight competencies including Leadership, with a pass mark of "3/10 per competency and 40/80 in total" [6].

The 2023 Competency Framework replaced that list with eight new general competencies: critical thinking, analysing and creative problem-solving; decision-making and getting results; information management; self-management; working together; learning as a skill; communication; and intrapreneurship [3]. **None of them is called Leadership.** The leadership content is carried by **Intrapreneurship** — "Drives change, inspires and empowers others in implementing change and offers guidance at all levels" — whose published anchors include "Takes on new challenges and initiates change, takes the lead when required", "Inspires and encourages others to take action and move ahead", "Guides and empowers others in order to grow and better their performance", and "Acts as a role model and an ambassador for the department" [3]. Notably, these anchors describe leadership behaviour without presupposing line-management authority.

Competencies are assessed by the tests set out in each competition's notice; the Competency Based Interview uses structured questions and runs approximately ten minutes per competency [4]. At an assessment centre, candidates are observed "in a systematic way by at least 2 trained assessors", and final results are "based on the evaluation of each competency tested through the Assessment Centre exercises" [7].

## Policing (England and Wales): the CVF 2024

The College of Policing's Competency and Values Framework was introduced in 2016 and updated in 2024 to reflect the new Code of Ethics [9]. The 2024 edition consists of six competencies at three levels plus three core values (courage; respect and empathy; public service) [8] [9]. A structural change matters for anyone working from older material: the **clusters** used in the 2016 framework "have been removed for simplicity" [9], and the six competencies are now named *We take ownership*, *We collaborate*, *We support and inspire*, *We analyse critically*, *We are innovative and open-minded*, and *We are emotionally aware* [8]. Forces had until 1 May 2025 to transition [8].

The leadership material sits principally in **We support and inspire**, whose description states: "We work to create the right climate for people to get the job done to the best of their abilities, ensuring a culture of mutual respect and support", and, decisively for candidates without rank, "This behaviour is not restricted to those who are in formal or senior management positions. We all have a positive contribution to make by operating at our best … We are focused on helping our colleagues to improve and learn and are active in supporting them through activities such as coaching and mentoring" [8]. **We collaborate** carries the cross-boundary leadership content — partnership-building, influence and negotiation [8].

Each competency is described at three levels of increasing complexity, and these levels "are not assigned to specific ranks", though they broadly relate to level 1 (practitioners), level 2 (supervisors/managers) and level 3 (senior managers/executive) [9].

## NHS: leadership at board level

NHS England published its **Leadership Competency Framework for board members** (reference B0496i) on 28 February 2024, responding to a recommendation of the 2019 Tom Kark KC review and forming part of the Fit and Proper Person Test Framework [10]. It sets six leadership competency domains: driving high-quality and sustainable outcomes; setting strategy and delivering long-term transformation; promoting equality and inclusion, and reducing health and workforce inequalities; providing robust governance and assurance; creating a compassionate, just and positive culture; and building trusted relationships with partners and communities [10]. The framework directs that these domains "should be incorporated into all NHS board member job/role descriptions and recruitment processes" and "can be used to help evaluate applications and design questions to explore skills and behaviours in interviews, presentations and other aspects of the recruitment and assessment process" [10].

## Private sector and graduate schemes

There is no single published private-sector leadership framework, but the graduate market's treatment of leadership is well documented by named careers sources and carries real weight in this document because it is where most early-career candidates meet the competency. targetjobs identifies leadership and management as competencies employers assess in candidates applying for entry-level roles, and states that a candidate does not need to be the overall leader or a society president in order to develop them, giving the example of progressing from writing for a student newspaper to becoming a section editor [21]. Prospects lists leadership among the key competencies regularly sought by employers [19]. Skills Development Scotland's My World of Work maps the question "Could you tell us about a time you led a complex project? How did you handle it?" directly to leadership [23].

## What "leadership" means at each documented altitude

Reading the four frameworks together, the published descriptors distinguish three altitudes of leadership evidence — a grouping that is this knowledge base's organising device, but whose contents are quoted:

**Altitude 1 — leadership without authority.** Civil Service AA/AO and EO descriptors require personal accountability, fair and inclusive conduct, role-modelling an inclusive workplace, and giving praise and credit — none of which requires a reporting line [2]. Police CVF level 1 indicators include "I am conscientious in my approach, working hard to provide the best service and remove obstacles that could prevent or hinder delivery" and "I take on challenging tasks to help to improve the service continuously and support my colleagues" [8]. The CVF explicitly disclaims a management-position prerequisite for this competency [8]. EPSO's Intrapreneurship anchors likewise include taking the lead "when required" and acting as a role model [3].

**Altitude 2 — first-line management.** Civil Service HEO/SEO requires ensuring "colleagues and stakeholders have a clear understanding of objectives, activities and time-frames", recognising and praising achievements, and effectively managing "conflict, misconduct and non-inclusive behaviour" [2]. Police CVF level 2 indicators include "I support others to understand their aims and wider organisational goals so they can work more effectively", "I take time to understand and provide the support people need to work at their best", "I show support that helps people feel safe and valued in raising concerns", and "I create opportunities to support the professional development of others through knowledge sharing, coaching, mentoring and feedback" [8]. The legacy EPSO definition sits here squarely: "Manage, develop and motivate people to achieve results" [5].

**Altitude 3 — senior and strategic.** Civil Service Grade 7/6 introduces defending team decisions under pressure and seeking "shared interests beyond your own area of responsibility"; Deputy Director adds embedding a culture of inclusion and influencing "the strategy, direction and culture"; Director/DG requires presenting "a clear and focused strategy with direction for the future" and credibility "at the most senior levels across and outside of the Civil Service" [2]. Police CVF level 3 for *We support and inspire* includes "I communicate clear goals that give a compelling direction to people from a range of different backgrounds, to inspire them to work towards those goals" and "I demonstrate long-term strategic thinking, going beyond personal goals and considering how the police service operates in the broader societal and economic environment" [8]. CVF level 3 for *We collaborate* adds political astuteness and anticipating conflict from knowledge of partners' interests [8]. NHS England's board-level domains — setting strategy and long-term transformation, governance and assurance, compassionate culture, trusted partner relationships — describe the same altitude for a different sector [10].

## Where the frameworks agree

Four convergences are documented across sources rather than asserted:

1. **Inclusion is part of leadership, not adjacent to it.** The Civil Service definition itself contains "Value difference, diversity and inclusion, ensuring fairness and opportunity for all" [2]; EPSO's Working together anchors include fostering "an environment of inclusion" and striving "to treat all others equally and with fairness" [3]; the 2024 CVF update explicitly reflects a policing workforce where "diversity and inclusive behaviours are deeply embedded" [9]; NHS England makes promoting equality and inclusion one of six board domains [10].
2. **Developing others is leadership evidence.** CVF level 2 names coaching, mentoring and feedback [8]; EPSO Intrapreneurship anchors include "Guides and empowers others in order to grow and better their performance" [3]; the Civil Service lists Developing Self and Others as a separate but adjacent behaviour [2].
3. **Addressing poor behaviour is required, not optional.** Civil Service descriptors require dealing promptly with inappropriate language and behaviours from EO, and managing conflict and misconduct from HEO/SEO [2].
4. **Leadership is assessed by past behaviour.** The College of Policing advises that in recruitment and selection interviews "it is good practice to ask about examples of past behaviour, probing for evidence" and that while hypothetical questions are sometimes helpful, "most of the interview should focus on past behaviour" [9]. EPSO's CBI uses structured questions to measure competencies [4]. CIPD guidance holds that structured interviews predict job performance better than unstructured ones, with past-behavioural and situational questions the best question types [17].

## Where the frameworks differ

- **Whether leadership is assessed at all depends on the staff category.** EPSO's legacy rules restricted Leadership to administrators [5]; the Civil Service assesses Leadership at every grade band including the most junior [2]; the CVF applies its competencies at every level but decouples level from rank [9].
- **The unit of assessment differs.** Civil Service descriptors are written as expectations of the post-holder [2]; CVF indicators are written in the first person ("I take time to understand …") [8]; EPSO anchors are written in the third person as observable elements [3].
- **Only some frameworks publish scoring numbers.** See Assessor Perspective below.

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact.**

## Selecting the right evidence

- **Pitch to the published descriptor, not to the question.** The question wording is nearly identical across grades; the criterion is not. Before choosing an example, find the grade band or level the employer names — Civil Service adverts name the behaviours assessed and GOV.UK publishes the descriptor per band [2]; police assessments should tell candidates which competencies and values are being assessed [9]; EPSO's notice of competition sets out the competencies tested [4]. Then pick an example that reaches that language. A "clear understanding of objectives, activities and time-frames" answer [2] is HEO/SEO evidence; it will underwhelm at Grade 7, where the descriptors ask for defending decisions and looking beyond your own area [2].
- **Build three examples, not one.** One peer-influence story (no authority), one people-management story, one cross-boundary or strategic story. This mirrors the three documented altitudes above and protects you when an interviewer probes for a second example.
- **If you have never managed anyone, do not apologise for it — select differently.** The CVF states the supporting-and-inspiring behaviour is not restricted to formal or senior management positions [8], and targetjobs makes the same point for graduate applicants, noting that a progression such as writing for a student newspaper and later becoming a section editor still uses management and leadership skills [21]. Society roles, project coordination, training a new starter, running a shift, captaining a team and student-media editing are all legitimate sources.
- **Foreground your own actions.** targetjobs advises concentrating on personal contributions, using "I" rather than "we" and authoritative language such as "delegated" [21]. Prospects makes the complementary point that one situation can serve several competencies if you shift the emphasis — for a leadership question, emphasise decision-making and motivating others [19].
- **Include the people outcome, not just the delivery outcome.** Most leadership descriptors are about effect on people: praising achievements "to drive positivity within the team" [2], creating "the right climate for people to get the job done to the best of their abilities" [8], or empowering others "to grow and better their performance" [3]. An answer that ends only with a delivery metric leaves the actual criterion unevidenced.

## Worked STAR skeleton

Question: *"Tell me about a time you had to address someone's poor performance. What did you do, and what changed?"* (composed, first-line management group). Structured with STAR as defined by the National Careers Service — the situation you had to deal with, the task you were given, the action you took, and the result, including what you learned [18].

- **Situation (brief).** "A caseworker in my team of six had missed three consecutive weekly deadlines, and the backlog was starting to affect two other colleagues."
- **Task (brief).** "As their line manager I needed to restore delivery without losing a capable person, and to do it before the quarterly reporting date."
- **Action (the bulk).** "I checked the data first rather than relying on impressions. I then held a private one-to-one and asked open questions before offering a diagnosis — which surfaced that they had been given a new case type without training. I agreed a written improvement plan with three specific, dated checkpoints, arranged shadowing with an experienced colleague, and temporarily rebalanced two cases so the rest of the team was not silently absorbing the gap. I kept every checkpoint and documented each one."
- **Result (with the people outcome).** "They met all three checkpoints and were back to full caseload within six weeks, and the training gap I had found was fixed for the next two joiners. What I learned was to check for capability before assuming motivation."

Why this skeleton scores: the Action evidences the HEO/SEO requirement to ensure clear understanding of "objectives, activities and time-frames" and to "effectively manage conflict, misconduct and non-inclusive behaviour" [2]; the coaching and shadowing evidences CVF level 2's "create opportunities to support the professional development of others through knowledge sharing, coaching, mentoring and feedback" [8]; and the closing reflection matches EPSO's learning anchors on identifying opportunities to apply lessons learned [3]. The private one-to-one and the check-the-data-first sequence also demonstrate fairness of process — relevant given that performance conversations sit close to the adjustment duties noted below [13].

## Adapting one story across altitudes

The same underlying event usually contains evidence at more than one altitude. If asked a *leading-without-authority* question, foreground the influence you exercised over people who did not report to you and the obstacle you removed [8]. If asked a *first-line management* question, foreground objective-setting, feedback and development [2] [8]. If asked a *senior/strategic* question, foreground the decision you defended, the boundary you crossed, or the culture you changed [2] [10]. Prospects cautions against overusing a single example across a whole interview, since it can seem like you have limited experience [19].

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## Published scoring scales

Where scoring is published, it is specific. The Crown Office and Procurator Fiscal Service publishes the Success Profiles scale it applies at both sift and interview: 1 Not Demonstrated ("No positive evidence and/ or substantial negative evidence"), 2 Minimal, 3 Moderate, 4 Acceptable ("Adequate positive evidence and any negative evidence would not cause concern"), 5 Good ("Substantial positive evidence of the behaviour"), 6 Strong ("Substantial positive evidence; includes some evidence of exceeding expectations") and 7 Outstanding ("The evidence provided wholly exceeds expectation at this level") [11]. Candidates "must demonstrate the minimum essential criteria for all roles, this includes scoring 4 or more in all behaviours", though a single score of 3 can be tolerated where scores for all other behaviours are high; where a further benchmark is set, it is detailed in the application pack and the invitation to interview [11].

EPSO publishes different numbers. For the AD 6 competition EPSO/AD/389/21, the pass mark across the eight general competencies including Leadership was "3/10 per competency and 40/80 in total" [6]. The Competency Based Interview allocates approximately ten minutes per competency and uses structured questions so that evaluators can measure candidates' competencies [4]. At assessment centre, candidates are observed by at least two trained assessors and results derive from the evaluation of each competency across the exercises [7].

## What assessors are told to look for

- **Behavioural indicators are indicators, not checklists.** The College of Policing is emphatic: the example behavioural indicators "are just that – they are indicators of the sorts of behaviours that could demonstrate the competency", assessors "should retain flexibility and be open to diverse ways of demonstrating the behaviours, as long as they are in line with the definition of the competency", and "the CVF itself should not be used as a simple checklist" [9]. Assessors are directed to the competency *description*, not only its bullet list [8].
- **Exercise design is bounded.** CVF guidance advises that assessment exercises usually measure more than one competency "but not more than three or four in a large exercise", that content should give candidates opportunities to demonstrate the competencies being looked for, and that candidates "should be clearly told which competencies and values are being assessed" [9]. Assessors should be given clear guidance and training on what competency evidence they are looking for [9].
- **Past behaviour dominates.** Most of a police selection interview should focus on past behaviour, with hypotheticals used sparingly [9]. This matches CIPD's position that past-behavioural and situational questions are the strongest question types and that consistent questions with objective scoring criteria make responses comparable and reduce the influence of personal bias [17].
- **Level must be matched to the candidate's opportunity.** When assessing at a higher CVF level than a candidate's current or next role, "care should be taken to ensure that the context of the assessment is appropriate and fair given the experience and opportunities of the participant pool" [9]. This is the assessor-side statement of the leading-without-authority problem: a candidate who has never been given a team cannot evidence level 2 supervision, and a fair process does not penalise them for the absence of an opportunity.
- **Board-level leadership is designed into the job description.** NHS England directs that its six domains be incorporated into all board member job and role descriptions and recruitment processes, and used to help design questions for interviews and presentations [10].
- **Consistency and panel composition.** ACAS advises preparing questions in advance, asking each applicant the same questions where possible, using more than one interviewer to reduce the risk of discriminating, and basing decisions on how each applicant met each point in the job description and person specification [16].
- **Why the method is trusted.** In the most recent large re-analysis of selection-method validity, structured interviews emerged as the top-ranked selection procedure for predicting job performance [24].

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact.**

- **Answering at the wrong altitude.** The single most common failure. Giving an "I made sure everyone knew the deadline" answer to a Grade 7 question misses descriptors about defending decisions and seeking shared interests beyond your own area [2]. Read the band before choosing the story.
- **Assuming leadership means a job title.** Candidates without direct reports often deflect the question or hedge. Both the CVF, which states the behaviour is "not restricted to those who are in formal or senior management positions" [8], and targetjobs' graduate guidance [21] contradict this directly.
- **Dropping the inclusion dimension.** Every Civil Service Leadership descriptor contains fairness, inclusion or diversity language [2], and inclusion is a standalone NHS board domain [10]. An answer that is purely about delivery leaves a documented part of the criterion unevidenced.
- **Avoiding the difficult conversation.** From EO the descriptors require dealing promptly with inappropriate language and behaviour, and from HEO/SEO managing conflict and misconduct [2]. Stories where the underperformer or the poor behaviour is worked around rather than addressed evidence the opposite of the descriptor.
- **"We" answers.** targetjobs advises concentrating on personal contribution and using "I" [21]; assessors evaluating against anchors written as observable individual behaviour [3] have nothing to score in a collective narrative.
- **Delivery-only results.** Leadership criteria are largely about effect on people [2] [3] [8]; ending on a throughput figure alone under-evidences the competency.
- **Hypothetical answers to past-behaviour questions.** Police guidance puts most interview weight on past behaviour [9]; "I would…" answers fail unless the question is deliberately situational.
- **One story for every question.** Prospects warns that overusing one example across a whole interview can seem like limited experience [19].
- **For interviewers: treating the indicator list as a checklist.** Explicitly warned against in CVF guidance [9]; it penalises candidates who evidence the competency in unlisted ways and disadvantages those whose role context differs.
- **For interviewers: assessing a level the candidate has had no opportunity to reach.** CVF guidance flags the fairness problem directly [9]; ACAS's requirement to assess against the job description and person specification is the corrective [16].
- **For interviewers: assuming EPSO-style leadership applies to every profile.** It did not — the legacy competency was for administrators only [5], and the 2023 framework renamed the territory entirely [3]. Using stale framework labels produces criteria that no longer exist.

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.**

1. **Altitude audit (45 minutes).** Write your three leadership examples as one-line summaries. Against each, mark which Civil Service grade band its *strongest* evidence reaches, using the published descriptor wording [2]. If all three land in the same band, you have a preparation gap for roles above or below it.
2. **Descriptor rewrite (30 minutes).** Take your best example and rewrite the Action section twice: once to evidence HEO/SEO wording ("clear understanding of objectives, activities and time-frames"; "recognise and praise the achievements of others") and once to evidence Grade 7/6 wording ("stand by, promote or defend your own and your team's actions and decisions where needed") [2]. Note which sentences you had to invent — those mark experience you do not yet have, and must not be fabricated in a real interview.
3. **CVF level self-scoring (30 minutes).** Score one example against the published *We support and inspire* indicators at levels 1, 2 and 3 [8]. Remember the framework's own caution that the indicators are not a checklist [9] — you are looking for the level whose *description* your behaviour matches, not for bullet-point bingo.
4. **The no-authority drill (20 minutes).** Answer question 9 or 10 of the leading-without-authority group aloud in under three minutes without using the words "manager", "my team" or "reported to me". This forces out the influence behaviours that the CVF and EPSO Intrapreneurship anchors describe [3] [8].
5. **Probe resistance (pairs, 30 minutes).** Have a partner ask you a first-line management question and then all five follow-up probes in sequence. Most answers survive the first probe and fail on "How did you know it had worked?" — prepare your evidence of effect in advance.
6. **Timed STAR with a people outcome (3 × 10 minutes).** Answer three sourced questions from the bank using STAR [18], ending each on an effect-on-people result rather than a delivery metric. Record yourself; check that Action and Result occupy at least two-thirds of the time.
7. **Interviewer drill (pairs, 45 minutes).** Design three leadership questions for a named level, agree the scoring criteria *before* interviewing using a published scale such as the COPFS 1–7 descriptors [11], run the interview, then compare scores. Where you disagree by more than one point, the criteria were too vague — which is precisely the failure structured interviewing exists to prevent [17].

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — every claim cited. Signposting only; not legal advice.**

- **Leadership questions carry a specific opportunity-bias risk.** Asking for people-management evidence tends to reward candidates who have already been *given* teams. The College of Policing warns assessors directly to take account of "the experience and opportunities of the participant pool" when testing at a higher level than a candidate's current or next role [9]. ACAS's requirement to base decisions on how each applicant met each point in the job description and person specification is the practical control [16] — if the role does not require line management, do not score for it.
- **UK — Equality Act 2010.** Part 5 of the Act covers work, including recruitment [12]. Section 20 imposes the duty to make reasonable adjustments [13]. The CVF guidance applies this to competency assessment expressly: assessments should allow for different expression of the competencies, and "Reasonable adjustments should be provided for those who need them under the Equality Act (2010)", with accommodations also available for pregnancy, breastfeeding, injury or illness [9]. The 2024 CVF adds that reasonable adjustments "should always be made for people who have disabilities that impact on their ability to demonstrate the competencies" [8].
- **Neurodiversity and differing expression of leadership.** The CVF states that people differ in strengths and preferences and that the framework "should be used in ways which allow for differing abilities, including those which stem from disabilities or neurodiversity", with assessments allowing candidates to demonstrate strengths in different areas [9]. This matters acutely for leadership, where confident verbal narration is easily mistaken for the competency itself.
- **EU — equal treatment in selection.** Council Directive 2000/78/EC establishes a general framework for equal treatment in employment and occupation, expressly covering conditions for access to employment including selection criteria and recruitment conditions, and requires reasonable accommodation for disabled persons [14].
- **Candidate data.** Interview notes and competency scores about identifiable candidates are personal data; in the EU their processing falls under Regulation (EU) 2016/679 [15]. Leadership assessments that capture judgements about a candidate's handling of named third parties — an underperforming colleague, for example — warrant particular care in note-taking.
- **Fairness by design.** Consistent questions and criterion-based scoring are themselves the control: CIPD holds that structured interviews with consistent criteria make responses comparable and reduce the influence of personal bias [17], and ACAS advises more than one interviewer to reduce discrimination risk [16].

# Risks & Caveats

- **The three-altitude grouping is editorial.** "Leading without authority / first-line / senior-strategic" is this knowledge base's organising device. Its *contents* are quoted from published descriptors [2] [3] [8] [10], but no framework uses these three labels. Employers score against their own named levels.
- **EPSO's framework has changed and older material is still circulating.** The Leadership competency and its "(for administrators only)" restriction come from the pre-2023 General Rules [5] and are evidenced in a 2021 competition notice [6]. The 2023 framework has no Leadership competency [3]. Any preparation material listing "Leadership" as a current EPSO general competency is out of date; always check the notice of competition for the specific competition [4].
- **The CVF's 2016 clusters no longer exist.** Material referring to CVF clusters such as "We are collaborative" describes the 2016 framework; the 2024 CVF removed clusters entirely and the current competency names are *We collaborate* and *We support and inspire* [8] [9]. Forces had until 1 May 2025 to transition, and it is not mandatory for a force to use the CVF at all — some use force-specific frameworks [9]. Verify which framework and version a given force applies.
- **The 1–7 scale is central; the benchmarks are not.** Cabinet Office guidance states that "Experience, technical skills and behaviours should be scored using the 7 point scale. Strengths should be scored using the 4 point scale" [25], so the scale is Civil Service-wide rather than a COPFS variant. Three qualifications hold: that central pack dates from 2020, and no refreshed central scoring publication was found after the January 2025 behaviour-guide refresh; the seven descriptor labels are published by COPFS [11], still the only located source for them; and the "4 or more in all behaviours" rule is a benchmark set per campaign, so it should not be assumed identical across departments. EPSO pass marks are competition-specific [6].
- **The graduate and private-sector strand rests on careers sources.** targetjobs, Prospects, Oxford Careers Service and My World of Work [19] [20] [21] [22] [23] evidence *what is published as typical*, not what any named employer asks. No published private-sector leadership competency dictionary was obtained.
- **One targetjobs source is dated 2023.** The leadership and management guidance was last updated 21 June 2023 [21]; graduate-market practice moves faster than that.
- **Source recency generally.** Framework pages are periodically revised on GOV.UK [1]; review is due 2027-07-20.

# Open Questions

- **Civil Service Leadership-specific interview questions.** GOV.UK publishes the descriptors [2] but no official bank of Leadership interview questions was located; departmental candidate packs may contain them. Most sourced leadership questions in this document are therefore careers-service published rather than employer-published.
- **Whether the 2023 EPSO framework assesses Intrapreneurship differentially by staff category.** The legacy rules restricted Leadership to administrators [5]; whether an equivalent restriction applies to Intrapreneurship for AST or contract-agent profiles was not established from the sources examined [3] [4].
- **NHS leadership questioning below board level.** NHS England's 2024 framework covers board members [10]; how leadership is assessed in NHS band-level recruitment, and how it relates to values-based recruitment, is not covered here and belongs to the planned NHS framework document.
- **CVF scoring scales.** The CVF publishes levels and indicators [8] [9] but the numeric scales used in national police promotion assessments were not located in the cited documents.
- **Published corporate leadership frameworks.** Obtaining a named, publicly documented private-sector leadership competency dictionary would let the graduate and private-sector strand rest on primary rather than careers-site evidence.
