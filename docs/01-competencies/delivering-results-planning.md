# Executive Summary

This is the Tier-2 deep-dive for the **delivering results, planning and organising** competency family — the family the parent foundation document maps to Civil Service *Delivering at Pace* and *Managing a Quality Service*, EPSO *Decision-making & getting results*, and the graduate-scheme labels *time management* and *planning and organisation*. It goes to depth on the evidence for how this family is defined, levelled and scored: the official Civil Service definitions and grade-by-grade level descriptors for both behaviours [1], the EPSO 2023 Competency Framework definitions and observable anchors for *Decision-making and getting results* and *Self-management* [3], the pre-2023 EPSO competency labels *Delivering quality and results* and *Prioritising and organising* as assessed and scored in a published Notice of Competition [4], and the published scoring scales assessors use — the Success Profiles 1–7 rating scale with a benchmark of 4 [7], a Civil Service Resourcing 1–5 evidence-based evaluation scheme reproduced by the University of Oxford Careers Service [12], and EPSO's marks-out-of-10 per competency with a combined pass mark [4].

Around that evidence sits a question bank of 32 questions — prioritisation and deadlines, quality under pressure, project delivery, and follow-up probes — each carrying provenance (*sourced* from a named publisher, or *composed* for this knowledge base in the style of the cited frameworks), plus coaching on evidence selection (quantified, verifiable Results), a worked STAR skeleton for a tight-deadline question, pitfalls tied to the cited descriptors, and practice drills. The document serves candidates preparing delivery examples and interviewers designing and scoring delivery questions, with deliberate weight on private-sector and graduate-scheme usage.

Document-level confidence is **Medium**: the framework definitions, anchors and scoring facts rest on official sources and would alone merit High, but the question bank mixes composed items with careers-service sources, the family mapping is inherited synthesis from the foundation document, and the Oxford question set dates from 2016.

# Key Takeaways

- The Civil Service defines **Delivering at Pace** as "Take responsibility for delivering timely and quality results with focus and drive" and **Managing a Quality Service** as "Deliver service objectives with professional excellence, expertise and efficiency, taking account of diverse customer needs" — pace and quality are two separately scored behaviours, not one. *(documented)* [1]
- Both behaviours are levelled by grade: at AA/AO the expectation is organising your own work to time and standard; by Grade 7/6 it is delivering through resources and performance-management processes; at Director level it is setting direction and driving a performance culture. *(documented)* [1]
- EPSO's 2023 framework splits the family the same way: **Decision-making & getting results** ("translates [decisions] into action and works towards tangible results") and **Self-management** ("Organises own tasks, demonstrates responsibility"), each with published observable anchors that assessors listen for. *(documented)* [3]
- Under the pre-2023 EPSO model still visible in published notices, **Delivering quality and results** was tested in the competency-based interview and **Prioritising and organising** in the situational competency-based interview, each marked out of 10 with a combined pass mark of 40/80 across eight competencies. *(documented)* [4]
- Published scoring is explicit and mechanical: Success Profiles interviews rate each behaviour 1–7 ("Not Demonstrated" to "Outstanding Demonstration") against a typical benchmark of 4 [7]; a Civil Service Resourcing example scheme rates 1–5 by weighing positive against negative evidence [12]. *(documented)*
- The single highest-leverage preparation move for this family is a **quantified Result**: the frameworks reward "timely and quality results" [1] and "tangible results" [3], and STAR ends with "what happened as a result of your action and what you learned" [15]. *(guidance)*
- Assessors probe the *mechanism* of prioritisation — the Oxford question set pairs its planning questions with probes such as "How do you decide what to do first?" — so a prepared example needs a visible method, not just a happy ending. *(documented question set; guidance on its use)* [12]
- Pace-focused, timed assessment engages adjustment duties: ACAS tells employers they must ask interviewees about reasonable adjustments, giving "more time to do a written test" as its example, and Equality Act 2010 s.20 places the underlying duty on provisions, criteria and practices. *(documented)* [8] [10]

# Definitions

- **Delivering at Pace** — Civil Service Behaviour: "Take responsibility for delivering timely and quality results with focus and drive" [1].
- **Managing a Quality Service** — Civil Service Behaviour: "Deliver service objectives with professional excellence, expertise and efficiency, taking account of diverse customer needs" [1].
- **Decision-making and getting results** — EPSO general competency (2023 framework): "Takes decisions in a shifting environment, translates them into action and works towards tangible results" [3].
- **Self-management** — EPSO general competency (2023 framework): "Organises own tasks, demonstrates responsibility and a constructive attitude at all times" [3].
- **Delivering quality and results / Prioritising and organising** — two of the eight general competencies of the pre-2023 EPSO model, still the assessed labels in notices of competition published under that model [4].
- **Benchmark** — in Success Profiles scoring as described by the Crown Office and Procurator Fiscal Service, the minimum rating (normally 4 on the 1–7 scale) a candidate must reach on behaviours to be appointable [7].
- **Anchor** — an observable element of an EPSO competency used to recognise and assess it [3]. Defined in the parent document; repeated here because the anchors for this family carry the assessment weight.

## Civil Service: the two behaviours, levelled by grade

GOV.UK publishes both the definitions and grade-by-grade level descriptors [1]. The definitions are quoted in Definitions above. The descriptor progression below is a condensed paraphrase of the published levels [1]; the full text should be read at source before high-stakes use.

| Grade level | Delivering at Pace — expectation (paraphrase of [1]) | Managing a Quality Service — expectation (paraphrase of [1]) |
|---|---|---|
| AA/AO | Work with focus and pace to complete own tasks on time and to high standards; organise own work; keep stakeholders updated on progress | Understand customer needs; plan and manage own time to deliver quality service; identify and report problems early |
| EO | Review progress and spot barriers; identify resources needed; support colleagues; stay focused and positive despite setbacks; check performance against objectives | Work with customers to understand needs; create clear plans and set priorities; balance quality and cost effectiveness; fix common service problems |
| HEO/SEO | Keep the team focused on top priorities; monitor work against milestones; reassess workloads when demands conflict; give individuals space while retaining responsibility | Develop, implement and review systems and services; set priorities with stakeholders; identify and resolve risks; build feedback mechanisms |
| Grade 7/6 | Clarify roles and responsibilities; give honest, motivating messages about priorities; set performance-management processes; deliver timely quality outcomes through appropriate resources | Make clear, practical plans; deliver efficient, cost-effective services considering new technology and accessibility; proactively manage risks; benchmark against best practice |
| Deputy Director | Translate strategic priorities into outcome-focused objectives; deliver own objectives while holding others to account; model efficiency and focus | Use customer insight to drive delivery; break complex aims into clear, practical plans; identify resource requirements; monitor and manage risks |
| Director/DG | Set and maintain clear direction with focused priorities; swiftly adjust priorities as situations change; drive a performance culture | Ensure professional excellence across government; negotiate service levels with delivery partners; promote value for money |

The structural point the table documents: at junior grades the evidence sought is **personal organisation** (own workload, own deadlines); at middle grades it becomes **planning and monitoring through others** (milestones, conflicting demands, resourcing); at senior grades it becomes **direction-setting and performance culture** [1]. Candidates and interviewers should pitch questions and examples at the advertised grade's level.

## EPSO 2023: definitions and observable anchors

The Competency Framework "defines the competencies that are considered essential to perform effectively in any given job, by the EU institutions and bodies that EPSO serves" [2]. For this family the published anchors are [3]:

**Decision-making and getting results** — "Takes decisions in a shifting environment, translates them into action and works towards tangible results." Anchors: takes decisions even when confronted with unknown circumstances or incomplete information; comes to an informed decision, checking the advantages and disadvantages of different alternatives; adopts a practical course of action, flexibly adapting and choosing the most opportune action considering the situation; proactively suggests action and solutions on their own initiative; proposes targeted action in order to achieve tangible results for the set objectives [3].

**Self-management** — "Organises own tasks, demonstrates responsibility and a constructive attitude at all times." Anchors: organises own work effectively; takes responsibility for own results and demonstrates commitment to achieve their goals; responds calmly to obstacles and is able to distance themselves from frustration and negativity; applies strategies to help prevent stress and setbacks (e.g. asking for help or support when necessary); is adaptable concerning changing work conditions and responds to fluctuating circumstances in a positive manner [3].

In the competency-based interview, "Candidates are interviewed for about 10 minutes per competency", with questions structured so assessors can evaluate the selected general competencies; which competencies are tested depends on the competition and grade as specified in the Notice of Competition [5]. The situational competency-based interview assesses general competencies "by asking the candidate how he/she would react in a specific situation" [6].

## EPSO pre-2023 model: how the delivery competencies were tested and scored

Notice of open competition EPSO/AD/399/22 (Administrators, AD 7, audit) lists eight general competencies assessed at the assessment-centre phase: analysis and problem-solving; communication; **delivering quality and results**; learning and development; **prioritising and organising**; resilience; working with others; leadership [4]. *Delivering quality and results* was assessed in the competency-based interview (with communication, learning and development, and resilience); *prioritising and organising* was assessed in the situational competency-based interview (with analysis and problem-solving, working with others, and leadership) [4]. Each competency was marked out of 10, with no individual pass mark per competency but a combined pass mark of 40/80 across the eight [4]. Candidates preparing against older notices, or CAST-era material, will still meet these labels.

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact.**

- **Structure with STAR and land on a measured Result.** The National Careers Service defines STAR as the situation you had to deal with, the task you were given, the action you took, and "what happened as a result of your action and what you learned" [15]. For this family the Result carries unusual weight: the Civil Service definition is "timely and quality results" [1] and the EPSO anchor is "tangible results for the set objectives" [3]. A Result with a number — hours saved, deadline met early, error rate down, backlog cleared, volume handled — is far harder to score down than "it went well". If you have no number, give a verifiable outcome (delivered on the agreed date; passed audit first time; adopted as the standard process).
- **Choose examples with a real constraint.** A delivery story without a genuine deadline, volume spike, resource gap or competing demand gives the assessor nothing against the pressure-facing descriptors (e.g. EO-level "maintain focused and positive attitudes despite setbacks" [1]). The constraint is the story.
- **Show the prioritisation mechanism, not just the outcome.** The Oxford probes ask directly how you decided what to do first and what goals you set [12]. Name your method in one sentence — deadline-and-impact triage, a task list re-ranked each morning, a milestone plan, agreement with your manager on what to defer. Composed probes 4 and 7 above are the test: could you answer them about your own example?
- **Separate pace from quality, then reconnect them.** The frameworks score them separately (Delivering at Pace vs Managing a Quality Service [1]; getting results vs self-management [3]). A strong answer says explicitly what the quality bar was, what speed pressure threatened it, and what you did so that neither collapsed — mirroring the EO-level descriptor of balancing quality and cost effectiveness [1].
- **Prefer sustainable pace over heroics.** The EPSO Self-management anchors reward responding calmly to obstacles and applying "strategies to help prevent stress and setbacks (e.g. asking for help or support when necessary)" [3]. Asking for help early, escalating a slipping deadline, and renegotiating scope are creditable behaviours, not confessions.
- **Worked STAR skeleton** (for targetjobs' "Give an example of a situation when you worked on a tight deadline" [13]):
  - *Situation* — one sentence: "In my placement year, our team's monthly client report was due in five working days when the analyst who normally produced it went on sick leave."
  - *Task* — one sentence, with the constraint and the standard: "I had to produce it to the client's format, error-free, by the same deadline, alongside my own workload."
  - *Action* — the scored substance; aim for two-thirds of the airtime: triaged your own tasks and agreed with your manager what to defer (prioritisation mechanism); broke the report into daily milestones and checked progress each evening (monitoring); reused the previous month's checked figures as a validation baseline (quality control); asked the analyst's manager for one hour of handover by phone (help-seeking, per the Self-management anchors [3]).
  - *Result* — quantified and reflective: "The report went out a day early with no corrections requested — the first month that year without an errata note — and I wrote up the checklist so the team kept using it. Next time I would ask for the handover call on day one, not day two." The learning sentence serves the "what you learned" element of the R [15].
- **Pitch at the advertised level.** Use the grade table in Core Reference: an example about organising your own week evidences AA/AO-level descriptors; one about reassessing a team's workloads when demands conflict evidences HEO/SEO [1]. Read the advert's grade and choose accordingly.

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

- **Success Profiles interviews score each behaviour on a 1–7 scale.** As set out by the Crown Office and Procurator Fiscal Service, the ratings run 1 "Not Demonstrated", 2 "Minimal Demonstration", 3 "Moderate Demonstration", 4 "Acceptable Demonstration", 5 "Good Demonstration", 6 "Strong Demonstration", 7 "Outstanding Demonstration"; the standard benchmark is a score of 4 or above in all behaviours, with one behaviour permitted at 3 if the others are high, and role-specific benchmarks notified in the application pack [7].
- **An evidence-weighing scheme underlies the ratings.** The Oxford Careers Service question set reproduces an example evaluation scheme from Civil Service Resourcing best-practice recruitment guidance: 5 = "positive evidence of a high level of competence with no substantial negative evidence" down to 1 = "little or no positive evidence of competence, along with considerable negative evidence" [12]. Assessors are weighing positive against negative evidence in the same answer — a strong delivery story that also reveals poor planning yields both kinds.
- **EPSO marks per competency with a combined pass mark.** Under EPSO/AD/399/22, each of the eight general competencies — including delivering quality and results, and prioritising and organising — was marked out of 10, with no individual pass mark but a combined pass mark of 40/80 [4]. In the current model, the competency-based interview allows "about 10 minutes per competency", with questions structured for assessors to evaluate the selected competencies [5]; the situational variant asks how the candidate would react in a specific situation [6].
- **Anchors define what counts as evidence.** For this family, an EPSO assessor is listening for anchor behaviours such as "organises own work effectively", "takes responsibility for own results", and "proposes targeted action in order to achieve tangible results for the set objectives" [3] — observable actions, not self-description.
- **Structure and consistency are the professional standard.** CIPD's selection guidance describes structured interviews as a predefined set of questions asked in the same order to all candidates with responses scored against consistent, pre-agreed criteria, and reports that this outperforms unstructured interviewing [11]. ACAS advises preparing questions in advance, asking each applicant the same questions where possible, and checking that decisions rest on how each applicant met each point in the job description and person specification [10].

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact. Each pitfall names the documented criterion it violates.**

- **No measurable Result.** Ending on "and it all worked out" leaves the assessor nothing against "timely and quality results" [1], "tangible results for the set objectives" [3], or the R of STAR [15]. Expect the composed probe "What did the result look like in numbers?" — and have an answer.
- **Busyness dressed as delivery.** Listing everything you had on is not evidence of delivering; the descriptors reward completing work to time and standard and monitoring against milestones [1], not workload volume.
- **Pace bought by silently sacrificing quality.** If your fast delivery skipped checks, the answer scores against Managing a Quality Service descriptors (identifying and resolving risks, balancing quality and cost effectiveness [1]) even as it scores for pace. Say what protected quality.
- **The heroic all-nighter.** Stories whose only mechanism is personal overwork run against the Self-management anchors, which credit stress-prevention strategies and asking for help when necessary [3], and against EO-level expectations of using colleagues and resources [1].
- **No visible planning method.** If you cannot answer "How did you decide what to do first?" [12] with a mechanism, the prioritisation claim is unevidenced.
- **"We" answers.** Assessors score your behaviour against the anchors and descriptors [1] [3]; a team achievement with your actions unisolated gives them little to rate — the evidence-weighing scheme needs *your* positive evidence [12].
- **Hiding the failure question.** The Oxford set explicitly asks for a time you were *not* able to deliver [12]. Refusing the premise ("that's never happened") supplies negative evidence under an evidence-weighing scheme [12]; a real example with early escalation and a recovery plan supplies positive evidence against the anchors on responsibility and calm response to obstacles [3].
- **For interviewers: one question, two behaviours.** Asking "tell me about delivering a quality service at pace" and scoring it against both behaviours blurs two separately defined criteria [1] and undermines the consistent, pre-agreed scoring CIPD describes [11]. Ask and score them separately.
- **For interviewers: skipping the probe.** Without follow-ups of the kind Oxford publishes [12], polished candidates can pass on narrative alone; consistent probes across candidates preserve the like-for-like comparison ACAS advises [10].

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.**

1. **Results audit (45 minutes).** List your best five delivery examples from the last two–three years. For each, write the Result as a number or verifiable fact. Any example where you cannot is a research task (dig out the metric) or a discard.
2. **Timed STAR drill (3 × 8 minutes).** Answer sourced question 2 (tight deadline [13]) aloud in under three minutes. Record it; check Action plus Result took at least two-thirds of the time and that the Result contained a number.
3. **Anchor self-score (30 minutes).** Score your main example against the five EPSO Self-management anchors and five Decision-making-and-getting-results anchors [3], one point per anchor evidenced. Under 4/10: rewrite the Action section to surface organisation, responsibility-taking, and help-seeking you actually did.
4. **Benchmark calibration (pairs, 40 minutes).** With a partner, score each other's answer to question 1 (juggling priorities [12]) on the 1–7 scale using the COPFS labels [7], independently, then compare. A two-point gap means the answer's evidence is ambiguous — identify what detail would settle it.
5. **Probe survival (20 minutes).** Have a partner fire all eight follow-up probes from the Question Bank at your prepared example. Every probe you cannot answer identifies a hole in the example, not in you — fix the example.
6. **Level lift (30 minutes, for promotion candidates).** Take your current-grade example and check it against the next grade's descriptors in the Core Reference table [1]. If your example never touches monitoring others' work, conflicting demands, or resourcing, source a new example that does.

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — every claim cited. Signposting only — not legal advice.**

- **Adjustments and timed, pace-focused assessment (UK).** ACAS states that "By law you must ask anyone coming to an interview to tell you if they need reasonable adjustments so they can attend", giving as an example "giving someone more time to do a written test that is part of an interview" [10]. The underlying duty is Equality Act 2010 s.20: where a provision, criterion or practice puts a disabled person at a substantial disadvantage, reasonable steps must be taken to avoid the disadvantage, and the disabled person cannot be required to bear the cost [8]. Timed exercises and pace-weighted scoring are provisions, criteria or practices; employers assessing "delivering at pace" should apply their adjustments process to the assessment method itself.
- **EU selection.** Directive 2000/78/EC applies to "conditions for access to employment … including selection criteria and recruitment conditions" (Article 3(1)(a)) and requires reasonable accommodation for disabled persons — appropriate measures to enable access to and participation in employment unless a disproportionate burden (Article 5) [9].
- **Consistency as the fairness control.** ACAS advises asking each applicant the same questions where possible to ensure equal treatment and basing decisions on the job description and person specification [10]; CIPD's structured-interview description — same questions, same order, pre-agreed scoring criteria — is the operational form of that control [11]. Candidate-data handling in interviews is covered in the parent document and is not repeated here.

# Risks & Caveats

- **Level descriptors are paraphrased.** The grade table condenses GOV.UK's published descriptors [1]; the definitions are quoted verbatim but the level cells are paraphrase. Anyone drafting formal scoring materials should work from the source page, not this table.
- **EPSO is mid-transition.** The 2023 framework [2] [3] coexists with pre-2023 labels in older notices [4]; which competencies and marks apply to a given competition is defined only by its Notice of Competition [5]. Verify against the live notice before preparing.
- **The Oxford question set is dated May 2016** [12]. Its questions and the reproduced 1–5 scheme remain illustrative, but the Civil Service Resourcing guidance it excerpts predates the current Success Profiles materials; the 1–7 scale in [7] is the current published articulation.
- **COPFS describes its own practice.** The 1–7 scale and benchmark-4 rule are published by one prosecuting authority applying Success Profiles [7]; departments can set different benchmarks per vacancy, as the page itself notes.
- **Careers-site questions are typical, not actual.** Sourced questions from targetjobs and My World of Work [13] [14] evidence published practice, not any employer's live question bank; composed questions are this knowledge base's own and attributed to no employer.
- **Family boundaries are porous.** Deadline-pressure questions are also asked as resilience questions, and quality-of-service questions as customer-focus questions; sibling documents cover those framings. Double-preparation is efficient, not wasted.

# Open Questions

- **A stable official URL for cross-government behaviour scoring guidance.** The 1–5 evidence-weighing scheme survives via Oxford's 2016 reproduction of a Civil Service Resourcing PDF [12]; a current authoritative equivalent on GOV.UK has not been located. The uk-frameworks deep-dive should pursue it.
- **Official short definitions of the pre-2023 EPSO competencies.** EPSO/AD/399/22 confirms the labels, test allocation and marking [4], but a live official page giving each old competency's one-line definition was not found this cycle (legacy epso.europa.eu URLs 404 after migration); an Internet Archive snapshot would strengthen the record.
- **Named private-sector delivery frameworks.** Graduate-scheme evidence here rests on careers sources [12] [13] [14]; a published corporate competency dictionary naming a delivery/planning competency with level indicators would raise the private-sector layer above practitioner evidence.
- **New-model EPSO per-competency scoring.** Whether current-model competitions publish per-competency marks and pass thresholds comparable to [4] should be checked against a post-2023 Notice of Competition.
