# Key Takeaways

- The Civil Service defines Making Effective Decisions as: "Use evidence and knowledge to support accurate, expert decisions and advice. Carefully consider alternative options, implications and risks of decisions." — the reasoning, not just the outcome, is in the definition. *(documented)* [1]
- The published level descriptors escalate from using guidance and asking colleagues for input (AA/AO) through inviting challenge and making unpopular calls confidently (HEO/SEO) to bold, unbiased strategic decisions (Deputy Director) [1]. Pitch your example at the level of the role. *(documented, applied as guidance)*
- EPSO splits this family in two: *Critical thinking, analysing & creative problem-solving* ("Thinks holistically and translates complex information into creative and innovative solutions") and *Decision-making & getting results* ("Takes decisions in a shifting environment, translates them into action and works towards tangible results") — each with five published observable anchors [3]. *(documented)*
- One EPSO decision-making anchor rewards exactly what candidates often apologise for: "Takes decisions, even when confronted with unknown circumstances or incomplete information" [3]. Deciding under uncertainty is the tested behaviour, not a confession. *(documented, applied as guidance)*
- In Civil Service-style scoring, behaviours are commonly rated on a numeric scale — COPFS publishes a 1–7 scale with a benchmark requiring 4 or more in all behaviours (one score of 3 tolerated where other scores are high) [7]; Oxford's careers service reproduces a 1–5 positive/negative-evidence rating scheme drawn from Civil Service recruitment guidance [14]. *(documented)*
- Competency questions about problem-solving ("tell me about a time…") are different instruments from case-study interviews ("how many petrol stations are there in Paris?"); the latter are used mainly in consulting and law and are covered here only as a signpost [14] [15]. *(documented)*
- The single most damaging answer pattern in this family is presenting the solution without the reasoning: the cited descriptors score option-generation, evidence-weighing and risk consideration, e.g. the EO-level expectation to "explain how decisions have been reached in a clear and concise way" [1]. *(guidance, tied to [1] [3])*
- Follow-up probes are predictable — published examples include "What was most important in making that decision?" and "What would you do differently in future?" [14] — so a prepared example must survive two layers of "why?". *(documented, applied as guidance)*

# Definitions

- **Making Effective Decisions** — the Civil Service Behaviour covering evidence-based decisions and advice; defined in full in Framework Context [1].
- **Seeing the Big Picture** — the Civil Service Behaviour concerned with understanding "how your role fits with and supports organisational objectives" [1]; treated here as the strategic-context input to analysis.
- **Anchor** — in the EPSO Competency Framework, an observable element of a general competency; each competency in this family has five [3].
- **CBI / SCBI** — EPSO's Competency-Based Interview and Situational Competency-Based Interview; the CBI evaluates selected general competencies from past behaviour, the SCBI "aims to assess general competencies by asking the candidate how he/she would react in a specific situation" [5] [6].
- **Case-study interview** — an interview built around a business or analytical problem posed live (estimation, market entry, profitability), used mainly in consulting and law [14] [15]. Not a competency interview; signposted only.
- **Benchmark** — in Success Profiles scoring practice, the minimum rating a candidate must reach; COPFS publishes a pass benchmark of 4 on a 1–7 scale for behaviours [7].

# Framework Context

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## UK Civil Service: Making Effective Decisions, with Seeing the Big Picture as the analysis overlap

Within Success Profiles, the behaviour that carries this competency family is **Making Effective Decisions**: "Use evidence and knowledge to support accurate, expert decisions and advice. Carefully consider alternative options, implications and risks of decisions." [1]. The analytical, contextual side of the family overlaps with **Seeing the Big Picture**: "Understand how your role fits with and supports organisational objectives. Recognise the wider Civil Service priorities and ensure work is in the national interest." [1]. At HEO/SEO level, Seeing the Big Picture expects candidates to "understand the strategic drivers for your area of work" and "align activities to contribute to wider organisational priorities" [1] — in practice, the context-scanning that feeds a well-made decision. Behaviours are assessed through written examples at application, interviews, and job-related simulations such as written analysis exercises [1].

## EU institutions: two EPSO competencies split the family

The EPSO Competency Framework "defines the competencies that are considered essential to perform effectively in any given job" [2]. Where the Civil Service uses one decision behaviour, EPSO splits the family across two of its eight general competencies, each defined and anchored in the published framework leaflet [3]:

- **Critical thinking, analysing & creative problem-solving** — "Thinks holistically and translates complex information into creative and innovative solutions."
- **Decision-making & getting results** — "Takes decisions in a shifting environment, translates them into action and works towards tangible results."

The full anchors for both are recorded in Core Reference. Which competencies are assessed in a given competition is specified in the Notice of Competition, the only official document containing detailed information about the tests [4]. Both are typically interview-assessed: the CBI is "designed to evaluate a number of selected general competencies, depending on the competition and grade" [5], and the SCBI assesses general competencies situationally [6].

## Private sector and graduate schemes

There is no single private framework, but problem-solving and decision-making appear as standard competencies in published graduate-recruitment question sets: targetjobs lists "Give an example of a time when you solved a problem" and "Describe a time when you had to be an effective decision maker" among its common competency questions [16]; Skills Development Scotland's My World of Work maps "Name a time you had to make a challenging decision in the workplace" to decisiveness [17]; Oxford's careers service publishes competency questions under the headings *Initiative and problem-solving* and *Commercial awareness/seeing the big picture* [14]. Employers signal the same demand in their own words — EY UK tells applicants: "We look for people with global, innovative and analytics mindsets, so be prepared to show us how your mindset fits ours." [18].

## The boundary with case-study interviews

Consulting and some professional-services and law employers assess analysis through *case-study interviews* rather than (or as well as) competency questions. Oxford's careers service notes that case study interviews are used mainly in consulting and law — "the two sectors where case study interviews are most often used (although used differently)" — and treats them as a separate interview type from competency questions, which are "structured to reflect the competencies sought by an employer for a particular job" [15]. Its published examples of case prompts (airline seat mix, market entry, estimation problems) are reproduced, clearly labelled, in the question bank below [14]. This document covers the competency instrument; case interviews get depth in the formats documents.

# Core Reference

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## Making Effective Decisions: the published level descriptors

GOV.UK publishes example behaviours for each grade level. Representative descriptors for Making Effective Decisions, quoted from the published set [1]:

| Level | Published example behaviour (excerpt) [1] |
|---|---|
| AA/AO | "use guidance, analyse relevant information and ask colleagues for input to support decision making" |
| EO | "take responsibility for making effective and fair decisions, in a timely manner"; "analyse and research further information to support decisions"; "explain how decisions have been reached in a clear and concise way" |
| HEO/SEO | "invite challenge and where appropriate involve others in decision making"; "display confidence when making difficult decisions, even if they prove unpopular" |
| Grade 7/6 | "clarify your own understanding and stakeholder needs and expectations, before making decisions" |
| Deputy Director | "act decisively and make bold, unbiased decisions at a strategic level" |
| Director / Director General | "develop departmental or functional strategies and priorities while considering a wide range of political, national and international pressures" |

Three properties of the progression are visible in the published text [1]: the *inputs* broaden with seniority (guidance and colleagues → a range of credible internal and external information → political and international pressures); the *social act* of deciding changes (asking for input → inviting challenge and empowering others → bold strategic calls); and the expectation of *explaining the decision* appears explicitly from EO level ("explain how decisions have been reached in a clear and concise way") [1].

## EPSO anchors: what "analysis" and "decision-making" observably mean

The published anchors for the two competencies in this family [3]:

**Critical thinking, analysing & creative problem-solving** — anchors:

- "Differentiates the essential from less crucial elements and manages to integrate information coming from different sources."
- "Identifies possible ways forward taking a range of points of view and positions into consideration."
- "Manages complex information and concepts well."
- "Establishes connections between elements that go beyond their own area of responsibility and the here and now. Anticipates possible impact with regard to the bigger picture."
- "Generates original ideas and innovative solutions to problems by thinking out-of-the-box."

**Decision-making & getting results** — anchors:

- "Takes decisions, even when confronted with unknown circumstances or incomplete information."
- "Comes to an informed decision, checking the advantages and disadvantages of different alternatives."
- "Adopts a practical course of action: flexibly adapts and chooses the most opportune action considering the situation."
- "Proactively suggests action and solutions on their own initiative."
- "Proposes targeted action in order to achieve tangible results for the set objectives."

Note the convergence with the Civil Service definition: both frameworks document alternatives-checking ("checking the advantages and disadvantages of different alternatives" [3]; "carefully consider alternative options, implications and risks" [1]) and both reward deciding under uncertainty ([3] explicitly; [1] at senior levels, "act decisively and make bold, unbiased decisions") — a documented basis for the coaching advice below that reasoning under uncertainty, not certainty, is what scores. The fourth critical-thinking anchor ("beyond their own area of responsibility … the bigger picture" [3]) is the EPSO counterpart of the Seeing the Big Picture overlap noted above [1].

## How the family is interviewed

- **EPSO CBI**: evaluates selected general competencies; "Candidates are interviewed for about 10 minutes per competency", with questions structured to let assessors evaluate the selected competencies [5].
- **EPSO SCBI**: "aims to assess general competencies by asking the candidate how he/she would react in a specific situation" — a hypothetical-scenario variant; EPSO publishes mock assignments for it [6].
- **Civil Service**: behaviours including Making Effective Decisions are assessed via application examples, interview questions and simulations (e.g. written analysis) [1].
- **Structured competency interviewing generally**: CIPD documents the structured method — predefined questions, consistent order, criterion-based scoring — and reports that structured interviews predict performance better than unstructured ones [12]; in the most recent large re-analysis of selection-method validity, structured interviews were the top-ranked predictor of job performance [19].

## Competency questions vs case-study prompts

The published Oxford question set shows the two instruments side by side: competency questions under *Initiative and problem-solving* ask for past behaviour ("Tell me about a time when the project you were working on went wrong or faced difficulties"), while its *Case study & technical interviews* examples pose live analytical problems ("How would you choose the optimal balance of economy/business class seats on an airline?") [14]. The distinction matters operationally: a competency answer is scored against behavioural criteria from your past example [12] [14], while the case prompt tests analysis performed in the room. Oxford directs candidates to separate preparation resources for consulting and law case interviews [15].

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact. It applies the cited descriptors [1] [3]; it does not add to them.**

## Selecting decision evidence

- **Choose examples where the decision was genuinely yours.** The descriptors score *your* reasoning — "take responsibility for making effective and fair decisions" [1]. A story where a committee decided and you implemented belongs to the delivery family, not this one.
- **Prefer examples with visible alternatives.** Both frameworks document alternatives-checking ([1]: "carefully consider alternative options, implications and risks"; [3]: "checking the advantages and disadvantages of different alternatives"). An example with only one possible course of action gives you nothing to reason about aloud.
- **Uncertainty is an asset, not a flaw.** Pick a situation where information was incomplete or shifting and say so plainly — that is the literal wording of the EPSO anchor "Takes decisions, even when confronted with unknown circumstances or incomplete information" [3]. Do not manufacture false certainty in the retelling.
- **Match altitude to grade.** Use the level-descriptor table in Core Reference as a selector: an AA/AO-level answer can legitimately centre on using guidance and asking colleagues for input; an HEO/SEO answer should show challenge invited and an unpopular call handled; a Grade 7/6 answer should show stakeholder needs clarified before deciding [1].
- **Bring the big picture in one sentence.** A line of strategic context — why the decision mattered beyond your desk — evidences the Seeing the Big Picture overlap ("understand the strategic drivers for your area of work" [1]) and the EPSO anchor on connections "beyond their own area of responsibility" [3] without derailing the story.

## Worked STAR skeleton — "Give an example of a time when you had to make a difficult decision." [14]

STAR per the National Careers Service: Situation, Task, Action, Result — with the R including "what happened as a result of your action and what you learned" [13]. Weight the answer towards Action: targetjobs' advice on competency answers is that "Proportionally, you should dedicate most of your response to the Action part" [16] — and in this family, Action means *reasoning steps*, not activity.

- **Situation (2–3 sentences):** A supplier's delivery slipped, threatening a launch date; reliable delay estimates did not exist. Name the uncertainty explicitly.
- **Task (1 sentence):** As coordinator, the go/no-go recommendation was yours, needed within a week.
- **Action (the scored core — narrate the reasoning):** (i) *Frame the options* — delay launch, launch partial, switch supplier — mirroring option-generation in [1] [3]; (ii) *gather proportionate evidence* — supplier call, one comparator quote, impact estimate from the sales lead — showing "analyse and research further information to support decisions" [1] while trading speed against thoroughness deliberately; (iii) *weigh advantages, disadvantages and risks of each option aloud* — the anchor behaviour "checking the advantages and disadvantages of different alternatives" [3]; (iv) *invite challenge* — test the provisional call with a sceptical colleague ("invite challenge and where appropriate involve others in decision making" [1]); (v) *decide and explain* — recommend partial launch, and communicate the reasoning and rejected options concisely ("explain how decisions have been reached in a clear and concise way" [1]).
- **Result + learning (2–3 sentences):** Launch held with the reduced scope; the full range shipped three weeks later; you now build a supplier-risk check into every launch plan — closing with learning as the NCS structure specifies [13].

The skeleton's proportions are the message: one option, no evidence-weighing and no challenge leaves an assessor nothing to score against the cited criteria, however good the outcome was.

## Adapting across formats

- **EPSO CBI**: expect roughly ten minutes per competency [5] — prepare each example to survive sustained probing on the same story, not just a two-minute telling.
- **EPSO SCBI / situational questions**: the question is hypothetical ("how he/she would react in a specific situation" [6]), but the scored behaviours are the same anchors [3] — narrate the same reasoning steps prospectively ("I would first separate the essential facts…, then frame options…").
- **Case-style prompts**: if the question poses a live problem rather than asking for a past example (see the signposted entries above), switch instruments — structure the analysis out loud; do not answer with a STAR story [14] [15].

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

- **Numeric rating against the behaviour.** Crown Office and Procurator Fiscal Service (a Success Profiles user) publishes its scale: behaviours at sift and interview are rated 1–7, from no positive evidence to evidence that "wholly exceeds expectation"; its benchmark is that candidates must score "4 or more in all behaviours", with the stated exception that "a candidate can score a single score of 3 where scores for all other behaviours are high" [7].
- **Positive- and negative-evidence balancing.** The example evaluation scheme reproduced by Oxford's careers service from Civil Service recruitment guidance rates competency answers 1–5, where 5 "demonstrates positive evidence of a high level of competence with no substantial negative evidence" and 3 "demonstrates similar amounts of positive evidence and negative evidence" [14] — assessors are weighing evidence both ways, not tallying achievements.
- **Anchors and descriptors are the mark scheme.** EPSO's published anchors [3] and the GOV.UK level descriptors [1] are the observable behaviours assessors listen for; in EPSO's CBI the questions are structured specifically so assessors can evaluate the selected competencies, at about ten minutes per competency [5]. For any given EPSO competition, the competencies assessed are those specified in the Notice of Competition [4].
- **Probing is designed in.** Oxford's published question set includes the assessor's follow-up probes alongside the main questions — e.g. "What was most important in making that decision?" [14] — and COPFS describes boards seeking specific examples with detailed probing questions against the STAR structure [7].
- **Structured and criterion-scored by design.** CIPD documents the structured method — predefined questions, same order, responses scored against consistent, pre-agreed criteria — and reports evidence that scoring rubrics improve fairness [12]; the validity evidence ranking structured interviews as the top selection predictor [19] is why this machinery is trusted.

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact. Each pitfall names the cited criterion it violates.**

- **Describing the solution without the reasoning.** "The system was failing, so I built a tracker, and it worked" contains no options, no evidence, no risk-weighing — the substance the descriptors score ("carefully consider alternative options, implications and risks" [1]; "checking the advantages and disadvantages of different alternatives" [3]). Narrate the *deciding*, not just the *doing*.
- **Manufacturing certainty.** Polishing the story until the answer looks obvious erases the tested behaviour — deciding "when confronted with unknown circumstances or incomplete information" [3]. Say what you did not know.
- **No alternatives.** If nothing was rejected, nothing was decided. Assessors balancing positive against negative evidence [14] will read a one-option story as thin evidence.
- **Outcome as vindication.** "It worked out" is not evidence the decision process was sound; the rating schemes score demonstrated competence, not luck [7] [14]. Show *why* the call was right to make with the information then available — and note the review or learning afterwards [13].
- **The committee's decision.** "We decided" answers leave your personal judgement unscored against descriptors written in the second person singular ("take responsibility for making effective and fair decisions" [1]).
- **Wrong altitude.** A stationery-ordering decision offered at Grade 7 fails the published level expectation of clarifying stakeholder needs and expectations before deciding [1]; conversely, junior candidates need not force strategy into an AA/AO answer, where using guidance and asking colleagues for input is the documented standard [1].
- **Answering a case prompt with a STAR story (or vice versa).** The instruments are documented as distinct [14] [15]; matching the response to the instrument is part of the assessed analysis.
- **For interviewers: probing only the outcome.** The published probes target process — approach, help sought, decisive factors, hindsight [14]. Outcome-only probing rewards lucky stories and penalises honest ones, against the balanced-evidence intent of the rating schemes [12] [14].
- **For interviewers: scoring criteria drift.** If the advertised behaviour is Making Effective Decisions, score its descriptors [1], not general impressiveness — consistency of criteria is the documented core of the structured method [12].

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.**

1. **Decision inventory (45 minutes).** List every non-trivial decision you have made in two years. For each: What were the options? What evidence did you use? What was uncertain? Who challenged it? What happened? The rows with all five filled are your interview examples; rows missing "options" or "uncertain" are weak for this family.
2. **Anchor audit (30 minutes).** Score your best example against the five Decision-making & getting results anchors [3], one point per anchor genuinely evidenced. Under 3/5: rewrite the Action section — usually by restoring the rejected options and the incomplete information.
3. **Reasoning-first retell (3 × 5 minutes).** Answer question 20 (difficult decision [14]) aloud three times, capping Situation+Task at 30 seconds, per the Action-weighted proportions advised for competency answers [16]. Record and check: did you say the word "options" (or equivalents) out loud?
4. **Probe survival (pairs, 20 minutes).** Have a partner ask one main question, then only the published probes — "What was most important in making that decision?", "What would you do differently in future?" [14], "When it didn't go so well, how did you handle that and what happened?" [18]. If the second probe beats the story, the example lacks reasoning depth.
5. **Level ladder (20 minutes).** Take one example and retell it at AA/AO, HEO/SEO and Grade 7/6 altitude using the descriptor table [1] — shifting what you foreground (guidance used → challenge invited → stakeholder needs clarified). This builds the selector habit for real adverts.
6. **Interviewer drill (30 minutes).** Draft one main question plus three process probes for this family; agree a 1–7 scale and a benchmark of 4 before the mock interview, mirroring published practice [7]; interview a colleague and compare intended vs awarded scores. Disagreement localises your vague criteria.

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — every claim cited. Signposting only — not legal advice.**

- **UK.** Recruitment, including interviews, sits under Part 5 (Work) of the Equality Act 2010 [8]. ACAS guidance operationalises fairness for interviews: prepare questions in advance, ask each applicant the same questions where possible, use more than one interviewer to reduce discrimination risk, and ask anyone invited to interview whether they need reasonable adjustments [11]. For this family specifically, timed live-analysis elements (e.g. case-style or written-analysis exercises [1] [14]) are where adjustment needs such as extra time most commonly arise — the ACAS duty to invite adjustment requests applies to those exercises as to the interview itself [11].
- **EU.** Council Directive 2000/78/EC prohibits direct and indirect discrimination in "conditions for access to employment … including selection criteria and recruitment conditions", with reasonable-accommodation obligations for disabled persons [9].
- **Candidate data.** Interview scoring records — behaviour ratings on a 1–7 scale [7], assessor notes against anchors [3] — are personal data about identifiable candidates; in the EU their processing falls under Regulation (EU) 2016/679 (GDPR), and candidates may exercise data-subject rights over them [10].
- **Fairness by structure.** Consistent questions and pre-agreed criteria are themselves the fairness control: CIPD documents that structured, criterion-scored interviewing makes responses comparable and reduces bias [12].

# Risks & Caveats

- **Level-descriptor excerpts are partial.** The Core Reference table quotes representative excerpts of the GOV.UK example behaviours per level, not the complete bullet lists [1]; readers preparing for a specific grade should read the full published set for that grade.
- **The Oxford question set is dated May 2016** [14]. Its questions, probes and reproduced evaluation scheme remain the best openly published set of their kind, but the underlying Civil Service recruitment guidance it reproduces may have been revised since.
- **COPFS practice is one employer's published implementation** of Success Profiles scoring [7]; other departments' scales and benchmarks may differ in detail, even where the 1–7 pattern is common.
- **EPSO is mid-reform.** The Competency Framework cited is the 2023 model [2] [3]; test formats and their pages (CBI, SCBI [5] [6]) have changed before and may change again — the Notice of Competition governs [4].
- **Private-sector evidence is indicative, not contractual.** targetjobs, My World of Work and EY pages [16] [17] [18] evidence what is published as typical practice and employer messaging, not any employer's actual current question bank or mark scheme.
- **Composed questions are unvalidated.** The *(composed)* entries follow the cited descriptors' style but have not been piloted; interviewers should calibrate them against their own criteria before live use.

# Open Questions

- **A current, citable Civil Service interviewer mark scheme.** The 1–5 scheme survives via Oxford's 2016 reproduction [14] and the 1–7 pattern via COPFS [7]; a live GOV.UK-hosted assessor-guidance document for behaviour scoring has not been located. Finding one would raise the Assessor Perspective from reconstructed to fully current.
- **EPSO per-competency pass marks.** How CBI/SCBI competency scores aggregate, and any per-competency thresholds, are not stated on the cited pages [5] [6]; competition-specific Notices would need systematic review.
- **Named corporate analytical frameworks.** Consulting and banking employers clearly assess this family (and case interviews besides [15]), but publicly documented corporate competency dictionaries with decision-making descriptors remain unfound; employer-page evidence [18] is thin by comparison.
- **Seeing the Big Picture as a standalone.** This document covers only its analysis overlap; whether the UK-frameworks cluster should give it fuller treatment (e.g. for policy roles where it is the lead behaviour) is an orchestrator call.
