# Executive Summary

This is the deep-dive companion to `eu-frameworks-foundation`. Where the
foundation document maps the EU selection landscape, this document does one
thing thoroughly: it sets out EPSO's **general competency framework** — all
eight competencies with their official definitions and all **forty published
anchors, quoted verbatim** from EPSO's own leaflet [2] — and then traces
exactly how each has been assessed, marked and passed in published competition
notices.

Two facts dominate and are the reason this document exists. First, **the
current framework contains no "Leadership" competency**. Leadership was a
standalone general competency in the model published in the 2015 *General rules
governing open competitions*, where it appears as "Leadership (for
administrators only) — Manage, develop and motivate people to achieve
results" [4]. Comparing the two frameworks' anchors, that content is best read
as now sitting inside **Intrapreneurship**, defined as "Drives change, inspires
and empowers others in implementing change and offers guidance at all
levels" [2] — a mapping this document reasons out rather than quotes, since
EPSO publishes no concordance. Likewise the standalone legacy competency
**Resilience** [4] is best read as carried by **Self-management** [2]. A great deal of freely circulating EPSO preparation
material still teaches the legacy eight; a candidate who prepares only those
labels is preparing for a framework that EPSO no longer publishes. Section
*Framework Context* gives a dated mapping table so older material can be
translated rather than discarded.

Second, **the tests that once assessed these competencies orally no longer
exist in EPSO competitions**. The Management Board decision of 31 January 2023
provides that "The 'Assessment Centre' and its related oral tests cease to
exist" and that "Candidates' general competences will still be reviewed via
recruitment interviews, after the publication of reserve lists by EPSO" [3].
The current AD 5 competition notice bears this out: EPSO/AD/427/26 tests
reasoning, EU knowledge, digital skills and a free-text essay, and contains no
competency interview at all [7]. The framework therefore still matters
enormously — but the interview in which it is assessed is now run by the
recruiting institution, not by EPSO.

Confidence is rated **Medium**, and the reason is worth stating precisely. The
*facts* are as strong as this knowledge base gets: the competency definitions,
all forty anchors, the test-to-competency allocations and every mark and pass
threshold quoted here come from EPSO's own leaflet and from competition notices
published in the *Official Journal of the European Union*. What is **not**
documented anywhere located is the *supersession mapping itself* — EPSO has
published no concordance saying that Leadership became Intrapreneurship or that
Resilience became Self-management. That mapping is this document's reasoned
comparison of the two published frameworks, is labelled as such wherever it
appears, and caps the document-level rating at Medium. Likewise, how
institutions structure their post-reserve-list recruitment interviews is not
centrally published, and this document leaves that gap open rather than filling
it with inference.

# Key Takeaways

- The framework defines **8 general competencies** built from **40 observable
  anchors** (five per competency); EPSO defines anchors as "elements taken into
  consideration by test markers (i.e. assessors) when assessing a given
  competency". *(documented)* [2] [14]
- **There is no "Leadership" competency in the current framework** *(documented)*
  [2]. It was a standalone competency in the 2015 general rules, restricted to
  administrators — "Leadership (for administrators only)" [4]. This is the
  single most misreported point in EPSO preparation material. That its substance
  is now carried by **Intrapreneurship** is this document's reasoned mapping,
  not an EPSO-published concordance. *(guidance)*
- **"Resilience" is likewise gone as a standalone competency** *(documented)*
  [2] [4]; the reading that its calm-under-pressure and adaptability content now
  sits inside **Self-management** rests on comparing the two frameworks'
  anchors [2]. *(guidance)*
- **Marking mechanics differed between competitions and must be read from the
  notice.** EPSO/AD/389/21 set "Pass marks: 3/10 per competency and 40/80 in
  total" [6]; EPSO/AD/399/22 marked each competency 1 to 10 but stated "There
  is no pass mark per competency" with only a combined 40/80 [5].
  *(documented)*
- **The AD/AST difference is visible in EPSO's own materials.** The AD mock
  SCBI assesses six competencies including Leadership; the AST mock assesses
  the same five *minus* Leadership [10] [11] — the practical meaning of "for
  administrators only". *(documented)*
- **Under the current model there is no EPSO competency interview.**
  EPSO/AD/427/26 (AD 5, 2026) tests only reasoning, EU knowledge, digital
  skills and a free-text essay, weighted 35/25/25/15 % in the final combined
  score [7]. *(documented)*
- **Anchors survive in current testing even without interviews.** EPSO
  publishes five written-test anchors — including "Structures written
  communication with a logical flow of ideas" — against which the current
  written test is marked [15]. *(documented)*
- **CAST is a different animal:** contract-agent "competency" is a 25-question
  field-knowledge MCQ, pass 13/25 (FG II–III) or 16/25 (FG IV), no negative
  marking [13] — not a behavioural assessment at all. *(documented)*
- For candidates: build your evidence against the **anchors**, not the
  competency titles — the anchors are what markers are told to look
  for. *(guidance)*

# Purpose

This document answers, at anchor-level depth:

- **For candidates** — what precisely does each EPSO general competency mean,
  what observable behaviour does EPSO tell its markers to look for, which test
  has historically assessed which competency, what marks and pass thresholds
  applied, and how do I translate preparation material written for the older
  framework?
- **For interviewers and recruiting services** — what does the official
  framework actually say, what did EPSO's own published marking mechanics look
  like, and what can legitimately be reused when designing a post-reserve-list
  recruitment interview?

It deliberately goes *deeper*, not wider, than `eu-frameworks-foundation`. The
foundation document states that the framework has eight competencies and that
the 2023 reform abolished oral tests; this document quotes all forty anchors,
reproduces the published marking tables, and supplies the dated legacy mapping
the foundation document does not carry.

# Definitions

Prefer the shared `GLOSSARY.md`. Used here in the narrowed EPSO sense:

- **General competency** — a competency "considered essential to perform
  effectively in any given job, by the EU institutions and bodies that EPSO
  serves", as distinct from field-related competencies [1].
- **Anchor** — an "observable element" of a general competency [2]; EPSO's FAQ
  defines anchors as the "elements taken into consideration by test markers
  (i.e. assessors) when assessing a given competency" [14].
- **CBI (competency-based interview)** — an EPSO test in which "questions are
  structured to allow the assessors to evaluate the candidate's selected
  general competencies", with candidates "interviewed for about 10 minutes per
  competency" [8].
- **SCBI (situational competency-based interview)** — "An interview that aims
  to assess general competencies by asking the candidate how he/she would react
  in a specific situation" [9], built on a fictitious assignment booklet issued
  in advance [12].
- **Competency passport** — the record "giving qualitative feedback from the
  Selection Board" made available to institutions alongside the reserve
  list [6].
- **EUFTE** — Free-Text Essay on EU Matters, the written test used in the 2026
  AD 5 competition, which "assesses written communication skills only" [15]
  and is marked against published anchors [7] [15].

# Framework Context

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## The framework and its dating

EPSO publishes the framework as a leaflet titled *Competency Framework
Anchors*, which states: "The new EPSO Competency Framework defines the 8
general competencies that EU officials are expected to demonstrate throughout
their career. Each general competency is composed of various observable
elements, called anchors. This leaflet contains all 8 general competencies and
their respective anchors" [2].

A dating caution, because sources differ on the label and this document will
not paper over it. EPSO's 2023 information note on the new competition model
records under "Competencies" that "The general competency framework (April
2022) remains valid" [3], while the EN leaflet carrying the anchors is
published at EPSO's `2023-04` asset path and the framework's overview page
offers the leaflet in 24 languages [1] [2]. The most defensible reading — and
the one used throughout this document — is that the framework was **adopted in
April 2022** and its **anchors leaflet published in April 2023**. Referring to
it simply as *the current framework* avoids the ambiguity entirely; nothing
substantive turns on the label.

The framework explicitly splits where competencies are assessed: "Some of those
competencies are assessed by EPSO during the selection phase, others are
assessed by the EU institutions and bodies themselves during the recruitment
phase" [1] [14].

## The legacy framework, and what happened to it

The eight general competencies used from 2010 until the reform are set out
verbatim in the *General rules governing open competitions* of 27 February
2015, under the heading "What is the general profile sought?" [4]:

| Legacy competency (2015 general rules) | Official definition [4] |
|---|---|
| Analysis and Problem Solving | "Identify the critical facts in complex issues and develop creative and practical solutions" |
| Communicating | "Communicate clearly and precisely both orally and in writing" |
| Delivering Quality and Results | "Take personal responsibility and initiative for delivering work to a high standard of quality within set procedures" |
| Learning and Development | "Develop and improve personal skills and knowledge of the organisation and its environment" |
| Prioritising and Organising | "Prioritise the most important tasks, work flexibly and organise own workload efficiently" |
| Resilience | "Remain effective under a heavy workload, handle organisational frustrations positively and adapt to a changing work environment" |
| Working with Others | "Work cooperatively with others in teams and across organisational boundaries and respect differences between people" |
| **Leadership (for administrators only)** | "Manage, develop and motivate people to achieve results" |

Note the qualifier in the source: the competency is titled "Leadership (for
administrators only)" — it was never applied to assistant-grade candidates [4].
That restriction is observable in EPSO's own mock materials: the AD-profile
SCBI mock states it "is designed to assess the following general competencies:
Analysis & Problem Solving, Leadership, Learning & Development, Prioritising &
Organising, Resilience and Working with Others", while the AST-profile mock
lists the same set **without Leadership** — "Analysis & Problem Solving,
Learning & Development, Prioritising & Organising, Resilience and Working with
Others" [10] [11].

## The 2015-model → current-model mapping

**This table is a reasoned mapping, not an EPSO-published crosswalk.** EPSO has
not, so far as could be verified on 2026-07-20, published an official
concordance between the two frameworks. The mapping below is derived by
comparing the legacy definitions [4] against the current definitions and
anchors [2]; each row cites the anchor wording that carries the legacy content.
Treat it as a translation aid for older preparation material, not as an
official equivalence.

| Legacy competency (to 2015 rules) [4] | Current competency [2] | Anchor carrying the legacy content [2] |
|---|---|---|
| Analysis and Problem Solving | Critical thinking, analysing & creative problem-solving | "Differentiates the essential from less crucial elements…"; "Generates original ideas and innovative solutions to problems by thinking out-of-the-box." |
| Delivering Quality and Results | Decision-making & getting results | "Proposes targeted action in order to achieve tangible results for the set objectives." |
| Communicating | Communication | "Conveys information and opinions clearly and concisely (verbally as well as in writing)." |
| Learning and Development | Learning as a skill | "Identifies their own learning needs and develops their own skills through continual learning." |
| Prioritising and Organising | Self-management | "Organises own work effectively." |
| **Resilience** *(no longer a standalone competency)* | Self-management | "Responds calmly to obstacles and is able to distance themselves from frustration and negativity"; "Is adaptable concerning changing work conditions…" |
| Working with Others | Working together | "Encourages cooperation between teams…"; "Embraces diversity and fosters an environment of inclusion…" |
| **Leadership (for administrators only)** *(no longer a standalone competency)* | Intrapreneurship | "Inspires and encourages others to take action and move ahead"; "Guides and empowers others in order to grow and better their performance." |
| *(no legacy equivalent)* | Information management (digital and data literacy) | "Evaluates the credibility and reliability of sources, data, information and content in digital environments." |

Two structural changes follow from this table. **Information management
(digital and data literacy) is genuinely new** — it has no counterpart in the
2015 list [4], and it is the competency most likely to be missing from
older preparation material. And **two legacy competencies were absorbed rather
than retained**: Resilience and Leadership. Neither name appears in the current
framework [2].

# Core Reference

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.** The definitions and all forty anchors

## The eight general competencies and their anchors

**1. Critical thinking, analysing & creative problem-solving** — "Thinks
holistically and translates complex information into creative and innovative
solutions." Anchors [2]:

- "Differentiates the essential from less crucial elements and manages to
  integrate information coming from different sources."
- "Identifies possible ways forward taking a range of points of view and
  positions into consideration."
- "Manages complex information and concepts well."
- "Establishes connections between elements that go beyond their own area of
  responsibility and the here and now. Anticipates possible impact with regard
  to the bigger picture."
- "Generates original ideas and innovative solutions to problems by thinking
  out-of-the-box."

**2. Decision-making and getting results** — "Takes decisions in a shifting
environment, translates them into action and works towards tangible results."
Anchors [2]:

- "Takes decisions, even when confronted with unknown circumstances or
  incomplete information."
- "Comes to an informed decision, checking the advantages and disadvantages of
  different alternatives."
- "Adopts a practical course of action: flexibly adapts and chooses the most
  opportune action considering the situation."
- "Proactively suggests action and solutions on their own initiative."
- "Proposes targeted action in order to achieve tangible results for the set
  objectives."

**3. Information management (digital and data literacy)** — "Translates
knowledge and skills through digital tools and technologies, optimises ways of
working by utilising the digital environment." Anchors [2]:

- "Uses relevant sources and different search strategies to fulfil the
  information needs arising from complex tasks."
- "Evaluates the credibility and reliability of sources, data, information and
  content in digital environments."
- "Selects and applies appropriate digital tools and technologies to work with
  others, interact and share information."
- "Recognizes the value of digital competencies and shows willingness to invest
  time proactively in keeping up to date with digital evolution."
- "Indicates and explains technological needs clearly to colleagues, service
  providers and stakeholders."

**4. Self-management** — "Organises own tasks, demonstrates responsibility and
a constructive attitude at all times." Anchors [2]:

- "Organises own work effectively."
- "Takes responsibility for own results and demonstrates commitment to achieve
  their goals."
- "Responds calmly to obstacles and is able to distance themselves from
  frustration and negativity."
- "Applies strategies to help prevent stress and setbacks (e.g. asking for help
  or support when necessary)."
- "Is adaptable concerning changing work conditions and responds to fluctuating
  circumstances in a positive manner."

**5. Working together** — "Demonstrates cross-functional cooperation and
fosters diversity and inclusion. Cooperates well by seeking compromise, sharing
knowledge and assisting others." Anchors [2]:

- "Encourages cooperation between teams and identifies themselves with the
  common goals and interests."
- "Discusses differences of opinion openly with the individuals concerned,
  understands other points of view, and strives for compromise in situations of
  conflict."
- "Embraces diversity and fosters an environment of inclusion, strives to treat
  all others equally and with fairness."
- "Offers assistance and help to others."
- "Proactively shares knowledge for the benefit and learning of others on an
  ongoing basis."

**6. Learning as a skill** — "Actively manages own learning, growth and
self-improvement." Anchors [2]:

- "Identifies their own learning needs and develops their own skills through
  continual learning."
- "Purposefully manages personal growth: strives to understand own strengths
  and weaknesses."
- "Responds positively to feedback and to constructive criticism."
- "Seizes every opportunity to learn, even from failure."
- "Identifies opportunities to apply lessons learned and improve their
  performance."

**7. Communication** — "Conveys information and opinions clearly, generates
buy-in by facilitating interaction and engaging effectively with others."
Anchors [2]:

- "Conveys information and opinions clearly and concisely (verbally as well as
  in writing)."
- "Tailors the message to respond to the needs of the person or persons with
  whom they are communicating."
- "Uses convincing arguments and solid reasoning to put the message across."
- "Takes into account the point of view of others."
- "Communicates in ways that generate buy-in and create a win-win situation for
  the parties involved."

**8. Intrapreneurship** — "Drives change, inspires and empowers others in
implementing change and offers guidance at all levels." Anchors [2]:

- "Demonstrates initiative, removes barriers and thinks ahead."
- "Takes on new challenges and initiates change, takes the lead when required."
- "Inspires and encourages others to take action and move ahead."
- "Guides and empowers others in order to grow and better their performance."
- "Acts as a role model and an ambassador for the department."

## How each competency was assessed: the published test allocations

The assessment-centre model allocated named competencies to named tests in the
competition notice itself. Two published allocations, both under the legacy
competency set, show how this worked and how much it varied.

**EPSO/AD/389/21** (Administrators AD 6, sustainable agriculture and rural
development) stated that "Eight general competencies, as well as the
field-related competencies required for the competition, will be tested at the
assessment centre through five tests (general competency-based interview, case
study, situational competency-based interview, field-related interview and
written test in the field)", with each competency assessed by **two** tests [6]:

| Competency [6] | Tests |
|---|---|
| Analysis and problem-solving | Situational competency-based interview; Case study |
| Communication | Case study; General competency-based interview |
| Delivering quality and results | Case study; General competency-based interview |
| Learning and development | Situational competency-based interview; General competency-based interview |
| Prioritising and organising | Situational competency-based interview; Case study |
| Resilience | Situational competency-based interview; General competency-based interview |
| Working with others | Situational competency-based interview; General competency-based interview |
| Leadership | Situational competency-based interview; General competency-based interview |

**EPSO/AD/399/22** (Administrators AD 7, audit) used a leaner assessment
centre — "reasoning tests, a field-related interview, and tests to assess
general competencies (a competency-based interview and a situational
competency-based interview)" — with each competency assessed by exactly **one**
test [5]:

| Competency [5] | Test | Marking |
|---|---|---|
| Analysis and problem-solving | Situational competency-based interview | 1 to 10 |
| Communication | Competency-based interview | 1 to 10 |
| Delivering quality and results | Competency-based interview | 1 to 10 |
| Learning and development | Competency-based interview | 1 to 10 |
| Prioritising and organising | Situational competency-based interview | 1 to 10 |
| Resilience | Competency-based interview | 1 to 10 |
| Working with others | Situational competency-based interview | 1 to 10 |
| Leadership | Situational competency-based interview | 1 to 10 |

## Marking mechanics as published

The mechanics differed materially between the two notices, which is why the
notice — not general guidance — governs:

- **EPSO/AD/399/22:** "Each competency will be marked out of 10. There is no
  pass mark per competency. However, candidates must reach a pass mark of at
  least 40/80 in total for all competencies combined." The mark "will count
  towards the final overall mark" [5]. Reasoning tests carried a "Combined pass
  mark: 30/50" and "The scores obtained in these tests will not count towards
  the final overall mark"; the field-related interview was marked 0 to 200 with
  a pass mark of 120/200 [5].
- **EPSO/AD/389/21:** "Pass marks: 3/10 per competency and 40/80 in total" —
  a per-competency floor *in addition to* the combined threshold, so a single
  very weak competency could end the competition regardless of the total [6].
  Field-related competencies were marked separately: field-related interview
  25/50 and written test in the field 25/50 [6].

Both notices set the assessment-centre invitation ceiling at three times the
number of successful candidates sought [5] [6]. EPSO/AD/389/21 also records
that "The reserve list and the competency passports, giving qualitative
feedback from the Selection Board, of successful candidates will be made
available to the EU institutions for recruitment procedures and future career
development" [6].

The 2015 general rules describe the assessment-centre exercise types that
carried these competencies, including the "case study: a written test based on
a relevant scenario"; the "oral presentation: an individual test of analysis
and presentation, in which you are asked to come up with a proposal concerning
a fictitious work-related problem"; and "competency-based interview(s): an
individual exercise designed to obtain, in a structured way, relevant
information about your general skills… based on past experience" [4].

## The interview instruments themselves

EPSO describes the **CBI** as "designed to evaluate a number of selected
general competencies, depending on the competition and grade the candidate has
applied for, as specified in the Notice of Competition", with "Candidates…
interviewed for about 10 minutes per competency" and questions "structured to
allow the assessors to evaluate the candidate's selected general
competencies" [8]. The **SCBI** is "An interview that aims to assess general
competencies by asking the candidate how he/she would react in a specific
situation" [9] — the past-behaviour/hypothetical distinction between the two.

The SCBI ran on an assignment booklet issued in advance. The published
assignment for EPSO/AD/365-370/19 tells candidates: "This assignment describes
the working situation to which the Situational Competency Based Interview will
refer in some parts. The problems have not been fully elaborated, the
information is partial, additional research is allowed, and candidates will be
allowed to ask questions during the interview." Candidates "may rearrange the
documents in any order you wish, add remarks or make notes as necessary, add
other documents, and keep it available during the SCBI", and "No previous
knowledge is required" [12]. The booklet carries a controlled-instrument
notice: "This exercise may only be administered and interpreted by persons
trained and authorised by EPSO and only under the conditions stipulated by
EPSO" [12]. The AD mock allows 40 minutes and the AST mock 35 minutes [10] [11].

## What the current model tests

The Management Board decision of 31 January 2023 provides that "The 'Assessment
Centre' and its related oral tests cease to exist. Therefore, oral tests will
no longer be part of the selection procedures", while "The general competency
framework (April 2022) remains valid" and "Candidates' general competences will
still be reviewed via recruitment interviews, after the publication of reserve
lists by EPSO" [3].

The 2026 graduate competition **EPSO/AD/427/26** (AD 5, OJ C/2026/711 of
5 February 2026; 1 490 successful candidates sought) demonstrates the result.
Its phases are "application… testing: reasoning tests, an EU knowledge test, a
digital skills test, and a free-text essay on EU matters ('EUFTE')… test
scoring and eligibility check… establishment of the reserve list" [7]. There is
no competency interview, and no general competency is separately marked.
Published thresholds and weightings [7]:

| Test | Questions / duration | Pass score | Weighting (final combined score) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Verbal reasoning | 20 questions, 35 min | 10/20 | 35 % |
| Numerical + abstract reasoning | 10 + 10 questions, 20 + 10 min | Combined 10/20 | not included |
| EU knowledge test | 30 questions, 40 min | 15/30 | 25 % |
| Digital skills test | 40 questions, 30 min | 20/40 | 25 % |
| EUFTE | 40 min | 5/10 | 15 % |

Competency **anchors nevertheless survive in current testing**. The notice
states of the EUFTE that "The EUFTE is neither a language test nor a test of
factual knowledge. The assessment will be based on the specific anchors
published on the EPSO website" [7], and EPSO publishes those five written-test
anchors [15]:

- "Structures written communication with a logical flow of ideas."
- "Writes concisely, avoiding unnecessary words and sentences."
- "Presents subject matter in a clear and understandable way."
- "Adapts and tailors writing to suit the intended audience and purpose."
- "Uses the information provided effectively to address the assignment."

EPSO's testing page confirms the narrowed current scope, describing the
reasoning tests and directing candidates to the Competency Framework "For more
information on the competencies assessed", while noting that its sample tests
"are not training materials" and that "EPSO does not offer preparatory courses
or endorse any external publications or training services" [16].

## CAST: a different meaning of "competency"

For contract agents the word "competency" denotes something else entirely. The
CAST competency-based test is "A multiple-choice question (MCQ) test to
evaluate the knowledge in the field of a specific profile" — 25 questions in
50 minutes (12 questions in 30 minutes for translators and proofreaders), taken
in the candidate's second language, where "Each question yields one point and
wrong answers are not penalised". Pass marks are 13/25 for function groups II
and III, 16/25 for function group IV, 6/12 for proofreaders and 7/12 for
translators [13]. No behavioural competency is assessed by this test.

# Question Bank

Provenance rule for this section: **EPSO publishes almost no verbatim general
competency interview questions.** Its SCBI assignment booklets and mock
exercises publish the *scenario*, not the questions, and are marked "may only
be administered and interpreted by persons trained and authorised by
EPSO" [12]. Accordingly the great majority of entries below are
***(composed)*** — written for this knowledge base against the published
anchors [2]. They are **not** real, leaked, or reconstructed EPSO questions,
and must never be presented as such. The ***(sourced: …)*** entries come from a
named practitioner preparation site [21] and are questions that site publishes,
not questions EPSO publishes.

Each composed question names the anchor it targets so you can self-score
against the *Core Reference* wording.

## Critical thinking, analysing & creative problem-solving

1. Give an example of when you had to analyse complex information to provide a creative and effective solution. *(sourced: Prepari.eu, "93 EU Job Interview Questions to Prepare For")* [21]
2. Describe a situation where you had to sift through large amounts of information to identify what was most important. *(sourced: Prepari.eu)* [21]
3. Describe a situation where you identified links or consequences beyond your immediate area of responsibility. *(sourced: Prepari.eu)* [21]
4. Tell us about a problem where the obvious solution was not available to you. What alternative did you generate, and how did you test it? *(composed — targets "Generates original ideas and innovative solutions to problems by thinking out-of-the-box")*
5. Describe a time you had to reconcile information from several sources that did not agree. How did you decide what was essential? *(composed — targets "Differentiates the essential from less crucial elements…")*

## Decision-making & getting results

1. Tell me about a time when you had to act quickly without all the facts. *(sourced: Prepari.eu)* [21]
2. Tell me about a time when a decision you took affected others unexpectedly. *(sourced: Prepari.eu)* [21]
3. Walk us through a decision where you weighed the advantages and disadvantages of two workable options. What tipped it? *(composed — targets "Comes to an informed decision, checking the advantages and disadvantages of different alternatives")*
4. Describe an occasion when you proposed a course of action before anyone asked you to. What happened? *(composed — targets "Proactively suggests action and solutions on their own initiative")*
5. Tell us about a target you were set and the specific actions you took to deliver a tangible result against it. *(composed — targets "Proposes targeted action in order to achieve tangible results for the set objectives")*

## Information management (digital and data literacy)

1. Describe a time you had to judge whether a data source could be relied upon before using it. What did you check? *(composed — targets "Evaluates the credibility and reliability of sources, data, information and content in digital environments")*
2. Tell us about a complex task where your first search strategy failed. How did you change your approach? *(composed — targets "Uses relevant sources and different search strategies…")*
3. Describe a digital tool you introduced or chose for a piece of collaborative work, and why you selected it over the alternatives. *(composed — targets "Selects and applies appropriate digital tools and technologies to work with others…")*
4. Tell us how you have kept your digital skills current over the past two years, and what prompted you to invest that time. *(composed — targets "Recognizes the value of digital competencies and shows willingness to invest time proactively…")*
5. Describe explaining a technical requirement to a non-technical colleague or supplier. How did you make it land? *(composed — targets "Indicates and explains technological needs clearly to colleagues, service providers and stakeholders")*

## Self-management

1. Tell us about a period when your workload rose sharply. How did you organise your work? *(composed — targets "Organises own work effectively")*
2. Describe a result that fell short of what you intended. What did you take responsibility for? *(composed — targets "Takes responsibility for own results…")*
3. Tell us about an obstacle that was genuinely frustrating. How did you keep it from affecting your work or your colleagues? *(composed — targets "Responds calmly to obstacles and is able to distance themselves from frustration and negativity")*
4. What do you actively do to prevent pressure building up — and can you give an example of asking for help before a problem grew? *(composed — targets the Self-management anchor on applying strategies to help prevent stress and setbacks, e.g. asking for help or support when necessary)*
5. Describe a change in your working conditions that you did not choose. How did you respond? *(composed — targets "Is adaptable concerning changing work conditions…")*

## Working together

1. Describe when collaboration was essential to achieve your goal. *(sourced: Prepari.eu)* [21]
2. When did you have to compromise on conflicting views to reach a solution? *(sourced: Prepari.eu)* [21]
3. Describe working with diverse colleagues. How did you ensure inclusion? *(sourced: Prepari.eu)* [21]
4. Tell us about a disagreement you raised directly with the person concerned rather than around them. What was the outcome? *(composed — targets "Discusses differences of opinion openly with the individuals concerned…")*
5. Describe knowledge or a method you shared with colleagues without being asked. What difference did it make to them? *(composed — targets "Proactively shares knowledge for the benefit and learning of others on an ongoing basis")*

## Learning as a skill

1. How do you deal with constructive criticism? What did you learn? *(sourced: Prepari.eu)* [21]
2. Describe realizing a personal skill gap. What did you do? *(sourced: Prepari.eu)* [21]
3. Describe learning a valuable lesson from an error. *(sourced: Prepari.eu)* [21]
4. Tell us what you consider your main professional weakness, and what you are concretely doing about it. *(composed — targets "Purposefully manages personal growth: strives to understand own strengths and weaknesses")*
5. Give an example of a lesson from one piece of work that you deliberately applied to a later one. *(composed — targets "Identifies opportunities to apply lessons learned and improve their performance")*

## Communication

1. Describe convincing others who were initially reluctant. *(sourced: Prepari.eu)* [21]
2. Describe a disagreement with your manager. How did you handle it? *(sourced: Prepari.eu)* [21]
3. Tell us about a time you had to explain the same decision to two audiences with very different needs. How did each version differ? *(composed — targets "Tailors the message to respond to the needs of the person or persons with whom they are communicating")*
4. Describe an argument you had to make in writing under length or time pressure. How did you keep it clear and concise? *(composed — targets the Communication anchor on conveying information and opinions clearly and concisely, verbally as well as in writing)*
5. Tell us about a negotiation or discussion where you found an outcome both sides could accept. *(composed — targets "Communicates in ways that generate buy-in and create a win-win situation for the parties involved")*

## Intrapreneurship

1. Tell us about a change you initiated rather than were asked to make. How did you get it moving? *(composed — targets "Takes on new challenges and initiates change, takes the lead when required")*
2. Describe a barrier in a process or a way of working that you removed. *(composed — targets "Demonstrates initiative, removes barriers and thinks ahead")*
3. Tell us about someone whose performance improved because of guidance you gave. What did you actually do? *(composed — targets "Guides and empowers others in order to grow and better their performance")*
4. Describe an occasion when you had to bring colleagues along with a change they were not enthusiastic about. *(composed — targets "Inspires and encourages others to take action and move ahead")*
5. Give an example of representing your team or department to an outside audience. *(composed — targets "Acts as a role model and an ambassador for the department")*

## Situational (SCBI-style) prompts

These are hypothetical-form prompts in the style of EPSO's published SCBI
description — "asking the candidate how he/she would react in a specific
situation" [9]. They are written for this knowledge base and are not EPSO
material.

1. You take over a colleague's file two weeks before a deadline and find the underlying data is incomplete. How would you proceed, and what would you tell the project lead? *(composed)*
2. Two units you depend on give you contradictory instructions on the same task, and both are senior to you. What would you do? *(composed)*
3. A working group you chair has stalled because two members disagree publicly and neither will move. How would you handle the next meeting? *(composed)*
4. You are asked to deliver a briefing in three days on a topic outside your expertise, using only the documentation provided. How would you approach it? *(composed)*
5. Midway through implementing a change you introduced, a colleague raises a serious objection you had not considered. What would you do? *(composed)*

## Assessor follow-up probes

For interviewers: probes that push an answer from assertion towards
anchor-level evidence. Use after the candidate's initial answer.

1. What exactly was your own contribution, as distinct from the team's? *(composed)*
2. What alternatives did you consider and reject, and why? *(composed)*
3. Who disagreed with you, and what did they say? *(composed)*
4. What was the measurable outcome, and how do you know? *(composed)*
5. What would you do differently if the same situation arose next month? *(composed)*
6. What did that experience change about how you work now? *(composed)*

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — this is guidance for this knowledge base, not documented
EPSO instruction.**

- **Prepare against anchors, not titles.** The competency title is the label;
  the anchors are what markers are told to consider [2] [14]. "Working
  together" scored well is not "I am a team player" — it is compromise-seeking
  in a live disagreement, active inclusion, and unprompted knowledge sharing,
  because those are three of the five published anchors [2]. Take each
  competency's five anchors as a five-box checklist and confirm your story
  fills at least three.
- **Cover the new competency deliberately.** Information management (digital
  and data literacy) has no legacy equivalent [4], so most candidates arrive
  with no rehearsed story for it. Build one: a task where you chose sources,
  tested their reliability, picked a tool, and explained a technical need to
  someone non-technical.
- **Do not let Intrapreneurship become a leadership story.** Its anchors are
  about initiating change, removing barriers, empowering others and acting as
  an ambassador [2] — none of which require line management. A candidate with
  no direct reports can score well; a candidate who only describes managing a
  team may miss the change-and-initiative anchors entirely.
- **Worked answer skeleton — Self-management, targeting the obstacle and
  adaptability anchors [2]:**
  - *Situation (2 sentences).* Name the setting, the workload or disruption,
    and what was at stake. Resist scene-setting beyond this.
  - *Task (1 sentence).* State your specific responsibility. Use "I".
  - *Action (the bulk).* Sequence what you did, and make the anchors visible:
    how you re-organised the work ("Organises own work effectively"); the
    concrete step you took when it went wrong and how you stayed constructive
    ("Responds calmly to obstacles…"); whether and when you asked for support
    ("Applies strategies to help prevent stress and setbacks…"); how you
    adjusted as conditions shifted ("Is adaptable concerning changing work
    conditions…").
  - *Result (2 sentences).* Give the outcome with a number or a verifiable
    fact, and one sentence on what you took from it — which also feeds Learning
    as a skill.
- **Match the answer form to the interview form.** A CBI question asks about
  past experience [4]; answer in the past tense with a real example. An SCBI
  question asks "how he/she would react" [9]; answer in the conditional,
  reasoning from the scenario documents rather than from your CV. Candidates
  routinely answer one in the register of the other.
- **Budget your time against the published rate.** EPSO's own CBI ran at "about
  10 minutes per competency" [8] — that is a question plus several probes, not
  a single monologue. Prepare a two-minute core answer and expect to be pushed;
  the probes in the Question Bank above are the shape of that pushing.
- **Translate, don't discard, older material.** If your preparation book teaches
  the legacy eight, use the mapping table in *Framework Context* to re-file your
  Resilience stories under Self-management and your Leadership stories under
  Intrapreneurship, then check them against the current anchors [2].

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

- **Anchor-based scoring is EPSO's stated method.** EPSO defines anchors as the
  "elements taken into consideration by test markers (i.e. assessors) when
  assessing a given competency" [14], and each competency is composed of these
  "observable elements" [2]. Scoring is therefore evidence-matching against
  published behavioural statements rather than global impression.
- **Marks were per competency, and the arithmetic was published.** Under
  EPSO/AD/399/22 each general competency was marked 1 to 10, with "no pass mark
  per competency" and a combined requirement of "at least 40/80 in total for
  all competencies combined"; those marks counted towards the final overall
  mark, whereas reasoning-test scores did not [5]. Under EPSO/AD/389/21 the
  rule was stricter — "Pass marks: 3/10 per competency and 40/80 in total" [6].
  Assessors designing an equivalent scheme should note that the two notices
  encode different tolerance for uneven profiles.
- **Multiple instruments per competency were used to triangulate.**
  EPSO/AD/389/21 assessed all eight general competencies across five tests,
  with every competency picked up by two different instruments — for example
  Working with others by both the SCBI and the general CBI [6]. EPSO/AD/399/22
  used one instrument per competency [5].
- **Instruments were controlled.** SCBI materials state the exercise "may only
  be administered and interpreted by persons trained and authorised by EPSO and
  only under the conditions stipulated by EPSO" [10] [12]. Candidates were told
  "No previous knowledge is required" and could annotate and retain the booklet
  during the interview [12] — the exercise deliberately tests reasoning from
  supplied material, not domain knowledge.
- **Grade shaped the competency set.** Leadership applied to administrators
  only [4], and EPSO's mock exercises reflect it: six competencies including
  Leadership for AD, five without it for AST [10] [11]. AD candidates also had
  the longer exercise — 40 minutes against the AST's 35 [10] [11].
- **Qualitative feedback travelled with the reserve list.** EPSO/AD/389/21
  provided that competency passports "giving qualitative feedback from the
  Selection Board" would be made available to the institutions "for recruitment
  procedures and future career development" [6] — so recruiting services
  inherited a competency record, not only a name.
- **Anchor-based marking continues in the current written test.** EPSO
  publishes the five anchors against which the current written test is scored,
  beginning "Structures written communication with a logical flow of ideas" and
  "Writes concisely, avoiding unnecessary words and sentences" [15]; the 2026
  AD 5 notice confirms "The assessment will be based on the specific anchors
  published on the EPSO website" and that the test is "neither a language test
  nor a test of factual knowledge" [7].
- **Where competency assessment now sits.** Since the 2023 decision, oral tests
  are no longer part of EPSO selection, and "Candidates' general competences
  will still be reviewed via recruitment interviews, after the publication of
  reserve lists by EPSO"; EPSO offers institutions "training, guidance, tools
  and evaluation of general competences", but "The choice of whom to recruit
  will remain entirely with the institutions" [3].

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — analysis, not documented fact.**

- **Preparing "Leadership" as a current EPSO competency.** It is not one [2].
  The label survives in the 2015 general rules and in pre-2023 competition
  notices [4] [5] [6], and preparation material repeats it long after the fact.
  The behaviour is still assessable — but under Intrapreneurship, whose anchors
  emphasise initiating change and empowering others rather than managing
  people [2]. Interviewers reusing an old EPSO question set import the same
  error.
- **Preparing "Resilience" as a current competency.** Same problem, less
  noticed [4] [2]. Resilience stories are still valuable; file them under
  Self-management and make the "responds calmly to obstacles" and
  "applies strategies to help prevent stress" anchors explicit [2].
- **Having no Information management story.** The competency is new relative to
  the legacy set [4] [2], so it is the most common blank in an otherwise
  well-prepared candidate.
- **Assuming the marking scheme.** A candidate who assumes a 3/10 floor per
  competency prepares differently from one who assumes only a combined 40/80 —
  and the two published notices differ on exactly this [5] [6]. The notice
  governs; nothing else does.
- **Preparing for an EPSO oral test that no longer exists.** EPSO/AD/427/26
  contains no interview [7], yet much of the market still sells assessment-centre
  interview preparation. The corresponding interviewer error is assuming EPSO
  has already interview-tested general competencies before the reserve list — it
  has not since 2023 [3].
- **Treating composed practice questions as real EPSO questions.** Every
  composed entry in the Question Bank above was written for this knowledge base.
  EPSO's real instruments are controlled documents [12]; any list circulating as
  "actual EPSO interview questions" is third-party reconstruction.
- **Answering an SCBI prompt with a CV story.** The SCBI asks how you *would*
  react in the given scenario [9]; substituting a past example ignores the
  supplied material the exercise is built to test.
- **Buying preparation on the assumption EPSO endorses it.** EPSO states plainly
  that its sample tests "are not training materials" and that it "does not offer
  preparatory courses or endorse any external publications or training
  services" [16].

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.**

1. **The 40-anchor audit (90 minutes).** Open the anchors leaflet [2]. Make a
   grid of 8 competencies × 5 anchors. Against each anchor write the single
   strongest example you have, in six words. Empty cells are your preparation
   plan; a column with fewer than three filled cells is a competency you cannot
   currently evidence.
2. **Legacy translation drill (45 minutes).** Take any preparation material you
   already own that uses the legacy labels [4]. Re-file every story using the
   mapping table in *Framework Context*, then re-check each against the current
   anchors [2]. Note specifically what your Leadership stories are missing when
   judged as Intrapreneurship.
3. **Ten-minute competency drill (repeatable).** Set a timer for ten minutes —
   EPSO's published CBI rate [8]. Answer one Question Bank entry, then have a
   partner ask all six follow-up probes from the *Assessor follow-up probes*
   set. Score yourself out of 10 against the anchors and, to mirror
   EPSO/AD/389/21, check whether you would clear a 3/10 floor [6].
4. **SCBI dry run (half a day).** Download the AD or AST mock exercise [10] [11]
   and give yourself the published preparation conditions — annotate freely,
   keep the booklet, no outside research needed [12]. Then answer the five
   situational prompts from the Question Bank in the conditional tense, reasoning
   only from the documents.
5. **Written-anchor rehearsal (40 minutes).** Write a short essay on a current EU
   topic in 40 minutes — the EUFTE duration [7] — then score it strictly against
   the five published written-test anchors [15], one point each. Anything below
   3/5 on "Writes concisely, avoiding unnecessary words and sentences" usually
   means cutting a third of the text.
6. **Interviewer calibration drill.** Using the anchors [2] and the published
   1-to-10 marking convention [5], have two colleagues independently score the
   same recorded answer, then compare. Discuss only after both have scored — and
   agree in advance which of the two published threshold models you are applying
   [5] [6].

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.** Signposting only; not legal advice.

- **Legal basis.** EPSO competitions operate under the Staff Regulations, whose
  Title III Chapter 1 governs recruitment and whose Annex III lays down the
  competition procedure [17]. The 2026 AD 5 notice cites the Staff Regulations
  directly for its language requirements [7].
- **The notice is the binding instrument.** Both the 2015 general rules ("These
  general rules are an integral part of the competition notice, and together
  with the notice they constitute the binding framework of the competition
  procedure" [4]) and the 2026 notice ("This notice of competition and its
  annexes, including Annex I 'General rules', form the legally binding framework
  for this competition" [7]) make the published notice — not EPSO's website or
  any guide — the governing document. Candidates contesting a competency mark
  are contesting it against the notice.
- **Equal opportunities and adjustments.** EPSO states that it "endeavours to
  apply equal opportunities, treatment and access to all candidates" and invites
  candidates with a disability or medical condition affecting their ability to
  sit tests to request adjustments in the application form [6]; the 2026 notice
  provides that "EPSO may grant reasonable accommodations where deemed
  necessary" on the basis of the candidate's request and supporting
  documents [7]. Anchor-based scoring supports this: assessing named observable
  behaviours is more defensible against a challenge than global impression.
- **Inclusive drafting.** The 2026 notice records that "EPSO endeavours to use
  gender-neutral and inclusive language" [7]. The framework itself carries an
  equality anchor — "Embraces diversity and fosters an environment of
  inclusion, strives to treat all others equally and with fairness" [2] — so
  inclusion is both an assessment criterion and an obligation on the assessor.
- **Equal treatment in the wider EU labour market.** Council Directive
  2000/78/EC establishes a general framework for equal treatment in employment
  and occupation and applies to "conditions for access to employment… including
  selection criteria and recruitment conditions" [18] — which reaches
  competency-interview design and scoring by EU employers generally.
- **Candidate data.** Personal data processed by the EU institutions, bodies,
  offices and agencies — including competency passports and selection records —
  falls under Regulation (EU) 2018/1725 [19]. Note that EPSO/AD/389/21 made
  competency passports available to institutions for "recruitment procedures and
  future career development" [6], so a candidate's competency assessment
  persists beyond the competition that produced it.
- **No guarantee of recruitment.** "Inclusion on a reserve list does not confer
  any right to or guarantee of recruitment" [6].

# Risks & Caveats

- **The mapping table is inference, not an EPSO crosswalk.** No official
  concordance between the 2015 and current frameworks could be located on
  2026-07-20. The table in *Framework Context* is derived by comparing published
  definitions and anchors [2] [4] and is labelled as such. It is the weakest
  link in an otherwise primary-sourced document, and is the main reason a reader
  should not treat it as authoritative for an appeal or a formal scoring design.
- **Framework dating is genuinely ambiguous in the sources.** EPSO's own note
  says "April 2022" [3]; the anchors leaflet is published at a `2023-04` path
  and the leaflet calls the framework "new" [2]. This document uses "current
  framework" and flags the discrepancy rather than resolving it.
- **The marking mechanics quoted are from pre-2023 notices.** EPSO/AD/389/21 and
  EPSO/AD/399/22 [5] [6] describe an assessment centre that no longer exists [3].
  They are cited here as the best published evidence of how EPSO marked these
  competencies — genuinely useful to interviewers designing schemes and to
  candidates facing institution-run interviews — but they are **not** the
  current EPSO procedure. Where a competition is live, EPSO/AD/427/26 [7] is the
  better model of current practice.
- **Institution recruitment interviews are not centrally documented.** The one
  stage where these competencies are now actually interviewed is the stage with
  the least published guidance [3]. This document declines to describe how those
  interviews are structured or scored because no primary source was found that
  does so.
- **Question Bank provenance is deliberately thin on "sourced" entries.** EPSO
  does not publish competency interview questions; the sourced entries come from
  a commercial preparation site [21] and are that site's questions. They are
  included as practitioner evidence of what candidates are being asked to
  rehearse, never as evidence about EPSO's actual instruments.
- **Link durability.** EPSO migrated from `epso.europa.eu` to
  `eu-careers.europa.eu` and legacy URLs 404; note that EPSO/AD/399/22 [5] and
  the 2015 general rules [4] still reference `epso.europa.eu` addresses in their
  own text. All URLs cited here were verified live on 2026-07-20. EUR-Lex serves
  a JavaScript challenge to automated fetchers, so its pages were verified in a
  browser.

# Open Questions

- Will EPSO publish an official mapping between the 2015-model and current
  competency frameworks? That would replace the inferred table in this document
  and is the single highest-value correction available.
- Will EPSO publish the "tools and evaluation of general competences" it offers
  recruiting institutions [3]? If those become public, the assessor-perspective
  section could be rewritten around current rather than legacy marking.
- Do institutions apply per-competency thresholds at recruitment interviews, and
  do they use the competency passport [6] as an input? No public source was
  found either way.
- Will any oral or competency-based element return to EPSO competitions? The
  2026 AD 5 notice [7] suggests not in the near term, but the model is only
  three years old.
- Are the five written-test anchors [15] formally derived from the Communication
  competency's anchors [2]? The overlap is strong but no source states the
  relationship.
