# Executive Summary

This document is the deep dive promised by `eu-frameworks-foundation` on the
**assessment-centre model of the EU institutions**: the case study, the oral
presentation, the group exercise, the structured/competency-based interview,
the situational competency-based interview (SCBI), situational judgement
tests, the e-tray, and the language rules that govern all of them. It records,
exercise by exercise, the **format, duration, competencies assessed and
marking** that EPSO itself has published, and it separates the current model
from the superseded one with dates.

The headline fact is a discontinuity. On **31 January 2023** EPSO's
interinstitutional Management Board adopted a new competition model whose
published note states flatly that "The 'Assessment Centre' and its related
oral tests cease to exist. Therefore, oral tests will no longer be part of the
selection procedures" [1]. The most recent generalist notice of competition —
**EPSO/AD/427/26**, published in the *Official Journal* on 5 February 2026 —
bears this out: its four test blocks are reasoning, EU knowledge, digital
skills and a free-text essay on EU matters, all multiple-choice except the
essay, all sat remotely under proctoring, with no interview, no oral
presentation, no group exercise and no assessment-centre day [2]. The legacy
model is documented here in full anyway, because it is what almost all
circulating preparation material describes, because published EPSO exercise
material for it is still online, and because — as this document shows — the
exercises themselves have not disappeared from EU-institution hiring; they
have **moved**, to institutional recruitment interviews after the reserve list
and to EU agencies' own selection procedures, where assessment centres are
still used for managerial posts [3] [4].

Confidence is **Medium**. The factual spine — the 2023 reform, the current
test battery with its durations and pass marks, the legacy exercise
inventory with its durations and competency matrix, and the pre-2023 marking
scheme — rests entirely on primary EPSO and *Official Journal* documents. The
inferential part, flagged throughout, is the **current status of EPSO's legacy
exercise pages**, which remain reachable on eu-careers.europa.eu, still
describe the case study, oral presentation, CBI and SCBI, and still use the
pre-2023 competency names — but are no longer linked from any current
staff-category test page. That is read here as stale content, not as evidence
of a surviving assessment centre; a reader should treat the notice of
competition as decisive.

# Key Takeaways

- **The EPSO assessment centre was abolished in 2023.** The Information Note
  of 3 May 2023 records the Management Board decision of 31 January 2023 and
  states that the assessment centre and its oral tests "cease to exist"
  *(documented)* [1].
- **The current AD5 competition contains no oral or interactive exercise at
  all**: verbal reasoning (20 questions / 35 min), numerical (10 / 20 min),
  abstract (10 / 10 min), EU knowledge (30 / 40 min), digital skills
  (40 / 30 min) and the EUFTE free-text essay (40 min) — every one remotely
  proctored *(documented)* [2].
- **The written test survived the reform and is the descendant of the case
  study.** EPSO now publishes three written formats — WT, FRWT and EUFTE —
  and, unusually, publishes the **five marking anchors** used to score them
  *(documented)* [5].
- **The legacy assessment centre had a published exercise-by-competency
  matrix**: case study 1h30, structured interview 40 min, oral presentation
  50 min, group exercise 60 min, each general competency observed in at least
  two different exercises, each exercise run by at least two trained assessors
  *(documented)* [6].
- **The group exercise was dropped before the 2023 reform, not by it.** It is
  listed in the 2015 general rules [7] and in the assessment centre
  brochure [6]; it is absent from EPSO/AD/399/22 (2022), where the general
  competencies were assessed by a CBI and an SCBI instead [8]. Practitioner
  reporting describes the SCBI as its replacement *(documented, with a
  practitioner-sourced causal claim)* [9].
- **Pre-2023 marking was competency-by-competency, not exercise-by-exercise**:
  eight general competencies each marked 1–10, *no* pass mark per competency,
  but a combined pass mark of 40/80, plus a field-related interview marked
  0–200 with a 120/200 pass mark *(documented)* [8].
- **Language rules loosened sharply.** EPSO/AD/399/22 (2022) demanded C2 in
  language 1 and C1 in **English or French** as language 2 [8];
  EPSO/AD/427/26 (2026) demands C1 in language 1 and B2 in **any** of the
  remaining 23 official languages *(documented)* [2] [8].
- **Assessment-centre exercises still exist in EU-institution hiring — outside
  EPSO competitions.** EMA runs full-day assessment centres via an external
  provider for managerial posts, "including, for example, cognitive exercises,
  case studies or role play"; Eurojust names assessment centres for managerial
  selections *(documented)* [3] [4].
- **For candidates:** prepare the written test against EPSO's five published
  anchors, and prepare interview-style evidence for the institution's
  recruitment interview — not for an EPSO oral test that no longer runs
  *(guidance)*.

# Purpose

This document answers three questions that the foundation document could only
signpost:

- **What exercises do the EU institutions actually use, and what does each one
  look like?** Format, length, competencies assessed and marking, per
  exercise, all cited.
- **What changed in 2023, and what is left?** A dated separation of the
  current model from the superseded one, so that a candidate reading a 2019
  preparation book, a 2022 notice of competition and the 2026 notice can tell
  which is which.
- **How do you prepare for each exercise, and how is it marked?** A coaching
  layer for candidates and an assessor-practice layer for the interviewers and
  HR staff who design equivalent exercises.

It is written for **both audiences**: candidates preparing for an EU selection
procedure or an institution/agency recruitment interview, and
interviewers/assessors designing exercises against a competency framework.

# Scope

Covered:

- The current EPSO competition test battery (2026), exercise by exercise, with

- The superseded EPSO assessment centre (to 2023): case study, structured /

# Definitions

Prefer the shared `GLOSSARY.md`. Used here in a narrowed EU sense:

- **Assessment centre (EPSO sense)** — the final testing phase of a pre-2023
  open competition, in which candidates were "assessed in a standardised way:
  individually and in a group; by several assessors; during different types of
  exercises; against clearly defined competencies" [6].
- **Case study** — "a written test based on a relevant scenario, in which you
  are faced with various problems that you are asked to solve or to which you
  must react, relying solely on the material provided" [7].
- **Oral presentation** — "an individual test of analysis and presentation, in
  which you are asked to come up with a proposal concerning a fictitious
  work-related problem" and present it to a small group [7].
- **Group exercise** — an exercise in which, "after absorbing a certain amount
  of information on your own, you are put in a group with several other
  participants to discuss your conclusions and reach a collective
  decision" [7].
- **CBI (competency-based interview)** — an individual interview "designed to
  evaluate a number of selected general competencies", at "about 10 minutes
  per competency" [10].
- **SCBI (situational competency-based interview)** — "an interview that aims
  to assess general competencies by asking the candidate how he/she would
  react in a specific situation" [11], built on an assignment booklet issued
  in advance.
- **E-tray** — "a computer-based test in which you are asked to answer a
  number of questions using the documentation provided in an e-mail
  inbox" [7].
- **Situational judgement test (SJT)** — a preselection test "to assess your
  typical behaviour in a working context" [7].
- **EUFTE** — Free-Text Essay on EU Matters; a written test assessing written
  communication only [5].
- **Anchor** — "the elements considered by test markers—i.e., assessors—when
  evaluating a given competency" [5].
- **Competency passport** — the qualitative per-competency feedback document
  issued to successful candidates and made available to recruiting
  services [8].

# Framework Context

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## What each model assesses

Both the old and the new model assess *general competencies* defined centrally
and *specific/field competencies* defined per competition. The difference is
where and how.

Under the **pre-2023 model**, the general competencies were the eight set out
in the 2015 general rules — analysing and problem solving; communicating;
delivering quality and results; learning and development; prioritising and
organising; resilience; working with others; and "Leadership (for
administrators only)", defined as "Manage, develop and motivate people to
achieve results" [7]. These were tested behaviourally across assessment-centre
exercises, each competency observed in at least two exercises [6].

Under the **current model**, the reference standard is the **EPSO Competency
Framework of April 2022**, which the 2023 note confirms "remains valid" [1].
Its eight competencies are published with observable anchors in EPSO's
leaflet [12] and on its framework page [13], and — as recorded in
`eu-frameworks-foundation` and in the source registry — it contains **no
"Leadership" competency**; that content sits under *Intrapreneurship*. Any
material presenting "Leadership" as a current EPSO general competency is
citing the superseded 2015-era list [7].

The framework page is explicit that assessment is split across two phases:
some competencies are assessed by EPSO during selection, others by the
institutions during recruitment [13]. That split is what the 2023 reform
sharpened: EPSO now tests reasoning, knowledge, digital literacy and drafting;
the institutions test the behavioural competencies at the recruitment
interview [1].

# Core Reference

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## 1. The 2023 reform, dated

On **31 January 2023** EPSO's governing body, the interinstitutional
Management Board, decided to adopt a new competition model intended to
"significantly improve the speed of competitions"; the decision was published
in an Information Note by EPSO's Outreach & External Relations unit dated
**3 May 2023** [1]. The note lists the main novelties, of which the first is
that "The 'Assessment Centre' and its related oral tests cease to exist.
Therefore, oral tests will no longer be part of the selection procedures" [1].

The other published novelties are that reasoning tests remain but require only
a pass mark; that generalist competitions gain an **EU knowledge test** (a
field MCQ in specialist competitions); that all competitions include a written
test, "usually a case study", to assess drafting skills; that all competitions
run online via remotely proctored tests; that a digital skills test may be
introduced; and that competitions should shorten from an average of 13 months
to six [1]. The note states the changes "will be effective for all open
competitions as of now" [1].

The note also records where behavioural assessment went: "Candidates' general
competences will still be reviewed via recruitment interviews, after the
publication of reserve lists by EPSO", with EPSO offering the institutions
"training, guidance, tools and evaluation of general competences", while "The
choice of whom to recruit will remain entirely with the institutions" [1].

## 2. The current competition, exercise by exercise (EPSO/AD/427/26, 2026)

The most recent generalist notice — *Notice of open competition –
EPSO/AD/427/26 – Administrators (AD 5)*, OJ C/2026/711, published
**5 February 2026**, seeking **1 490** successful candidates — organises the
competition in four phases: application; testing; test scoring and eligibility
check; establishment of the reserve list [2]. It is being run at unprecedented
scale: press reporting puts participation at 50 000–60 000 candidates against
22 644 in the previous (2019) AD5 round, for 1 490 reserve-list places —
roughly a 3 % success rate [31]. The testing phase comprises
"reasoning tests, an EU knowledge test, a digital skills test, and a free-text
essay on EU matters ('EUFTE')" [2]. No interview, presentation, group exercise
or assessment-centre day appears anywhere in the notice [2].

All tests "will be held and proctored (invigilated) remotely" [2].

| Test | Language | Questions | Duration | Scoring | Pass score |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Verbal reasoning | Language 1 | 20 | 35 min | 0–20 | 10/20 |
| Numerical reasoning | Language 1 | 10 | 20 min | 0–10 | combined 10/20 with abstract |
| Abstract reasoning | Language 1 | 10 | 10 min | 0–10 | (as above) |
| EU knowledge | Language 2 | 30 | 40 min | 0–30 | 15/30 |
| Digital skills | Language 2 | 40 | 30 min | 0–40 | 20/40 |
| EUFTE | Language 2 | — | 40 min | 0–10 | 5/10 |

*(All figures from the notice's Tables 2–5 [2].)*

Two further documented features matter for anyone modelling the process [2]:

- The **EU knowledge test** covers "the EU, its institutions, procedures, and
  the main policies", and "the references to the sources used to develop this
  test will be made available on the EPSO website after the notice of
  competition is published" — a deliberate transparency measure also flagged
  in the 2023 note [1].
- The **digital skills test** is aligned to the European Digital Competence
  Framework (DigComp) and draws on five areas: information and data literacy;
  communication and collaboration; digital content creation; safety; problem
  solving.
- The **EUFTE** is "neither a language test nor a test of factual knowledge";
  candidates respond to assignments based on documentation published ahead of
  the test date and supplied again during it, and "the assessment will be
  based on the specific anchors published on the EPSO website" [2].

## 3. The written test — what replaced the case study

EPSO's current staff-category test pages describe one written test with three
possible formats, chosen "depending on the needs expressed by the recruiting
services for each competition" [5] [16] [17]:

- **Written Test (WT)** — related to the field(s) of the competition; assesses
  written communication skills only.
- **Field-Related Written Test (FRWT)** — assesses both written communication
  skills and field knowledge.
- **Free-Text Essay on EU Matters (EUFTE)** — assesses written communication
  skills only.

Unusually for a selection body, EPSO publishes the **marking anchors**. The
assessment is based on these five [5]:

1. Structures written communication with a logical flow of ideas.
2. Writes concisely, avoiding unnecessary words and sentences.
3. Presents subject matter in a clear and understandable way.
4. Adapts and tailors writing to suit the intended audience and purpose.
5. Uses the information provided effectively to address the assignment.

EPSO's legacy case-study page — still reachable, and describing the **AST**
case study — is consistent on marking philosophy: the case study "assesses the
written communication competency", markers "base their assessment on the
specific anchors of this competency", and "EPSO markers are advised that
grammar or spelling mistakes in themselves should not be taken into
consideration in the assessment" [18].

For secretaries (AST/SC), EPSO currently publishes a distinct written format:
the **Field-Related Short Text Questionnaire (FRSTQ)** — "short text questions
that require concise, experience-based answers", 10 questions in 60
minutes [19]. This is the closest thing in the current battery to a written
competency question set.

## 4. The legacy assessment centre — exercises, durations, competency matrix

The **General rules governing open competitions** (2015/C 70 A/01) set out the
authoritative inventory. Assessment-centre tests, "validated by the selection
board", "may include the following" [7]:

- case study; oral presentation; competency-based interview(s); group
  exercise; e-tray; practical language tests; other exercises relating to
  specific skills; and verbal, numerical, abstract reasoning and situational
  judgement tests where not already sat at preselection.

EPSO's own **Assessment Centre Brochure** goes further, publishing per-exercise
durations and the exercise-by-competency matrix for an administrator
competition [6]:

| General competency (pre-2023) | Case study | Structured interview | Group exercise | Oral presentation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Analysing & problem solving | ✓ | | | ✓ |
| Communicating | ✓ | | | ✓ |
| Delivering quality & results | ✓ | | | ✓ |
| Learning & development | | ✓ | ✓ | |
| Prioritising & organising | ✓ | | ✓ | |
| Resilience | | ✓ | | ✓ |
| Working with others | | ✓ | ✓ | |
| Leadership | | ✓ | ✓ | |

Published indicative durations [6]:

| Exercise | Duration | Format notes |
|---|---|---|
| Case study | 1h30 (plus 20 min language-1 test) | Written; EU-related scenario; solve or react "solely on the basis of the material available" |
| Structured interview | 40 min | Individual; "at least two assessors"; premised on "past behaviour is generally the best predictor of future performance"; focuses on past experience |
| Oral presentation | 50 min | Individual; develop a proposal on a fictitious job-related problem and present to an audience of at least two assessors; no electronic aids; flip-chart permitted |
| Group exercise | 60 min | Process information individually, then discuss with other participants and reach a collective decision; assessors observe but do not participate |

The brochure also documents the assessment-centre operating model: the centre
was "in general … organised in Brussels and will last 1 to 1.5 days"; run in
three languages (English, French, German), in the candidate's second language;
candidates were grouped five or six at a time; the day ran from about 08:30 to
about 17:15; each general competency was "observed and measured in at least
two different exercises" whereas specific field competencies were measured
"only in the context of the case study"; and "during the simulations, you will
be observed/evaluated in a systematic way by at least two trained
assessors" [6]. EPSO's assessment-centre FAQ, as captured in September 2022,
repeats the two-assessor rule and adds that "your final results are based on
the evaluation of each competency tested through the Assessment Centre
exercises", that the case study "may be organised in test centres in the
Member States, separately from the other Assessment Centre tests", and that
"the other Assessment Centre tests will normally be held remotely" [20].

## 5. The interviews: CBI and SCBI

EPSO's still-published test pages describe both interview types.

The **CBI** "is designed to evaluate a number of selected general
competencies, depending on the competition and grade the candidate has applied
for, as specified in the Notice of Competition"; "candidates are interviewed
for about 10 minutes per competency"; and "the questions are structured to
allow the assessors to evaluate the candidate's selected general
competencies" [10]. That 10-minutes-per-competency rate is the single most
useful published planning figure in the whole EPSO corpus: a four-competency
CBI is a 40-minute interview, which matches the brochure's structured
interview [6].

The **SCBI** is "an interview that aims to assess general competencies by
asking the candidate how he/she would react in a specific situation" [11].
EPSO publishes complete mock assignment booklets for AD and AST profiles,
which document the format precisely [14] [15]:

- **Time allowed: 40 minutes** for the AD interview [14]; **35 minutes** for
  the AST interview [15].
- The candidate is given the booklet in advance — "you will have until the day
  of the interview to go through the information individually in order to
  prepare for the interview" [14].
- The scenario is fictitious and self-contained: "neither your knowledge in
  the field nor your knowledge on the topic of this exercise will be assessed
  during the interview; therefore, conducting any additional research is
  unnecessary" [14].
- Candidates may "print the documents, rearrange them in any order you wish
  and add comments or make notes as necessary" [14].
- Competencies assessed are named in the booklet. The AD mock assesses
  "Analysis & Problem Solving, Leadership, Learning & Development, Prioritising
  & Organising, Resilience and Working with Others" [14]; the AST mock assesses
  the same set **without Leadership** [15].
- The booklets carry a control notice: the exercise "may only be administered
  and interpreted by persons trained and authorised by EPSO and only under the
  conditions stipulated by EPSO" [14] [15].

The scenarios are workplace simulations with a document dossier. The AD mock
casts the candidate as "a senior investigator at the Anti-Fraud Bureau (AFB)"
facing a case-selection strategy review, a workload-management problem and a
time-constrained international meeting, with a staff chart, minutes, a
discussion forum and an internal communication channel to read [14]. The AST
mock casts the candidate as "an assistant in the Department for Crisis
Management at the European Agency for Diplomatic Relations (EADR)" [15]. A
real published assignment — for open competitions EPSO/AD/365-370/19,
administrators specialising in legal research — differs on one point of
procedure: there, "additional research is allowed, and candidates will be
allowed to ask questions during the interview" [21].

## 6. Marking under the legacy model (EPSO/AD/399/22, 2022)

The last generation of assessment-centre notices shows how the marking worked.
*Notice of open competition EPSO/AD/399/22 – Administrators (AD 7) in the
field of Audit* (OJ C 114A, 10 March 2022) organised an assessment centre
comprising "reasoning tests, a field-related interview, and tests to assess
general competencies (a competency-based interview and a situational
competency-based interview)" [8]. Note what is *not* there: no case study, no
oral presentation, no group exercise.

Documented marking [8]:

- Invitation cap: candidates invited to the assessment-centre phase "will not
  exceed three times the number of successful candidates sought".
- **Reasoning tests** — verbal 20 questions / 35 min, numerical 10 / 20 min,
  abstract 10 / 10 min; combined pass mark **30/50**; "the scores obtained in
  these tests will not count towards the final overall mark".
- **Field-related interview** — in language 2, marked **0 to 200**, pass mark
  **120/200**, counting towards the final overall mark.
- **General competencies** — eight competencies, each "marked out of 10",
  allocated between the two interviews: analysis and problem-solving,
  prioritising and organising, working with others and leadership to the
  **SCBI**; communication, delivering quality and results, learning and
  development, and resilience to the **CBI**. "There is no pass mark per
  competency. However, candidates must reach a pass mark of at least 40/80 in
  total for all competencies combined."
- The reserve list took eligible candidates with "the highest overall marks,
  out of 280", across the general and field-related competency tests.
- Successful candidates received a **competency passport** "containing
  qualitative feedback from the Selection Board", made available both to them
  and to the recruiting services.

## 7. Marking under the current model

The 2026 notice replaces competency marking with a weighted ranking
machine [2]:

| Test | In preliminary combined score | Weight | In final combined score | Weight |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Verbal reasoning | Yes | 40 % | Yes | 35 % |
| Numerical + abstract reasoning | No | — | No | — |
| EU knowledge | Yes | 30 % | Yes | 25 % |
| Digital skills | Yes | 30 % | Yes | 25 % |
| EUFTE | No | — | Yes | 15 % |

The mechanics are sequential and unforgiving [2]: MCQ tests are scored in
order (reasoning, then EU knowledge, then digital skills), "each time, a test
will be scored only for those candidates who obtained at least the required
pass score(s) in the previous test"; failing any pass score ends
participation; candidates are then ranked on the preliminary combined score,
and **only about 1.5 times the number of successful candidates sought** have
their EUFTE scripts marked and their eligibility checked. Results are notified
only at the end of the competition. Inclusion on a reserve list "does not
confer any right to or guarantee of recruitment" [2].

## 8. Language requirements

Language rules shape every exercise, because most of them are not sat in the
candidate's best language.

Under **EPSO/AD/399/22** (2022): thorough knowledge (**C2**) of one official
language ("language 1") and satisfactory knowledge (**minimum C1**) of
**English or French** ("language 2"); reasoning tests in language 1, "the rest
of the tests of this competition" — including both interviews — in language 2.
The notice justifies the English/French restriction by the European Court of
Auditors' drafting/pivot languages under its Decision No 22-2004 [8].

Under **EPSO/AD/427/26** (2026): thorough knowledge (**minimum C1**) of at
least one of the 24 official languages, and satisfactory knowledge (**minimum
B2**) of "a different language to be chosen among the remaining 23 official EU
languages"; the minima "apply to each linguistic ability (listening, reading,
spoken interaction, spoken production, and writing)" and map to the Common
European Framework of Reference. Reasoning tests are in language 1; EU
knowledge, digital skills and the EUFTE are in language 2 [2].

Under the legacy assessment centre, the centre itself ran "in three languages:
English, French and German", and candidates used their chosen second language
for the exercises unless told otherwise [6].

## 9. Where assessment-centre exercises survive: the agencies

EU agencies recruit temporary and contract agents under their own procedures,
and there the exercises persist. The **European Medicines Agency** publishes a
staged model: preliminary virtual interviews by the selection committee of
20–40 minutes; preliminary computer-based tests of 1–3 hours, which may be
"multiple-choice questions, job-specific tasks, essays, or ability
assessments"; final interviews with a selection committee of 3–5 people
lasting 30–60 minutes in English, at which candidates may be asked about
"European integration, EU institutions and EMA's role"; final-stage tests of
1–3 hours; and **assessment centres**, "normally reserved for managerial
positions", which are "full-day sessions" "conducted by an external provider,
based on a combination of tasks and exercises (including, for example,
cognitive exercises, case studies or role play)" [3].

**Eurojust** likewise states that shortlisted applicants "will usually be
invited to an interview and a written test", that "other assessment methods
can be included, for example assessment centres for managerial selections",
and that "the interview and written test will be conducted in English, though
knowledge of other EU languages declared in your application may also be
tested"; a Selection Board appointed by the Administrative Director assesses
applications against the vacancy notice [4].

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — guidance, not documented fact. It points back to the cited
criteria above and adds no new facts.**

## A worked approach to the case study / written test

EPSO publishes its five marking anchors [5]. Treat them as a checklist, and
work backwards from them. A four-stage approach that maps onto the anchors:

1. **Read the assignment first, then the dossier (10 % of the time).** The
   assignment is the only thing being marked against; the mock AST case study
   states three specific tasks and gives 90 minutes in total [22]. Write the
   tasks at the top of your page and keep them visible. This serves the anchor
   "uses the information provided effectively to address the assignment".
2. **Skim-map the dossier (25 %).** The published mocks are deliberately
   heterogeneous — emails, minutes, a manual, a stock overview, a memo, web
   pages, a discussion forum, a blog [22]. Build a one-line index: document,
   what it adds, who wants what. Contradictions between documents are usually
   planted deliberately.
3. **Plan before drafting (15 %).** Decide your structure and your
   recommendation before you write a sentence. This is what the anchor
   "structures written communication with a logical flow of ideas" rewards,
   and it is the difference between a note and a brain-dump.
4. **Draft to the audience, then cut (50 %).** The anchors reward tailoring to
   audience and purpose, and concision. If you are writing to a Head of Unit,
   lead with the recommendation and the decision required. Then delete every
   sentence that does not serve a task.

Two things you may stop worrying about: **spelling and grammar are not
themselves marked** on the case study, per EPSO's published marker
guidance [18]; and the EUFTE is explicitly "neither a language test nor a test
of factual knowledge" [2]. Time spent polishing prose is better spent covering
a task you have skipped.

## Preparing for the oral presentation

The exercise asks you to *develop a proposal* on a fictitious problem and
present it to at least two assessors, without electronic aids, with a
flip-chart available [6] [23]. Practical implications: build a three-part
skeleton you can fill under time pressure (problem → options → recommendation
with next step); write your flip-chart headings, not sentences; and reserve a
third of your preparation time for the questions, because the competencies
assessed included resilience [6] — which in this exercise is mostly observed
in how you handle challenge.

## Preparing for the group exercise

The published definition is doing a lot of work: you process information
*individually first*, then discuss and reach a *collective decision* [7] [24].
The exercise assessed learning and development, prioritising and organising,
working with others and leadership [6] — so it rewarded structuring the
group's process (agreeing an agenda and a timekeeper), bringing in quiet
participants, and converging on a decision, not winning the argument. It is
not a debate.

## Preparing for the CBI and the recruitment interview

Budget roughly ten minutes per competency [10], which means one or two
substantial examples per competency and no more. Use STAR — situation, task,
action, result — and lean hard on the *action*: the structured interview was
premised on "past behaviour is generally the best predictor of future
performance" [6], so the assessor is listening for what *you* specifically
did. Under the current model this preparation is aimed at the **institution's
recruitment interview**, which is where general competencies are now reviewed
[1], so map your examples to the 2022 framework's eight competencies [12]
[13] rather than the pre-2023 list.

## Preparing for the SCBI

The SCBI is the one EPSO exercise you could genuinely prepare *content* for,
because the booklet arrives in advance [14]. A sound routine:

- Build a **stakeholder map** and a **timeline** from the documents before you
  do anything else. In the published AD mock, the timeline is the exercise:
  quorum, chair-appointment lead times, comment windows and a fixed meeting
  date all interact [14].
- Identify the **two or three tensions** the designer planted — in the AD
  mock, checklist versus cost-benefit analysis, and centralised resource
  coordination versus investigator autonomy [14].
- Rehearse answers that state a position, acknowledge the counter-argument and
  give a next step. "How would you react" questions reward decisiveness with
  reasoning, not neutrality.
- Do **not** research the subject matter. The booklet says prior knowledge is
  not assessed and research is unnecessary [14] — though note the one
  published real assignment where research *was* permitted [21], so read your
  own booklet's wording rather than assuming.

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

- **Multiple observers, per exercise, by design.** EPSO's brochure states that
  in every simulation "you will be observed/evaluated in a systematic way by at
  least two trained assessors", whose task "is to look for various
  competencies that have been determined in advance" [6]; the assessment-centre
  FAQ repeats the two-assessor minimum [20]. The structured interview
  specifically "will be conducted by at least two assessors" [6], and the oral
  presentation was presented to "a small audience which consists of at least
  two assessors" [6].
- **Redundant measurement.** "Each general competency is observed and measured
  in at least two different exercises whereas specific, field-related
  competencies are measured only in the context of the case study" [6]. Final
  results were "based on the evaluation of each competency tested through the
  Assessment Centre exercises" [20], not on exercise-level scores — which is
  why the brochure could tell candidates that a poor performance in one
  exercise "is not necessarily a problem" [6].
- **Content validated by the Selection Board.** The general rules state that
  "the content of the assessment centre tests is validated by the selection
  board of the competition" [7]; the FAQ says the same [20]. Under the current
  model the Selection Board still owns eligibility decisions and may enlarge
  the EUFTE-marking cohort where candidates tie [2].
- **Published marking scales.** Legacy: each of eight general competencies
  marked 1–10, no per-competency pass mark, combined pass mark 40/80;
  field-related interview 0–200 with a 120/200 pass; overall ranking out of
  280 [8]. Current: fixed pass scores per test and published percentage
  weightings, with EUFTE scripts marked only for approximately the top
  1.5× of the reserve-list target [2].
- **Published anchors.** EPSO publishes the five written-communication anchors
  its markers apply [5], and instructs markers that "grammar or spelling
  mistakes in themselves should not be taken into consideration" [18] — a rare
  public statement of a scoring exclusion.
- **Controlled instruments.** SCBI booklets carry the notice that the exercise
  "may only be administered and interpreted by persons trained and authorised
  by EPSO and only under the conditions stipulated by EPSO" [14] [15].
- **Structured feedback to candidates and recruiters.** Under the legacy
  model, candidates received a **competency passport** with qualitative
  Selection Board feedback, shared with recruiting services [8] [6]; the
  reserve list under the current model is simply "made available to the
  recruiting services" [2].
- **Practitioner-reported detail (not official).** A named training provider
  describes the pre-2023 structured interview as run by two EPSO assessors
  working from "an already developed questionnaire", with responses "rated on
  a scale from 1 'very weak' to 10 'outstanding'" [25]. The 1–10 range matches
  the competency marking published in EPSO/AD/399/22 [8]; the descriptor
  labels are not corroborated by an official source.
- **Agency practice.** EMA's final interviews are conducted by a selection
  committee of 3–5 people [3]; Eurojust's Selection Board is appointed by the
  Administrative Director [4]; EMA's assessment centres are outsourced to an
  external provider [3].
- **Professional-body benchmark.** CIPD's selection-methods guidance treats
  interviews, psychometric tests, skill-based assessment tasks and assessment
  centres as the main selection methods, and advocates structured interviews —
  identical questions in the same order, pre-agreed scoring criteria, panels,
  and independent scoring before discussion — as the way to minimise bias [30].
  EPSO's documented practice (pre-agreed competencies, at least two trained
  assessors, published anchors) is consistent with that standard [6] [5].

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — guidance, not documented fact.**

- **Preparing for an assessment centre that no longer runs.** The commonest
  and costliest error. The reform note is unambiguous and dated [1], and the
  2026 notice contains no oral exercise [2]. Rehearsing group-exercise
  technique for an EPSO competition is wasted effort; rehearsing it for an
  agency managerial selection is not [3] [4].
- **Trusting EPSO's own legacy pages as current.** The case-study, oral
  presentation, CBI and SCBI pages remain reachable and still name the
  pre-2023 competencies — the oral presentation page lists "Analysis & Problem
  Solving, Communicating (Oral communication), Delivering Quality & Results,
  and Resilience" [23], which is the 2015-era set [7], not the 2022 framework
  [12]. They are not linked from any current staff-category test page [5] [16]
  [17] [19]. Treat them as archive.
- **Citing "Leadership" as a current EPSO competency.** It is in the 2015
  general rules [7] and in the 2022 AD7 notice [8] and the AD SCBI mock [14] —
  all superseded. It is not in the 2022 framework [12].
- **Writing beautifully and answering the wrong question.** The anchors reward
  addressing the assignment; grammar and spelling are explicitly excluded from
  the case-study assessment [18]. Candidates routinely over-invest in prose
  and under-deliver on the third task.
- **Treating the SCBI as a memory test.** The booklet says field knowledge is
  not assessed and research is unnecessary [14]; candidates who arrive having
  researched real anti-fraud policy have prepared the wrong thing. Prepare the
  *structure* of the scenario instead.
- **Ignoring the language allocation.** Most tests are sat in language 2 [2]
  [8], and under the legacy model so were all the oral exercises [6].
  Candidates who rehearse only in their strongest language are surprised on
  the day.
- **For interviewers designing equivalents:** dropping the two-assessor rule,
  or scoring an exercise rather than scoring competencies across exercises,
  discards the two design features EPSO documents most insistently [6] [20].
- **Assuming a reserve-list place is a job.** The 2026 notice states in terms
  that inclusion "does not confer any right to or guarantee of
  recruitment" [2]; the interview that matters comes afterwards [1].

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.**

1. **Anchor self-scoring (90 min).** Download the published AST case-study
   mock [22]. Do it under the stated 90-minute limit, covering all three
   tasks. Then score your own answer against EPSO's five published written
   anchors [5], 1–5 on each. The anchor you score lowest on is your training
   target.
2. **SCBI cold-open drill (2 × 45 min).** Take the published AD SCBI mock
   booklet [14]. Session one: build a stakeholder map and a dated timeline
   from the documents only. Session two: have a partner ask you questions 1–5
   from the SCBI group in the Question Bank above, in 40 minutes total —
   matching the booklet's stated interview length [14].
3. **Ten-minutes-per-competency rehearsal (40 min).** Pick four competencies
   from the 2022 framework [12]. Have a partner run a 40-minute interview at
   the published CBI rate of about ten minutes per competency [10]. The
   discipline being trained is stopping, not starting.
4. **Oral presentation under constraint (70 min).** Give yourself 20 minutes
   with any dense policy document, then present a proposal for 10 minutes with
   nothing but a flip-chart, and take 10 minutes of hostile questions —
   approximating the 50-minute published exercise [6] plus review.
5. **Group-exercise observation drill (60 min, for assessors).** Run the
   group-exercise definition [7] with six participants and two silent
   observers, each scoring only the competencies the brochure assigns to that
   exercise [6]. Compare observer scores before discussing — the redundancy
   the model relies on only works if observers score independently first.
6. **Currency check (15 min, before every application).** Open the live notice
   of competition and read section "How will the competition be organised?"
   end to end [2]. Write down the exact list of tests. Anything a preparation
   provider tells you that is not in that list is not in your competition.

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.** Signposts only; not legal advice.

- **Legal basis.** Recruitment of EU officials is governed by the Staff
  Regulations, which require that an official produce "evidence of a thorough
  knowledge of one of the EU languages and a satisfactory knowledge of another
  EU language to the extent necessary for the performance of their duties" —
  the provision from which the language-1/language-2 architecture of every
  exercise derives [2] [27]. Each notice of competition and its annexes "form
  the legally binding framework for this competition" [2].
- **Reasonable accommodations.** Both the 2022 and 2026 notices state that
  EPSO "endeavours to apply an equal opportunities policy to all candidates"
  and set out a request procedure for candidates with a disability or medical
  condition that may affect their ability to take the tests; EPSO "may grant
  reasonable accommodations where deemed necessary" after examining the
  request and supporting documents [2] [8]. The move to remote testing was
  justified in part on accessibility grounds [1].
- **Language restriction is a live fairness question.** Where a competition
  restricts language 2 to English and French, the notice must justify it: in
  EPSO/AD/399/22 the justification is the European Court of Auditors' own
  decision on drafting/pivot languages and the requirement that recruits be
  "immediately operational" [8]. The 2026 generalist notice imposes no such
  restriction [2].
- **Equal treatment in employment.** Council Directive 2000/78/EC establishes
  a general framework for equal treatment in employment and occupation and
  applies expressly to conditions for access to employment "including
  selection criteria and recruitment conditions" [28] — the frame against
  which exercise design and scoring are judged for EU employers generally.
- **Candidate data.** Personal data processed by EU institutions, bodies,
  offices and agencies — including exercise scripts, assessor notes and
  competency passports — fall under Regulation (EU) 2018/1725 [29].
- **Transparency as a fairness measure.** EPSO publishes the source materials
  for the EU knowledge test at the time the notice is published, explicitly to
  level "the playing field among candidates" [1] [2], and publishes the
  written test's marking anchors [5]. Both are unusual and worth noting as
  practice benchmarks by interviewers designing equivalent exercises.

# Risks & Caveats

- **The legacy-page problem is the biggest risk in this document.** EPSO's
  case-study [18], oral-presentation [23], CBI [10] and SCBI [11] pages are
  live and were read on 2026-07-20; they describe pre-2023 exercises using
  pre-2023 competency names. This document treats them as authoritative
  *descriptions of those exercises* and **not** as evidence that the exercises
  are currently used in EPSO competitions — a judgement, based on the reform
  note [1], the 2026 notice [2] and the absence of any such test from the
  current staff-category pages [5] [16] [17] [19]. If EPSO reinstates oral
  testing, that judgement dates immediately.
- **The group exercise's exact end date is not established here.** It is in
  the 2015 general rules [7] and the brochure [6], and absent from the 2022
  AD7 notice [8]. The claim that the SCBI *replaced* it rests on a named
  practitioner source [9], not on an EPSO statement.
- **The Assessment Centre Brochure is undated and competition-specific.** It
  was published for competition EPSO/AD/244 and includes a Croatian
  language-1 test, dating it to around Croatia's accession period [6]. Its
  durations and competency matrix are indicative of that competition, and the
  brochure itself says "the primary documents are the notice of competition
  and the guide to open competitions" [6].
- **EUR-Lex access is fragile.** The notices cited at [2], [7], [8] were read
  in a browser on 2026-07-20; EUR-Lex serves an automated-traffic challenge to
  scripted fetchers, so automated link checks of those URLs may behave
  inconsistently even when the pages are live.
- **One competition is not the model.** EPSO/AD/427/26 is a *generalist* AD5
  competition; specialist competitions substitute a field MCQ and may use the
  WT or FRWT rather than the EUFTE [1] [5]. Test counts, durations and
  weightings vary per notice.
- **Agency practice is illustrated, not surveyed.** Only EMA [3] and
  Eurojust [4] were examined. Other agencies and the institutions' own
  recruitment interviews may differ materially, and no public document
  standardises them.
- **Practitioner sources carry practitioner weight.** The 1–10 rating
  descriptors [25], the question lists [26] and the group-exercise replacement
  claim [9] are named, dated third-party material, never the sole support for
  a factual claim about EPSO's process.

# Open Questions

- **Will oral assessment return to the competition phase?** The 2023 note
  leaves EPSO offering the institutions "evaluation of general competences" at
  the recruitment stage [1] — will EPSO publish instruments or guidance for
  that, as it published SCBI booklets before? EPSO news pages would resolve
  it.
- **When exactly was the group exercise withdrawn, and by what decision?** A
  primary EPSO statement has not been located; only its presence in 2015 [7]
  and absence in 2022 [8].
- **Will EPSO retire or update the orphaned exercise pages?** Their continued
  presence with pre-2023 competency names [23] is the single largest source of
  candidate confusion identified in this research.
- **How do individual institutions structure the post-reserve-list recruitment
  interview?** Still unresolved from the parent document; nothing located in
  this research standardises it.
- **Does any current EPSO procedure still use the CBI or SCBI** — for example
  in calls for expression of interest for temporary staff, which the oral
  presentation page still references [23]? A survey of live calls would
  settle it.
- **What are the published EUFTE assignment materials like in practice?** The
  notice says documentation is published ahead of the test date [2]; no
  example was located during this research.
