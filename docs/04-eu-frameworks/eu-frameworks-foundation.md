# Executive Summary

This is the foundation document for the EU Frameworks category. It surveys how
competencies are defined and assessed for careers in the European Union
institutions — where the European Personnel Selection Office (EPSO) runs the
selection procedures — and, briefly, how competency-based interviewing is used
by private-sector employers across the EU market.

The single most important fact for any reader is that EPSO's selection model
changed fundamentally in 2023. The old model, in place since 2010, tested eight
general competencies at an assessment centre that included a competency-based
interview. Under the new model, decided by EPSO's Management Board on
31 January 2023, the assessment centre and its oral tests "cease to exist":
competitions now run on remotely proctored written tests (reasoning, EU
knowledge or field-specific MCQ, a written/case-study test, and in some
competitions a digital-skills test), and candidates' general competencies are
instead reviewed via **recruitment interviews conducted by the institutions
after EPSO publishes the reserve list** [3]. A new eight-competency framework
(2022) — from "Critical thinking, analysing & creative problem-solving" to
"Intrapreneurship" — remains the reference standard for what EU officials are
expected to demonstrate [1] [2].

The document is rated **Medium** confidence: the core claims (the competency
framework, the new competition model, contract-agent selection, reserve-list
recruitment) rest on official EPSO/EU Careers and EUR-Lex documents, but the
description of how recruitment-stage interviews run in practice involves some
synthesis from practitioner sources, and EPSO's process is still visibly in
flux (its first big post-reform generalist competition ran in early 2026).

# Key Takeaways

- EPSO is the interinstitutional office that selects staff for the EU
  institutions; permanent officials are recruited through open competitions
  that end in a **reserve list**, from which institutions recruit —
  Chapter 1 (Articles 27–34) and Annex III of the Staff Regulations govern
  this. *(documented)* [4] [13]
- The **EPSO Competency Framework (2022)** defines 8 general competencies, each
  with observable "anchors": critical thinking/analysing & creative
  problem-solving; decision-making & getting results; information management
  (digital and data literacy); self-management; working together; learning as a
  skill; communication; intrapreneurship. *(documented)* [2]
- Since the **2023 reform**, EPSO competitions contain **no oral tests and no
  assessment centre**; selection runs on remotely proctored written tests, and
  general competencies are reviewed at **recruitment interviews run by the
  institutions** after the reserve list is published. *(documented)* [3]
- **Contract agents** (function groups I–IV) are selected differently: via the
  CAST database and computer-based reasoning plus competency MCQ tests, with
  interviews organised by the recruiting service, not EPSO. *(documented)*
  [7] [8]
- Under the pre-2023 model, EPSO's assessment centre included a **situational
  competency-based interview (SCBI)** built on a fictitious workplace
  assignment — official assignment booklets are still published and remain
  useful preparation material for institution-led interviews. *(documented)* [12]
- EU private-sector and multinational employers widely use structured,
  competency-based interviews; professional-body guidance (CIPD) recommends
  identical questions, pre-agreed scoring criteria and panel interviews to
  reduce bias. *(documented)* [16]
- For candidates: prepare STAR-format stories mapped to the 8 framework
  competencies — they are assessed at the recruitment interview even though
  they are no longer tested in the competition itself. *(guidance)*
- EPSO's process is under active change (new test platform, record 2026 AD5
  participation with roughly 3% reaching the reserve list) — always check the
  current Notice of Competition, the only official statement of what a given
  competition tests. *(documented)* [11] [17]

# Definitions

Prefer the shared `GLOSSARY.md`; the following are used in the narrowed EU
sense in this document.

- **EPSO** — European Personnel Selection Office; the interinstitutional
  office that organises selection procedures for the EU institutions and
  bodies. Its careers portal is eu-careers.europa.eu (epso.europa.eu
  redirects there).
- **Open competition** — the selection procedure for permanent EU staff,
  announced by a Notice of Competition and ending in a reserve list [4].
- **Reserve list** — "a database containing information on the profile,
  competencies, and availability of all the successful candidates in a
  particular selection procedure", used by institutions to recruit [10].
- **AD / AST** — administrator and assistant function groups for permanent
  staff; graduates enter at AD5, specialists at AD6–AD9 or AST3–AST9 [4].
- **Contract agent / CAST** — contract staff selected via the "CAST Permanent"
  database in function groups I–IV, with EPSO-run reasoning tests and
  service-run interviews [7] [8].
- **Anchor** — an observable element of a general competency in the EPSO
  Competency Framework [2].
- **SCBI** — situational competency-based interview; the assignment-based
  interview used in EPSO assessment centres under the pre-2023 model [12].
- **Notice of Competition** — the official published document that specifies
  the tests and requirements of a given competition; EPSO's FAQ describes it
  as "the only official document that contains detailed information about the
  actual tests used by EPSO for a given competition" [11].

# Framework Context

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

EPSO's Competency Framework is the EU-institution counterpart of national
schemes such as the UK Civil Service Success Profiles: it "defines the
competencies that are considered essential to perform effectively in any given
job, by the EU institutions and bodies that EPSO serves", and it explicitly
splits assessment between the two phases — "Some of those competencies are
assessed by EPSO during the selection phase, others are assessed by the EU
institutions and bodies themselves during the recruitment phase" [1].

The framework in force was introduced from 2022–2023, replacing the
eight-competency framework used since 2010; EPSO's 2023 information note on
the new competition model confirms "The general competency framework
(April 2022) remains valid" [1] [3]. The 8 general competencies, each defined
with observable anchors, are [2]:

| # | General competency (2022 framework) | Official one-line definition |
|---|-------------------------------------|------------------------------|
| 1 | Critical thinking, analysing & creative problem-solving | "Thinks holistically and translates complex information into creative and innovative solutions." |
| 2 | Decision-making & getting results | "Takes decisions in a shifting environment, translates them into action and works towards tangible results." |
| 3 | Information management (digital and data literacy) | "Translates knowledge and skills through digital tools and technologies, optimises ways of working by utilising the digital environment." |
| 4 | Self-management | "Organises own tasks, demonstrates responsibility and a constructive attitude at all times." |
| 5 | Working together | "Demonstrates cross-functional cooperation and fosters diversity and inclusion. Cooperates well by seeking compromise, sharing knowledge and assisting others." |
| 6 | Learning as a skill | "Actively manages own learning, growth and self-improvement." |
| 7 | Communication | "Conveys information and opinions clearly, generates buy-in by facilitating interaction and engaging effectively with others." |
| 8 | Intrapreneurship | "Drives change, inspires and empowers others in implementing change and offers guidance at all levels." |

For orientation against the previous model: the pre-2023 assessment centre's
SCBI was "designed to assess the following competencies: Analysis & Problem
Solving, Learning & Development, Prioritising & Organising, Resilience,
Working with Others, and Leadership" [12] — readers will still meet these
older labels in preparation materials and older competition documents.

The legal frame sits above all of this: the Staff Regulations of Officials of
the European Union (Regulation No 31 (EEC), 11 (EAEC), consolidated) devote
Chapter 1 of Title III (Articles 27–34) to recruitment and Annex III to the
competition procedure, and the same instrument contains the Conditions of
Employment of Other Servants covering non-permanent staff [13].

# Core Reference

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## The current EPSO selection procedure for permanent officials

Permanent EU staff are "appointed for an indefinite period" and recruited
through open competitions that produce reserve lists. Two competition types
are published: **graduate competitions** (university graduates without work
experience, recruited as administrators at entry grade AD5) and **specialist
competitions** (graduates and non-graduates with field expertise, recruited at
AD6–AD9 or as assistants at AST3–AST9). Candidates apply via the Single
Candidate Portal; EPSO verifies eligibility (nationality, education,
languages); successful candidates join a reserve list, and "being on this
list means you are eligible for recruitment but not automatically
employed" [4].

The current competition model was set by EPSO's interinstitutional Management
Board on 31 January 2023. Its documented main features are [3]:

- "The 'Assessment Centre' and its related oral tests cease to exist.
  Therefore, oral tests will no longer be part of the selection procedures."
- Verbal, numerical and abstract/logical reasoning tests remain, but on a
  **pass/fail** basis.
- Generalist competitions (e.g. AD5) include an **EU knowledge test**;
  specialist competitions replace it with a **field-related MCQ** (ranked).
- All competitions include a **written test** (usually a case study) assessing
  drafting skills, corrected only for candidates with the best MCQ results.
- A **digital-skills test** may be included for some competitions.
- All tests are **online and remotely proctored**, in a single testing
  session, with a target timeline of six months from Notice of Competition to
  reserve list.
- Crucially for this knowledge base: "Candidates' general competences will
  still be reviewed via recruitment interviews, after the publication of
  reserve lists by EPSO", with EPSO offering the institutions recruitment-stage
  support including "evaluation of general competences"; "The choice of whom
  to recruit will remain entirely with the institutions."

The model is being exercised at scale: the 2026 AD5 generalist competition —
reported as the EU's first major recruitment drive of this kind after a
seven-year hiatus — expected 50,000–60,000 candidates (against 22,644 in the
last, 2019, AD5 round) for 1,490 reserve-list places, roughly a 3% success
rate, run fully digitally and remotely in all 24 EU languages, and including
the new digital-skills test [17]. The Notice of Competition remains "the only
official document that contains detailed information about the actual tests
used by EPSO for a given competition" [11].

## How the institutions assess competencies

Under the current model, interview-based competency assessment happens at the
**recruitment phase**: "The institutions check the profiles of the successful
candidates in the reserve list to identify those who best match their needs,
then they invite those selected for an interview", and candidates "may be
invited by several institutions or services" where lists are open to multiple
institutions [9]. Candidate CVs and competency data on the reserve list are
visible to recruiting institutions through the database [9]. Being listed is
no guarantee of a post, though EPSO's FAQ notes that on average around half of
listed candidates are recruited within seven months [10].

Under the pre-2023 model, the competency-based interview lived inside EPSO's
assessment centre as the **SCBI**: candidates received an official assignment
booklet describing a fictitious workplace scenario (e.g. an EU agency
preparing for the accession of three fictional Member States), were told the
content "is fictitious… derived from actual events, key details have been
changed", could annotate and keep the booklet during the interview, and were
assessed against named competencies with "No previous knowledge…
required" [12]. These booklets are still published on the EU Careers site and
show, in primary-source form, how EU-institution interviewers anchor
competency questions in a controlled scenario [12].

## Contract agents vs officials (survey level)

Contract staff are recruited through two routes: **CAST Permanent** — an
open-ended database in which applicants register and are shortlisted by
recruiting services when needs arise — and **ad hoc vacancies** with their own
procedures and deadlines [7]. The four function groups cover: "I. manual and
administrative support-service tasks; II. clerical or secretarial tasks,
office management and other equivalent tasks; III. executive tasks, drafting,
accountancy and other equivalent technical tasks; IV. administrative,
advisory, linguistic and equivalent technical tasks" [8].

Shortlisted CAST candidates sit computer-based multiple-choice tests: verbal,
numerical and abstract reasoning, plus a competency test (field knowledge) or
a language-comprehension test for linguist profiles [7]. The competency test
comprises 25 scenario-based MCQs (12 for translators/proofreaders) in the
candidate's second language, 50 minutes in total, one point per question with
no negative marking; pass marks are 13/25 for function groups II–III and
16/25 for function group IV [6]. Test results are valid for 10 years and for
the tested function group and any lower group; candidates who pass are then
invited to **recruitment interviews organised by the hiring services**, not by
EPSO [8]. Contracts are time-limited (initially 6–12 months, potentially
renewable), in contrast with officials' indefinite appointments [8]. The
Conditions of Employment of Other Servants, contained in the same consolidated
instrument as the Staff Regulations, provide the legal basis for these
non-permanent engagements [13].

## The wider EU market: private-sector competency interviewing

For candidates targeting the EU market beyond the institutions, structured
competency-based interviewing is standard professional practice rather than a
single published framework. CIPD guidance on selection identifies interviews,
psychometric tests, skill-based assessment tasks and assessment centres as the
main methods, strongly advocates structured interviews as "an effective way to
minimise bias and ensure there is a level playing field for candidates", and
describes their core elements: identical questions in the same order for all
candidates, pre-agreed scoring criteria, panel rather than sole interviewers,
and independent scoring before discussion [16]. Multinationals operating in
the EU typically publish their own values/competency models; the transferable
point is the method — structured questions scored against pre-defined
criteria — which this knowledge base treats in depth in
`techniques-foundation` and `best-practice-foundation`.

## EPSO-framework-style competency questions (recruitment-interview practice)

1. Give an example of when you had to analyse complex information to provide a creative and effective solution. *(sourced: Prepari.eu, "93 EU Job Interview Questions to Prepare For")* [18]
2. Tell me about a time when you had to act quickly without all the facts. What was the outcome? *(sourced: Prepari.eu)* [18]
3. Describe a situation where you faced an unexpected obstacle. How did you adapt? *(sourced: Prepari.eu)* [18]
4. Describe when collaboration was essential to achieve your goal. *(sourced: Prepari.eu)* [18]
5. Please describe a very stressful situation from your working life. *(sourced: Euphorum EPSO assessment-centre training guide, pre-2023 model)* [19]
6. Tell us about a time you had to judge the reliability of conflicting information from different digital sources before advising a colleague or stakeholder. *(composed — in the style of "Information management / digital and data literacy")*
7. Describe a piece of critical feedback that changed the way you work. What did you do differently afterwards? *(composed — in the style of "Learning as a skill")*
8. Give an example of a proposal you had to win support for from people holding different points of view. How did you generate buy-in? *(composed — in the style of "Communication")*
9. Tell us about a change you initiated in your team or organisation. How did you bring others along with it? *(composed — in the style of "Intrapreneurship")*
10. Describe a decision you took with incomplete information that did not work out as planned. What would you do differently now? *(composed — in the style of "Decision-making & getting results")*
11. Tell us about a period when your workload and priorities changed at short notice. How did you organise yourself and stay constructive? *(composed — in the style of "Self-management")*

## Motivation and EU-context questions (institution recruitment interviews)

1. Why do you want to work for the EU institutions — and why this institution in particular? *(composed)*
2. Which EU policy area interests you most, and how does your experience prepare you to contribute to it? *(composed)*

## Contract-agent-style questions (function groups III–IV)

1. Tell us about a time you had to draft a clear, accurate document under a fixed deadline. How did you assure its quality? *(composed)*
2. Describe a time you supported a team delivering a task outside your usual responsibilities. What did you contribute? *(composed — in the style of "Working together")*

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — guidance, not documented fact.**

- **Map your stories to the 2022 framework.** Prepare two or three STAR
  (Situation–Task–Action–Result) stories per competency in the table above,
  using the anchors in the official leaflet [2] as your checklist of what to
  evidence — e.g. for "Working together", show compromise-seeking, knowledge
  sharing and inclusion, not just "we got on well". The STAR structure itself
  is standard structured-interview practice (see `techniques-foundation`).
- **Aim your interview preparation at the recruitment stage.** Under the
  current model there is no oral test in the competition; the interview that
  decides whether you convert a reserve-list place into a job is run by the
  recruiting institution [3] [9]. Prepare it like any structured competency
  interview, plus motivation and EU-context questions.
- **Practise in your second language.** CAST competency tests are taken in the
  candidate's second language [6], and institution interviews commonly probe
  language ability; rehearse your STAR stories in both of your declared
  languages.
- **Use the published SCBI booklets as rehearsal material.** They are official,
  free, and show the level of scenario complexity EU assessors have used [12];
  practise extracting priorities and stakeholder positions, then answering
  competency probes about the scenario.
- **Keep answers concise and evidenced.** A useful discipline is 1.5–2 minutes
  per STAR answer, with your personal contribution ("I", not "we")
  foregrounded — this is coaching consensus, not an EPSO rule.

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

- **What is scored at selection (current model).** Reasoning tests are
  pass/fail; the EU-knowledge or field MCQ is ranked; the written/case-study
  test is corrected only for the best MCQ scorers; eligibility is verified
  before listing [3]. In CAST procedures, published pass marks are explicit:
  13/25 (FG II–III) and 16/25 (FG IV), one point per question, no negative
  marking [6].
- **Who decides recruitment.** "The choice of whom to recruit will remain
  entirely with the institutions"; EPSO offers the recruiting services
  training, guidance, tools and "evaluation of general competences" at the
  recruitment stage [3]. Institutions shortlist from the reserve-list
  database — where candidate profiles and competencies are visible — and
  interview those who best match their needs [9].
- **How the legacy EPSO interview was run.** The SCBI was administered under
  controlled conditions ("may only be administered and interpreted by persons
  trained and authorised by EPSO"), against named competencies, with the
  scenario booklet available to the candidate throughout [12]. A practitioner
  guide to the pre-2023 assessment centre describes two EPSO assessors working
  from a pre-developed questionnaire and scoring responses on a 1–10
  scale [19] — practitioner evidence, not an official EPSO publication.
- **Professional-body standard for interview scoring.** CIPD guidance tells
  interviewers to ask identical questions in the same order, score against
  pre-agreed criteria, use panels, and have panel members score independently
  before discussing — noting that multiple interviewers in one interview
  produce fairer, more accurate results than multiple one-on-one
  interviews [16].

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — guidance, not documented fact.**

- **Preparing for the wrong stage.** Candidates drill for an EPSO oral test
  that no longer exists, and under-prepare the institution recruitment
  interview where competencies are actually reviewed [3]. Interviewers
  hiring from reserve lists can make the mirror error: assuming EPSO has
  already interview-tested general competencies — it has not, since 2023.
- **Using the old competency labels.** Much free preparation material still
  teaches the 2010–2023 set (Analysis & Problem Solving, Resilience,
  Leadership, …) [12] [19]; answers mapped only to those labels miss the
  2022 framework's new emphases — digital/data literacy, learning,
  intrapreneurship [2].
- **Treating the reserve list as a job offer.** Listing is eligibility, not
  employment [4]; candidates who stop engaging (stale CV, unwatched EPSO
  account) miss short application windows [9].
- **Ignoring the Notice of Competition.** Tests vary by competition; the
  Notice is the only official specification [11]. Generic advice — including
  this document — never overrides it.
- **Composed-question confusion.** Practice questions labelled *(composed)*
  above are stylistic imitations; presenting them (or any coach's list) as
  "real EPSO questions" misleads candidates — EPSO's real instruments are
  controlled documents [12].
- **Interviewer drift in unstructured interviews.** For EU private-sector
  panels: departing from identical questions and pre-agreed criteria is the
  documented bias risk CIPD guidance warns against [16].

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.**

1. **Framework mapping (60–90 min).** Download the official competency
   leaflet [2]. For each of the 8 competencies, write one STAR story and
   check it against every anchor; flag competencies where you have no story —
   that is your preparation gap.
2. **SCBI dry run (half day).** Take a published SCBI assignment booklet [12],
   spend 45 minutes extracting the problems, stakeholders and a schedule, then
   have a partner ask you questions 1–5 from the Question Bank about the
   scenario. Self-score against the anchors [2].
3. **Second-language rehearsal (30 min, repeatable).** Deliver two of your
   STAR stories in your second language; record and review for clarity and
   concision.
4. **Interviewer drill (for hiring panels).** Using the 8 competencies [2] and
   CIPD's structure rules [16], design a five-question interview with a
   pre-agreed 1–5 scoring rubric per question; pilot it on a colleague and
   compare independent scores before discussion.
5. **Current-state check (15 min).** Before any application, open the live
   Notice of Competition and the EU Careers testing pages [5] [11] and note
   what the specific competition tests — write down the stages so you are
   preparing against the current, not remembered, process.

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.** Signposts only; this is not legal
advice.

- **Staff Regulations.** Recruitment of EU officials is governed by Title III
  Chapter 1 (Articles 27–34) of the Staff Regulations, with the competition
  procedure laid down in Annex III; the same consolidated instrument contains
  the Conditions of Employment of Other Servants covering contract
  staff [13].
- **Equal treatment in the wider EU market.** Council Directive 2000/78/EC
  establishes a general framework for equal treatment in employment and
  occupation, prohibiting direct and indirect discrimination on grounds of
  religion or belief, disability, age and sexual orientation, and applies
  expressly to "conditions for access to employment… including selection
  criteria and recruitment conditions, whatever the branch of activity and at
  all levels of the professional hierarchy" [14] — i.e. it covers
  competency-interview design and scoring by EU employers.
- **Candidate data.** Processing of personal data by the EU institutions,
  bodies, offices and agencies — including candidates' selection data — falls
  under Regulation (EU) 2018/1725, which protects "persons whose personal data
  are processed by Union institutions and bodies in any context
  whatsoever" [15]. (Private-sector EU employers process candidate data under
  the GDPR; see the best-practice foundation document.)
- **Accessibility.** EPSO's published model emphasises online testing as "more
  accessible to the vast majority of candidates, wherever they are" [3];
  candidates needing adjustments should follow the procedure in the relevant
  Notice of Competition [11].

# Risks & Caveats

- **EPSO is mid-transformation.** The 2023 model note, a new test platform and
  the record-scale 2026 AD5 competition [3] [17] show a process still being
  tuned; details (test mix, timelines, providers) can change per competition.
  This document describes the model as published at 2026-07-19; the Notice of
  Competition always prevails [11].
- **URL churn.** epso.europa.eu redirects to eu-careers.europa.eu, and several
  older EPSO pages now return 404; links here were verified on 2026-07-19 but
  the site is being reorganised.
- **Legacy materials dominate the preparation market.** Most freely available
  EPSO interview guidance (including two sources cited here [12] [19])
  describes the pre-2023 assessment centre; it remains useful for interview
  practice but does not describe the current competition phase.
- **Recruitment-interview practice varies by institution.** Official sources
  confirm that institutions interview from reserve lists and that recruitment
  choice is theirs [3] [9], but no single public document standardises how
  those interviews are structured or scored; statements here about their
  conduct are deliberately limited.
- **Private-sector coverage is directional.** The private-sector section
  states method-level facts from professional-body guidance [16], not claims
  about any named employer's process.
