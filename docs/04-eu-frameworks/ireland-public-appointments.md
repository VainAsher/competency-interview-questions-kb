# Executive Summary

This document maps how competency-based selection works in the Irish public
service, an area the knowledge base previously left blank despite Ireland being
an English-language EU member state that publishes its frameworks openly. It is
the first Ireland-specific document in the knowledge base and a deepening child
of `eu-frameworks-foundation`, which covers only the EU institutions and EPSO.

The central body is the **Public Appointments Service (PAS)**, which trades as
**publicjobs** and is the centralised recruiter for the Irish Civil Service and
much of the wider public service [1] [8]. The single most important currency
fact is that Ireland has **moved from a competency model to a "Capability
Framework"**: PAS designed a new Civil Service Capability Framework, launched in
**February 2024**, spanning every grade from Clerical Officer to Principal
Officer [3] [4] [5] [16]. It does not simply rename the old competencies — it widens the
lens to seven "capability dimensions" (behaviours, skills, strengths, knowledge,
values, motivation and interests) organised under four Capabilities [1] [4].
Crucially, **both systems are live**: PAS states plainly that "some roles may
utilise the Civil Service Competency Model and others may rely on the new
Capability Framework", and candidates are told in advance which applies [5].

Interviews are structured and evidence-based. PAS runs competency-based and
capability-based interviews (increasingly by automated or live video), and — a
gift for this knowledge base — **publishes its own example questions** for both
formats [5]. Selection is governed by the statutory regulator, the **Commission
for Public Service Appointments (CPSA)**, whose Code of Practice sets six
recruitment principles (probity, merit, best practice, consistency,
transparency, equality/diversity/inclusion) and mandates review and complaint
routes [9] [10] [14].

The document is rated **Medium** confidence. The core facts rest on primary PAS
(publicjobs), gov.ie, CPSA, Garda and HSE documents that were opened and read
directly. The
rating is held at Medium because the UK/EPSO comparison table is this knowledge
base's own labelled analysis (no publisher states it), and because some
sector-body detail — notably the specific competencies used in the Garda
interview — is issued to candidates privately and is a **verified negative** in
the public record rather than a documented fact.

# Key Takeaways

- The **Public Appointments Service (PAS / publicjobs)** is the centralised,
  independent recruiter for the Irish Civil Service and public service, and runs
  open competitions through stages: application, shortlisting, online tests, and
  interview [5] [8]. *(documented)*
- Ireland has a **new Civil Service Capability Framework, launched February
  2024**, covering Clerical Officer up to Principal Officer; it is broader than
  the old competency model, assessing seven capability dimensions — behaviours,
  skills, strengths, knowledge, values, motivation and interests. *(documented)*
  [1] [3] [4] [16]
- The **legacy Civil Service Competency Model has not been abolished** — PAS
  confirms some roles still use it and tells candidates in advance which model
  applies. Do not assume the Capability Framework governs your competition. [5]
  *(documented)*
- The four Capabilities are **Building Future Readiness, Evidence Informed
  Delivery, Leading and Empowering, and Communicating and Collaborating**, with
  sub-dimensions that grow with grade (Clerical Officer has no "Leading"
  capability; senior grades add strategy/reform and influencing sub-dimensions).
  *(documented)* [1] [2] [3]
- PAS **publishes example interview questions** for both competency-based and
  capability-based interviews, and recommends the **STAR** structure for
  competency answers. *(documented)* [5]
- **Sector bodies run their own competency selection**: An Garda Síochána (a
  publicjobs-run competency-based interview plus a role-play), the HSE
  (competency-based application and interview), and local authorities (a
  distinct competency set) each publish candidate guidance. *(documented)*
  [11] [12] [13]
- Selection is regulated by statute: the **Public Service Management
  (Recruitment and Appointments) Act 2004** created the CPSA, whose **Code of
  Practice** sets six recruitment principles and requires review/complaint
  procedures. *(documented)* [9] [10] [14]
- The comparison of Irish capabilities with UK Civil Service Behaviours and EPSO
  competencies in this document is **the knowledge base's own analysis**, not a
  published concordance. *(guidance)*

# Purpose

This document answers, for both audiences:

- **Candidates** targeting an Irish public-service competition (Civil Service,
  An Garda Síochána, HSE, local authority): which framework governs the role,
  what the named competencies/capabilities are, how the competition and
  interview are structured, and what PAS itself tells candidates to do.
- **Interviewers and HR** in or around the Irish public service, and readers
  comparing systems across markets: what the official framework and process
  documents actually say, how PAS structures and governs selection, and where
  the Irish scheme sits relative to the UK Civil Service and EPSO.

It exists because the knowledge base's EU coverage was **EPSO-only**; Ireland,
the largest English-language gap, appeared in no document. It goes deeper than
`eu-frameworks-foundation` rather than restating it.

# Definitions

Prefer the shared `GLOSSARY.md`; the following are used in the narrowed Irish
sense here.

- **PAS / publicjobs** — the Public Appointments Service, the independent,
  centralised recruiter for the Irish Civil Service and public service; its
  candidate portal and trading name is publicjobs.ie [1] [8].
- **Capability Framework** — the Civil Service framework launched February 2024
  that assesses seven capability dimensions (behaviours, skills, strengths,
  knowledge, values, motivation, interests) under four Capabilities [1] [4] [16].
- **Competency Model** — the legacy Civil Service framework of per-grade
  competencies with "effective performance indicators", still used for some
  roles [5] [6] [7].
- **Capability-Based Interview** — a structured interview that builds a profile
  across capability dimensions and sub-dimensions, exploring the "whole person"
  beyond past behaviour alone [5].
- **CPSA** — the Commission for Public Service Appointments, the statutory
  regulator of public-service recruitment created by the 2004 Act [10] [14].
- **Order of merit / panel** — successful candidates are placed on a panel in
  order of merit, from which vacancies are filled; a panel place is not a
  guarantee of appointment [8].
- **Grades** — the Civil Service general-service grades, ascending: Clerical
  Officer (CO), Executive Officer (EO), Administrative Officer (AO), Higher
  Executive Officer (HEO), Assistant Principal Officer (AP), Principal Officer
  (PO) [1] [6].

# Framework Context

**Evidence layer — every factual claim cited.**

## Two frameworks, both operative

PAS (publicjobs) committed to "review and update the Civil Service Competency
model to reflect current and anticipated knowledge, skills and behaviour needs,
for all roles from Clerical Officer to Principal Officer"; the resulting
**Civil Service Capability Framework** was developed by Work and Organisational
Psychologists in the PAS Assessment Services Unit and launched in **February
2024** [1] [4] [5] [16]. PAS is explicit that the two systems coexist: "some
roles may utilise the Civil Service Competency Model and others may rely on the
new Capability Framework. Candidates will be informed in advance, about whether
the Competency Model, Capability Framework, or another method/model will be
used" [5]. This document therefore treats **neither** as fully superseded.

## The Capability Framework (2024)

The Capability Framework widens assessment from competencies alone to **seven
capability dimensions**: Behaviours, Skills, Strengths, Knowledge, Values,
Motivation and Interests [1] [3]. These are organised under **four
Capabilities**, each carrying sub-dimensions whose depth increases by grade
[1] [2] [3]:

| Capability | What it covers, verified from the EO framework | Illustrative sub-dimensions across grades |
|-----------|--------------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------|
| **Building Future Readiness** | Embracing change, innovation, digital-first working and continual upskilling | Digital Focus; Openness to Change; Innovation & Creative Solutions; Upskilling for the Future; senior grades add Strategic Awareness & Change / Strategy, Change & Reform |
| **Evidence Informed Delivery** | Evidence-based judgement, prioritisation, resource management and quality outcomes | Delivering Excellence; Managing Work Effectively; Delivering Quality Outcomes & Service; Handling / Analysis, Judgement & Decision Making |
| **Leading and Empowering** | Leading, supporting, developing and empowering people, and leading with specialist insight | Leading, Supporting & Developing; Empowerment, Trust & Honesty; Social & Emotional Awareness; Leading with Specialist Insight |
| **Communicating and Collaborating** | Building relationships and communicating clearly across a whole-of-Government approach | Collaboration, Teamwork & Building Relationships; Effective Communication; Listening, Consulting & Engaging; senior grades split into Communicating & Influencing / Engaging & Collaborating |

Grade shape verified during research: **Clerical Officer** carries only three
Capabilities — Building Future Readiness, Evidence Informed Delivery, and
Communicating and Collaborating — and no "Leading" Capability; the **Executive
Officer** framework adds **Leading and Empowering** [1] [2] [3]. From HEO
upward, "Building Future Readiness" gains a strategy/change sub-dimension and
"Communicating and Collaborating" splits into "Communicating & Influencing" and
"Engaging & Collaborating"; at **Principal Officer** the leadership
sub-dimension is "Leading, Motivating & Inspiring" and delivery adds
"Accountability, Responsibility & Governance" [2]. The framework is published
per grade as an on-screen framework and a downloadable PDF plus an accessible
plaintext version [2] [3].

## The legacy Competency Model

The pre-2024 **Civil Service Competency Model** is a more conventional
competency dictionary: each grade has a fixed set of competencies, each expanded
into "effective performance indicators" [6] [7]. Two competencies are **constant
across every grade** — *Specialist Knowledge, Expertise and Self Development* and
*Drive & Commitment to Public Service Values* — with the remaining competencies
varying by grade [6] [7]. The following are verified from the PAS competency
model documents:

| Grade | Grade-specific competencies, legacy model |
|-------|--------------------------------------------|
| Executive Officer | People Management; Analysis & Decision Making; Delivery of Results; Interpersonal & Communication Skills [6] |
| Higher Executive Officer | Team Leadership; Judgement, Analysis & Decision Making; Management & Delivery of Results; Interpersonal & Communication Skills [7] |

Both grades then add the two constants above [6] [7]. Higher grades shift the
leadership competency upward — for example "Leadership & Strategic Direction" at
Principal Officer — while the delivery, decision-making and communication
competencies persist under grade-appropriate labels [6].

## Comparison with the UK and EPSO — knowledge base analysis

**The mapping below is this knowledge base's own analysis, offered as an
orientation aid. It is not a published concordance; no Irish, UK or EU publisher
states it.** It aligns the four 2024 Irish Capabilities with the UK Civil
Service Behaviours (see `uk-civil-service-success-profiles`) and the EPSO
general competencies (see `epso-competency-framework`).

| Irish Capability (2024) | Nearest UK Civil Service Behaviour(s) | Nearest EPSO competency(ies) |
|-------------------------|----------------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| Building Future Readiness | Changing and Improving; Seeing the Big Picture | Intrapreneurship; Information management; Learning as a skill |
| Evidence Informed Delivery | Making Effective Decisions; Delivering at Pace; Managing a Quality Service | Decision-making & getting results; Critical thinking, analysing & creative problem-solving |
| Leading and Empowering | Leadership; Developing Self and Others | EPSO has no standalone leadership competency; nearest is Intrapreneurship |
| Communicating and Collaborating | Communicating and Influencing; Working Together | Communication; Working together |

The structural family resemblance is real — all three are behaviour/competency
schemes assessed through structured, evidence-based interviewing — but the
Irish Capability Framework is distinctive in explicitly assessing **strengths,
motivation and interests** alongside behaviours, which the UK scheme handles
through a separate Strengths element and EPSO does not foreground [1] [4].

# Core Reference

**Evidence layer — every factual claim cited.**

## PAS and how a Civil Service competition is structured

PAS conducts the selection process and "will establish a panel of suitably
qualified individuals to fill vacancies" [8]. A large-volume Civil Service
competition — the 2022 Executive Officer campaign is the worked example — may
comprise, in sequence: computer- and/or paper-based **assessment tests**;
**shortlisting**; an **online video interview**; where relevant **language
tests**; **competitive interview(s)**; and possibly a **presentation/analysis
exercise** or **work-sample test** [8]. Candidates who succeed are placed on a
**panel in order of merit**, from which appointments are made; PAS states
plainly that "qualification and placement on a panel is not a guarantee of
appointment", and candidates may not be reached before the panel expires [8].
The Executive Officer grade is defined as "the entry level to junior management
in the Civil Service", recruited for potential to take on higher responsibility
[8].

## How the interview runs

PAS interviews are structured and take three delivery forms [5]:

- **Automated video interview** — no live panel; questions appear on screen and
  the candidate records responses, which "are later reviewed by our interview
  board members" [5].
- **Live video interview** — a video conference with an interview panel,
  functionally equivalent to a face-to-face interview [5].
- **In-person interview** — a panel of interviewers for some roles [5].

PAS describes its interviews as following "a structured format, with candidates
being questioned and assessed on the competencies or capabilities relevant to
the role. This ensures that all candidates are assessed equally and
transparently" [5].

For **competency-based interviews**, PAS tells candidates the board probes each
example further, and recommends structuring answers with the **STAR** model —
Situation, Task, Action, Result — with PAS's own gloss on each element [5]. For
**capability-based interviews**, PAS explains the aim is "to create a two-way
dialogue that gives insight into the whole person and assesses candidates on
their capability, suitability and readiness for the role", with the board
establishing "a profile and understanding of the candidates' capability across
relevant Capability Dimensions and Sub-Dimensions" [5]. Candidates are told the
capability interview also explores "skills, strengths, knowledge, values,
motivation and interests" — not past behaviour alone [5].

## Sector bodies with their own published selection material

- **An Garda Síochána (national police).** Recruitment of Garda Trainees is run
  in stages: application and selection are "carried out by publicjobs", with
  appointment and training by An Garda Síochána [11]. **Stage 2 is a
  "Competency Based Interview carried out via the Zoom platform"** conducted by
  publicjobs [11]. The official 2025 information booklet documents the process
  and reasonable-accommodation support but **does not list the specific
  competencies assessed** — these are notified to candidates via their
  publicjobs message board before interview [11]. (Third-party preparation
  providers name five — interpersonal skills, service ethos, resilience,
  conscientiousness and problem solving — but this is practitioner material, not
  an official PAS/Garda publication, and is treated here as unverified: see
  Risks & Caveats.)
- **Health Service Executive (HSE).** HSE recruitment is competency-based. Its
  candidate guidance instructs applicants to "describe a situation from your own
  experience" demonstrating each skill, base answers on different situations,
  **not use the same example for more than two competency areas**, and keep each
  answer to a maximum of one A4 page because "the selection board will assess
  your written communication skills on what you write" [12]. At interview, "the
  board may look for more examples of how you demonstrate the skills or
  competencies required" [12]. The guidance stresses that the board will not
  assume competence from job title or length of service alone — evidence of what
  you actually did is required [12].
- **Local authorities.** Local-authority competitions publish a distinct
  competency set. A current Clerical Officer candidate booklet names, for
  example, **Customer Service & Communication Skills, Teamwork, and Information
  Management / Organisational Skills**, and further competencies, with candidates
  required to "demonstrate sufficient evidence within their application form of
  competence under each of these"; shortlisting and interview are based on that
  evidence [13]. The competency labels differ from the Civil Service model,
  confirming that Irish local government maintains its own scheme [13].

## Building Future Readiness — practice

1. Tell us about a time you adopted a new technology or digital tool to improve how work was done. What efficiency did it deliver? *(composed in the style of the Building Future Readiness capability, Digital Focus)* [3]
2. Describe an occasion when you tried a new approach and it did not work first time. What did you learn and share? *(composed in the style of Openness to Change)* [3]
3. Give an example of a small innovation or improvement you put forward, no matter how minor. *(composed in the style of Innovation & Creative Solutions)* [3]
4. Tell us about how you have kept your skills current for the demands of your role. *(composed in the style of Upskilling for the Future)* [3]

## Evidence Informed Delivery — practice

1. Describe a time you planned and prioritised a heavy workload to hit competing deadlines. *(composed in the style of Managing Work Effectively)* [3]
2. Give an example of a decision you made from incomplete or conflicting information, and the evidence you relied on. *(composed in the style of Informed Judgement & Decision Making)* [3]
3. Tell us about a time you maintained quality and accuracy on a routine task under time pressure. *(composed in the style of Delivering Quality Outcomes & Service)* [3]
4. Describe how you took ownership of a task that had stalled and saw it through. *(composed in the style of the legacy Delivery of Results competency)* [6]

## Leading and Empowering — practice

1. Tell us about a time you got the best out of a team member who was underperforming. *(composed in the style of the legacy People Management competency)* [6]
2. Describe how you developed or coached a colleague. What changed as a result? *(composed in the style of Leading, Supporting & Developing)* [3]
3. Give an example of when you delegated work and showed trust in a colleague. *(composed in the style of Empowerment, Trust & Honesty)* [3]
4. Tell us about a challenging situation where you managed your own emotions and supported others' wellbeing. *(composed in the style of Social & Emotional Awareness)* [3]

## Communicating and Collaborating — practice

1. Describe a time you adapted your communication style to a difficult audience. *(composed in the style of Effective Communication)* [3]
2. Tell us about a situation where you built a working relationship across teams to get something delivered. *(composed in the style of Collaboration, Teamwork & Building Relationships)* [3]
3. Give an example of when you had to influence or negotiate to reach an outcome. *(composed in the style of the senior-grade Influencing & Negotiation sub-dimension)* [3]
4. Describe how you listened to and acted on feedback or stakeholder concerns. *(composed in the style of Listening, Consulting & Engaging)* [3]

## Frontline and sector-body practice

1. Tell us about a time you dealt with a distressed or difficult member of the public and how you responded. *(composed for An Garda Síochána-style frontline assessment)* [11]
2. Describe a situation that tested your resilience. How did you keep going? *(composed for a policing or frontline competency interview)* [11]
3. Give an example, from your own experience, of when you delivered excellent customer service under pressure. *(composed in the style of the HSE and local-authority customer-service competency)* [12] [13]
4. Describe a time you shared information and worked as part of a team to meet a shared objective. *(composed in the style of the local-authority Teamwork competency)* [13]

## Public-service values and specialist knowledge

1. What draws you to a career in the public service, and how do you demonstrate commitment to it? *(composed in the style of Drive & Commitment to Public Service Values)* [6]
2. Tell us about how you have built and applied specialist expertise in your area, and shared it with others. *(composed in the style of Specialist Knowledge, Expertise and Self Development)* [6] [7]
3. Describe a situation where you upheld honesty, ethics or integrity under pressure. *(composed in the style of Drive & Commitment to Public Service Values)* [7]

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — guidance, not documented fact. It points back to the cited
evidence and adds no new facts.**

- **First, find out which framework applies.** PAS notifies candidates in
  advance whether a competition uses the Competency Model, the Capability
  Framework, or another method [5]. Confirm this before you prepare — the two
  demand different preparation, as below.
- **For a competency-based interview, use STAR — PAS says so.** PAS explicitly
  recommends Situation, Task, Action, Result and gives its own prompts:
  *when and where*, *what you were expected to do*, *who else was involved*,
  *what you did*, then *how it turned out and how you measured it* [5]. Keep the
  Situation and Task brief and spend most words on your own Action and the
  Result — this mirrors PAS's own emphasis on "what YOU did" [5].
- **For a capability-based interview, prepare more than stories.** PAS says the
  board explores the "whole person" — skills, strengths, knowledge, values,
  motivation and interests [5]. For each relevant Capability, prepare not only a
  behavioural example but also: which key skills you hold, what value your
  specialist knowledge adds, what motivates you, and where your development gaps
  are [5]. PAS's published questions ask about knowledge gaps and motivation
  directly — see the Question Bank — so rehearse honest, self-aware answers, not
  just success stories.
- **Map examples to the sub-dimensions of your grade.** Download the Capability
  Framework for the exact grade [2] [3] and, for each sub-dimension, have at
  least one example ready. Senior grades (HEO and above) will probe strategy,
  reform and influencing — junior grades will not [2] [3].
- **Worked answer skeleton (competency-based, "worked under pressure", a
  Question Bank item).**
  - *Situation/Task (brief):* the deadline or pressure and your specific
    responsibility.
  - *Action (the bulk):* how you prioritised, what you personally did, how you
    kept quality up, who you kept informed — evidence-led, first person.
  - *Result:* the measurable outcome, and what you learned — PAS asks "how did
    you know it went well? how did you measure this?" [5], so quantify.
- **For HSE and local-authority written stages, respect the format rules.** HSE
  caps each competency answer at one A4 page and warns against reusing an
  example for more than two competencies [12]; the written form itself is
  assessed for communication [12]. Plan your examples across competencies so you
  do not double-spend a strong story.

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — every factual claim cited.**

- **Structured and equalised by design.** PAS states its interviews follow a
  structured format so "all candidates are assessed equally and transparently",
  questioning candidates on the competencies or capabilities relevant to the
  role [5]. In automated video interviews the recorded responses are reviewed
  afterwards by "interview board members" [5].
- **Evidence over inference.** HSE assessor-facing guidance is explicit that the
  review team "will not assume you demonstrate a skill only because of your
  current role, length of experience or educational qualifications" and relies
  on the detail the candidate provides about "what you actually did and how you
  did it" [12]. This is a documented articulation of the evidence principle that
  underlies competency scoring.
- **Written communication is itself scored.** For competency application forms,
  HSE tells candidates "the selection board will assess your written
  communication skills on what you write on your application form", and imposes a
  one-A4-page limit the board "will take into account" [12].
- **Order of merit and panels.** PAS places successful candidates on a panel in
  order of merit and fills vacancies from it; a place is not an offer, and the
  panel may expire before a candidate is reached [8]. This is the Irish
  equivalent of a reserve list.
- **Governed selection.** Under the CPSA Code of Practice, a public body must run
  a fair, transparent and merit-based selection process; appointment on merit
  means "the appointment of the best person to any given post … through a
  transparent, competitive recruitment process", with selection criteria
  "related directly to the qualifications, personal attributes and skills
  required" of the post [9]. The Code sets **six recruitment principles**:
  probity, merit, best practice, consistency, transparency, and appointments
  promoting equality, diversity and inclusion [9]. Review and complaint
  procedures are mandatory [9] [10].
- **What is not published.** No PAS document located in this pass publishes a
  numerical marking scale or per-competency scoring descriptors for interviews,
  unlike, say, the UK Success Profiles 7-point scale. PAS provides departments
  with "draft interview and scoring templates" as internal client tools [16], but
  these are not public. Treat any specific Irish interview scoring rubric in
  circulation as unverified.

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — analysis, clearly labelled. Not cited fact.**

- **Assuming the framework.** The commonest error is preparing for a
  capability-based interview when the role uses the legacy Competency Model, or
  vice versa. PAS tells you which applies in advance [5] — read the message
  board and information booklet.
- **Treating a capability interview like a competency interview.** Bringing only
  STAR stories to a capability-based interview under-answers it: PAS also probes
  strengths, knowledge gaps, values, motivation and interests [5]. Candidates
  who cannot articulate a development gap or what motivates them lose ground.
- **Reusing one strong story everywhere.** HSE explicitly limits an example to
  at most two competency areas [12]; recycling a single achievement signals thin
  evidence to any Irish public-service board.
- **"We" instead of "I".** PAS's own prompts foreground "what YOU did" and how
  you personally measured success [5]; boards cannot credit a contribution you
  do not claim clearly.
- **Ignoring the one-page limit or format rules.** On HSE and local-authority
  forms, overrunning the page limit or writing vaguely costs marks because the
  written form is itself assessed [12] [13].
- **Confusing composed practice questions with real ones.** The *(composed)*
  questions above are stylistic imitations built from the cited descriptors —
  presenting them, or any coach's list, as "real PAS questions" misleads.
  PAS's genuinely published examples are only those labelled *(sourced)* [5].

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.**

1. **Framework check (10 min).** Before anything else, confirm from your
   competition's information booklet and publicjobs message board whether the
   role uses the Capability Framework or the Competency Model [5]. Write it at
   the top of your prep notes.
2. **Sub-dimension mapping (60–90 min).** Download the Capability Framework PDF
   for your exact grade [2] [3]. For each sub-dimension, draft one STAR example
   and note the skills, strengths and knowledge you would cite; flag any
   sub-dimension where you have no example — that is your gap to close.
3. **PAS-question drill (45 min).** Take the twelve published capability
   questions above [5]. Answer each aloud in under two minutes, then self-check:
   did you give a specific example *and* address the skill/strength/value the
   question targets?
4. **Whole-person rehearsal (30 min).** Practise the three PAS questions that are
   not behavioural — knowledge gaps, what motivates you, why evidence matters
   [5]. These trip up candidates who only rehearse success stories.
5. **Written-form discipline (for HSE / local authority).** Write one competency
   answer to the exact one-A4-page limit; check you have not reused the example
   elsewhere and that a stranger could tell exactly what *you* did [12] [13].
6. **Mock board (half day, for panels).** Using the four Capabilities and their
   grade sub-dimensions [3], build a five-question structured interview with an
   agreed evidence checklist per question; run it, and compare notes before
   discussing to reduce single-assessor drift.

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — every factual claim cited. Signposts only; not legal advice.**

- **Statutory basis of selection.** The **Public Service Management (Recruitment
  and Appointments) Act 2004** established the Public Appointments Service and
  the Commission for Public Service Appointments, and underpins the licensing and
  regulation of public-service recruitment in Ireland [14].
- **Regulator's Code of Practice.** The CPSA Code of Practice requires
  appointments to be made fairly, transparently and on the basis of merit, built
  on six recruitment principles, with mandatory review and complaint procedures
  for candidates dissatisfied with a process [9] [10]. Selection criteria must
  relate directly to the requirements of the post [9].
- **Equality law.** Irish recruitment is subject to the **Employment Equality
  Act 1998** (as amended), which prohibits discrimination in employment —
  including in the arrangements for deciding who should be offered employment —
  across nine grounds: gender, civil status, family status, sexual orientation,
  religion, age, disability, race, and membership of the Traveller community
  [15]. Interview design, questioning and scoring fall within its scope.
- **Reasonable accommodations.** PAS states it is "committed to positively
  supporting candidates with disabilities throughout the selection process" and
  invites candidates to request accommodations via the recruitment unit or its
  Disability Champion [5]; the Garda Trainee process gives the same undertaking
  [11]. The duty to make reasonable accommodation for disabled applicants is a
  requirement of the Employment Equality Acts [15].
- **Candidate data.** As an EU member state, Ireland processes candidate
  personal data under the GDPR; PAS manages applications and communications
  through the publicjobs message-board account [5] [8]. For the EU data regime
  see `best-practice-foundation`.

# Risks & Caveats

- **Two live frameworks — high confusion risk.** Because the Competency Model and
  the Capability Framework coexist and are chosen per role [5], any general
  advice — including this document — can mislead if the reader does not confirm
  which governs their competition.
- **Transition is recent and ongoing.** The Capability Framework was launched
  February 2024 and, per gov.ie, was being rolled out across large-volume
  campaigns with a Principal Officer pilot [4] [5] [16]; grade-level detail and
  templates may still evolve. Verified as published on 2026-07-20.
- **Garda competencies are a verified negative in the public record.** The
  official 2025 booklet documents a publicjobs-run competency interview and
  role-play but does **not** publish the specific competencies [11]; the widely
  cited "five competencies" come only from third-party training providers and
  are treated here as unverified per the source-priority rule — practitioner
  material is never the sole support for a process claim.
- **No public interview marking scale.** Unlike the UK Success Profiles 7-point
  scale, no PAS numerical interview scoring rubric or per-competency descriptor
  set was located publicly; scoring templates exist but are internal client
  tools [4]. Do not import a UK or EPSO scale and imply it is Irish.
- **Sector coverage is uneven.** HSE and local-authority coverage rests on a
  candidate-guidance page and one representative local-authority booklet
  [12] [13]; practice varies by employer and competition. The Defence Forces
  were not covered for want of a located primary framework.
- **The UK/EPSO comparison is inference.** The mapping table is the knowledge
  base's analysis, not a concordance any body publishes; it caps the document at
  Medium confidence.
