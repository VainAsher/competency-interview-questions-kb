# Executive Summary

This is the practice document for candidates facing a **Civil Service behaviour
interview**, and for panels designing one. It is organised the way the
interview itself is organised: one section per **Behaviour**, all nine of them,
each opening with the official GOV.UK definition [1] [2], followed by a note on
how the published level descriptors escalate from AA/AO up to Director General
[2], then a set of practice questions carrying explicit provenance. Sixty-six
questions in total, plus a section of follow-up and probing prompts.

The single most under-served skill in Civil Service interview preparation is
**grade calibration**. The Behaviours are not nine separate things — they are
nine things defined *six different ways*, once for each grade band, and the
official framework is explicit that "Civil Service Behaviours are specific to
the grade level of the job role" [2]. The same true story about the same
project can score well at HEO and poorly at Grade 7, not because the candidate
is weaker but because the evidence sits at the wrong altitude. This document
therefore devotes its coaching layer to reading the descriptor for your target
band and lifting an answer to it, with a worked skeleton showing one example
pitched twice.

Confidence is **Medium**. The evidence layer is strong: the behaviour
definitions, the six-level descriptors, the assessment methods, the
four-behaviour recommendation and — following a correction in this edition —
the **1–7 scoring scale for behaviours** all rest on official Civil Service
documents [1] [2] [5] [6] [7] [17] [18] [19]. The scale is now confirmed
central, not departmental: Cabinet Office guidance states in identical words at
three grade tiers that "Experience, technical skills and behaviours should be
scored using the 7 point scale" [17] [18] [19]. What keeps the rating at Medium
is the question bank rather than the framework: the Civil Service publishes
behaviour descriptors but **no official question bank** [1] [2], so every
*(sourced)* question here comes from a careers service or an HR consultancy,
and their grouping under the nine Behaviours is this document's editorial
mapping — synthesis, not documented fact. Two smaller caveats survive: the
central scoring packs are dated 2020, and the seven rating labels themselves are
published departmentally [8] rather than centrally.

# Key Takeaways

- Behaviours are defined at **six grade bands** — Level 1 AA/AO, Level 2 EO,
  Level 3 HEO/SEO, Level 4 Grade 7/6, Level 5 Deputy Director, Level 6
  Director/Director General — and are "specific to the grade level of the job
  role". *(documented)* [2]
- Civil Service HR guidance to vacancy holders recommends that **no more than
  four behaviours** are prioritised as critical to a role, so the panel can
  test them properly at interview — identical wording appears in the EO,
  HEO/SEO and Grade 6/7 guides. *(documented)* [5] [6] [7]
- The job advert tells you which behaviours are assessed; you will **not** be
  asked to demonstrate all nine for one role. *(documented)* [2] [3]
- Behaviours can be assessed by past-behaviour questions ("give examples of
  when you have demonstrated…"), by **situational** questions ("how would you
  behave in a given situation"), or in real time through a structured
  behavioural assessment — so prepare for both tenses. *(documented)* [2]
- Behaviours are scored on a **1–7 scale** across the Civil Service: Cabinet
  Office guidance instructs, in identical words at three grade tiers, that
  "Experience, technical skills and behaviours should be scored using the 7
  point scale. Strengths should be scored using the 4 point scale."
  *(documented)* [17] [18] [19]
- Benchmarks sit on top of the scale and vary by campaign; COPFS normally
  requires **4 or more in all behaviours**, and at sift the progression
  benchmark "can be flexed in accordance with the quality of applications and
  the number of roles available". *(documented)* [8] [17]
- Each panellist scores each question **independently first**, and scores are
  reconciled afterwards by arithmetic average into a merit list.
  *(documented)* [17]
- The escalation pattern across bands is consistent and learnable: from *doing
  the task* → *organising the team* → *shaping the system* → *setting
  direction*. Read the descriptor for your target band before you choose which
  story to tell. *(guidance)*
- The Civil Service publishes behaviour **descriptors**, not an official
  behaviour **question bank** — every question in circulation, including the
  sourced ones below, comes from a third party. Treat wording as illustrative.
  *(documented)* [1] [2]
- Recruitment is governed by the Civil Service Commission's Recruitment
  Principles: selection on **merit** on the basis of **fair and open
  competition**, which is why structured, criteria-scored interviewing is the
  norm. *(documented)* [9]

# Purpose

This document answers one practical question for a **candidate**: *given the
behaviours named in my job advert and the grade I am applying for, what will I
be asked and how good does an answer have to be?* It gives practice material
organised behaviour by behaviour, and it teaches the calibration move — pitching
evidence at the right grade band — that the official descriptors demand but no
official guidance explains.

For an **interviewer or panel chair**, it answers: *what does a defensible
behaviour question set look like, and against what documented standard do I
score it?* The Assessor Perspective section records the published scales and
benchmarks rather than inventing one.

It is deliberately not a Success Profiles explainer. Read
`uk-civil-service-success-profiles` for how the five elements fit together and
`uk-frameworks-foundation` for where the Civil Service sits in the UK
landscape; this document assumes you already know a behaviour interview is
coming and want to prepare for it.

# Scope

**Covered:** all nine Civil Service Behaviours, each with its official
definition, an escalation note across the six published grade bands, and
practice questions; a section of follow-up/probing prompts; grade-calibration
coaching with a worked answer skeleton pitched at two grades; published scoring
scales and benchmarks; the fairness obligations that constrain question design.

**Not covered:** the other four Success Profile elements — Strengths, Ability,
Experience and Technical — which are summarised in
`uk-civil-service-success-profiles` (a small number of strengths-style prompts
appear only where the contrast with behaviours is instructive); the mechanics
of STAR and its variants, which belong to `techniques-foundation`; the
framework-agnostic competency families such as teamwork or communication in
general, which are the tier-2 documents listed under Related Documents. This
document is Civil-Service-specific by design and does not restate them.

**Not covered either:** devolved administrations and public bodies that have
adapted Success Profiles locally; the Fast Stream's own assessment sequence;
and Northern Ireland Civil Service recruitment, which uses a separate
competency framework.

# Definitions

Prefer the shared `GLOSSARY.md`. Used in this document's narrowed sense:

- **Behaviour** — "the actions and activities that people do which result in
  effective performance in a job", as defined by the Civil Service [2] [3].
- **Level** — one of the six published bands at which every behaviour is
  described: Level 1 (AA/AO), Level 2 (EO), Level 3 (HEO/SEO), Level 4
  (Grade 7 and 6), Level 5 (Deputy Director), Level 6 (Director and Director
  General) [2].
- **Descriptor** — the paragraph of example behaviours published for one
  behaviour at one level, introduced on the official page as "Examples of
  behaviours at this level are" [2].
- **Grade calibration** *(this document's term)* — choosing and framing
  evidence so that its scope, autonomy and time horizon match the descriptor
  for the band you are applying to, rather than the band you currently occupy.
- **Situational (behaviour) question** — a question asking "how you would
  behave in a given situation" rather than for a past example; the official
  framework lists this as a legitimate way to assess a behaviour [2].
- **Benchmark** — the minimum score a candidate must reach on each assessed
  behaviour to be appointable. The **scale** is central — behaviours are scored
  on the 7-point scale [17] [18] [19] — while the **benchmark** on top of it
  varies by campaign; COPFS normally requires 4 or more [8].

# Framework Context

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

The Civil Service assesses recruits against Success Profiles, a framework of
five elements — Behaviours, Strengths, Ability, Experience and Technical — of
which not all are relevant to every role [2] [4]. Behaviours are the element
this document addresses. The framework explains its own purpose: it "moves
recruitment away from using a purely competency based system of assessment"
towards a more flexible mix of elements and selection methods [2].

Nine behaviours are defined. Their full official definitions, as published in
the Civil Service Behaviours framework document, are longer than the one-line
summaries usually quoted [2]:

| # | Behaviour | Official definition |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Seeing the Big Picture | "Understand how your role fits with and supports organisational objectives. Recognise the wider Civil Service priorities and ensure work is in the national interest." [2] |
| 2 | Changing and Improving | "Seek out opportunities to create effective change and suggest innovative ideas for improvement. Review ways of working, including seeking and providing feedback." [2] |
| 3 | Making Effective Decisions | "Use evidence and knowledge to support accurate, expert decisions and advice. Carefully consider alternative options, implications and risks of decisions." [2] |
| 4 | Leadership | "Show pride and passion for public service. Create and engage others in delivering a shared vision. Value difference, diversity and inclusion, ensuring fairness and opportunity for all." [2] |
| 5 | Communicating and Influencing | "Communicate purpose and direction with clarity, integrity and enthusiasm. Respect the needs, responses and opinions of others." [2] |
| 6 | Working Together | "Form effective partnerships and relationships with people both internally and externally, from a range of diverse backgrounds, sharing information, resources and support." [2] |
| 7 | Developing Self and Others | "Focus on continuous learning and development for self, others and the organisation as a whole." [2] |
| 8 | Managing a Quality Service | "Deliver service objectives with professional excellence, expertise and efficiency, taking account of diverse customer needs." [2] |
| 9 | Delivering at Pace | "Take responsibility for delivering timely and quality results with focus and drive." [2] |

Each behaviour is then described separately at six levels, mapped to grade
bands as follows [2]:

| Level | Grade band |
|---|---|
| Level 1 | AA and AO or equivalent |
| Level 2 | EO or equivalent |
| Level 3 | HEO and SEO or equivalent |
| Level 4 | Grade 7 and 6 or equivalent |
| Level 5 | Deputy Director or equivalent |
| Level 6 | Director and Director General |

Two framework facts shape everything a candidate should do. First, "Civil
Service Behaviours are specific to the grade level of the job role", and
"recruiting managers will choose a selection of behaviours which are best
suited to the specific job role" — "You will not be asked to demonstrate all
Civil Service Behaviours for one role" [2]. Second, behaviours may be assessed
at many points and by many methods: application form, CV, Civil Service
Judgement Test, interview, presentation, in-tray exercise, written
analysis/exercise, personality test, job related simulation, group
exercise/discussion, role-play, oral briefing and assessment centre [2].

The interview is therefore only one of the places a behaviour is tested, and
the questions in this document rehearse one method among several.

# Core Reference

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## How many behaviours you will face

Civil Service HR's guidance to vacancy holders and specialist recruitment teams
states: "As a guide, it is recommended that no more than four behaviours are
prioritised as critical to the role. This will allow the panel to sufficiently
test the behaviours at interview stage, along with the selected strengths and a
candidate's experience" [5]. The identical recommendation appears in the
Executive Officer and SEO/HEO editions of the same guide series, so the
four-behaviour ceiling is a cross-grade norm rather than a senior-grade one
[6] [7]. The same guidance sets a scoping benchmark for recruiters: "if an
aspect of the role can be learnt on the job within six months this should not
be assessed at the recruitment stage" [5].

Cabinet Office guidance covering all delegated grades (EO–Grade 6) repeats the
recommendation in the same terms — "no more than four behaviours are prioritised
as critical to the role. This will allow the panel to sufficiently test the
criteria at interview stage" — which makes the four-behaviour ceiling central
guidance rather than a job-family quirk [17].

For grades from EO up to Grade 6, the guides record that most roles test
technical, experience, behaviours and strengths, with ability tested at the
vacancy holder's discretion [5] [6] [7] [17].

## Behaviours are graded; strengths are not

The contrast is worth holding onto, because it explains why grade calibration
applies to only part of the interview. The Civil Service Strengths Dictionary
defines 36 strengths and states plainly that "Civil Service Strengths are not
defined by grade" [20]; behaviours, by contrast, are "specific to the grade
level of the job role" [2]. The Dictionary also publishes an official mapping
of strengths to "the most relevant Civil Service Behaviour(s)", while noting
that "any strength can be assessed if suitable for the role" [20] — so the
strengths portion of a blended interview can arrive attached to a behaviour you
were expecting, but pitched at no particular level.

The preparation consequence is opposite in each case. For behaviours, the
Dictionary's sibling guidance tells candidates to prepare examples [2]; for
strengths it says the reverse — "Do not rehearse your answers because we are
looking for your initial response" [20]. Recruiters are correspondingly advised
not to name the strengths being assessed in adverts or candidate packs [5] [17].

## Both tenses are legitimate

The framework is explicit that a behaviour can be probed two ways. Candidates
"may be asked to give examples of when you have demonstrated a particular
behaviour" — and the example need not be from paid work: "This might be at work
or somewhere else such as work experience, volunteering or in connection with a
hobby" [2]. Alternatively candidates "may be asked how you would behave in a
given situation, for example by using Situational Judgement Tests or through
situational interview questions", or asked "to demonstrate a behaviour in
real-time, for example during a structured behavioural assessment" [2].

Departmental candidate guidance describes the interview in the same terms: DWP
Digital tells applicants "The panel will ask you four to six questions on your
skills, knowledge and experience, based on the topics listed under 'essential
criteria' in the advert", and that "you are scored on the essential criteria.
There are no trick questions" [11]. Defra's digital blog notes that behaviour
questions "may start with 'Tell me about a time when…'" and that assessors "may
ask you follow-up questions from your example" [12].

## How the level descriptors escalate

The published descriptors are not the same sentence at increasing volume; they
change what is being described. Read end to end, four shifts recur across all
nine behaviours [2]:

1. **Scope** — from your own responsibilities, to your team's, to your
   directorate's, to the department's place in government. *Seeing the Big
   Picture* moves from "Understand what your responsibilities are and how these
   contribute to the priorities of your team" (Level 1) to "Understand the
   strategic drivers for your area of work" (Level 3) to "Adopt a
   government-wide perspective to ensure alignment of activity and policy"
   (Level 4) to "Shape the Department's role and purpose in delivering civil
   service priorities which are in the national interest" (Level 6) [2].
2. **Agency** — from doing, to enabling, to creating the conditions. *Changing
   and Improving* runs from "Review ways of working and suggest improvements"
   (Level 1), through "Work with others to identify areas for improvement and
   simplify processes" (Level 3), to "Create an environment where people feel
   safe to challenge and know their voice will be heard" (Level 4) and
   "Proactively champion and lead change" (Level 6) [2].
3. **Tolerance of ambiguity and unpopularity** — *Making Effective Decisions*
   moves from "Use guidance, analyse relevant information and ask colleagues
   for input" (Level 1) to "Display confidence when making difficult decisions,
   even if they prove to be unpopular" (Level 3) to "Make decisions confidently
   even when details are unclear" (Level 4) to "be able to make unpopular
   decisions and defend them at the highest level" (Level 6) [2].
4. **Time horizon** — *Delivering at Pace* begins with "Always work with focus
   and pace to get the job done on time and to a high standard" (Level 1) and
   ends with "Set and maintain a clear direction for the Department, with
   highly focused priorities and project and programme outcomes" (Level 6) [2].

The same movement is visible in the behaviours about people. *Leadership* at
Level 1 is "Show enthusiasm for your work and take personal accountability for
your role"; at Level 2 "Show pride and passion for your work and positive,
inclusive engagement with your team"; at Level 3 "Ensure colleagues and
stakeholders have a clear understanding of objectives, activities and
time-frames"; at Level 4 "Inspire and motivate teams to be fully engaged in
their work"; at Level 5 "Work to influence the strategy, direction and culture
to increase effectiveness"; at Level 6 "Actively develop and protect the
reputation of the Civil Service" [2]. *Working Together* likewise runs from
"Proactively contribute to the work of the whole team" (Level 1) through
"Encourage joined up team work within own team and across other groups"
(Level 3) and "Actively build and maintain a network of colleagues and contacts
to achieve progress on shared objectives" (Level 4) to "Confront issues and
challenge assumptions at the highest levels, no matter how uncomfortable"
(Level 6) [2].

Note also what does **not** escalate. Inclusion, wellbeing and the
unacceptability of bullying, harassment and discrimination appear in the
descriptors at *every* level, from "Understand that bullying, harassment and
discrimination are unacceptable" at Level 1 to "Create a culture where bullying,
harassment and discrimination are unacceptable" at Level 6 [2]. Accessibility
and the "diverse range of end users" recur similarly across *Changing and
Improving* and *Managing a Quality Service* at every band [2]. These are
constants of the framework, not seniority markers.

## What the Civil Service does not publish

GOV.UK publishes the behaviours, their definitions and their level descriptors,
and tells candidates to "think about examples you can give of times when you
have shown the behaviours outlined in the job advert" using the STAR approach
[1] [3]. It does **not** publish an official bank of behaviour interview
questions. Every question set in circulation — including the *(sourced)* items
below — originates with a third party. The Civil Service blog's advice to
candidates is to "Research common Civil Service interview questions for the
grade and similar roles", which is an acknowledgement that the question wording
itself is not centrally issued [10].

# Question Bank

Sixty-six questions. Provenance per the house rule: *(sourced …)* questions were
published by the named source and carry its citation; *(composed)* questions
were written for this knowledge base in the style of the cited behaviour
descriptors [2] and are **not** real, leaked or official Civil Service
questions. No question here is attributed to the Civil Service.

Each sub-section opens with the official definition and a one-paragraph note on
how the descriptors escalate, both cited to the framework [2].

## Seeing the Big Picture

*Definition:* "Understand how your role fits with and supports organisational
objectives. Recognise the wider Civil Service priorities and ensure work is in
the national interest." [2]

*How it escalates:* at Level 1 the descriptor asks you to understand your own
responsibilities and how they contribute to team priorities; by Level 3 to
"Understand the strategic drivers for your area of work" and remain "alert to
emerging issues and trends"; by Level 4 to maintain an understanding of
"economic, social, political, environmental and technological developments" and
adopt a government-wide perspective; by Level 5 to "Anticipate the long-term
impact on the Department… at both national and international levels" [2].

1. Please can you tell us about a time when you worked in partnership with another team? *(sourced: Clover HR, "Competency interview questions for Civil Service behaviours")* [24]
2. I'd be interested to hear about a time when you created a new approach to an issue or started something new. *(sourced: University of Oxford Careers Service, "Example interview questions", May 2016)* [25]
3. Tell me about a time when you changed how you did your own work because you understood something about your organisation's wider priorities. *(composed)*
4. Describe a time when you had to explain to someone outside your team why a piece of work mattered beyond your own objectives. *(composed)*
5. Give an example of when you spotted an emerging issue or trend before it affected your work area. What did you do with it? *(composed)*
6. Tell us about a piece of work where you had to weigh the needs of several different groups of end users. How did you take account of them? *(composed)*

## Changing and Improving

*Definition:* "Seek out opportunities to create effective change and suggest
innovative ideas for improvement. Review ways of working, including seeking and
providing feedback." [2]

*How it escalates:* Level 1 asks you to "Review ways of working and suggest
improvements" and "Learn new procedures and help colleagues to do the same";
Level 2 adds encouraging others and acting on the effects of change; Level 3
adds simplifying processes, taking "managed risks", and creating an environment
where colleagues "can challenge decisions and issues safely"; Level 4 adds
articulating the business benefit of change and providing "constructive
challenge to senior management on change proposals"; Levels 5–6 move to leading
transformation and creating "a culture of innovation, flexibility and
responsiveness" [2].

1. Can you tell us about a situation when you had to deal with change? *(sourced: Clover HR, "Competency interview questions for Civil Service behaviours")* [24]
2. Tell me about a time when you were part of a project and you needed to change something — tell me what you did. *(sourced: University of Oxford Careers Service, "Example interview questions", May 2016)* [25]
3. Tell me about a situation where you had to adjust quickly to changes over which you had no control. What strategies did you use to handle the situation? *(sourced: University of Bath, "Behavioural questions for interviewing")* [23]
4. Describe an improvement you suggested that was not adopted. What did you do next? *(composed)*
5. Give an example of when you used a new technology or digital tool to make a process work better. How did you make sure it was accessible to everyone who needed it? *(composed)*
6. Tell us about a time you had to help colleagues who were unhappy about a change you did not design. *(composed)*

## Making Effective Decisions

*Definition:* "Use evidence and knowledge to support accurate, expert decisions
and advice. Carefully consider alternative options, implications and risks of
decisions." [2]

*How it escalates:* Level 1 is about using guidance, analysing relevant
information, spotting "errors or gaps in information" and asking for input;
Level 3 introduces empowering others to decide and "Display confidence when
making difficult decisions, even if they prove to be unpopular"; Level 4 adds
presenting "reasonable conclusions from a wide range of complex and sometimes
incomplete evidence"; Level 6 adds giving "unbiased evidence-led advice to
Ministers and other senior officials" [2].

1. Can you tell us about a time when you had to choose between a number of different options and how you approached it? *(sourced: Clover HR, "Competency interview questions for Civil Service behaviours")* [24]
2. Tell me about a time when you had to make an unpopular decision. How did you manage the situation? *(sourced: University of Bath, "Behavioural questions for interviewing")* [23]
3. Give an example of a time when you had to make a difficult decision. *(sourced: University of Oxford Careers Service, "Example interview questions", May 2016)* [25]
4. Describe a decision you made where the evidence was incomplete. How did you handle what you did not know? *(composed)*
5. Tell us about a time you identified an error or a gap in the information you had been given before acting on it. *(composed)*
6. Give an example of a time you decided *not* to decide — you escalated, delegated or deferred. Why was that the right call? *(composed)*

## Leadership

*Definition:* "Show pride and passion for public service. Create and engage
others in delivering a shared vision. Value difference, diversity and
inclusion, ensuring fairness and opportunity for all." [2]

*How it escalates:* this behaviour does **not** require line management at the
lower bands. Level 1 is "Show enthusiasm for your work and take personal
accountability for your role" and acting "in a fair, inclusive and respectful
way"; Level 2 adds role-modelling an inclusive workplace and "promptly dealing
with inappropriate language and behaviours"; Level 3 adds clarity of objectives
for colleagues and stakeholders and effectively managing "conflict, misconduct
and non-inclusive behaviour"; Level 4 adds standing by and defending your
team's decisions and inspiring teams; Levels 5–6 move to visibility, reputation
and organisational culture [2].

1. Can you tell us about a time when you showed leadership skills and what you learned from the experience? *(sourced: Clover HR, "Competency interview questions for Civil Service behaviours")* [24]
2. Tell me about a time you had to demonstrate exceptional leadership. *(sourced: DWP Digital Careers, "How to ace your job interview")* [11]
3. Give me an example of a time that you showed initiative and took the lead. *(sourced: University of Bath, "Behavioural questions for interviewing")* [23]
4. Describe a time you challenged language or behaviour that was not inclusive. What did you do and what happened? *(composed)*
5. Tell us about a time you had to stand behind a decision your team had made when it came under criticism. *(composed)*
6. Give an example of when you got people who did not report to you to commit to a shared goal. *(composed)*

## Communicating and Influencing

*Definition:* "Communicate purpose and direction with clarity, integrity and
enthusiasm. Respect the needs, responses and opinions of others." [2]

*How it escalates:* Level 1 asks for views put "in a clear, constructive and
considerate manner", "plain and simple language" and checking written work for
errors; Level 2 adds handling "challenging conversations with confidence and
sensitivity"; Level 3 adds choosing "appropriate styles to maximise
understanding and impact"; Level 4 adds explaining "complex issues in a way that
is easy to understand" and delivering "difficult messages with clarity and
sensitivity, being persuasive when required"; Level 5 adds communicating "with
conviction and clarity in the face of tough negotiations" [2].

1. Please tell me about a time when you communicated effectively with others. *(sourced: Clover HR, "Competency interview questions for Civil Service behaviours")* [24]
2. Tell me about a time when you have had to use written communication to successfully influence someone. *(sourced: University of Oxford Careers Service, "Example interview questions", May 2016)* [25]
3. Describe a situation where you had difficulty getting others to agree with your decisions. How did you handle it? *(sourced: University of Bath, "Behavioural questions for interviewing")* [23]
4. Give an example of a time you had to deliver a difficult or unwelcome message. How did you prepare, and what did you say? *(composed)*
5. Tell us about a time you had to explain something technical or complex to an audience with no background in it. *(composed)*
6. Describe an occasion when you changed how you were communicating part-way through because it was not landing. *(composed)*

## Working Together

*Definition:* "Form effective partnerships and relationships with people both
internally and externally, from a range of diverse backgrounds, sharing
information, resources and support." [2]

*How it escalates:* Level 1 is contributing to the team, listening to
"alternative perspectives and needs", asking for help and being aware of
wellbeing; Level 2 adds developing contacts outside your own team; Level 3 adds
encouraging "joined up team work… across other groups" and investing time in
"genuine positive team spirit"; Level 4 adds actively building a network and
"Challenge assumptions while being willing to compromise if beneficial to
progress"; Level 5 adds networks across and beyond the organisation [2].

1. Please tell us about a time when you worked together with a colleague to achieve a goal. *(sourced: Clover HR, "Competency interview questions for Civil Service behaviours")* [24]
2. Tell me about a time when you worked with a colleague who was not completing their share of work. What did you do? *(sourced: University of Bath, "Behavioural questions for interviewing")* [23]
3. Describe a time when you had to negotiate a compromise or had to help others arrive at a compromise. *(sourced: University of Bath, "Behavioural questions for interviewing")* [23]
4. Give an example of when you built a working relationship with someone outside your organisation to get something done. *(composed)*
5. Tell us about a time you deliberately sought out a perspective that was missing from a discussion. What difference did it make? *(composed)*
6. Describe a time you noticed a colleague was struggling. What did you do, and how did you balance it against your own workload? *(composed)*

## Developing Self and Others

*Definition:* "Focus on continuous learning and development for self, others
and the organisation as a whole." [2]

*How it escalates:* Level 1 is identifying gaps in your own skills, acting on
feedback, and sharing what you learn; Level 2 adds identifying gaps in the
team's skills and delegating "to aid the learning and development of all";
Level 3 adds taking "time to coach, mentor and develop other colleagues to
support succession planning"; Level 4 adds ensuring development opportunities
"are available for all individuals regardless of their background or desire to
achieve promotion"; Level 6 adds reverse mentoring "to increase your knowledge
and insights of challenges faced by under-represented groups" [2].

1. Can you tell us about a time when you helped a colleague develop their knowledge? *(sourced: Clover HR, "Competency interview questions for Civil Service behaviours")* [24]
2. Can you give me an example of how you helped someone else to improve? *(sourced: University of Oxford Careers Service, "Example interview questions", May 2016)* [25]
3. Tell me about a time when you felt you had not performed to the best of your ability. What did you do about it? *(sourced: University of Bath, "Behavioural questions for interviewing")* [23]
4. Describe a time you received feedback you did not agree with. What did you do with it? *(composed)*
5. Give an example of when you identified a skills gap — in yourself or your team — and did something concrete about it. *(composed)*
6. Tell us about something you learned in the past year and how you passed it on to others. *(composed)*

## Managing a Quality Service

*Definition:* "Deliver service objectives with professional excellence,
expertise and efficiency, taking account of diverse customer needs." [2]

*How it escalates:* Level 1 is understanding customers' needs, managing your
own time to deliver "a high quality service which gives taxpayers a good return
for their money", and acting to prevent problems; Level 2 adds creating plans
and priorities that meet both customer and business needs; Level 3 adds
developing and reviewing "systems and services to ensure delivery of
professional excellence" and establishing feedback routes; Level 4 adds
adherence to "legal, regulatory and security requirements" and comparing the
business area with industry best practice; Level 5 adds using customer insight
to drive delivery [2].

1. Please can you tell me about a time when you dealt with a difficult client/customer situation? *(sourced: Clover HR, "Competency interview questions for Civil Service behaviours")* [24]
2. What was the most difficult customer service situation you ever had to deal with? How did you handle the situation? Would you do anything differently if you handled a similar problem in the future? *(sourced: University of Bath, "Behavioural questions for interviewing")* [23]
3. Describe a project you worked on that required close attention to detail. *(sourced: University of Bath, "Behavioural questions for interviewing")* [23]
4. Tell us about a time you spotted a recurring problem in a service and did something about the cause rather than the symptom. *(composed)*
5. Give an example of when you had to balance quality against cost or speed. How did you decide where the line was? *(composed)*
6. Describe a time you changed how a service worked because of what users told you. *(composed)*

## Delivering at Pace

*Definition:* "Take responsibility for delivering timely and quality results
with focus and drive." [2]

*How it escalates:* Level 1 is working "with focus and pace to get the job done
on time and to a high standard", following procedures, and keeping managers
updated; Level 2 adds reviewing team activities to identify "barriers to
progress" and setting clear goals and areas of responsibility; Level 3 adds
acting "promptly to reassess workloads and priorities when there are conflicting
demands" while allowing individuals "the space and authority to meet
objectives"; Level 4 adds ensuring everyone "clearly understands and owns their
roles, responsibilities and business priorities"; Level 5 adds translating
"strategic priorities into clear outcome-focused objectives" [2].

1. Please can you tell us about a time when you have had to re-prioritise your workload, and how you approached it? *(sourced: Clover HR, "Competency interview questions for Civil Service behaviours")* [24]
2. Tell me about a time when a project you were in charge of fell behind schedule. What did you do about it? *(sourced: University of Bath, "Behavioural questions for interviewing")* [23]
3. Give me an example of a high-pressure situation you have faced this past year and how you resolved it. *(sourced: University of Bath, "Behavioural questions for interviewing")* [23]
4. Tell me about a time when you have had to balance or juggle a number of conflicting priorities. *(sourced: University of Oxford Careers Service, "Example interview questions", May 2016)* [25]
5. Describe a time you had to keep a team focused on delivery when something went badly wrong. *(composed)*
6. Give an example of when you kept quality up under a deadline you thought was unrealistic. What did you have to give up? *(composed)*

## Situational (future-tense) behaviour questions

The framework permits asking "how you would behave in a given situation"
through situational interview questions [2]. These are pitched at HEO/SEO and
above, where the descriptors involve others.

1. You inherit a service that is meeting its targets but generating repeat complaints from a particular group of users. What would you do in your first month? *(composed)*
2. A senior stakeholder asks you to bring forward a delivery date by six weeks. Your team says it cannot be done without cutting checks. How would you handle it? *(composed)*
3. A change you are implementing is being quietly resisted by experienced colleagues who think it will not work. What would you do? *(composed)*
4. You must give advice on an option you personally disagree with, but the evidence supports it. How would you proceed? *(composed)*
5. Halfway through a project, new data suggests your original assumption was wrong. What would you do and who would you tell? *(composed)*
6. You notice a colleague repeatedly talking over a quieter team member in meetings. What would you do? *(composed)*

## Follow-up and probing questions

Departmental guidance notes that assessors "may ask you follow-up questions
from your example" and frames this as a good sign [12]. The prompts below are
what a panel typically uses to convert a narrative into scorable evidence
against a descriptor; candidates should rehearse against them, and panels can
use them as a standard probe set.

1. What exactly was *your* contribution, as opposed to the team's? *(composed)*
2. Who else was involved, and what did you need from them? *(composed)*
3. What were the options you rejected, and why? *(composed)*
4. What did you not know at the time, and how did that affect what you did? *(composed)*
5. Who disagreed with you, and how did you handle that? *(composed)*
6. What was the measurable outcome, and how do you know? *(composed)*
7. What did it cost — in time, money, goodwill or other work not done? *(composed)*
8. What would you do differently if the same situation arose tomorrow? *(composed)*
9. How did you make sure the people affected by this could actually use or access it? *(composed)*
10. What happened after the point where your story ended? *(composed)*
11. How did you know when to stop and hand it over? *(composed)*
12. If you had had twice the time, what would you have added? *(composed)*

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact. It points back to
the descriptors cited above; it introduces no new facts.**

## Step 1: read the descriptor for the band you are applying to, not the one you are in

This is the whole game. The official framework says behaviours are "specific to
the grade level of the job role" [2], and the level texts differ in scope,
agency, ambiguity tolerance and time horizon (see Core Reference). Before
choosing a story, open the level for the advertised grade and highlight the
verbs. If the descriptor says *create an environment*, a story about diligently
completing your own tasks cannot reach it, however well told.

A practical test: for each descriptor sentence, ask "does my story contain a
sentence that could be swapped for this one?" If your story only matches the
band below, you have three options — pick a different story, expand the frame of
the one you have (see below), or accept that the descriptor genuinely describes
work you have not done and say so honestly rather than inflating it.

## Step 2: the lift is real, not rhetorical

Lifting an answer a band is not saying the same thing more grandly. In the
published texts, each step up adds something concrete: more people, more
autonomy, longer horizon, more disagreement absorbed [2]. So the lift is
usually done by **widening the frame in time or stakeholders**, not by changing
adjectives. Genuinely ask: who outside my team did I have to bring with me? How
long did the effect last? What did I decide without asking permission?

If the honest answer is "nobody" and "a fortnight", you are describing a
lower-band example, and dressing it up is the most common way candidates lose
marks they could have kept by choosing a different story.

## Step 3: structure

The Civil Service tells candidates to use STAR — Situation, Task, Action,
Result — when giving behaviour examples [1] [3], and the National Careers
Service sets out the same four steps [26]. DWP Digital's practical refinement is
worth taking seriously: do not spend too long on the situation, and concentrate
on what you did, how, and the result [11]. `techniques-foundation` covers STAR
variants in depth; this document assumes it.

## Worked skeleton: one example, pitched at two grades

*Coaching illustration, not a model answer to memorise. The underlying event is
the same in both versions: a team's monthly reporting pack was late and
error-prone, and the candidate fixed it.*

**Pitched at HEO/SEO (Level 3), for *Changing and Improving*.** Target
descriptor language: "Work with others to identify areas for improvement and
simplify processes to use fewer resources"; "Clearly explain the reasons for
change to colleagues and how to implement them"; "Encourage an environment
where colleagues know that they can challenge decisions and issues safely" [2].

- **Situation** (2 sentences) — the monthly pack took four people three days and
  still went out with errors; two directorates had complained.
- **Task** — I owned the pack and decided to rebuild the process rather than
  keep patching it.
- **Action** — ran a 45-minute session with the four people who did the work and
  asked them where it broke; deliberately invited the most sceptical colleague
  to challenge first; rebuilt the collection template so figures were entered
  once; wrote a one-page note explaining *why* to the wider team and how to use
  the new template; sat with the two people who found it hardest.
- **Result** — pack production fell to one day and one person; zero errors over
  the next four cycles; the sceptical colleague later proposed a further
  simplification.
- **Reflection** — I would have piloted with one directorate first.

*Why it sits at Level 3:* the scope is one team's process; the candidate
personally simplified it and personally explained it; the "safe to challenge"
element is present but small-scale.

**The same event pitched at Grade 7 (Level 4).** Target descriptor language:
"Encourage, recognise and share innovative ideas from a diverse range of
colleagues and stakeholders"; "Make changes which add value and clearly
articulate how changes will benefit the business"; "Consider the full impact of
implementing changes on culture, structure, morale and the impacts on the
diverse range of end users, including accessibility needs"; "Provide
constructive challenge to senior management on change proposals" [2].

- **Situation** — the reporting problem was not local: three teams produced
  overlapping packs, and the directorate was spending roughly 30 person-days a
  month on reporting that senior readers did not use.
- **Task** — I was not asked to fix this; I proposed to the SLT that we stop
  producing two of the three packs and rebuild the third.
- **Action** — quantified the cost and tested it with the senior readers to find
  out what they actually used; ran workshops across the three teams so the
  proposal came from the people doing the work, not from me; made the case to
  the SLT including the argument *against* my own proposal (loss of a detailed
  audit trail) and how I would mitigate it; checked the new format against
  accessibility requirements for a colleague using a screen reader; set out a
  transition plan with named owners.
- **Result** — reporting effort down about 60%; one pack replaced three; the
  format was adopted by a fourth team the following quarter.
- **Reflection** — I under-estimated the morale effect on the team whose pack
  was retired, and would build that in earlier.

*Why it sits at Level 4:* the scope crosses teams; the candidate initiated it
rather than being tasked; there is constructive challenge upward; the
accessibility and cultural impacts are considered explicitly, matching the
descriptor language [2].

Notice what did **not** change: honesty about what went wrong, a specific
result, and the personal "I". Notice what did: who was involved, whether the
candidate was asked or chose, how far the effect travelled, and whether senior
people were challenged.

## Step 4: prepare for both tenses and for the probe

Because the framework allows situational as well as past-behaviour questions
[2], rehearse at least two of your stories in "what would you do" form. And
because follow-ups are routine [12], make sure every story you take into the
room survives the twelve probes in the Question Bank — especially "what was
*your* contribution" and "what did it cost".

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

Civil Service recruitment is governed by the Civil Service Commission's
Recruitment Principles, which require selection on **merit** on the basis of
**fair and open competition** [9]. Structured, criteria-based behaviour
questions exist to make that requirement operable, and both Acas and the CIPD
describe the same core mechanic for defensible interviewing: ask candidates the
same predetermined questions and score responses against pre-agreed criteria
rather than impressions [21] [22].

**The scale is central, not departmental.** Cabinet Office / Civil Service HR
guidance on applying Success Profiles instructs recruiters, in identical words
at three separate grade tiers: "Experience, technical skills and behaviours
should be scored using the 7 point scale. Strengths should be scored using the
4 point scale" [17] [18] [19]. The same sentence appears in the delegated-grade
(EO–Grade 6) guidance of May 2020 [17], the HR Deputy Director guidance [18]
and the HR Director guidance [19] — so **behaviours are scored 1–7 across the
Civil Service**, from EO to Director, and a departmental publication of that
scale is a local statement of a central rule rather than a local invention. The
Deputy Director and Director editions add that the 7-point scale "aligns with
the vX application tracking system" [18] [19].

**The seven descriptor labels.** The central guidance names the scale but does
not print its rating definitions. The Crown Office and Procurator Fiscal
Service does, and remains the source located in this pass for what each of the
seven points means; it applies the scale at both sift and interview [8]:

| Score | Rating | Definition [8] |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Not demonstrated | "No positive evidence and/ or substantial negative evidence" |
| 2 | Minimal demonstration | "Limited positive evidence and/ or mainly negative evidence" |
| 3 | Moderate demonstration | "Moderate positive evidence but some negative evidence" |
| 4 | Acceptable demonstration | "Adequate positive evidence and any negative evidence would not cause concern" |
| 5 | Good demonstration | "Substantial positive evidence of the behaviour" |
| 6 | Strong demonstration | "Substantial positive evidence; includes some evidence of exceeding expectations" |
| 7 | Outstanding demonstration | "The evidence provided wholly exceeds expectation at this level" |

**Benchmarks.** COPFS states that candidates "must demonstrate the minimum
essential criteria for all roles, this includes scoring 4 or more in all
behaviours", with limited flexibility: "a candidate can score a single score of
3 where scores for all other behaviours are high"; additional benchmarks may be
set per role and are given in the application pack [8].

**Other scales exist for other purposes.** At sift in high-volume campaigns,
the central guidance permits scoring against a **3-point** scale instead —
"A - candidate meets all the criteria", "B - borderline/candidate meets some of
the criteria", "C - candidate does not meet the criteria" — and states that the
benchmark for progression "can be flexed in accordance with the quality of
applications and the number of roles available" [17]. Separately, a legacy
**5-point** competency scheme is documented: the University of Oxford Careers
Service reproduces an "example evaluation scheme" attributed to Civil Service
Resourcing best-practice recruitment guidance, running from 5 ("demonstrates
positive evidence of a high level of competence with no substantial negative
evidence") down to 1 ("demonstrates little or no positive evidence of
competence, along with considerable negative evidence") [25]. All of these
share one logic: score the *evidence*, weighing positive against negative, not
the candidate's likeability.

**How the panel converts answers into a score.** The central guidance sets out
the process: "Panel members should observe, record, classify, evaluate and
score each question independently, then calculate an overall score before
jointly agreeing a merit list using the arithmetic method" [17]. Variances
between panellists "can be discussed", and it "may be useful to discuss
evidence underlying scores when these variances are particularly marked";
panellists "may decide to change their score(s) or stand by them", but scores
are ultimately reconciled by arithmetic average [17]. For a candidate, the
operational implication is that each answer is scored on its own by each
panellist before any discussion — so an answer that leaves one panellist
unconvinced costs marks even if the others were persuaded.

The same guidance instructs that "Interview questions should be designed to
allow the candidate ample opportunity to provide the required level of evidence
to demonstrate their capability for the role", and recommends that "all
interview panels should be diverse, and all panellists should have undertaken
training on interview skills prior to sitting on an interview panel" [17].

Three further documented points matter to both chairs of panels and candidates:

- **Score against the level, not the field.** The COPFS wording anchors the top
  of the scale to "expectation at this level" [8] — the descriptor for the
  advertised grade [2] — which is why an outstanding EO-scope answer cannot
  earn a 7 in a Grade 7 competition.
- **Test few behaviours, properly.** Civil Service HR's recommendation of no
  more than four critical behaviours exists precisely so "the panel can
  sufficiently test the behaviours at interview stage" [5] [6] [7]; the
  delegated-grade guidance repeats it for all EO–Grade 6 roles [17].
- **Assess against advertised criteria only.** DWP Digital tells candidates the
  panel asks "four to six questions… based on the topics listed under 'essential
  criteria' in the advert" and that "you are scored on the essential criteria"
  [11]; Acas likewise advises deciding on how each applicant met the job
  description and person specification [21].

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact.**

- **Answering at the wrong altitude.** The commonest and costliest error.
  Descriptors are level-specific [2] and scoring is anchored to "expectation at
  this level" [8], so a vivid story about personally doing the work will score
  modestly in a Grade 7 competition no matter how well delivered.
- **Over-lifting: claiming a level you did not operate at.** The mirror image.
  Probes such as "what exactly was *your* contribution" and "who disagreed with
  you" (Question Bank, follow-ups) collapse inflated stories quickly, and the
  scales explicitly weigh negative evidence [8] [25].
- **Nine stories, one for each behaviour.** Wasteful: you will normally face no
  more than four [5] [6] [7], named in the advert [2]. Prepare depth on the
  named ones instead.
- **Treating the descriptor as a script.** Reciting descriptor phrases
  ("I adopted a government-wide perspective") without an event underneath
  produces positive-sounding text with no evidence in it — which under the
  published scales is not scored highly [8] [25].
- **Spending half the answer on the situation.** DWP Digital's published advice
  is explicitly against this [11]; the Action and Result carry the evidence.
- **Ignoring the constants.** Inclusion, wellbeing and accessibility run through
  the descriptors at every level [2]. Candidates who never mention how they
  considered the people affected leave documented criteria untouched.
- **Assuming the interview is past-tense only.** Situational questions are an
  approved method [2]; a candidate who has only rehearsed STAR stories can be
  wrong-footed by "what would you do".
- **For panels: drifting off the advertised criteria.** Scoring on anything not
  advertised undercuts the merit and fair-and-open-competition basis of the
  appointment [9] and the comparability that structured interviewing exists to
  create [21] [22].
- **For panels: reading a strong narrator as a strong candidate.** The published
  scales score evidence, weighing positive against negative [8] [25], not
  fluency.

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.**

1. **Descriptor highlighting (20 min).** Print the descriptor for your target
   grade band for each behaviour named in your advert [2]. Highlight every verb.
   Write, in one line each, the story you would tell — then check whether the
   story contains those verbs. Any behaviour where it does not is your gap.
2. **The two-grade rewrite (45 min).** Take one story. Write it at your current
   band, then rewrite it at the band above using the worked skeleton in Model
   Answer Guidance as a shape. List exactly what you had to add: people,
   initiative, horizon, upward challenge. If you cannot add them truthfully,
   you have learned something useful about which story to take into the room.
3. **Self-scoring against the published scale (30 min).** Record one answer.
   Score it 1–7 using the COPFS definitions [8], writing the sentence of
   evidence that justifies your score. If you cannot quote a sentence, the score
   is lower than you think.
4. **Probe survival (paired, 25 min).** Give a partner the twelve follow-up
   prompts from the Question Bank. Answer one behaviour question, then take all
   twelve probes back to back. Note which one you least wanted to be asked —
   that is the weak seam in the story.
5. **Tense-switch drill (15 min).** Take three of the past-tense questions in
   your behaviour's set and convert them to "what would you do" form, then
   answer them; the framework permits both [2].
6. **Panel simulation (60 min, for interviewers).** Design a four-behaviour
   question set for a real vacancy, one question per behaviour plus probes,
   within the four-behaviour recommendation [5]. Score two mock candidates
   independently against the 1–7 scale [8] and compare where you diverged —
   that divergence is where your criteria are under-specified.
7. **Advert audit (10 min, weekly).** Pull three live Civil Service Jobs
   adverts at your target grade. Record which behaviours recur. Prepare for the
   frequent ones first; the blog's own advice is to research questions "for the
   grade and similar roles" [10].

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

- Civil Service recruitment must be on **merit** on the basis of **fair and open
  competition** under the Civil Service Commission's Recruitment Principles [9].
  Behaviour questions that are not tied to the advertised criteria weaken that
  basis.
- Under **section 20 of the Equality Act 2010** employers owe a duty to make
  reasonable adjustments for disabled people [14]; GOV.UK confirms this applies
  to applicants during recruitment and gives examples of adjustments to
  interviews and tests [16]. The Success Profiles candidate guidance operates
  this duty directly, telling candidates to request adjustments when applying
  and naming examples such as "providing documents in large print or braille",
  "allowing more time for a test or interview" and "providing assistance at an
  assessment centre" [3].
- **Section 60 of the Equality Act 2010** restricts asking about health or
  disability before a job offer, subject to limited exceptions [15]. Behaviour
  questions should probe the *evidence* in the example, never the candidate's
  health, caring responsibilities or personal circumstances — a live risk with
  probes such as "what did it cost you" if asked carelessly.
- The framework's own text builds fairness into the criteria: inclusion,
  equality of opportunity, accessibility and the unacceptability of bullying,
  harassment and discrimination appear in the descriptors at every level [2],
  so a panel that ignores them is under-using its own scoring criteria.
- Civil servants are bound by the **Civil Service Code**'s core values —
  integrity, honesty, objectivity and impartiality [13]. Behaviour answers that
  describe bending rules to hit a deadline are hazardous for exactly this
  reason, and *Making Effective Decisions* at senior levels explicitly requires
  "unbiased evidence-led advice" [2].
- Central guidance reports that diversity outcomes for blended interviews were
  "analysed, for all groups, to monitor impact on protected characteristics",
  and that analysis "indicated that, on the whole, there are no adverse impacts
  indicated with outcomes based on protected characteristics - as long as
  selection methods are delivered in a structured and consistent way, and
  training is provided to and/or official guidance is followed" [17]. The
  fairness claim is therefore explicitly **conditional on structure and
  training** — which is also why the same guidance recommends diverse,
  interview-trained panels [17].
- Where interviews are conducted by video, central guidance requires checks
  that the technology "is available, accessible and compatible for all panel
  members and candidates", and that the panel considers whether reasonable
  adjustments are needed, "arising either from access to technology or a range
  of other conditions" [17].
- Acas guidance for employers on interviewing job applicants — same questions
  where possible, decisions based on how each applicant met the job description
  and person specification — is the general-law-facing counterpart of the
  Recruitment Principles [21].

# Risks & Caveats

- **No official question bank exists.** The Civil Service publishes behaviour
  definitions and level descriptors [1] [2] but not interview questions. Every
  *(sourced)* question here comes from a careers service or an HR consultancy
  [23] [24] [25] and none should be presented to a candidate as a real or
  official Civil Service question.
- **The scoring scale is central; the benchmarks are not.** That behaviours are
  scored 1–7 is Cabinet Office instruction repeated at three grade tiers
  [17] [18] [19]. What each of the seven points *means* is quoted here from a
  departmental publication [8], and the pass mark on top of the scale genuinely
  varies: COPFS's "4 or more in all behaviours" is COPFS's benchmark [8], and
  central guidance expressly allows sift benchmarks to be flexed by campaign
  [17]. The application pack remains the authority for any given competition
  [8]. The 5-point scheme reproduced by Oxford is legacy Civil Service
  Resourcing guidance from 2016 and should not be assumed current [25].
- **The central scoring packs are dated 2020.** The delegated-grade guidance is
  marked May 2020 [17] and the Deputy Director and Director editions are
  undated in-document [18] [19]; they reference an application tracking system
  ("vX") that may since have changed [18] [19]. The scoring instruction is
  consistent across all three, but re-verify before relying on it for a live
  campaign.
- **The Success Profile Guides are dated.** The HR Success Profile Guides cited
  for the four-behaviour recommendation are Civil Service HR products from the
  2019–2020 rollout period [5] [6] [7]; the recommendation is consistent across
  all three grade editions, but it is guidance to recruiters, not a rule, and
  vacancy holders may exceed it.
- **The behaviours framework is live and has moved before.** The GOV.UK
  behaviours and candidate-overview pages were last updated 29 January 2025
  [1] [3]. Re-verify the descriptors for your grade at the time of use; this
  document's escalation notes summarise, and summaries go stale.
- **One published question was mapped by this document, not by its publisher.**
  The Bath and Oxford question sets are general behavioural/competency banks
  [23] [25]; grouping them under Civil Service Behaviours is this document's
  editorial mapping. The wording is theirs; the placement is ours.
- **The worked skeleton is illustrative fiction.** It is a coaching device
  showing the shape of a lift between bands, not a real case study or a model
  answer to reproduce.

# Open Questions

- **Resolved in this edition:** a central scoring instruction does exist —
  Cabinet Office guidance at three grade tiers requires behaviours to be scored
  on the 7-point scale and strengths on the 4-point scale [17] [18] [19]. What
  remains open is narrower: **no published descriptors for the 4-point strengths
  scale** were located, and the seven rating labels for the behaviour scale are
  published departmentally [8] rather than centrally. *(Would resolve: the
  "Success Profiles: Interview methodology" and "Success Profiles: Scoring case
  studies" documents referenced in the further-guidance list of the central
  pack [17], neither of which was located as a public URL in this pass.)*
- Are the HR Success Profile Guides [5] [6] [7] still current Civil Service HR
  policy, or have they been superseded by unpublished internal guidance? The
  four-behaviour recommendation is load-bearing for candidate preparation.
- Does the **Fast Stream** apply the same six-level descriptors, and if so at
  which level are its assessments benchmarked? University careers pages refer to
  Level 3 for Fast Stream, but no official confirmation was located in this
  pass.
- Is there a documented Civil Service position on whether *situational*
  behaviour questions are scored against the same level descriptors as
  past-behaviour questions? The framework permits both methods [2] but does not
  say whether the scoring anchor differs.
- Do any departments publish anonymised **exemplar answers** at a stated score
  point? These would be the single most useful addition to this document, and
  none were found.

  Technical, linked from the collection page [4] — the other four elements a
  Civil Service interview may test alongside behaviours.

  candidate-facing walkthroughs of the process around the question set.
- Civil Service Jobs live adverts at your target grade — the authoritative
  statement of which behaviours a specific campaign assesses.

  practitioner accounts of panel practice; useful colour, not authority.

  pack [17] but not located as public URLs in this pass; the most likely source
  of published rating descriptors for the 4-point strengths scale.
