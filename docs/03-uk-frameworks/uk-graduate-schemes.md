# Executive Summary

This document is the private-sector deep-dive for the UK Frameworks category.
Its parent, `uk-frameworks-foundation`, establishes that graduate and
early-careers recruitment has no single published framework the way the Civil
Service, NHS and policing do. This document goes deeper into what *is*
published: the end-to-end selection funnel that named UK graduate employers put
on their own careers sites, the competency, values and strengths vocabulary
those employers name in public, the documented shift toward strengths- and
values-based assessment in early careers, and what the Institute of Student
Employers (ISE) says publicly about volumes and methods.

The structural finding is that the graduate funnel is long and the competency
interview arrives late. Deloitte publishes four assessed stages before an
offer — online application, immersive online assessment, job simulation, final
stage assessment [18]. KPMG publishes four — apply, two online assessments, an
AI-avatar-delivered video interview, and the in-person Launch Pad [19]. Aldi
publishes five, ending with a competency-based interview and a Regional
Managing Director interview [17]. Unilever publishes four, ending at a
Discovery Centre [16]. Barclays publishes three, with an assessment centre of
"two or three stages" including a motivational interview with a leadership team
member [20]. By the time a human asks a competency question, the candidate has
usually already passed automated screening.

The honest finding is the one this document is required to state plainly: **no
major UK consultancy, bank or technology employer surveyed here publishes a
full competency dictionary** — a named, levelled list of competencies with
behavioural indicators, of the kind GOV.UK publishes for Civil Service
Behaviours [1]. What employers publish is a stage list plus a short values or
attribute vocabulary (EY's "integrity, respect, and collaboration" [21];
Amazon's sixteen Leadership Principles [22]; PwC's five "PwC Professional"
attributes, which reach the public mainly through careers-service coverage
rather than a UK early-careers page this document could open [26]). Everything
below that level of granularity is careers-service reporting or candidate
recall. Confidence is therefore **Medium**: process facts are well-sourced to
employers' own pages; the competency content beneath them is not.

# Key Takeaways

- The graduate competency interview is a *late* stage. Named employers publish
  three to five assessed stages before it, typically online tests or an
  immersive assessment, then a recorded video interview, then an assessment
  centre. *(documented)* [16] [17] [18] [19] [20]
- No major private-sector graduate employer reviewed here publishes a full,
  levelled competency dictionary with behavioural indicators. Employers publish
  stage lists and a short values/attributes vocabulary instead.
  *(documented — by absence; see Risks & Caveats)* [18] [19] [20] [21]
- Employers do name what they assess, at a high level: EY names integrity,
  respect and collaboration plus global, innovative and analytics mindsets
  [21]; Amazon publishes sixteen Leadership Principles [22]; Barclays says
  assessment centres test "relevant strengths, behaviours or technical skills"
  [20]. *(documented)*
- Strengths-based assessment is now mainstream in early careers, and the reason
  given is specific to graduates: they have "limited track record in the
  workplace", so strengths evaluate potential rather than accumulated
  experience. *(documented)* [8]
- Competition is at a three-decade high — an average **140 applications per
  graduate vacancy** in the ISE's 2025 survey, and 290 in retail, FMCG and
  tourism. *(documented)* [9] [13]
- Employers report graduate shortfalls precisely in the areas competency
  interviews probe: 54% said graduates did not meet expectations on
  self-awareness and 46% raised resilience. *(documented)* [9]
- AI has become an integrity issue in the interview itself: 61% of ISE
  respondents cited candidates using AI during interviews without disclosure,
  and 79% of employers are redesigning or reviewing processes in response.
  *(documented)* [9] [13]
- Candidates with no career history should build an evidence bank from studies,
  part-time work, volunteering, societies, course-rep and placement roles —
  careers services treat these as legitimate evidence sources.
  *(documented)* [35]

  competency answers: "Why us?" and industry-knowledge questions are the most
  consistently published question type across employers. *(guidance)*

# Purpose

This document answers two questions.

For **candidates**: "I am applying to UK graduate schemes — what will actually
happen to me, stage by stage, what will I be assessed against, and how do I
build competency evidence when I have never had a career job?"

For **interviewers, HR and early-careers teams**: "what does the published
private-sector practice actually look like, where is it thin, and what can I
responsibly borrow from it?"

It deliberately goes below its parent, `uk-frameworks-foundation`, which
surveys the whole UK landscape in one section per segment. Everything here is
private-sector and graduate-specific.

# Scope

**Covered.** The end-to-end UK graduate-scheme selection funnel (application →
online tests and situational judgement → recorded video interview → assessment
centre → final interview); what named employers publish about their own stages
and assessed constructs (PwC, Deloitte, KPMG, EY, Unilever, Aldi, Barclays,
Amazon); the two public-sector flagship graduate schemes that compete in the
same market (Civil Service Fast Stream, NHS Graduate Management Training
Scheme) at the level of where they differ from private schemes; the documented
shift toward strengths- and values-based assessment; public ISE data on volumes
and methods; and a 43-question bank with provenance.

**Not covered.** Full Civil Service Success Profiles mechanics
(`uk-civil-service-success-profiles`) or behaviour question banks
(`uk-civil-service-behaviour-questions`); NHS values-based interviewing in
depth (`nhs-values-based-interviews`); policing
(`police-competency-values-framework`); psychometric-test technique; salary and
market data beyond what frames selection; internships and apprenticeships
except where ISE data reports them alongside graduate hiring; and EU
institutions.

**A hard exclusion.** This document does not reproduce unattributed "top 10
graduate interview questions" listicles, and does not present any composed
question as a real or leaked employer question.

# Definitions

Prefer the shared `GLOSSARY.md`. These terms are used here in a narrowed sense.

- **Graduate scheme** — a structured, fixed-term entry programme with a
  defined recruitment cycle and cohort intake, distinct from direct entry to a
  standing vacancy. Used here for the private-sector and public flagship
  programmes named in Scope.
- **Immersive online assessment / job simulation** — an online stage combining
  situational judgement, written and video responses in a simulated workplace
  context. Deloitte publishes both as separate named stages [18].
- **Asynchronous (recorded) video interview** — a stage where the candidate
  records answers to pre-set questions with no live interviewer. KPMG publishes
  six pre-recorded questions delivered by an AI avatar, with answers reviewed
  and scored by its Student Recruitment team [19].
- **Assessment centre (graduate sense)** — a half-day or day-long event
  combining group and individual exercises with an interview; KPMG's is
  branded "Launch Pad" [19], PwC's "Career Focus" [26], Unilever's "Discovery
  Centre" [16].
- **Strengths-based interview** — an interview that "looks at what they enjoy
  doing and have a natural aptitude for", as distinct from what they can
  demonstrably do [8].
- **Evidence bank** — a candidate's structured store of experiences mapped to
  competencies, drawn from studies, paid work, volunteering, societies,
  placements and course-rep roles [35].

# Framework Context

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## What "framework" means in this segment

In the public-sector frameworks covered by sibling documents, the framework is
a document: GOV.UK publishes nine Civil Service Behaviours with example
statements at six grade bands [1], within the wider Success Profiles model of
five assessable elements [2]. In graduate schemes, "framework" is usually
either (a) a global leadership or values model published for brand purposes and
referenced obliquely in recruitment, or (b) nothing public at all beyond a
stage list.

The table below records what each reviewed employer publishes.

Two context points follow directly from that table.

**First, the public sector is the outlier, not the norm.** The Civil Service
publishes the full behaviour set with level descriptors [1]; the NHS scheme
publishes four skill areas [3]; the private-sector employers publish neither.
For candidates this inverts the usual assumption that big private employers are
better documented than government ones.

**Second, the assessed construct is deliberately blended.** Barclays'
assessment centre wording — "strengths, behaviours or technical skills" [20] —
is the clearest public statement of the blend. Deloitte separates a behavioural
"Job Simulation" from a "skills and motivation interview" [18]. The three
lenses (competency, strengths, values) are complements, and the ISE data
reported by Luminate for 2022 showed employers running all three: 68% reported
a competency-based lens, 56% a strengths-based perspective and 35%
values-based exercises [15].

## The strengths and values shift

The move toward strengths-based assessment in graduate recruitment is
documented, and the stated reason is graduate-specific. CIPD's guidance
describes a strengths-based interview as looking at "what they enjoy doing and
have a natural aptitude for", in contrast with competency interviews that
assess what a candidate can do, and reports the argument that for graduates —
who "often have a limited track record in the workplace" — the technique
"offers a good way to evaluate potential" [8].

Careers services report the same shift with named adopters. The University of
Manchester's careers service names the Civil Service (Success Profiles), Mott
MacDonald and EY as strengths users [34]; targetjobs names Jaguar Land Rover
and describes professional-services firms and banks as leading adopters [24].
The Civil Service link is documented at source: Strengths is one of the five
Success Profiles elements [2].

Values assessment travels with it. EY frames the point as "Your mindset is as
important as your skillset" [21]; Barclays looks for candidates who "share the
same key attributes and Values as Barclays colleagues" [20]; Amazon publishes
its sixteen Leadership Principles as the operating vocabulary it uses "every
day" [22].

## Market conditions that shape the interview

Selection design is downstream of volume, and volume is extreme. Public ISE
summaries for the 2025 Student Recruitment Survey report an average of **140
applications per graduate vacancy** — described as the highest recorded in the
three decades since the ISE began collecting the data in 1991 — rising to 290
per vacancy in retail, FMCG and tourism [9] [13]. Graduate vacancies fell 8%
while apprentice vacancies rose 8% [9]. The survey drew on 155 employers who
managed over 1.8 million applications and hired more than 31,000 students [13].
The prior year's survey, reported by Luminate, put the figure at 140
applications per graduate job following a 59% year-on-year increase, from 145
employers hiring over 40,000 graduates from more than 1.2 million applications
[14].

Entry standards have loosened over the same decade: employers requiring
specified A-level grades fell from 26% to 13% and those requiring a 2:1 from
71% to 47%, while employers with no minimum requirement rose from 11% to 23%
[13]. The practical consequence is that more of the selection decision now
rests on assessment rather than on academic filters — which raises the stakes
of the competency and strengths stages.

# Core Reference

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## Stage 1 — Application and eligibility screen

The published application stage is thin by design. KPMG's first stage assesses
academic background and work experience against entry criteria and adds a
non-assessed "job preview quiz using everyday scenarios" to help candidates
self-select [19]. Deloitte's online application collects education,
qualifications, personal details and experience [18]. PwC, as reported by
targetjobs, asks for languages, skills and education history without a CV or
cover letter [26]. Aldi requires region and route selection plus educational
and work history, with published minimum criteria of 96 UCAS points, a 2:1
degree, a valid UK driving licence and right to work [17].

## Stage 2 — Online assessment and situational judgement

This is where most candidates are eliminated, and it is increasingly not a
classic aptitude test. KPMG publishes two components: an untimed ~20-minute
Skills Assessment in which candidates "rank how you would think, feel or
respond in each situation", and a timed 36-minute Cognitive Assessment covering
"numerical and logical reasoning" [19]. Deloitte's Immersive Online Assessment
asks candidates to "look at possible work scenarios and answer honestly how
you'd respond, deciding on your most to least likely actions", alongside
preferred-working-style questions and numerical and verbal reasoning — and
notes that some questions are "time factored, meaning the time you take to
respond is considered as part of your performance score" [18]. Unilever's
Profile Assessment examines "cognitive, emotional, and social traits" [16].
Aldi runs an online psychometric test "designed to measure your core skills and
personal qualities" [17]. Barclays describes "two short interactive
assessments, which will tell us how you might perform in the workplace, and
your typical or preferred ways of working" [20].

CIPD's selection-methods factsheet places situational judgement tests in this
family, describing them as skill-based assessments that "assess skills and
abilities relevant to the role" and noting they have shown superiority as
"better predictors of performance on the job" compared with traditional methods
[7]. CIPD also advises employers to provide examples of what test questions
cover and to link to practice tests, "especially where candidates may not have
come across psychometric assessment before" [7] — a fairness point candidates
can legitimately hold employers to.

## Stage 3 — The recorded video interview

The asynchronous video interview is now standard, and it is the first stage at
which competency and motivation questions appear.

KPMG publishes the mechanics unusually clearly: six pre-recorded questions
asked by an AI avatar named Leah, 40 minutes in total, assessing whether skills
"are aligned with those required for success at KPMG" and motivation for the
role — with answers reviewed and scored by a member of the Student Recruitment
team, not by AI [19]. Deloitte's Job Simulation is a separately named stage
assessing behavioural skills through work-based scenario ranking, written
responses and video responses, after which candidates receive "a feedback
report about your strengths" that "isn't the outcome of your assessment" [18].
Unilever's Digital Interview is three short questions plus a business case
solving real-world problems using Unilever scenarios [16]; targetjobs describes
the three as hypothetical and says the stage assesses problem-solving —
"Can you think on the spot and process new information quickly?" — and whether
the candidate can "take Unilever's priorities and objectives into account"
[31]. Aldi's video stage is a five-minute submission in which candidates
"showcase your knowledge of Aldi and brilliant leadership skills" [17]. PwC's
video stage, per targetjobs, combines motivational questions with a case study
[26].

The NHS Graduate Management Training Scheme runs the public-sector equivalent:
NHS Health Careers publishes online tests assessing "communication, numeracy,
planning and analytical thinking skills" ahead of interviews and an assessment
centre [3].

## Stage 4 — The assessment centre

Assessment centres survive the automation of everything before them, and public
ISE commentary reports face-to-face assessment centres and face-to-face
interviews as the methods employers consider ideal for making final hiring
decisions [13].

Published formats:

- **KPMG Launch Pad** — an in-person half-day event of assessed and
  non-assessed activities evaluating the "skills needed to succeed in your
  role", with networking built in [19].
- **Deloitte Final Stage Assessment** — two exercises: a discussion of a
  mandatory prepared topic (~30 minutes) and a "skills and motivation
  interview" (~50 minutes) [18].
- **Unilever Discovery Centre** — a face-to-face day including project
  meetings, business challenges, a team meeting and an interview [16].
- **Aldi Individual Assessment and Final Interview** — a short presentation
  followed by "a one to one competency based interview", then a final interview
  with the Regional Managing Director [17].
- **Barclays assessment centre** — "two or three stages, including a
  motivational interview with a member of the leadership team", assessing
  "relevant strengths, behaviours or technical skills required for the role",
  attendable in person or virtually [20].

CIPD describes the underlying method: assessment centres require candidates "to
complete several different tasks", combining "behavioural ratings, cognitive
and personality assessments", with tasks that "clearly relate to the person
specification and reflect the reality of the job" and are "administered in a
systematic way" [7].

## Stage 5 — The final competency and motivation interview

Two things are consistently documented about the final interview.

It is explicitly motivational as well as behavioural. Deloitte names its final
exercise a "skills and motivation interview" [18]; Barclays names a
"motivational interview with a member of the leadership team" [20]; KPMG's
video stage assesses "motivation for the role" [19]. EY's published interview
guidance tells candidates to give "concrete examples, with facts and figures",
to "tell your stories succinctly", and to show research on EY, the role and the
interviewers [21].

And it is where the competency label is used most explicitly. Aldi is the
clearest: its published Individual Assessment is "a one to one competency based
interview" [17].

## What employers publish about the competencies themselves

This is the thin layer, and the honesty requirement bites here.

- **EY** publishes a values and mindset vocabulary — integrity, respect and
  collaboration; energy, enthusiasm and courage to lead with purpose;
  appreciation of diversity and inclusiveness; a continuous personal
  development mindset; and global, innovative and analytics mindsets, framed by
  a "Transformative Leadership model" [21]. It does not publish behavioural
  indicators or levels.
- **Amazon** publishes the fullest named list found: sixteen Leadership
  Principles — Customer Obsession; Ownership; Invent and Simplify; Are Right, A
  Lot; Learn and Be Curious; Hire and Develop the Best; Insist on the Highest
  Standards; Think Big; Bias for Action; Frugality; Earn Trust; Dive Deep; Have
  Backbone, Disagree and Commit; Deliver Results; Strive to be Earth's Best
  Employer; and Success and Scale Bring Broad Responsibility — and states that
  it uses them "every day, whether we're discussing ideas for new projects or
  deciding on the best way to solve a problem" [22]. The page reviewed here
  does not itself set out how they are scored at interview.
- **PwC** operates a global leadership framework, "the PwC Professional",
  whose five attributes are whole leadership, technical capabilities, business
  acumen, global acumen and relationships; careers-service reporting states
  that PwC assesses graduate candidates against it [26] [27]. This document
  could not open PwC's own UK early-careers pages (see Risks & Caveats), so the
  attribution rests on targetjobs, not on PwC's own site.
- **Unilever's** values are reported by targetjobs as integrity, respect,
  responsibility and pioneering [31]; Unilever's own UFLP page names assessed
  *traits* only as "cognitive, emotional, and social" [16].
- **Deloitte, KPMG and Barclays** publish process and a generic construct label
  ("skills", "strengths, behaviours or technical skills") but no named
  competency set on the pages reviewed [18] [19] [20].

By contrast, the Civil Service publishes all nine Behaviours with example
statements at six grade bands [1] and defines the five Success Profiles
elements [2] — which is why the sibling documents on Success Profiles can go
into a depth this one cannot.

## What the market says graduates get wrong

ISE's published 2025 statistics identify shortfalls that map directly onto
interview content: 54% of employers reported that graduates did not meet
expectations on self-awareness, and 46% raised concerns about resilience [9].
Separately, 77% of employers agreed that former interns were more work-ready
[9]. On integrity, the most commonly reported form of misconduct was candidates
using AI during interviews without disclosure or permission, cited by 61% of
respondents; 79% of employers are redesigning or reviewing recruitment
processes because of AI; and only 15% of employers in 2025 reported that they
had never suspected or identified cheating, down from 22% the year before
[9] [13].

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact. It points back to the
evidence above and adds no new facts.**

## Building an evidence bank with no career history

The single biggest practical problem in graduate interviews is that the
candidate has no professional examples. Careers services treat this as normal,
not a deficiency: the University of Manchester states that skills can be
evidenced "through your studies and extra-curricular activities such as paid
work, volunteering, involvement with a student society, work experience, a
placement year or experience gained as a course rep" [35], and the National
Careers Service tells candidates to "use examples that work for you - these
could be from work, home or volunteering" [36].

A workable method:

1. **Inventory, don't curate.** List every context of the last three years:
   modules and group projects, dissertation, part-time job, society or sports
   role, volunteering, course rep, family or caring responsibilities, side
   projects, hackathons, placements. Twenty entries is a healthy list.
2. **Extract two to four episodes per context.** An episode is a bounded event
   with a decision in it, not a role description. "Bar work" is a context;
   "the night the till system failed during a 200-cover service" is an episode.
3. **Map episodes to the vocabulary you are being assessed against.** For an
   employer that publishes a vocabulary (EY's integrity/respect/collaboration
   [21]; Amazon's Leadership Principles [22]; the Civil Service Behaviours [1])
   map to that. Where the employer publishes only a stage list, map instead to
   the recurring published question themes in the Question Bank above:
   motivation, commercial awareness, teamwork, leadership, resilience,
   initiative, decision-making, influencing, deadlines, customer service,
   explaining complexity, ethics, innovation.
4. **Require two episodes per theme.** One for the written or video stage, one
   held back for the interview, so you are not repeating yourself across a
   funnel that may ask about teamwork three times.
5. **Score your own bank honestly.** Mark each episode for whether your
   personal action is clearly separable from the group's, and whether you can
   state a result. Episodes failing both are decoration.

## Structuring the answer

Use STAR — the National Careers Service defines it as Situation ("the
situation you had to deal with"), Task ("the task you were given to do"),
Action ("the action you took") and Result ("what happened as a result of your
action and what you learned") [36]. Careers services also teach CAR (context,
action, result) for shorter answers [35]. Weight your time: roughly 15%
situation, 10% task, 55% action, 20% result. The action is what is scored.

Where the employer's own guidance exists, follow it in preference to any
generic mnemonic. EY tells candidates to give "concrete examples, with facts
and figures" and to "tell your stories succinctly" [21] — which in practice
means numbers in the Result and a 90-second, not five-minute, answer.

## Worked skeleton

*Question:* "Tell us about a time when your contribution made a big difference
to a team's success." *(question 6 in Teamwork above, published by targetjobs
[25])*

*Evidence source:* university society, no professional experience required.

- **Situation (2 sentences).** I was treasurer of a 300-member student society
  that ran a paid annual ball. Six weeks out, ticket sales were at 40% of the
  break-even number and the committee was arguing about whether to cancel.
- **Task (1 sentence).** I took responsibility for establishing whether the
  event was financially recoverable and, if so, for the plan to recover it.
- **Action (5–6 sentences, first person singular).** I rebuilt the budget in a
  spreadsheet, separating fixed venue costs from per-head catering, and found
  our break-even was 30 tickets lower than the committee believed because
  catering was billed on confirmed numbers. I took that to the committee with
  two options rather than a recommendation, so the decision stayed collective.
  We chose to continue. I then negotiated a revised catering deadline with the
  venue, which bought us eleven days. I asked the social secretary to run a
  referral offer and personally called the eight course reps to push it through
  year-group channels rather than relying on our own feed.
- **Result (2–3 sentences, with numbers).** We sold 212 of 240 tickets and
  finished £310 in surplus rather than the £900 loss the original model
  implied. The referral mechanic was reused the following year.
- **Reflection (1 sentence — add this; several careers services teach a
  reflection step alongside STAR [35]).** I learned that the useful
  contribution was checking the assumption rather than working harder inside
  it.

Note what makes this work without a career: a real number, a decision that was
the candidate's own, other people visibly involved, and a consequence.

## Preparing for each stage specifically

- **Online assessment.** Deloitte publishes that some items are "time
  factored" [18] — practise pacing, not just accuracy. CIPD's guidance that
  employers should link to practice tests [7] means it is reasonable to ask
  the recruiter what practice material exists.
- **Recorded video interview.** KPMG publishes six questions in 40 minutes with
  human scoring [19]. Rehearse to a phone camera; the constraint is that you
  cannot read the interviewer, so signpost structure aloud ("the situation was
  … what I did was …").
- **Assessment centre.** Deloitte publishes a mandatory prepared topic
  discussed for ~30 minutes [18]; Unilever publishes business challenges and a
  team meeting [16]; Aldi publishes a presentation before the competency
  interview [17]. Prepare the *prepared* elements to a professional standard —
  they are the only part you can fully control.
- **Final interview.** Expect motivation to carry equal weight with competency
  [18] [20]. Have three specific, current reasons for this employer that a
  competitor's page could not also justify.

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## What employers say they are looking for

- **EY** publishes that it looks for people demonstrating integrity, respect
  and collaboration; energy, enthusiasm and courage to lead with purpose;
  appreciation of diversity and inclusiveness; and a continuous personal
  development mindset — summarised as "Your mindset is as important as your
  skillset" [21].
- **Barclays** publishes that its assessment centre tests the "relevant
  strengths, behaviours or technical skills required for the role" and that it
  looks for candidates who "share the same key attributes and Values as
  Barclays colleagues" [20].
- **KPMG** publishes that its video interview assesses whether skills "are
  aligned with those required for success at KPMG" and motivation for the role,
  and that Launch Pad evaluates the "skills needed to succeed in your role"
  [19].
- **Deloitte** publishes a final "skills and motivation interview" and, at the
  simulation stage, provides candidates with "a feedback report about your
  strengths" that is explicitly not the assessment outcome [18].
- **Aldi** publishes that it seeks "drive, professionalism and leadership
  skills" and a "determined, charismatic leader" [17].
- **Amazon** publishes sixteen Leadership Principles used "every day" across
  the business [22].
- **NHS GMTS** publishes assessment of "communication, numeracy, planning and
  analytical thinking skills" [3]; targetjobs' guide to the scheme adds that it
  looks for resilience — "the ability to bounce back from difficulties and
  achieve results despite obstacles" — confidence and composure under pressure,
  critical reflection, and interpersonal skills, while noting that its guidance
  is expert commentary rather than official recruitment documentation [30].

## What professional bodies document about scoring

CIPD defines the structured interview that all of this sits inside: a
predefined set of questions asked in the same order to all candidates, scored
against pre-agreed criteria, preferably by a panel rather than a sole
interviewer, with interviewers assigning scores independently before group
discussion [7]. ACAS similarly advises asking each applicant the same questions
where possible and basing the decision on how each applicant met the job
description and person specification [11].

On strengths interviewing specifically, CIPD's guidance describes the technique
as testing fit — identifying what energises and motivates candidates and
assessing suitability for the culture — and reports the argument that it suits
graduates because it evaluates potential rather than track record [8].

The underlying evidence base for weighting these methods is contested in the
academic literature: Sackett and colleagues' re-analysis of meta-analytic
validity estimates argues that widely cited validity figures for common
selection predictors, including structured interviews, have been systematically
misestimated, with consequences for how selection systems should be designed
[37]. Employers designing graduate funnels should treat method-validity claims
in vendor material with corresponding caution.

## What the market data tells assessors

- Volume is the design constraint: 140 applications per graduate vacancy on
  average, 290 in retail, FMCG and tourism [9] [13].
- Employers report psychometric tests, CV screening, face-to-face assessment
  centres and automated interviews as the most effective ways to narrow the
  field, with face-to-face assessment centres and interviews reported as ideal
  for final hiring decisions [13].
- Selection and assessment took 35% of early-careers budgets, behind attraction
  and marketing at 47% [13].
- The integrity problem is now inside the interview: 61% of respondents cited
  undisclosed candidate AI use during interviews, 79% of employers are
  redesigning or reviewing processes, and the share never suspecting cheating
  fell from 22% to 15% [9] [13].
- Development gaps reported by employers — 54% on self-awareness, 46% on
  resilience [9] — are an argument for probing reflection explicitly rather
  than accepting a tidy STAR narrative at face value.

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact.**

**For candidates.**

- **Treating the video interview as a warm-up.** KPMG's is six scored
  questions reviewed by a human [19] and Deloitte's simulation is a named
  assessed stage [18]. Candidates routinely under-prepare the stage that
  eliminates most of them.
- **Answering "why us?" with the employer's own marketing.** Motivation is
  separately assessed at several employers [18] [19] [20]; a reason that would
  read identically on a competitor's page scores as no reason.
- **Bringing one story to a five-stage funnel.** With teamwork appearing in
  written, video and centre stages, a single episode will be visibly recycled.
  Build the bank (above) rather than the anecdote.
- **Hiding behind "we".** Employers assess the individual's contribution; the
  probes in the Question Bank exist precisely to separate it.
- **Skipping the result.** The Result step is where STAR carries its evidence
  [36], and EY explicitly asks for "facts and figures" [21]. An answer with no
  outcome is a description of activity.
- **Assuming no career history means no evidence.** Careers services list
  study, part-time work, volunteering, societies, placements and course-rep
  roles as evidence sources [35]; candidates who apologise for their examples
  discount them before the assessor does.
- **Preparing competency stories and ignoring strengths.** Strengths questions
  are answered honestly and promptly, not from a script [8] [24] — over-rehearsal
  reads as evasion in that mode.
- **Using AI in the live interview.** This is now the single most commonly
  reported form of candidate misconduct [9] [13]; the reputational and process
  consequences are the employer's to decide, and detection effort is rising.

**For interviewers and early-careers teams.**

- **Publishing a stage list without publishing criteria.** Most reviewed
  employers publish process and not constructs [18] [19] [20]. That asymmetry
  advantages candidates with insider access and disadvantages those relying on
  the public page.
- **Letting the funnel front-load automation and then interviewing
  unstructured.** The comparability rationale for structured interviewing is
  documented [7] [11]; an automated early funnel followed by an unstructured
  final chat concentrates subjectivity at the highest-stakes point.
- **Scoring "polish" as competence.** With entry standards loosening [13],
  variance in interview polish increasingly tracks access to coaching rather
  than potential.
- **Not signalling what is assessed.** CIPD advises telling candidates what
  test questions cover and linking practice material [7]; the same courtesy
  applied to interview criteria improves both fairness and signal quality.

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.**

1. **Evidence-bank build (90 minutes, once).** Work the five-step method in
   Model Answer Guidance. Target: 20 contexts, 40 episodes, every episode
   tagged to at least one of the thirteen recurring themes listed there. Mark
   the themes with fewer than two episodes — those are your gaps.
2. **Employer vocabulary audit (30 minutes per employer).** Open the employer's
   own early-careers pages. Write down (a) every named stage and (b) every
   named value, attribute or skill. If (b) is empty, note that and prepare
   against the published question themes instead — this is the normal case
   [18] [19] [20].
3. **Recorded-video rehearsal (40 minutes).** Set a phone to record. Answer six
   questions from the Question Bank with a two-minute cap each, no retakes —
   the KPMG format [19]. Watch back with the sound off first and note whether
   your energy reads on camera.
4. **Timed teamwork set (25 minutes).** Answer questions 4–8 in the Teamwork
   group aloud, 90 seconds each, using a *different* episode each time. This is
   the drill that exposes a thin evidence bank.
5. **Probe survival (paired, 20 minutes).** Give one STAR answer. Your partner
   asks only the four probes at the end of the Strengths group, in order.
   Debrief on whether you could separate your own contribution and state a
   measured result.
6. **Commercial-awareness sprint (20 minutes, weekly).** Answer questions 7–10
   in the Motivation group about your target employer using only this week's
   business press. Repeat weekly through the application cycle — this is the
   answer that ages fastest.
7. **Assessor's seat (30 minutes).** Score a peer's answer 1–5 against a single
   named criterion you have written yourself, then justify the score in two
   sentences without using the words "good" or "strong". Notice how much the
   published criterion does the work — and how little most employers publish.
8. **Strengths honesty drill (15 minutes).** Answer questions 1–5 in the
   Strengths group without preparation, on a timer, then check whether your
   answers are consistent with the episodes in your evidence bank. Inconsistency
   is the thing strengths interviewing is designed to surface [8].

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

- Employers must follow discrimination law when advertising, interviewing and
  deciding whom to appoint; ACAS sets out the recruitment application of the
  Equality Act 2010 [12].
- Section 20 of the Equality Act 2010 imposes a duty to make reasonable
  adjustments [4]. In a graduate funnel this bites on every automated stage as
  well as the interview: timed online assessments (Deloitte publishes that
  response time is scored [18]; KPMG publishes a 36-minute timed cognitive
  assessment [19]), recorded video interviews [19], and full-day assessment
  centres [16] [19]. Candidates should request adjustments at application, not
  at the final stage.
- Section 60 of the Equality Act 2010 restricts enquiries about health or
  disability before a job offer, subject to limited exceptions including
  establishing whether adjustments to the assessment are needed [5]. Video and
  simulation stages that capture health-relevant information incidentally sit
  close to this boundary.
- The ICO's employment guidance on recruitment and selection sets out data
  protection expectations for candidate information [6]. Graduate funnels
  generate unusually rich candidate data — game-based and simulation
  assessments, recorded video, behavioural telemetry such as response timing
  [18] — which makes transparency about what is collected, scored and retained
  a live obligation rather than a formality.
- Where AI is used in scoring, employers should be able to explain the role it
  plays. KPMG publishes that although the video interview is delivered by an AI
  avatar, answers are "reviewed and scored by a member of the Student
  Recruitment team" [19] — a model of the disclosure candidates are entitled to
  expect.
- Structured, criteria-based interviewing is itself presented by professional
  bodies as a fairness mechanism: consistent questions and independent scoring
  against pre-agreed criteria reduce the scope for personal bias [7] [11].

# Risks & Caveats

- **The core honest finding: no full private-sector competency dictionary
  exists in public.** Cluster 2 of this knowledge base reached this conclusion
  and this document's independent pass confirms it. Of the eight private-sector
  employers reviewed, none publishes a named, levelled competency set with
  behavioural indicators comparable to Civil Service Behaviours [1]. Amazon's
  sixteen Leadership Principles [22] are the closest — a named list, but
  without published level descriptors or scoring guidance on the page reviewed.
  Everything finer-grained in circulation is careers-service reporting,
  test-preparation vendor material, or candidate recall. This document does not
  fill that gap with unattributed listicles, and the reader should distrust
  sources that do.
- **PwC's own UK early-careers pages could not be opened.** Three PwC URLs
  (`pwc.co.uk` early-careers video-interview and assessment/selection pages, and
  `pwc.com/m1`) returned HTTP 403 to this document's fetchers on 2026-07-20, and
  the Internet Archive was unavailable to it. All PwC claims here are therefore
  attributed to targetjobs [26] [27], not to PwC. A human should verify the PwC
  process against pwc.co.uk in a browser before relying on it.
- **Civil Service Fast Stream stages are not cited here.** The
  `civil-service-careers.gov.uk` Fast Stream pages truncated for automated
  fetchers on 2026-07-20, so this document cites only GOV.UK's Success Profiles
  framework [1] [2] for the Fast Stream's assessed constructs and leaves the
  stage detail to `uk-civil-service-success-profiles`.
- **Several employer-specific questions are candidate-recall, not employer
  publications.** targetjobs states explicitly of its PwC set: "We can't tell
  you exactly what questions you'll be asked at your PwC interview" [27]. The
  Question Bank labels these as sourced *to targetjobs* for that reason. Treat
  them as representative, not as a script the employer will follow.
- **The ISE full survey is member-gated.** All ISE figures here come from ISE's
  own public insight pages [9] [10] and Luminate's reporting [13] [14] [15].
  The 2022 method-mix figures (68% competency / 56% strengths / 35% values)
  [15] are four years old and no public equivalent for 2025 was found — the
  strengths-shift claim in this document rests on CIPD's qualitative account [8]
  and careers-service reporting of named adopters [24] [34], not on a current
  percentage.
- **Employer processes change without notice and mid-cycle.** Every stage list
  here is a snapshot of 2026-07-20. Aldi's targetjobs coverage dates from 2023
  [29] and differs in stage naming from Aldi's own current page [17] — the
  latter is authoritative. Always re-check the employer's own page for the
  cycle you are applying to.
- **Careers-service question lists are undated in places and lightly curated.**
  The University of Kent commercial-awareness list [32] and the Cambridge
  interviews page [33] carry no publication date on the versions reviewed;
  their questions are stable in kind but the specific market questions (base
  rate, FTSE level, share price) age immediately.
- **Composed questions are illustrative only.** The four composed items in the
  bank mimic published vocabulary — Amazon's Leadership Principles [22], the
  ISE-reported resilience and self-awareness gaps [9] — and are not attributed
  to any employer.
