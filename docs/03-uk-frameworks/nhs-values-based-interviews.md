# Executive Summary

This is the deep-dive on NHS **values-based recruitment (VBR)** and the
**values-based interview (VBI)** it revolves around. The parent document
(`uk-frameworks-foundation`) establishes that the NHS recruits against the six
values of the NHS Constitution and that a structured, individual values
interview is a national core requirement [1] [4]. This document goes further:
it separates the two value-sets candidates routinely conflate (the six
**NHS Constitution values** and the **6Cs**), states precisely what makes an
interview "values-based" rather than merely competency-based, sets out what
trusts actually publish about scoring, and assembles a question bank drawn
largely from **real, published NHS trust question banks** rather than composed
material.

The single most useful technical fact is this: a VBI is *not* a different
question format. Health Education England's own guidance says a VBI "may appear
similar to other types of interviews" — the same "tell me about a time…"
openers — and that what differentiates it is **the probing questions**, which
are designed to elicit evidence of *learning and reflection* and to establish
*how and why* an applicant made particular choices [5]. The lead question is
shared with competency interviewing; the value is exposed in the follow-ups.
Candidates who prepare only the narrative and not the reasoning under it are
preparing for the wrong half of the interview.

Confidence is **Medium**. The national layer rests on official documents
(the NHS Constitution, the HEE VBR Framework and its companion structured-
interview guidance, Compassion in Practice, NHS Employers) and would support
High on its own. But two things pull it down: the national framework is dated
**March 2016** and its publisher, Health Education England, no longer exists as
a separate body [6]; and the band/role expectations section necessarily
generalises from a handful of individual trusts' published toolkits, because no
national document sets band-by-band values descriptors. Local practice varies
and this document says so rather than smoothing it over.

# Key Takeaways

- The NHS Constitution for England sets **six values**: working together for
  patients; respect and dignity; commitment to quality of care; compassion;
  improving lives; everyone counts [1]. VBR recruits against these.
  *(documented)*
- The **6Cs** — care, compassion, competence, communication, courage,
  commitment — are a *different, earlier* set, defined in *Compassion in
  Practice* (4 December 2012) for nursing, midwifery and care staff, and
  explicitly stated there to carry equal weight [2]. Conflating the 6Cs with
  the Constitution values is the most common candidate error on this topic.
  *(documented)*
- What makes an interview values-based is **the probe, not the question**: HEE
  guidance states the differentiator is probing questions designed to elicit
  *learning and reflection* and to reveal *how and why* choices were made [5].
  *(documented)*
- The national core requirement is an **individual, face-to-face structured
  interview including an assessment of values**, which may be delivered as a
  Multiple Mini Interview, inside a selection centre, or alongside an SJT
  screen [4] [5]. *(documented)*
- Trusts publish real question banks. Wirral asks recruiters to use **a minimum
  of two** values questions per interview, with a further two from a separate
  **senior/leadership** set for line-management roles [8]. Gloucestershire tells
  panels to choose **one question per value plus one on diversity and
  inclusion**, asked of all candidates [9]. *(documented)*
- Published scoring is **indicator-based, not numeric**, in most trust
  material: Gloucestershire supplies "Things to look out for" and "Negative
  indicators" per value, and a four-band Unsatisfactory / Satisfactory / Good /
  Excellent scale for its equality-and-diversity questions [9]; HEE's template
  pairs each criterion with positive and negative behavioural indicators [5].
  *(documented)*
- **Band expectations are thin in the published record.** Leeds and York states
  plainly that "no matter what level of role you are applying for, the same
  values and behaviours apply" [11]; what scales with seniority is the *number
  and complexity* of questions and the addition of leadership-values questions,
  not a different value-set [5] [8] [9]. *(documented)*
- Prepare reasoning, not scripts. Oxford University Hospitals tells candidates
  there are no right or wrong answers and that no advance preparation is needed
  because the technique probes authentic decision-making [12] — which is
  precisely why a rehearsed answer under-performs. *(guidance)*

# Purpose

This document answers, for **candidates**: what a values-based interview
actually is, how it differs from the competency interview you have prepared
for, which questions NHS trusts really publish, and how to build evidence of
your values without sounding coached. For **interviewers, recruiting managers
and HR**: what the national framework requires, what a defensible VBI question
template looks like, what published trust toolkits use as indicators and
scoring, and where the framework's authority currently sits after the
HEE–NHS England merger.

# Scope

Covered: NHS values-based recruitment in **England** — the NHS Constitution
values, the 6Cs and their relationship, the HEE VBR Framework and its
structured-interview companion guidance, how a VBI differs from a competency
interview, published trust-level implementation (question banks, indicators,
scoring, interview shape), what the published record does and does not say
about band and role expectations, and a question bank built primarily from
sourced trust material.

Not covered: NHS Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland recruitment (their
value-sets and toolkits differ and are out of scope for this edition);
Multiple Mini Interview design in depth; situational judgement test
construction; medical and dental specialty recruitment (a separate national
process); NHS Agenda for Change banding as a pay/job-evaluation system —
"band" here means only the seniority context of the interview. The general
mechanics of STAR and its variants sit in `techniques-foundation`.

# Definitions

Prefer the shared `GLOSSARY.md`. Used here in these narrowed senses:

- **VBR (values-based recruitment)** — an approach to attracting and selecting
  students, trainees and employees "on the basis that their individual values
  and behaviours align with the values of the NHS Constitution" [3].
- **VBI (values-based interview)** — "a type of structured interview which is
  particularly focused on understanding an applicant's values", differentiated
  from other structured interviews by its probing questions [5].
- **Lead question** — the opening question for a criterion or value, followed
  in a VBI by a pre-agreed set of probes; HEE recommends question banks be
  structured as lead question plus probes plus behavioural indicators [5].
- **The 6Cs** — care, compassion, competence, communication, courage,
  commitment: six fundamental values defined in *Compassion in Practice*
  (December 2012) [2]. Distinct from the NHS Constitution values.
- **Behavioural indicator** — a written description of a positive or negative
  demonstration of a criterion or value, used to evaluate an applicant's
  answer [5].

# Framework Context

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## Two value-sets, one recruitment system

| | NHS Constitution values | The 6Cs |
|---|---|---|
| Source | The NHS Constitution for England [1] | *Compassion in Practice*, NHS Commissioning Board / DH, 4 December 2012 [2] |
| The set | Working together for patients; respect and dignity; commitment to quality of care; compassion; improving lives; everyone counts [1] | Care; compassion; competence; communication; courage; commitment [2] |
| Original audience | The whole NHS [1] | Nursing, midwifery and care staff — but stated to be "the business of all health and care staff" [2] |
| Role in recruitment | The benchmark VBR recruits against [3] [4] | Recommended for embedding in recruitment and appraisal for nurses, midwives and care staff, then other NHS staff [2] |

Only **compassion** appears in both lists. *Compassion in Practice* states that
each of the 6Cs "carry equal weight" and that "not one of the 6Cs is more
important than the other five" [2]. Its definitions are worth reading closely
because trust question banks echo their language — for example **courage**,
defined as what "enables us to do the right thing for the people we care for,
to speak up when we have concerns" [2], is visibly the parent of the
"speaking up" questions that appear in trust banks (see Question Bank,
group 3).

The two frameworks are also historically linked to recruitment in the same
document: *Compassion in Practice* recommended that "values based recruitment
is included in the national standard contract for pre-registration education
programmes", and committed HEE to ensuring university undergraduate
recruitment was "based on values and behaviours as well as technical and
academic skills" [2]. That recommendation is the origin of the national VBR
mandate described below.

## The national mandate and its current status

VBR's national framework was published in **October 2014** and refreshed in
**April 2016**; higher education institutions delivering NHS-funded training
programmes were required to embed VBR into recruitment by March 2015, and NHS
employers are *encouraged* — not required — to adopt the same approach [3].
This asymmetry matters: for an NHS **job** (as opposed to an NHS-funded
training place), values-based interviewing is strong national guidance
implemented locally, not a statutory requirement, which is exactly why
trust-level practice varies as much as it does.

The framework's publisher, Health Education England, completed its merger into
NHS England, which "has assumed responsibility for all activities previously
undertaken by Health Education England" [6]. The VBR programme pages and the
framework PDF remain live on the legacy HEE domain [3] [4], and NHS Employers
continued to publish VBR delivery guidance as of **23 May 2024** [7] — so the
approach is plainly still operative, but the anchor framework document itself
has not been reissued under NHS England branding. See Risks & Caveats.

## Where the VBI sits in the process

NHS Employers describes VBR delivery through pre-screening assessments,
values-based interviewing techniques including role play and written responses
to scenarios, and assessment-centre approaches, supported by a values mapping
tool, a behaviour framework and a readiness checklist [7]. HEE's guidance says
the required structured interview "may occur in isolation or as part of a wider
process such as a selection centre or in conjunction with a screening method
such as a Situational Judgement Test" [5].

At trust level the interview is usually a single blended event rather than two
separate interviews. Leeds and York tells candidates its interview contains
opening questions, **values questions**, technical/competency questions and
closing questions, and lasts 40–50 minutes [11]. Barnsley states VBR "will be
incorporated into the existing competency-based interviews" [10]. HEE permits
either arrangement, noting it is appropriate for values questions to sit
alongside competency questions, but that the two may be separated into distinct
interviews where different assessors or different days are involved [5].

# Core Reference

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## What actually makes an interview "values-based"

HEE's structured-interview guidance is the authoritative statement and is worth
quoting closely. A VBI "may appear similar to other types of interviews in that
the format will involve asking applicants to provide examples of behaviour they
have previously demonstrated or to explain how they would respond to a
particular situation. However, it is the **probing questions**, designed as part
of a VBI, that are utilised to elicit detailed evidence in relation to
**learning and reflection** that differentiate it." Those probes "focus on how
and why an applicant makes particular choices in the workplace, providing
insight into the reasons and drivers for their behaviour" [5].

Two structural consequences follow, both documented:

1. **The evidential target moves from the action to the reflection.** Barnsley
   tells candidates that "the outcome of the situation is the focus here; you
   should spend most of your time discussing the learning or changes you took
   rather than just description of what happened", and publishes a STAR
   weighting of roughly **70% on Situation/Task/Action and 30% on Result** —
   where its "Result" prompts are *how did you feel about it, what impact did
   it have, what have you done differently, what did you learn from it* [10].
   Leeds and York gives candidates the same instruction [11].
2. **The probes are pre-agreed, not improvised.** HEE states that standardised
   questions "should be accompanied by a range of pre-agreed probing questions"
   and that a question bank should contain "a lead question followed by a series
   of possible relevant probe questions, as well as a set of defined behavioural
   indicators" [5].

## Structured interviewing: the underlying method

HEE defines a structured interview as one asking "a standardised set of
predetermined questions so that each applicant receives the same, or very
similar, questions", scored "against predetermined scoring criteria which are
the same for all applicants" [5]. It distinguishes **situational** interviews
(future-oriented, "what would you do if…") from **behavioural** interviews
(past-oriented, "tell me about a time when…"), notes a single interview may
combine both, and classes the Multiple Mini Interview as a specific type of
structured interview [5]. On evidence, it states that interviews achieving the
highest validity are those that are structured, ask relevant standardised
questions based on thorough role analysis, use a trained panel and use
validated scoring criteria [5].

This is consistent with the wider professional-body position: CIPD's selection
factsheet describes asking all candidates a predefined set of questions in the
same order and scoring against pre-agreed criteria [18], and Acas advises
asking each applicant the same questions where possible and deciding on how
each met the job description and person specification [17].

Gloucestershire's toolkit adds practical questioning discipline for panels:
candidates should do about **70–80% of the talking**; open questions should be
used to obtain information and closed questions only to check facts; and
hypothetical questions "should be used with care as they potentially generate
hypothetical answers that may not reflect the way the individual actually
behaves", with the recommendation to follow any hypothetical with a request for
a real comparable example. Leading questions and multiple questions are named
as types to avoid [9].

## The documented VBI question template

HEE publishes two worked templates. The first, for the criterion
**Collaboration** (mapped to the Constitution value *working together for
patients*), defines the criterion as working effectively with others, showing
consideration to their needs and perspectives, recognising the benefits others
bring and seeking to learn from them. Its lead question is "Describe a
situation where it was important for you to establish/develop an effective
working relationship with someone…", with probes including *why was this
relationship significant*, *what did you learn from this experience*, *what was
the outcome* and *how did you react/feel about developing this relationship*.
Positive indicators include considering the needs and perspectives of others,
articulating the benefits of working together, showing willingness to cooperate
and having strategies for working effectively alongside others; negative
indicators include being dismissive of others' views, making inappropriate
assumptions and demonstrating a negative attitude towards working with
others [5].

The second, for **Integrity** (mapped to *commitment to quality of care*),
defines the criterion as being open, honest and transparent, taking
responsibility for challenging inappropriate behaviour and being receptive to
feedback. Lead question: "Tell me about a time when you had to address a
difficult situation with a colleague…". Positive indicators: proactive in
identifying and addressing issues; sensitive and tactful when raising difficult
issues; aware of the risks associated with inaction. Negative indicators:
defers responsibility to others; jumps to conclusions; seeks to blame
others [5].

## Trust-level implementation: what is actually published

Four published trust documents give an unusually concrete picture.

**Wirral University Teaching Hospital NHS Foundation Trust** publishes a
recruiter-facing bank organised by its four core values — *Caring for Everyone*,
*Respect for all*, *Embracing Teamwork*, *Committed to Improvement* — with a
separate *Additional Questions for Senior Posts* section. Recruiters are asked
to assess attitudes and behaviours "by asking a minimum of two questions from
the bank of values based questions" during interviews; for leadership and line
management roles, examples from the additional section "should be considered
and a minimum of two questions asked" [8].

**Gloucestershire Hospitals NHS Foundation Trust** publishes a full recruiting-
manager toolkit (authored February 2021) built on three values — *we care*,
*we listen*, *we excel* — and four compassionate behaviours: *I am attentive*,
*I am understanding*, *I am empathetic*, *I am helpful*. Panels are instructed
to "choose one question for each value and one question for diversity and
inclusion from the examples below to ask all candidates at interview" [9]. Each
value block carries a "Things to look out for" list and a "Negative Indicators"
list, and a set of standard probes (*what did you learn; what would you do
different next time; how did it make you feel; who was involved; what was the
outcome*) [9].

**Barnsley Hospital NHS Foundation Trust** publishes candidate-facing guidance
(April 2015) organised around *Quality Service*, *Quality Care* and *Quality
Communication*, each with a behavioural framework expressed as three columns —
"We will…", "We…" and "We do not…" — the last being an explicit catalogue of
disqualifying behaviours (for example, speaking about people as if they are not
there, breaching confidentiality, keeping concerns to ourselves, avoiding
challenging those who show a lack of respect) [10]. It also states VBR is
adopted "in line with the Francis Report which emphasised the need for a
culture shift in the NHS and the importance of a common set of values" [10].

**Leeds and York Partnership NHS Foundation Trust** publishes a candidate
resource pack (October 2024) on values of *integrity, simplicity, caring*,
confirming the interview structure and 40–50 minute duration, that panel
members "have been trained to assess using values and behaviours", and that
the values-based journey begins at application, where the supporting statement
(1,500 words maximum) is read for evidence of values [11].

**Oxford University Hospitals NHS Foundation Trust** describes the VBI to
candidates as "relatively informal, but structured to ensure fairness and
consistency", with the panel asking **between two and four questions**
depending on the role, exploring how and why candidates handled real
situations [12].

## Band and role expectations — what the published record supports

There is **no national document setting band-by-band values descriptors**, in
sharp contrast to Civil Service Behaviours (six grade bands) or the College of
Policing CVF (three levels). What the published sources do support is a
consistent pattern of four variables:

| Variable | What changes with seniority | Source |
|---|---|---|
| The value-set | **Does not change.** "No matter what level of role you are applying for, the same values and behaviours apply" | Leeds and York [11] |
| Question level and number | Question banks are "designed to be used flexibly by recruiting managers developing interviews for roles of different levels and complexity"; "there may be variation in the level and number of interview questions selected from the bank"; questions may need adapting "to meet the level and complexity of the role" | HEE [5] |
| Additional leadership-values questions | A dedicated senior-post set covering compassionate leadership, leading a diverse workforce, reasonable adjustments, understanding staff issues, and reflecting on leadership handled differently | Wirral [8]; Gloucestershire "Managers Only" block [9] |
| Interview length / question count | Two to four questions depending on the role; 40–50 minute interview | Oxford [12]; Leeds and York [11] |

On **clinical versus non-clinical**, the published record shows VBR applied
across both rather than restricted to clinical roles. HEE's own case studies
record Guy's and St Thomas' developing a question bank "applicable to all staff
levels"; Plymouth using a values interview in its **healthcare assistant**
process (reporting lower attrition, reduced sickness absence and better HCA
vacancy management); Bradford District Care Trust piloting a values question
bank with clear scoring guides across **healthcare assistants, administrators,
speech and language therapists and community staff nurses**; University College
London Hospitals using a values-based structured interview to recruit
**medical and dental consultants**; and Peterborough and Stamford piloting a
values question bank "with all **Band 1 and Band 4** posts" with a view to
extending it trust-wide [5]. Wirral's bank is explicitly for all new staff,
with the senior section layered on for line-management roles [8].

The honest summary: **the values are flat across bands; the assessment depth is
not.** Treat any source offering a tidy "Band 2 vs Band 5 vs Band 7 values
matrix" as unsourced synthesis unless it names a trust document.

## Follow-up probes — the part that makes it a values interview

These are the questions the panel asks *after* your first answer. They are the
differentiating feature of a VBI [5], so they belong in any practice set.

1. Why was this relationship significant? *(sourced: Health Education England — VBI template probes)* [5]
2. What did you learn from this experience? *(sourced: Health Education England — VBI template probes)* [5]
3. How did you react/feel about developing this relationship? *(sourced: Health Education England — VBI template probes)* [5]
4. Why was it important to address this situation? *(sourced: Health Education England — VBI template probes)* [5]

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact. It points back to the
evidence above and adds no new facts.**

## Build a reasoning bank, not an answer bank

The documented differentiator of a VBI is the probe, aimed at learning and
reflection and at *how and why* you chose as you did [5]. So the preparation
unit is not "an example per value" — it is **an example plus the reasoning
under it**. For each story, write four lines before you write the narrative:

- What was actually at stake for the patient, service user or colleague?
- What did I weigh against what? (Name the competing pull: time, protocol,
  another patient, a colleague's feelings, my own discomfort.)
- Why did I decide the way I did?
- What did I do differently afterwards, and what does that tell me about what
  I think matters?

That fourth line is the one candidates skip and the one Barnsley's published
Result prompts explicitly ask for — *how did you feel, what impact did it have,
what have you done differently, what did you learn* [10].

## The weighting is published — use it

Barnsley publishes a STAR split of roughly 70% Situation/Task/Action and 30%
Result, with its Result block loaded with reflective prompts [10]. Leeds and
York gives the same steer: spend most of your time on the learning or changes
rather than describing what happened [11]. Practically: if your story runs
three minutes, budget the last minute for reasoning and consequence. Most
untrained answers spend 100% on narrative and stop at the outcome.

## How to prepare without sounding rehearsed

Oxford University Hospitals tells candidates there are no right or wrong
answers and that no advance preparation is needed, because the technique
assesses authentic decision-making — while also advising candidates to
*reflect on their working practice* beforehand [12]. That is not a
contradiction; it is the whole method. Read it as: **prepare the material, not
the wording.**

- Prepare *situations*, not sentences. Hold six to eight real events in mind
  with the four reasoning lines above; do not memorise phrasing. Memorised
  phrasing survives the lead question and collapses at the third probe.
- Do not name-drop the values. Panels are scoring against indicators like
  "considers the needs/perspectives of others" and "is sensitive and tactful
  when raising difficult issues" [5], not against whether you said the word
  "compassion". Demonstrating the indicator beats reciting the value.
- Use non-work material where it is genuine. Gloucestershire's own wording
  invites an example "at work or outside work" [9]; Leeds and York explicitly
  invites lived experience, volunteering and caring experience [11].
- Rehearse *being interrupted*. Have someone stop you mid-story and ask "why did
  that matter to you?" That is the actual test.
- Let uncertainty show where it is true. "I wasn't sure at the time, and what
  decided it for me was…" reads as reflection; a frictionless story reads as
  a script.

## Worked answer skeleton

Question: *"Please give an example of a situation where you've spoken up
because you had concerns. What was the outcome? How did this make you feel?"*
(Wirral [8]) — note the trust has built two probes into the question itself.

- **Situation (short).** Where, when, who was affected. One or two sentences.
  Anonymise: no patient-identifying detail.
- **Task.** What specifically concerned you, and what was your responsibility
  in that moment — not the team's, yours.
- **Action.** What you did, in order, including *who you raised it with and why
  you chose that route*. Escalation choice is values evidence.
- **Result.** What changed. Say so plainly if the outcome was partial.
- **Reflection (the load-bearing third).** Why you thought it was worth the
  discomfort; how you felt at the time and afterwards; what you would do
  differently; what you have done since when something similar arose.

Map that shape to the published Integrity indicators — proactive in
identifying and addressing issues, sensitive and tactful when raising difficult
issues, aware of the risks associated with inaction [5] — and check your draft
hits all three. Check too that you avoid the published negatives: deferring
responsibility, jumping to conclusions, seeking to blame [5].

## For interviewers designing the set

Build the bank as HEE specifies — lead question, pre-agreed probes, positive
and negative indicators per criterion [5] — and agree it before you meet the
first candidate. Decide up front how many values questions you will ask (two,
per Wirral [8]; one per value plus one on inclusion, per Gloucestershire [9];
two to four per Oxford [12]) and hold that constant across candidates. If you
improvise probes for one candidate and not another, you have forfeited the
comparability that is the documented point of the method [5] [17] [18].

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## What panels are told to look for

Gloucestershire publishes explicit indicator lists per value. For **Caring**,
"Things to look out for" include team building, recognising others' views,
communicating effectively, listening, personal awareness, professionalism,
confidentiality, accountability, honesty and trust, compassion and acting as an
ambassador; "Negative Indicators" include being critical and blaming others,
putting own agenda first, an impression of superiority, behaving with
disrespect, not responding to diverse needs of the team, lack of accountability
and a negative attitude [9]. For **Listening**, positives include listening with
passion, acting on feedback, respecting others' ideas and feelings, building
trust and rapport, paraphrasing to show understanding and paying attention to
non-verbal cues; negatives include resisting input from others, lack of
compassion, not taking criticism well, failure to involve others in the
decision process and failure to support staff [9]. For **Excelling**, positives
include ownership of actions, solution focus, change resilience, continuous
learning, knowledge sharing and reflection; negatives include resisting change,
lack of accountability, failing to constructively challenge and failure to
consult with others [9].

HEE's templates supply the same architecture at national level, pairing each
criterion with positive and negative behavioural indicators and stating that
criteria "should be developed into specific indicators describing positive and
negative demonstrations of behaviour", used to evaluate applicant responses and
to ensure all applicants for the same role are assessed against one standard
set [5].

## Published scoring

Most trust values material scores by indicator judgement rather than a numeric
scale. Where Gloucestershire does publish a rating scale — for its equality,
diversity and inclusion questions — it has four bands [9]:

| Rating | Published descriptor (abridged) |
|---|---|
| Unsatisfactory | Did not answer the question, or gave an answer which demonstrated discrimination |
| Satisfactory | Demonstrated a general understanding of the question and of equality/diversity/inclusivity and legal/statutory compliance |
| Good | Demonstrated a good understanding and could evidence this through a specific example or experience |
| Excellent | As above, plus gives examples of proactively encouraging anti-discriminatory behaviour themselves, within the team and within the organisation |

Note the shape of that scale: the step from Satisfactory to Good is
**evidence** (a specific example rather than a general understanding), and the
step from Good to Excellent is **proactivity and reach** (acting yourself, and
beyond yourself). That is a transferable model of what "strong" looks like in
values assessment, and it is published rather than inferred.

Gloucestershire's presentation scoring sheet uses a separate 4–3–2–1 scale
(Excellent / Proficient / Average / Poor) with written descriptors per assessed
area [9] — evidence that trusts run numeric scales where the exercise is more
easily anchored.

## Assessor process discipline

HEE prescribes the **FORCE** principles for conducting the interview:
**F**amiliarise with the materials, person specification and values of
importance; **O**bserve without making judgements and with awareness of
assessment bias; **R**ecord written notes on an observation sheet;
**C**lassify evidence of required competencies and values from the responses;
**E**valuate the quality of evidence against the scoring indicators [5]. It also
directs that interviewers be trained specifically in values-based interviewing
technique with refreshers, that notes be finalised immediately after the
interview because delay distorts recall, and that applicant consent be obtained
before any audio recording [5].

Two further points are unusual enough to be worth flagging to panels. First,
HEE warns of a **candidate-welfare risk specific to VBIs**: because the
questions explore motivations and drivers, "an applicant could feel demotivated
or that they have been 'exposed' in some way", and interviewers should be
trained to close the interview supportively [5]. Second, HEE recommends
**verbal rather than written feedback** where possible, describing performance
against the behavioural indicators, and notes this is "especially valuable when
conducting a values based interview" [5].

On panel composition, HEE states interviews may be conducted by a single
interviewer, pairs or a panel, and that "where resources allow, it is good
practice to use multiple interviewers as this helps to minimise bias and
enhance fairness" [5]. It also recommends involving staff and service users in
developing and refining the questions, since service users "may be able to
provide valuable insight into what is important in terms of demonstration of
skills or values as behaviours in practice" [5].

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact.**

- **Reciting the 6Cs when asked about the NHS values (or vice versa).** They are
  two different sets from two different documents, overlapping only on
  compassion [1] [2]. If the advert or person specification names a value-set,
  answer against that one; if it names the trust's own local values — Wirral's
  four [8], Gloucestershire's three [9], Leeds and York's three [11] — answer
  against those, since that is what the panel's indicator sheet is built from.
- **Preparing the narrative and not the reasoning.** The probes are the
  differentiator [5]. A polished story that dies on "why did that matter to
  you?" scores worse than a rougher story with real reasoning behind it.
- **Stopping at the outcome.** Barnsley and Leeds and York both instruct
  candidates to spend most of the answer on learning and change, not
  description [10] [11]. Ending at "and the patient was fine" leaves the
  highest-value 30% of the answer unclaimed.
- **Answering values questions hypothetically.** Gloucestershire's own panel
  guidance treats hypothetical answers with suspicion precisely because they
  "may not reflect the way the individual actually behaves" [9]. "I would
  always…" invites the panel to discount you.
- **Naming values instead of demonstrating indicators.** Panels score against
  indicator lists [5] [9]. Saying "I'm very compassionate" evidences nothing;
  describing what you noticed and adjusted evidences several indicators at
  once.
- **Treating the values interview as the soft half.** Barnsley states candidates
  must display **both** the competence required and the values required [10];
  they are not traded off against each other.
- **For interviewers — improvising probes.** Unequal probing is the most common
  way a values interview quietly stops being structured, and it undermines the
  comparability that is the documented rationale of the method [5] [17] [18].
- **For interviewers — closing carelessly.** HEE's specific warning that VBI
  candidates may feel "exposed" [5] is rarely acted on; a two-sentence
  supportive close costs nothing.
- **For interviewers — indicator lists as tick-boxes.** HEE frames indicators as
  descriptions used to evaluate the quality of evidence [5], not a checklist to
  be completed; an answer can meet a value definition through behaviour not
  listed.

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.**

1. **Value-set triage (15 min).** Take the advert and person specification for a
   real NHS vacancy. Write down which value-set it names: the NHS Constitution
   six [1], the 6Cs [2], or trust-specific values. Then list one real example
   per named value. Any value with no example is your preparation target.
2. **The reasoning-line drill (40 min).** Pick six of your own stories. For each,
   write only the four reasoning lines from Model Answer Guidance — stakes,
   competing pull, why you decided, what changed since. Do not write the
   narrative. You will find two stories collapse; drop them and find better
   ones.
3. **Probe survival (paired, 25 min).** Answer any question from the Question
   Bank. Your partner may only ask questions from the "Follow-up probes" group
   — nothing else — for three rounds. Score yourself on whether round three
   produced new insight or repetition. Repetition means you prepared a script.
4. **70/30 timing (20 min).** Record a three-minute answer. Time the point at
   which you switch from describing to reflecting. If it is later than the
   two-minute mark you are outside the weighting Barnsley publishes [10].
5. **Assessor's seat (30 min).** Take Gloucestershire's published Caring
   positive and negative indicator lists [9] and score a partner's recorded
   answer against them, writing one line of justification per indicator you
   mark. Then swap. Both of you will discover which indicators your answers
   never touch.
6. **Escalation rehearsal (20 min).** For the "spoken up because you had
   concerns" question [8], write three versions: raised with a peer, raised
   with a line manager, escalated formally. Notice how the choice of route is
   itself the values evidence, and pick the version that is true.
7. **Interviewer build (45 min, for panels).** Take one trust value and build a
   complete HEE-shaped template for it: criterion definition, one lead
   question, four probes, five positive indicators, three negative indicators
   [5]. Then have a colleague try to score a real answer with it. Any indicator
   you cannot apply consistently is badly written.

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

- **Consistency as a fairness mechanism.** HEE requires all applicants to
  experience the same interview process in its delivery and execution, which
  "ensures a fair, reliable and defensible process in accordance with best
  practice selection principles", and requires assessment against one standard
  set of relevant criteria and indicators [5]. Acas advises asking each
  applicant the same questions where possible and deciding on how each met the
  job description and person specification [17]; CIPD describes the same
  discipline for structured interviews [18].
- **Reasonable adjustments.** Under section 20 of the Equality Act 2010,
  employers owe a duty to make reasonable adjustments [14]. Trusts operationalise
  this at interview: Leeds and York asks about adjustment needs at every stage
  of recruitment and lists examples including changing interview format,
  providing questions in written format, providing questions in advance,
  interpreters, supported test sessions and additional time [11];
  Gloucestershire's toolkit tells recruiting managers to plan for accessibility
  and adjustments, including on digital platforms [9].
- **Health and disability enquiries.** Section 60 of the Equality Act 2010
  restricts asking about health or disability before a job offer, subject to
  limited exceptions such as establishing the need for adjustments to the
  assessment itself [13]. This bears directly on VBIs, whose probes deliberately
  push into motivation and personal experience — including, in some trust
  material, an explicit invitation to draw on lived experience of mental health
  [11]. That invitation must remain the candidate's choice; a panel must not
  turn it into a health enquiry.
- **Monitoring for differential impact.** HEE states that differentiation
  between applicants should be "only on the basis of demonstrated attributes"
  and that it is important to be aware of differential performance associated
  with membership of a particular ethnic group, gender or other protected
  characteristic, recommending data capture to inform evaluation and validation
  studies where possible [5]. This is a live risk for values assessment
  specifically, because "fit" judgements are more susceptible to similarity
  bias than technical ones.
- **Raising concerns questions and whistleblowing.** Questions asking candidates
  to describe times they spoke up [8] sit adjacent to the statutory
  whistleblowing regime: section 43B of the Employment Rights Act 1996 defines
  the disclosures qualifying for protection [15]. Panels should be alert that an
  answer may describe a protected disclosure, and should not treat a candidate's
  history of raising concerns as a negative.
- **Candidate data.** Interview notes, scoring sheets and any recordings are
  personal data; the ICO's employment guidance covers recruitment and selection
  [16]. HEE separately requires that applicants be told whether notes are being
  taken and that consent be obtained before any audio recording [5].

# Risks & Caveats

- **The national framework is a decade old and its publisher no longer exists.**
  The VBR Framework is dated **March 2016** [4] and Health Education England has
  merged into NHS England, which has assumed its functions [6]. The programme
  pages and the framework PDF remain live on the legacy HEE domain [3] [4], and
  NHS Employers was still publishing VBR delivery guidance as of 23 May 2024
  [7] — so the approach is current in practice. But no successor framework has
  been issued under NHS England branding at the time of writing, and readers
  should re-check whether one has appeared. This is the weakest link in the
  document's authority chain and the main reason confidence is Medium, not High.
- **Legacy-domain link risk.** Several primary citations resolve to
  `hee.nhs.uk` [3] [4] [5]. Legacy domains are periodically retired or
  redirected. If these break, the Internet Archive is the fallback and the
  document should be re-verified.
- **Band expectations are inferred from a small sample.** No national document
  sets band-by-band values descriptors. The pattern in Core Reference is drawn
  from five trusts' published material plus HEE's case studies [5] [8] [9] [11]
  [12]. It is a fair reading of the published record, not a national standard,
  and a sixth trust could contradict it.
- **Trust documents age at different rates.** Barnsley's candidate guidance is
  dated April 2015 [10] and Gloucestershire's toolkit February 2021 [9]; Leeds
  and York's pack is October 2024 [11]. Trust values themselves change under
  rebrands and mergers. Always check the current values on the trust's own site
  before an interview rather than relying on any of these.
- **Questions are published for recruiters, not leaked.** The sourced questions
  here come from documents the trusts themselves published for recruiting
  managers or candidates. They indicate house style reliably; they do not
  guarantee the questions you will face, and several are drawn from banks the
  trust instructs panels to *select* from.
- **Composed questions are illustrative.** They mimic the published style of
  cited frameworks, have no official status, and must never be presented to
  candidates as real past questions from any employer.
- **England only.** NHS Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland operate their own
  value frameworks and recruitment toolkits; nothing here should be applied to
  them without checking.
