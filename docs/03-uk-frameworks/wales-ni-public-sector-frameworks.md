# Executive Summary

This document fills a gap the England-centred UK Frameworks material leaves open:
how competency- and values-based interviews actually work in the public sectors
of **Wales** and **Northern Ireland**. It is a deliberate deep-dive beneath the
`uk-frameworks-foundation` landscape, which explicitly excludes the devolved
nations [1].

The single most important structural fact is a **divergence of civil services**.
The **Welsh Government is part of the Home Civil Service** and therefore recruits
using the same **Success Profiles** framework as Whitehall departments — verified
directly on GOV.WALES, which names the five Success Profiles elements and even
routes appointment complaints to the (UK) Civil Service Commission [3] [4]. The
**Northern Ireland Civil Service (NICS) is a separate civil service** with its
**own competency framework** (April 2014): ten competencies in three clusters,
assessed through Competence Based Interviews and overseen by the separate
**Civil Service Commissioners for Northern Ireland** under their own Recruitment
Code [11] [14] [15]. So "same as England" is the honest, verified answer for
Wales; genuine divergence is the honest, verified answer for NI.

Two further threads carry most of the document's distinctive value. First, the
**Welsh language** is a genuinely assessed recruitment requirement: the Welsh
Language Standards (made under the Welsh Language (Wales) Measure 2011) require
public bodies to categorise every post as one where Welsh is essential,
desirable or must be learned, to advertise such posts in Welsh, and to assess
language skill — often at interview — against defined levels [8] [9] [10].
Second, **Section 75 of the Northern Ireland Act 1998** imposes a statutory
equality duty across nine grounds plus a "good relations" duty that has no
Great Britain equivalent, shaping how NI public bodies design fair selection
[19]. On policing, the brief's premise was wrong and the correction matters:
**PSNI uses the College of Policing Competency and Values Framework**, adapted
under licence, assessing recruits against CVF Level 1 [16] [17].

Confidence is **High**: every evidence-layer claim rests on an official
framework document, statute or public-body page opened directly for this build.
The composed practice questions are labelled coaching, not fact.

# Key Takeaways

- The **Welsh Government uses Civil Service Success Profiles** — the same
  five-element framework as Whitehall (Behaviours, Experience, Strengths,
  Technical, Ability) — because it is part of the Home Civil Service; appointment
  complaints even go to the UK Civil Service Commission. *(documented)* [3] [4]
- The **NICS is a separate civil service with its own competency framework**
  (April 2014): ten competencies in three clusters (Setting Direction, Engaging
  People, Delivering Results), six levels, cumulative, assessed by Competence
  Based Interviews. Success Profiles does **not** apply. *(documented)* [11] [14]
- NICS recruitment merit is safeguarded by the **Civil Service Commissioners for
  Northern Ireland** under a distinct **Recruitment Code** — a different
  statutory body from the GB Civil Service Commission. *(documented)* [15]
- **Welsh-language skills are an assessed recruitment requirement.** The Welsh
  Language Standards oblige bodies to categorise each post (essential /
  desirable / need to learn / not necessary), advertise Welsh-essential posts in
  Welsh, and may assess skill at interview against defined levels. *(documented)*
  [8] [9] [10]
- **NHS Wales runs values-based recruitment** against its own set of core public
  values (quality and safety first; continuous improvement; prevention;
  partnership; investing in staff), distinct in wording from the six NHS England
  Constitution values used elsewhere in the KB. *(documented)* [6] [7] [22]
- **PSNI uses the College of Policing CVF**, adapted under licence — recruits are
  assessed against CVF Level 1 (Practitioner) at an assessment centre by
  professional and lay assessors. It is **not** governed by a separate NI police
  framework. *(documented)* [16] [17]
- **HSC Northern Ireland** recruits against four shared values — Working
  Together, Excellence, Openness & Honesty, Compassion — the NI counterpart to
  NHS England VBR. *(documented)* [18]
- **Section 75 of the Northern Ireland Act 1998** is a statutory equality duty
  distinct from the Equality Act 2010 public sector equality duty: nine equality
  grounds plus a unique "good relations" duty. *(documented)* [19] [21]
- For candidates, the practical rule is: **identify the nation and body first**,
  then prepare for its actual framework — Success Profiles in Cardiff, the 2014
  competency framework in Belfast, the CVF for PSNI. *(guidance)*

# Purpose

This document answers, for a candidate or an interviewer working in Wales or
Northern Ireland: *which* framework governs the interview, *how* it differs from
the England-centred material already in this knowledge base, and *what*
distinctive obligations — Welsh language, Section 75 — shape a fair process. It
exists because a backlog review found Welsh Government reduced to a single
passing mention across the KB and Northern Ireland almost absent, leaving
candidates in both nations served England-centric guidance.

It is a companion to `uk-frameworks-foundation` (the landscape) and the
England-focused deep-dives (`uk-civil-service-success-profiles`,
`police-competency-values-framework`, `nhs-values-based-interviews`), and a
sibling to `scotland-public-sector-frameworks`. It goes deeper on the devolved
detail rather than restating the shared framework mechanics those documents
already cover.

# Scope

**Covered.** Wales: Welsh Government recruitment (Success Profiles); NHS Wales
core values and HEIW values-based recruitment; Welsh-language requirements in
public-sector recruitment and the Welsh Language Standards; and Welsh public
bodies (Senedd Commission, HEIW) as worked examples. Northern Ireland: the NICS
Competency Framework and Competence Based Interviews; the Civil Service
Commissioners for NI and their Recruitment Code; PSNI's use of the College of
Policing CVF; HSC NI values; and Section 75 of the Northern Ireland Act 1998.

**Not covered.** Grade-by-grade behaviour or competency descriptors reproduced
in full (the framework documents themselves carry these); the full mechanics of
Success Profiles scoring (see `uk-civil-service-success-profiles`) and the CVF
(see `police-competency-values-framework`), which this document contrasts rather
than repeats; Scotland (see `scotland-public-sector-frameworks`); private-sector
employers in either nation; and Irish-language recruitment in NI, which is a
distinct topic from the Welsh-language material here.

# Definitions

Prefer the shared `GLOSSARY.md`; the following are used in this document's
narrowed sense.

- **Home Civil Service** — the single civil service serving UK Government and the
  devolved administrations of Scotland and Wales; the Welsh Government is part of
  it, which is why it uses Success Profiles [3] [4].
- **Northern Ireland Civil Service (NICS)** — the **separate** civil service
  serving the Northern Ireland Executive, with its own competency framework and
  its own recruitment oversight body [11] [15].
- **Competence Based Interview (NICS sense)** — the NICS interview method:
  candidates give specific past examples evidencing each required competence,
  scored against the competency framework [14].
- **Welsh Language Standards** — legally enforceable requirements placed on
  public bodies under the Welsh Language (Wales) Measure 2011, including duties
  on assessing and advertising the Welsh-language requirements of posts [8] [10].
- **Section 75** — the statutory equality duty on NI public authorities under the
  Northern Ireland Act 1998, s.75 [19].
- **CVF** — the College of Policing Competency and Values Framework, used
  (adapted) by PSNI [16].

# Framework Context

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

The two nations sit on opposite sides of one structural line: whether the
public-sector employer belongs to the Home Civil Service. The table maps the
main bodies to the framework each actually uses.

| Nation | Body | Framework actually used | Separate from England? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Wales | Welsh Government | Civil Service **Success Profiles** (5 elements) [3] [4] | No — part of the Home Civil Service; same framework as Whitehall [3] |
| Wales | NHS Wales employers | **Values-based recruitment** against NHS Wales core values [6] [7] | Distinct value set from NHS England Constitution values [6] [22] |
| Wales | All Welsh public bodies | **Welsh Language Standards** duties on top of the above [8] [10] | Yes — a distinctively Welsh statutory layer [10] |
| N. Ireland | NI Civil Service (NICS) | **NICS Competency Framework 2014** + Competence Based Interviews [11] [14] | Yes — separate civil service, own framework [11] [15] |
| N. Ireland | PSNI | **College of Policing CVF** (adapted under licence) [16] [17] | No — adopts the national policing framework |
| N. Ireland | HSC trusts | **Values-based recruitment** against HSC values [18] | Distinct value set from NHS England [18] |

Three context points frame the detail below.

First, **devolution did not fragment the civil service the same way in Wales and
Northern Ireland.** Wales's civil servants are Home Civil Service, so the Welsh
Government's published assessment guidance states plainly that it "use[s] Success
Profiles as the basis for our application and assessment process … a Civil
Service wide approach" and lists the same five elements documented for Whitehall
[3] [4] [1]. Northern Ireland, by contrast, has always had a separate civil
service; its competency framework was introduced in April 2014 to replace the
earlier Professional Skills for Government and NICS Core Competence Framework,
and it is used "for recruitment and promotion, performance management and
development" [11].

Second, **the oversight bodies differ.** GB civil-service appointments are
regulated by the Civil Service Commission and its Recruitment Principles [5];
Welsh Government guidance confirms it recruits under those Principles and even
gives the Commission's London address for complaints [4]. NICS appointments are
regulated by the **Civil Service Commissioners for Northern Ireland**, a separate
statutory body that publishes its own **Recruitment Code** interpreting the Merit
Principle through four principles and that chairs all open Senior Civil Service
competitions in NICS [15].

Third, **the health and policing frameworks converge on values, but through
different documents.** NHS Wales and HSC NI each run values-based recruitment,
but against their own value sets rather than the NHS England Constitution values
used in `nhs-values-based-interviews` [6] [18] [22]. Policing converges the other
way: PSNI adopts the College of Policing CVF used across England and Wales,
adapting it under a College licence rather than authoring its own [16].

# Core Reference

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## Wales — Welsh Government uses Success Profiles

Welsh Government recruitment "use[s] Success Profiles as the basis for our
application and assessment process … a Civil Service wide approach that uses a
number of different 'elements' (experience, strengths, ability, technical and
behaviours)" [3]. Its candidate guidance repeats the five elements and defines
each in terms identical to central Success Profiles guidance — Behaviours as
actions demonstrating effective performance; Experience as knowledge or mastery
gained through involvement; Strengths as what you do regularly, well and are
motivated by; Technical as professional skills and qualifications [4] [1] [2].

Several process facts are documented directly:

- **Structure.** Usually a minimum of two stages — an application/sift, then an
  interview or assessment process; the advert states which elements apply [3]
  [4].
- **Panel.** At least two members, "typically a panel chair and one other panel
  member", with at least one independent member "not directly involved in the
  role or team" [4].
- **Questions in advance.** Welsh Government now shares **opening Experience and
  Behaviour questions** with candidates in the interview invitation "at least
  five days before", where those elements are assessed; strengths and follow-up
  questions are **not** shared in advance, and technical opening questions are at
  the panel's discretion [4]. This is a notable divergence from typical Whitehall
  practice.
- **Answer technique.** The guidance recommends STAR, quoting the same
  proportioning instruction found in central guidance — "Keep the situation and
  task parts brief. Concentrate on the action and the result" — and "Use 'I' not
  'we'" [4].
- **Merit and appeals.** Recruitment is "on merit and on the basis of fair and
  open competition as outlined in the Civil Service Commission recruitment
  principles"; unresolved complaints about merit go to the **Civil Service
  Commission** [4] [5].
- **Disability Confident.** As a Disability Confident Leader, Welsh Government
  guarantees an interview to candidates meeting the minimum criteria (usually a
  minimum score of 4 per criterion) [4].

## Wales — NHS Wales core values and values-based recruitment

NHS Wales is governed by an all-Wales **Values and Standards of Behaviour
Framework**. Its core values are: **putting quality and safety above all else**;
**integrating improvement into everyday working and eliminating harm, variation
and waste**; **focusing on prevention, health improvement and inequality**;
**working in true partnerships** with partners, organisations and staff; and
**investing in staff** through training and development [6]. These sit within the
Seven Principles of Public Life (the Nolan Principles) [6] [20].

This is a **different value set from the six NHS England Constitution values**
(working together for patients; respect and dignity; commitment to quality of
care; compassion; improving lives; everyone counts) that the KB's
`nhs-values-based-interviews` material and NHS Employers guidance are built on
[22] [23]. The underlying method — values-based recruitment, selecting people
whose values align with the organisation's — is shared, but the Welsh wording,
publisher and governance are distinct [7] [22].

Health Education and Improvement Wales (HEIW), the statutory workforce body for
NHS Wales, operationalises this in its **Values Based Recruitment & Selection
Policy & Procedure** (issued December 2022). It requires recruiting managers to
"make recruitment decisions based on a candidate's ability to undertake the role
and demonstrate HEIW's Values and Behaviours", with "an emphasis on the
alignment of an individual's values with HEIW and not on experience alone", and —
distinctively — to "actively consider Welsh language skills as part of the
recruitment process" while never treating Welsh-language applicants less
favourably [7].

## Wales — the Welsh language as an assessed requirement

This is the document's most distinctive material and it is genuinely
statutory. The **Welsh Language Standards**, made under the **Welsh Language
(Wales) Measure 2011** [10], place enforceable duties on public bodies about the
Welsh-language dimension of recruitment. As set out by the Welsh Language
Commissioner:

- **Every new or vacant post must be assessed** for its Welsh-language
  requirement, and categorised as one where Welsh is **essential**, **desirable**,
  **needs to be learned**, or is **not necessary**; a record of the assessment
  must be kept [8].
- If a post is categorised as one where Welsh is essential, desirable or needs to
  be learned, **that must be stated when advertising and the post must be
  advertised in Welsh** [8].
- Bodies **may** additionally use skill **levels** (typically graded across
  Listening, Speaking, Reading and Writing) to tell candidates exactly what
  standard is required; there is no single mandated framework, though many
  organisations align their levels to the **Common European Framework of
  Reference for Languages (CEFR)**, and the Welsh Government is developing a
  CEFR-based framework [8].
- **Language skill can be assessed at interview** — conducting the interview in
  Welsh, or asking some Welsh questions within an English interview, is described
  as "a good way to assess skills", especially where a high level is required
  [8].
- A body **cannot re-advertise at a lower Welsh requirement** simply because it
  failed to appoint a qualified Welsh speaker: unless the job itself changes, the
  assessed requirement stands, and an essential-Welsh post is expected to be
  filled by someone who can meet it "from day one" [8].

A concrete, citable worked example is the **Senedd Commission**. Under its
Official Languages Scheme, since 16 July 2018 all new and vacant posts are
advertised with **courtesy-level Welsh as a minimum**, unless a higher level
(1–5) is identified; a **language-skills matrix** defines levels 1–5 across
Listening, Speaking, Reading and Writing; and **skills are assessed at interview
or in other assessment-stage exercises**, with successful candidates who fall
short of courtesy level committing to training during probation [9]. This shows
the abstract Standards translated into an operational, assessed recruitment
requirement.

The Welsh Government itself applies the interview-language dimension: candidates
may request an interview through the medium of Welsh, applications in Welsh "will
not be treated less favourably", and for some **Welsh Essential** roles "only
applications in Welsh will be accepted"; where a full Welsh-speaking panel is not
possible, simultaneous translation is arranged [4].

## Northern Ireland — the NICS Competency Framework

The **Northern Ireland Civil Service Competency Framework** (April 2014) is the
core NI divergence. It "sets out how all of us in the NICS should work", puts the
civil-service values of **integrity, honesty, objectivity and impartiality** at
its heart, and aligns to three high-level leadership behaviours — Set Direction,
Engage People, Deliver Results [11]. It outlines **ten competencies in three
clusters** [11]:

- **Setting Direction:** Seeing the Big Picture; Changing and Improving; Making
  Effective Decisions.
- **Engaging People:** Leading and Communicating; Collaborating and Partnering;
  Building Capability for All.
- **Delivering Results:** Delivering Value for Money; Managing a Quality Service;
  Delivering at Pace; Achieving Outcomes through Delivery Partners.

Structural facts documented in the framework itself [11]:

- **Six levels**, mapped to grades: Level 1 (Administrative Assistant / Officer);
  Level 2 (Executive Officer 2 / 1); Level 3 (Staff Officer / Deputy Principal);
  Level 4 (Grade 7 / Grade 6); Level 5 (Grade 5); Level 6 (Grade 3 / Permanent
  Secretary).
- Competencies are "discrete and cumulative … a person demonstrating a competency
  at level 3 should be demonstrating levels 1 and 2 as a matter of course".
- Each competency lists **effective and ineffective behaviours** at every level —
  the NICS analogue of the Civil Service Behaviours' example statements.
- Not all competencies apply to every post: "Most of us will need to focus on a
  number of competencies, usually **around six**, identified with our manager".

**Note the family resemblance without identity.** Several competency names
(Seeing the Big Picture, Changing and Improving, Making Effective Decisions,
Managing a Quality Service, Delivering at Pace) echo the Whitehall Behaviours,
because both descend from the earlier cross-government competency work — but the
NICS set has **ten competencies in three clusters**, whereas current Success
Profiles has **nine Behaviours with no clusters** [11] [2]. A candidate must
prepare for the NICS framework named in the pack, not the GOV.UK Behaviours.

## Northern Ireland — NICS recruitment process and oversight

NICS competitions publish a **Candidate Information Booklet (CIB)** setting out
the selection process and assessment methods; the general flow is application
validation, a sift against eligibility and shortlisting criteria, then one or
more assessment methods — tests, assessment centres, presentations, and an
interview [13]. "The NICS uses **Competence Based Interviews** to assess
candidates' suitability" [13] [14].

The NICS interview guidance tells candidates a competence-based interview does
**not** ask them to talk through previous jobs from start to finish or give
generalised background, but **does** require them to focus on the required
competences and give **specific examples** from experience; it offers a
Situation–Task–Action–Result structure for each example and invites examples
"from any area of your work / life experiences" [14].

Merit is safeguarded by the **Civil Service Commissioners for Northern Ireland**,
"established by statute to uphold the Merit Principle in selection for appointment
to the NICS" [15]. Their **Recruitment Code** interprets the Merit Principle
through four principles — appointments on merit; processes fit for purpose;
processes fair and consistently applied; appointments made openly, accountably
and transparently — and the Commissioners audit NICS recruitment, chair all open
Senior Civil Service competitions, and hear certain appeals under the NICS Code
of Ethics [15]. This is a **separate body** from the GB Civil Service Commission
that regulates Welsh Government and Whitehall recruitment [5] [15].

## Northern Ireland — PSNI uses the College of Policing CVF

**Correcting the brief's premise:** PSNI is not governed by a bespoke NI police
framework — it **uses the College of Policing Competency and Values Framework**,
reproduced and adapted under College Licence SF00312 [16]. The PSNI CVF document
carries the identical 2024 structure: three values (**Courage; Respect and
empathy; Public service**) and six competencies (**We are emotionally aware; We
take ownership; We collaborate; We support and inspire; We analyse critically;
We are innovative and open-minded**) [16]. These are the same 2024 CVF names
documented in `police-competency-values-framework` and the parent landscape [1].

In recruitment, PSNI candidates who pass the Initial Selection Test attend an
**Assessment Centre** of written exercises and role plays in a simulated policing
environment, "tested against the Competency Values Framework (CVF) for Policing
Professionals", specifically at **Level 1 – Practitioner** [17]. "Professional
assessors and lay assessors will assess you for defined values and behaviours
during exercises. They will independently mark and then agree your rating" — an
observe-and-agree model consistent with College guidance — and the PSNI process
(including the assessment centre) is a bespoke design delivered by Deloitte [17].

## Northern Ireland — HSC values-based recruitment

Health and Social Care in NI (the five HSC trusts) recruits against four shared
**HSC values**: **Working Together**, **Excellence**, **Openness & Honesty**, and
**Compassion**, which "provide clarity for all HSC staff, including prospective
staff, on the values you should live every day, and the behaviours expected of
you, regardless of the HSC organisation you work for" [18]. Behaviours "define
'how' we are expected to approach our work and sit alongside 'what' we do" [18].
This is NI's counterpart to NHS England VBR and NHS Wales VBR — the same
selecting-for-values method, a distinct value set [18] [22].

# Question Bank

## Wales — Welsh Government Success Profiles style

1. Tell us about a time you had to understand how your team's work fitted into a wider organisational or governmental priority, and how you focused your effort accordingly. *(composed — in the style of Success Profiles Behaviour "Seeing the big picture" [2])*
2. Describe a situation where you used evidence from several sources to reach a well-reasoned decision under time pressure. *(composed — in the style of Success Profiles Behaviour "Making effective decisions" [2])*
3. Give an example of a working relationship you built with another team or organisation to deliver something neither could deliver alone. *(composed — in the style of Success Profiles Behaviour "Working together" [2])*
4. Tell us about a time you improved the way something was done, even though the existing way was accepted. *(composed — in the style of Success Profiles Behaviour "Changing and improving" [2])*
5. What kinds of task do you find yourself naturally drawn to, and what does that tell us about you? *(composed — a strengths-style prompt of the kind Welsh Government asks unrehearsed at interview [4])*
6. Describe how you delivered a piece of work to a tight deadline while keeping the quality high. *(composed — in the style of Success Profiles Behaviour "Delivering at pace" [2])*

## Wales — NHS Wales values-based style

1. Tell us about a time you put the quality and safety of a service or a person above other pressures. What did you weigh up? *(composed — in the style of the NHS Wales core value "putting quality and safety above all else" [6])*
2. Describe a time you spotted waste, harm or unnecessary variation and did something to reduce it. *(composed — in the style of the NHS Wales core value on continuous improvement [6])*
3. Give an example of working in genuine partnership with another organisation or with colleagues to improve a service user's experience. *(composed — in the style of the NHS Wales core value "working in true partnerships" [6])*
4. Tell us about a time you helped someone develop, or invested in your own learning to do your job better. *(composed — in the style of the NHS Wales core value on investing in staff [6])*
5. Describe an occasion when you focused on prevention rather than only fixing a problem after it arose. *(composed — in the style of the NHS Wales core value on prevention and health improvement [6])*

## Wales — Welsh language and bilingual working

 1. This post is categorised as one where Welsh-language skills are desirable. Tell us how you would use, or develop, your Welsh in this role. *(composed — in the style of a Welsh Language Standards post categorisation [8])*
 2. How would you deal with a phone call from a member of the public who wants to speak Welsh, if your own Welsh is at courtesy level? *(composed — in the style of a courtesy-level Welsh assessment at interview [8] [9])*
 3. Describe how you would ensure a service you run treats Welsh no less favourably than English. *(composed — in the style of a Welsh Language Standards service duty [8] [10])*
 4. Tell us about a time you worked, or would work, effectively as part of a bilingual team. *(composed — in the style of the Senedd Commission bilingual-ethos recruitment aim [9])*

 3. Give an example of when you were sensitive, caring and respectful towards someone in distress. *(composed — in the style of the HSC value "Compassion" [18])*

## Shared — follow-up probes (Wales and NI)

 1. You have described what the team did. What specifically was your individual contribution? *(composed — a probe reflecting the guidance to "Use 'I' not 'we'" and evidence your own role [4] [14])*
 2. Why did you take that action rather than an alternative — what were you trying to achieve? *(composed — a values/reasoning probe in the style of the Situation–Task–Action–Result structure NICS recommends [14])*
 3. What was the outcome, and what did you learn — especially if it did not go entirely to plan? *(composed — in the style of the "what did you learn" prompt in NICS interview guidance [14])*
 4. Can you give a second, different example that shows the same competency in another setting? *(composed — reflecting the Welsh Government tip to prepare more examples than provided on the form [4])*
 5. Give an example of a time you handled conflict in the workplace. *(sourced: My World of Work, Skills Development Scotland, "A complete guide to competency-based interviews")* [24]

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact.**

- **Identify the nation and the body before you prepare anything.** The single
  biggest error is preparing for the wrong framework. In Cardiff you are almost
  certainly facing **Success Profiles** [3]; in Belfast's civil service you are
  facing the **NICS ten-competency framework** [11]; for PSNI you are facing the
  **College of Policing CVF at Level 1** [17]. Read the advert and the candidate
  pack (the CIB in NICS) and prepare against the *named* competencies or elements.
- **Use STAR, but weight it the way the guidance says.** Both Welsh Government and
  NICS point to a Situation–Task–Action–Result shape, and Welsh Government quotes
  the instruction to "keep the situation and task parts brief; concentrate on the
  action and the result" [4] [14]. The load-bearing parts are the **action** (what
  *you* did) and the **result**.
- **Bank more examples than you will be asked for.** Welsh Government explicitly
  advises preparing more examples than you put on the application, because the
  panel may probe for others [4]; NICS asks you to "come to the interview prepared
  to discuss … a range of examples" [14].
- **For values interviews, prepare the *why*.** NHS Wales and HSC NI interviews
  select for value alignment [6] [7] [18]. Do not just narrate what you did —
  articulate what mattered to you and why, because that is where the value shows.
- **Take the Welsh-language requirement at face value.** If a post is Welsh
  **essential**, expect to demonstrate the skill and be prepared for part or all
  of the interview in Welsh; if **desirable** or **to be learned**, be ready to
  say honestly what level you are at and how you would develop [4] [8]. Do not
  overstate your level on the form — bodies are advised to check [8].
- **Match the level.** NICS competencies are defined at six levels and Success
  Profiles behaviours at six grade bands; a Grade 7/Level 4 answer should show
  anticipation and shaping, not just personal diligence [2] [11]. Read the
  descriptor for the level of the post.
- **A worked skeleton (NICS "Collaborating and Partnering", Level 3).**
  *Situation/Task:* a cross-departmental project stalled because two teams held
  incompatible data standards. *Action:* "I convened a short working session,
  set out the shared objective, listened to each team's constraint, and proposed
  a mapping that let both keep their systems; I challenged one assumption
  respectfully rather than letting it block us." *Result:* "We agreed the standard
  in a fortnight; the dataset went live on time and is still in use." Note the
  first-person actions, the collaboration *across* a boundary, and the
  challenge-with-respect — all Level 3 effective behaviours in the framework [11].

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

- **Welsh Government panels** are structured: a minimum of two members with a
  chair, at least one independent of the role; the purpose is to test suitability
  against advertised criteria, give every candidate a fair chance to evidence
  them, and produce an **order of merit based on scores** [4]. Interviews run
  roughly 45–70 minutes depending on grade [3]. Assessors should note the
  advance-questions policy: opening Experience and Behaviour questions are shared
  in writing beforehand, but strengths and follow-up probes are not, preserving
  some spontaneity [4].
- **The Welsh Government sift discipline** mirrors the central rule that a panel
  "cannot assume what is not included in the example, they can only assess what
  you have written" — a candidate-facing statement of evidence-only scoring [3].
- **NICS assessors** work from the competency framework's **effective / ineffective
  behaviour** indicators at the relevant level, scoring specific examples rather
  than general background [11] [14]. Candidates may request their interview
  assessment booklet as feedback, though questions may be withheld where further
  interviews are possible [13].
- **NICS oversight** is external and auditable: the Civil Service Commissioners
  for NI audit recruitment against the Recruitment Code, require publication of
  recruitment information including any use of permitted exceptions to merit, and
  chair open Senior Civil Service competitions [15].
- **PSNI assessment centres** use trained **professional assessors plus lay
  assessors**, who mark **independently and then agree** a rating per exercise,
  assessing against defined CVF values and behaviours at Level 1 — an observe,
  record and agree model [17].
- **Merit and consistency** are the throughline in both nations: Welsh Government
  recruits under the Civil Service Commission Recruitment Principles [5], and
  NICS under the Commissioners' four principles — merit; fit for purpose; fair and
  consistent; open, accountable and transparent [15].

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact.**

- **Preparing for GOV.UK Behaviours when you are facing the NICS framework.** They
  overlap in name but not in set — ten competencies in three clusters, not nine
  behaviours [11] [2]. Preparing the wrong list leaves gaps against the pack.
- **Assuming Whitehall Success Profiles detail applies unchanged in Wales.** The
  framework is the same, but Welsh Government adds its own features — advance
  opening questions, Welsh-language options, name-free sifting — that a
  generic-England preparation misses [4].
- **Treating the Welsh-language line on the advert as decorative.** "Welsh
  essential" means assessed and expected from day one; some Welsh-essential roles
  accept applications only in Welsh [4] [8]. Under-reading this can end an
  application before interview.
- **Overstating your Welsh level.** Bodies are advised to check declared skill,
  and an essential post cannot simply be re-advertised at a lower level to fit the
  field [8]. Claim the level you can actually evidence.
- **Narrating team effort in the first person plural.** Both Welsh Government and
  NICS want *your* contribution; "we" answers under-evidence you [4] [14].
- **Assuming PSNI runs its own police framework.** It does not — it is the College
  of Policing CVF at Level 1; prepare from College CVF material [16] [17].
- **For interviewers: drifting from the structured plan.** Merit and consistency
  are the documented rationale of both nations' processes [5] [15]; unstructured
  interviewing undermines comparability and invites the fairness risks below.

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.**

1. **Framework-identification drill (20 min).** Take one live Welsh Government
   vacancy, one NICS vacancy and one PSNI/HSC advert. For each, list the named
   elements/competencies/values and the Welsh-language category. You now have
   three *different* preparation lists — that is the point.
2. **Level-lift drill (45 min).** Pick one NICS competency and read its effective
   behaviours at Levels 2 and 4 in the framework [11]. Write a STAR answer at
   Level 2, then rewrite it to evidence Level 4. Name exactly what changed
   (scope, others involved, anticipation).
3. **Values-probe rehearsal (paired, 20 min).** Answer question 7 or 29 while a
   partner asks only "why?" and "what mattered to you there?" — the reasoning is
   where NHS Wales / HSC values-based scoring lives [6] [18].
4. **Welsh-language readiness (15 min).** For a post marked Welsh *desirable*,
   draft an honest two-sentence statement of your current level across listening,
   speaking, reading and writing, and one concrete step you would take to develop
   it during probation, in the Senedd Commission style [9].
5. **Assessor's seat (30 min).** Using the four HSC values [18] or the six CVF
   competencies [16] as criteria, score a friend's recorded answer on a simple
   1–5 scale with written justification. Notice how much cleaner scoring is when
   the question targeted one named value or competency — the design logic of every
   framework here.
6. **Timed set (25 min).** Answer questions 16–21 aloud, one take each, 3–4
   minutes per answer, to build the density a competence-based interview expects
   [14].

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

- **The Equality Act 2010 applies across Great Britain and Northern Ireland's
  employment field**, so both nations' processes must avoid direct and indirect
  discrimination in selection; Welsh Government states its recruitment is
  underpinned by the Equality Act 2010 at every stage [4]. The public sector
  equality duty in **s.149** of that Act applies to Welsh public bodies [21].
- **Section 75 of the Northern Ireland Act 1998 is a distinct, additional
  statutory duty** on NI public authorities. It requires them, in carrying out
  their functions, to have "due regard to the need to promote equality of
  opportunity" across **nine grounds** — religious belief, political opinion,
  racial group, age, marital status, sexual orientation; between men and women
  generally; between persons with and without a disability; and between persons
  with and without dependants — **and**, separately, to have regard to the
  desirability of promoting **good relations** between persons of different
  religious belief, political opinion or racial group [19]. The good-relations
  limb and the "political opinion" and "dependants" grounds have no direct
  Great Britain PSED equivalent, which is why NI recruitment fairness is not
  simply the Equality Act 2010 duty [19] [21].
- **The Welsh Language Standards create enforceable recruitment obligations**
  distinct from equality law: assessing and recording each post's Welsh
  requirement, advertising qualifying posts in Welsh, and not treating Welsh
  applicants less favourably [8] [10]. Welsh Government implements the
  last of these directly [4].
- **Reasonable adjustments** run through both nations' processes: Welsh
  Government extends timescales and adapts assessments as adjustments and is a
  Disability Confident Leader [4]; HSC and NICS packs likewise provide for
  adjustments (candidates should request them early via the recruitment contact)
  [13] [18].
- **Merit and open competition** are the statutory fairness backbone: the Civil
  Service Commission Recruitment Principles for Welsh Government [5] and the NI
  Commissioners' Recruitment Code for NICS [15], both grounded in the Seven
  Principles of Public Life [20].

# Risks & Caveats

- **The NICS Competency Framework is dated April 2014.** It remains the live
  recruitment framework on the NICS recruitment site as verified 2026-07-20 [11]
  [12], but it is over a decade old; candidates and panels should confirm the
  version referenced in the current candidate pack, as a refresh could land
  without much fanfare.
- **PSNI's CVF document was reproduced/adapted under College licence and carries
  a June 2025 update marker** [16]; police-staff competitions may be in
  transition between CVF versions even where officer competitions use 2024 [17].
  Confirm the version in the specific competition.
- **Welsh-language "levels" are not standardised.** The Welsh Language Commissioner
  is explicit that there is no single mandated level framework and that levels
  vary between organisations (many use CEFR) [8]; do not assume one body's "Level
  3" equals another's. Treat any specific level scale as body-specific until
  confirmed.
- **NHS Wales / HSC value wordings are governance-level statements**; individual
  health boards and trusts may express or brand them locally. The core values
  cited here are the all-Wales framework [6] and the shared HSC set [18]; local
  trust adverts may add board-specific values.
- **No official open interview-question bank exists** for Welsh Government or
  NICS; every question in this document's bank is composed in the style of the
  cited framework or drawn from a general careers source, and must not be
  presented as a real employer question.
- **Legacy/registry currency notes carry over.** The England contrasts here rely
  on the NHS VBR framework (2016, publisher HEE now merged into NHS England) and
  the 2024 CVF, both flagged in `SOURCE_REGISTRY.md`; those caveats apply when
  reading the contrasts.

# Open Questions

- Does NICS publish, anywhere public, **level-specific scoring scales** (analogous
  to the Success Profiles 7-point scale) for its Competence Based Interviews? The
  framework gives effective/ineffective indicators [11] but no numeric scale was
  located; the NICS deep-dive should try to source one. *(Would resolve: a NICS
  assessor guidance document.)*
- Is there a **single all-NHS-Wales values-based interview framework** (an HEIW
  or Welsh Government equivalent of the England HEE VBR framework), beyond
  individual policies like HEIW's [7]? None was located as a national framework
  document in this pass.
- What **CEFR-based Welsh-language framework** is the Welsh Government developing
  under the Welsh Language and Education (Wales) Bill, and will it standardise the
  levels used in recruitment across bodies [8]? Worth re-checking as the Bill
  progresses.
- Do NI **local councils** and other s.75 public authorities publish their own
  competency frameworks distinct from the NICS one? This document covers the
  civil service, policing and HSC; council-level frameworks were not surveyed.
- How is **Irish-language** capability treated in NI public-sector recruitment —
  a parallel to, but out of scope of, the Welsh-language material here?
