# Executive Summary

This document answers a question the rest of the knowledge base leaves open:
what do Scottish public-sector candidates actually face, given that Scotland is a
devolved administration? The headline finding is a mix of "the same" and
"genuinely different", and both halves are useful.

**The Scottish Government uses Success Profiles — the UK Civil Service
framework — not a Scottish variant.** Its own candidate guide states plainly
that it uses "a recruitment framework called 'Success Profiles', which is also
used across the wider Civil Service", assessing the same three commonly-used
elements (Behaviours, Experience, Technical) against the identical nine Civil
Service Behaviours with the identical published definitions [1] [2] [3]. The
Crown Office and Procurator Fiscal Service (COPFS) — a Scottish organisation
the knowledge base already cites for the Success Profiles scale — likewise
assesses against the five Success Profiles elements [13]. So for the civil
service, "Scotland is the same as the rest of the UK Civil Service" is the
documented, honest answer; the distinctiveness is in process detail, most
notably that the Scottish Government now **shares interview questions in advance**
[6].

**Police Scotland is genuinely different.** It is not governed by the College of
Policing Competency and Values Framework, which is an England-and-Wales
instrument. Police Scotland publishes its *own* CVF, "modified with permission
from © College of Policing Ltd", which retains the **2016-style** structure the
England-and-Wales CVF abandoned in 2024 — six competencies grouped into three
clusters, including the pre-2024 names "We are collaborative" and "We deliver,
support and inspire" — and underpins them with Police Scotland's own values
(Integrity, Fairness, Respect and Human Rights) drawn from the *Code of Ethics
for Policing in Scotland* [14] [16] [17]. **NHSScotland** is also distinct: its
values-based recruitment rests on four NHSScotland values (care and compassion;
dignity and respect; openness, honesty and responsibility; quality and teamwork)
from the 2020 Workforce Vision, not the six NHS Constitution values that drive
NHS England VBR [18] [19] [20] [22].

Confidence is **High**: every central claim rests on an official Scottish
government page, a published Police Scotland or NHSScotland framework, or
legislation, opened and extracted directly. Cross-border contrasts are labelled
analysis pointing back to those primary sources.

# Key Takeaways

- The **Scottish Government uses Success Profiles**, the UK-wide Civil Service
  framework, with the same nine Behaviours and definitions — it is *not* a
  Scottish-only framework. *(documented)* [1] [2] [3]
- Distinctive Scottish Government practice: **interview questions are shared in
  advance** of the interview; interviews run 45–60 minutes before a panel of two
  to three, always paired with at least one additional exercise. *(documented)*
  [6]
- **COPFS** — cited elsewhere in this knowledge base for the Success Profiles
  scale — is a **Scottish** body and assesses against the five Success Profiles
  elements, with Strengths not currently used. *(documented)* [13]
- **Police Scotland is not on the College of Policing CVF.** It runs its own
  CVF, adapted from College material but retaining the **2016 three-cluster
  structure** and its own four values from the Code of Ethics for Policing in
  Scotland. *(documented)* [14] [16] [17]
- The Police Scotland assessment day ends in a **20-minute competency-based
  interview of four questions**, each mapped to a named CVF competency, after a
  group task and a situational judgement exercise. *(documented)* [15]
- **NHSScotland** values-based recruitment uses **four** NHSScotland values, not
  the six NHS Constitution values that anchor NHS England VBR. *(documented)*
  [18] [19] [22]
- **Scottish local government** has no single national framework: councils
  recruit through the shared **myjobscotland** portal but assess against each
  post's own person specification and values, competency-interview style with
  STAR. *(documented)* [29]
- For candidates, the practical rule is the same as anywhere: identify the exact
  employer and its named framework, then map evidence to *its* behaviours or
  values — a Success Profiles behaviour story is not a Police Scotland CVF
  answer. *(guidance)*

# Purpose

Scottish candidates preparing for public-sector interviews are, across the rest
of this knowledge base, served largely England-centric guidance: Police Scotland
and NHS Scotland each receive only a passing mention. This document exists to
give them, and the interviewers who assess them, a properly Scotland-specific
reference. It answers three questions: (1) does the Scottish Government use the
UK Civil Service Success Profiles framework, a variant, or its own scheme? (2)
what does Police Scotland use, given that the College of Policing CVF governs
England and Wales only? (3) how does NHSScotland values-based recruitment differ
from the NHS England model already covered here? It then adds Scottish local
government, COPFS and the wider devolved bodies, and marks clearly where Scottish
practice is the same as England-and-Wales and where it genuinely diverges.

# Scope

Covered: recruitment and interview frameworks for the **Scottish Government and
its agencies** (Success Profiles); **COPFS**; **Police Scotland** (its CVF and
officer/staff selection); **NHSScotland** values-based recruitment; **Scottish
local government** via myjobscotland; and named devolved bodies such as the
Scottish Courts and Tribunals Service. The emphasis is on how each body defines,
assesses and scores behaviours or values at interview, and on the points of
genuine devolved distinctiveness.

Not covered: the full grade-band descriptors of the nine Civil Service
Behaviours (see `uk-civil-service-success-profiles`); the England-and-Wales
College of Policing CVF in depth (see `police-competency-values-framework`); NHS
England VBR mechanics (see `nhs-values-based-interviews`); Wales and Northern
Ireland (see `wales-ni-public-sector-frameworks`); answer-technique theory (STAR
and variants); and non-recruitment HR uses of these frameworks. Scottish
university and NHS-clinical-training admissions selection are out of scope.

# Definitions

Prefer the shared `GLOSSARY.md`. Document-specific usages:

- **Success Profiles (Scottish Government sense)** — the UK Civil Service
  recruitment framework as adopted by the Scottish Government, assessing a
  per-vacancy mix of Behaviours, Experience and Technical (and, for some bodies,
  Ability and Strengths) [2] [13].
- **Police Scotland CVF** — Police Scotland's own Competency and Values
  Framework: six competencies in three clusters, three cumulative levels, and
  four values, adapted with permission from College of Policing material [14].
- **NHSScotland values** — the four shared values of Scotland's health service:
  care and compassion; dignity and respect; openness, honesty and
  responsibility; quality and teamwork [18].
- **Devolved administration** — a government (here, the Scottish Government)
  exercising powers devolved from the UK Parliament; the civil service serving it
  remains part of the *UK* Home Civil Service, which is why Success Profiles
  applies [1].

Two context points frame everything below.

First, the constitutional reason the civil service is *the same*: the people who
staff the Scottish Government are part of the UK Home Civil Service even though
they serve a devolved administration. The Scottish Government's own guide is
explicit that Success Profiles "is also used across the wider Civil Service" [1],
and appointments are made under the UK **Civil Service Recruitment Principles**,
which the guide cites directly [6]. So the civil-service half of Scottish
public-sector recruitment is a devolved *user* of a UK-wide framework, not a
separate framework.

Second, the reason policing and health *diverge*: policing and health are
devolved competences with their own Scottish statutes and bodies. Police Scotland
(the Police Service of Scotland) is a single national force created in 2013,
overseen by the Scottish Police Authority, and it authored its own CVF rather
than adopting the College of Policing instrument that governs the 43
England-and-Wales forces [14]. NHSScotland is a separate health system whose
shared values were set by the Scottish Government's 2020 Workforce Vision,
*Everyone Matters* [18], and are embedded through NHSScotland workforce policies
[19] — a different value set from the NHS Constitution for England [22].

## Scottish Government: Success Profiles, as the UK framework

The Scottish Government states that it uses "a recruitment framework called
'Success Profiles', which is also used across the wider Civil Service", and that
the guide covers applying "for roles in government, our agencies, and certain
public bodies in Scotland" [1]. It assesses **three** of the Success Profiles
elements as standard — **Behaviours, Experience and Technical** — with "not all
elements … relevant to every role" so each vacancy names its own mix [2].

The Behaviours are the **nine Civil Service Behaviours**, defined verbatim as in
the UK framework [3]:

1. **Seeing the big picture** — understand how your role fits with and supports
   organisational objectives; recognise wider Civil Service priorities and ensure
   work is in the national interest.
2. **Changing and improving** — seek out opportunities to create effective change
   and suggest innovative ideas; review ways of working.
3. **Making effective decisions** — use evidence and knowledge to support
   accurate, expert decisions and advice; weigh alternatives, implications and
   risks.
4. **Leadership** — show pride and passion for public service; create and engage
   others in delivering a shared vision; value difference, diversity and
   inclusion.
5. **Communicating and influencing** — communicate purpose and direction with
   clarity, integrity and enthusiasm.
6. **Working together** — form effective partnerships and relationships with
   people internally and externally, from diverse backgrounds.
7. **Developing self and others** — focus on continuous learning and development
   for self, others and the organisation.
8. **Managing a quality service** — deliver service objectives with professional
   excellence, expertise and efficiency, accounting for diverse customer needs.
9. **Delivering at pace** — take responsibility for delivering timely and quality
   results with focus and drive.

These are word-for-word the definitions on GOV.UK's Civil Service Behaviours page
[11]; the Scottish Government has adopted the UK framework wholesale, not a
Scottish rewrite. Behaviours are assessed through the Supporting Statement, an
interview, and assessments such as role play, presentation, analysis exercise or
judgement tests, and may be probed with situational questions [4].

**The application and assessment process** is a three-stage funnel: (1) online
application with an up-to-date CV and a Supporting Statement evidencing the
Success Profile criteria; (2) assessment — an interview plus at least one
additional exercise; (3) offer and pre-employment checks [5]. At interview,
"interviews typically last 45 to 60 minutes and are conducted by a panel of two
to three people", and — the most distinctive Scottish feature — "the interview
questions will be shared with you in advance as part of your interview
invitation, giving you time to prepare" [6]. The guide "strongly advise[s]
against writing or memorising full scripted answers" and directs candidates to
use the advance questions to select their strongest examples [6]. Candidates then
complete "at least one additional exercise" — presentation, role play, written
task, verbal response or portfolio review — normally on the same day [6].

**Scoring** is against the advertised Success Profile criteria: panels meet after
all interviews, give each candidate an overall score, and place candidates in
**merit order**, with offers made to the highest scorers and strong non-appointed
candidates offered a place on a **Reserve list** [6]. Application-stage sifting
scores the CV and Supporting Statement "as a whole" against each advertised
criterion, then ranks in merit order [4]. The guide recommends — but does not
require — the **STARR** structure (situation, task, action, result, reflection)
for the Supporting Statement and prompts candidates to include "specific facts
and figures" [9].

## COPFS: a Scottish body on the five-element framework

The Crown Office and Procurator Fiscal Service — Scotland's prosecution service,
and the source this knowledge base has repeatedly cited for the Success Profiles
descriptor scale — assesses applicants "against the Success Profile outlined in
the application pack" and points candidates to the UK Civil Service Success
Profiles material [13]. COPFS names all **five** elements — Behaviours, Strengths,
Experience, Ability, Technical — but records that **Strengths is "not currently
being used within COPFS"** [13]. Its personal statement has a **750-word** limit,
STAR is offered but candidates "do not have to" use it, and they are told to
"write in the first person — use 'I'" [13]. This is worth making explicit for
readers of the rest of the knowledge base: the organisation behind the most-cited
Success Profiles descriptor page is Scottish, and it is applying the *central* UK
Civil Service framework, not a devolved one.

## Police Scotland: its own CVF, not the College of Policing CVF

Police Scotland publishes a **Competency and Values Framework (CVF) for Police
Scotland**, whose cover states it is "Modified with permission from © College of
Policing Ltd" [14]. It is structurally the **2016** College model that
England-and-Wales policing has since replaced. It has **six competencies grouped
into three clusters**, each competency at **three cumulative levels** matched
broadly to Constable/Practitioner (Level 1), Sergeant–Chief Inspector/Middle
Manager (Level 2) and Superintendent and above/Senior Manager (Level 3) [14]:

- **Cluster: Resolute, compassionate and committed** — *We are emotionally
  aware*; *We take ownership* [14].
- **Cluster: Inclusive, enabling and visionary leadership** — *We are
  collaborative*; *We deliver, support and inspire* [14].
- **Cluster: Intelligent, creative and informed policing** — *We analyse
  critically*; *We are innovative and open-minded* [14].

Two of these names — **"We are collaborative"** and **"We deliver, support and
inspire"** — are the *pre-2024* wording. The England-and-Wales College of Policing
CVF was updated in 2024, which abolished the clusters and renamed those two
competencies to "We collaborate" and "We support and inspire" [16]. Police
Scotland has kept the older names and the cluster grouping, so a candidate
preparing from current England-and-Wales CVF material would be preparing against
the wrong competency labels.

The **values** differ from *both* College versions. Police Scotland's CVF is
underpinned by the **Code of Ethics for Policing in Scotland**, expressed as four
values — **Integrity, Fairness, Respect** and **Human Rights** — each with its own
behavioural statements [14]. For contrast, the 2016 College CVF used integrity,
impartiality, public service and transparency [17], and the 2024 College CVF uses
respect and empathy, courage, and public service [16] — neither matches Police
Scotland. The Human Rights value is explicit that "'Policing means protecting
human rights'" and ties behaviours to specific European Convention articles [14].

**Officer selection** runs: registration and application; the **Police Scotland
Entrance Test (PSET)**, assessing understanding of language, information handling
and numeracy; a **fitness assessment** — a multi-stage bleep test to level 5.4;
and an **assessment day** where "all of the exercises are assessed against the
Police Scotland Competencies and Values Framework (CVF)" [15]. The assessment day
comprises a **group task** — about 50 minutes: 10 minutes' reading of a ~500–600
word policing scenario, 30-minute group discussion and briefing, 10-minute
assessor Q&A; a **situational judgement exercise** — about 60 minutes, reactive,
verbally presented hypothetical scenarios; and, on success, a **20-minute
competency-based interview of four questions**, where "all questions align to a
competency within the Police Scotland CVF, the competency assessed will be
outlined to you with the delivery of the question" [15]. Vetting follows [15].

## NHSScotland: four values, not six

NHSScotland recruits on its shared values, set out in the Scottish Government's
2020 Workforce Vision, *Everyone Matters*: "the values that are shared across
Scotland's Health Service are: care and compassion; dignity and respect;
openness, honesty and responsibility; quality and teamwork" [18]. These four
values are embedded in the **NHSScotland workforce policies**, which are
"person-centred and should be applied using the NHSScotland values" [19]. In
practice, *Everyone Matters* says staff must "demonstrate our values in the way we
work", "use our values to guide the decisions we take", "identify and deal with
behaviours that don't live up to our expectations" and "be responsible for the
way we work and not just the work we do" [18].

For recruitment, NHSScotland Careers tells candidates that "if you're applying
for any of our roles at NHSScotland, you should know and share our core values",
and works through each value with the kinds of example a candidate should prepare
— for care and compassion, a time you volunteered or "taken a lot of care with a
piece of work"; for openness, honesty and responsibility, a time you "taken
charge of a task on behalf of a group" or "taken responsibility for something
when you didn't succeed" — recommending the **STAR** method to structure answers
[20]. Individual boards operationalise the values: **NHS 24**, the national
telehealth service, publishes a **Values and Behaviours Framework** giving each of
the four values a definition and **four behavioural indicators**, required "of
every person within NHS 24 as a base level, regardless of their role" [21].

This is a real contrast with NHS England, whose values-based recruitment is built
on the **six** values of the **NHS Constitution for England** — working together
for patients; respect and dignity; commitment to quality of care; compassion;
improving lives; everyone counts [22] — and the March 2016 Health Education
England VBR Framework [23]. Same *idea* — recruit for values, probe with
structured interviews — but a different *value set* and different governing
documents.

## Scottish local government and other devolved bodies

Scotland's 32 councils recruit through a shared jobs portal, **myjobscotland**,
but there is **no single national competency framework**: each vacancy is assessed
against its own person specification and the council's own values, in a
competency-interview style. myjobscotland's candidate guidance is generic —
prepare, research the panel, read the person specification — and points
candidates to Skills Development Scotland's **My World of Work** for competency
practice [29] [30].

The **Scottish Courts and Tribunals Service (SCTS)**, a devolved public body,
illustrates typical practice: "we use competency based interviews to evaluate your
competence … the questions will focus on the values and behaviours contained
within the Person Specification", each board member owns a set of values, and "a
0–4 scoring mechanism will be used for each of the values in the person
specification" [24]. This mirrors the Success Profiles logic — structured
questions, per-criterion scoring, panel reconciliation — without being badged as
Success Profiles.

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact.**

- **Identify the exact employer first — the framework is not interchangeable.**
  A Scottish Government or COPFS role is Success Profiles: prepare behaviour and
  experience stories against the nine Civil Service Behaviours [2] [13]. A Police
  Scotland role is the Police Scotland CVF: prepare against its six competencies
  and four values, using the *2016-style* names, not current England-and-Wales
  CVF wording [14]. An NHSScotland role is values-based on four values [18]. The
  single biggest avoidable error is bringing the wrong framework's vocabulary.
- **For the Scottish Government, exploit the advance questions properly.** Because
  the questions are shared before the interview [6], the preparation task shifts
  from "guess the questions" to "choose and sharpen your best evidence". The guide
  explicitly warns against memorising scripts [6]; instead note the key points and
  anticipate follow-ups (see probes 36–38).
- **Use STARR for Scottish Government supporting statements, STAR for
  NHSScotland.** The Scottish Government guide recommends STARR — with an explicit
  reflection step — and specific facts and figures [9]; NHSScotland Careers
  recommends STAR and, for a less-than-perfect result, reflecting on what you
  would do differently [20]. Either way the load-bearing parts are your *action*
  and the *result*.
- **Match the Police Scotland CVF level to the role.** Levels are cumulative:
  Level 1 is Constable/Practitioner, Level 2 supervisory, Level 3 senior [14]. For
  entry, evidence Level 1 indicators — "I take responsibility for my own actions,
  I fulfil my promises", "I work cooperatively with others" — rather than
  strategic, leadership-level material.
- **For values questions, answer the "why", not just the "what".** NHSScotland and
  Police Scotland both assess values, and values questions reward the reasoning
  that reveals what you consider important [20] [14]. Prepare the motive behind
  each story, not only the events.

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

- **Scottish Government.** Applications are scored against each advertised Success
  Profile criterion, assessing the CV and Supporting Statement "as a whole" and
  ranking candidates in merit order before inviting to interview [4]. At
  assessment, the interview and additional exercise(s) are "assessed against the
  Success Profile criteria set out in the job advert"; panels of two to three meet
  after all candidates are seen, agree an overall score, and place candidates in
  merit order, with offers to the highest scorers and a Reserve list for strong
  non-appointees [6]. The candidate-facing pages do not publish a numeric point
  scale, but the wider Civil Service scoring rule — a 7-point scale for
  behaviours, experience and technical, a 4-point scale for strengths — applies to
  Success Profiles competitions generally [12].
- **Police Scotland.** Every assessment-day exercise is "assessed against the
  Police Scotland Competencies and Values Framework (CVF)", and "all of the
  Competencies and Values are assessed throughout the course of the assessment
  day" [15]. The competency-based interview names the competency being assessed as
  each question is delivered — a transparency feature assessors are expected to
  apply [15]. The CVF states its levels are to be used flexibly across frontline
  and non-frontline roles and that they are cumulative [14].
- **NHSScotland.** Values are assessed as a baseline requirement: NHS 24's
  framework states a person "cannot be said to be successful if they do not meet
  and demonstrate these values on a regular basis", with four behavioural
  indicators per value giving assessors concrete evidence to look for [21].
- **Scottish Courts and Tribunals Service.** Each board member owns a set of
  values and behaviours from the person specification and applies "a 0–4 scoring
  mechanism … for each of the values", taking notes to support end-of-interview
  discussion and candidate feedback [24] — a documented, per-criterion Scottish
  scoring example.

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact.**

- **Preparing from England-and-Wales police material for a Police Scotland
  application.** The competency *names* differ — "We are collaborative" versus the
  2024 "We collaborate" — and the *values* differ entirely [14] [16]. Using
  current College of Policing CVF phrasing signals you researched the wrong force.
- **Assuming NHSScotland uses the NHS Constitution values.** It does not — it uses
  four NHSScotland values, not the six English Constitution values [18] [22].
  Naming "everyone counts" or "improving lives" at an NHSScotland interview
  imports the wrong framework.
- **Over-scripting Scottish Government answers.** With questions shared in advance,
  the temptation is to memorise full answers — which the guide explicitly advises
  against [6]; scripted delivery reads as inflexible and collapses under
  follow-ups.
- **Writing "we" throughout.** Both the Scottish Government STARR guidance and
  COPFS tell candidates to evidence what *you* did in the first person [9] [13];
  team-level narration hides your individual contribution from assessors.
- **Treating COPFS or the Scottish Government as "not really Civil Service".** They
  are: the framework, the Recruitment Principles and merit-order appointment are
  UK-wide [1] [6] [13]. Preparation that ignores the Success Profiles element
  definitions under-prepares you.
- **Ignoring the additional exercise.** A Scottish Government interview is never
  the whole assessment — there is always at least one further exercise
  (presentation, role play, written task) scored against the same criteria [6].

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.**

1. **Framework-identification drill (20 min).** Take three live Scottish vacancies
   — one from the Scottish Government jobs site, one Police Scotland role, one
   NHSScotland board. For each, write down which framework applies and list its
   named behaviours/competencies/values from the primary source [2] [14] [18].
   Confirm you are not mixing vocabularies.
2. **Advance-question rehearsal (30 min).** For a Scottish Government behaviour,
   draft the *notes* — not a script — you would prepare if the question were shared
   in advance [6]: the example, the three key points, and the likely answers to
   probes 36–38.
3. **Police Scotland level-match drill (30 min).** Pick one CVF competency and read
   its Level 1 indicators in the framework [14]. Write a STAR answer that hits two
   or three of those exact indicators for an entry-level role, then check you have
   not drifted into Level 2 leadership language.
4. **Values "why" drill, paired (20 min).** Answer question 25 or 27 while a
   partner asks only "why did that matter to you?" and "what were you thinking?"
   [20]. Debrief: did your answer reveal a value or just describe an action?
5. **Assessor's seat with a real scale (30 min).** Using the SCTS 0–4 per-value
   scoring approach [24], score a friend's recorded answer to any Question Bank
   item against one named value, with a written justification. Notice how a named
   target makes scoring more consistent.

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

- The **Equality Act 2010 applies across Great Britain**, including Scotland;
  equality law is largely reserved to the UK Parliament, so the core obligations
  are identical to those covered elsewhere in this knowledge base. The public
  sector equality duty under **section 149** binds Scottish public authorities
  when designing and running recruitment [25].
- **Genuine devolved distinctiveness:** Scotland has its **own specific duties**
  under the *Equality Act 2010 (Specific Duties) (Scotland) Regulations 2012*,
  separate from the England specific duties — requiring listed Scottish
  authorities to publish equality outcomes, report on mainstreaming, gather and
  use employee information, and publish pay-gap information [26]. Scottish
  public-sector recruiters therefore operate under a Scotland-specific reporting
  regime layered on the UK-wide Act.
- Under **section 20** of the Equality Act 2010, employers owe a duty to make
  reasonable adjustments for disabled applicants [27]; the Scottish Government
  operationalises this by supporting recruitment and workplace adjustments and
  running **guaranteed interview schemes** for disabled candidates and armed-forces
  veterans [10]. Police Scotland assesses adjustments case by case within its
  process [15].
- **Section 60** restricts questions about health or disability before a job
  offer, subject to limited exceptions such as establishing the need for
  assessment adjustments [28] — a boundary Scottish panels must respect just as
  elsewhere in the UK.

# Risks & Caveats

- **Frameworks and pages move.** The Scottish Government candidate guide was last
  updated 23 June 2026 — the very change that introduced sharing interview
  questions in advance [1] [6]. Re-verify the assessment pages before relying on
  the advance-questions practice, as it is recent.
- **Police Scotland CVF version.** This document reads the currently published
  Police Scotland CVF PDF, which retains the 2016 structure [14]. Police Scotland
  could align to a newer College model in future; a candidate should confirm the
  current framework from the live recruitment portal before preparing.
- **NHSScotland board-level variation.** The four values are national [18], but
  individual boards add their own behavioural frameworks (NHS 24 is the worked
  example here [21]); other boards may express indicators differently. Check the
  specific board's recruitment guidance.
- **Local government has no single framework.** The myjobscotland finding is,
  honestly, an *absence* of a national scheme rather than a documented one [29];
  council-level person specifications are the operative criteria and vary.
- **Composed questions are illustrative.** They mimic each Scottish framework's
  published style but have no official status and must not be presented to
  candidates as real past questions.
- **Cross-border contrasts are analysis.** Statements that Police Scotland "retains
  the 2016 structure" or that NHSScotland "differs from NHS England" are labelled
  comparisons of two primary sources [14] [16] [22], not claims from a single
  publisher asserting the contrast.

# Open Questions

- Does Police Scotland publish per-competency **example interview questions** or a
  marking guide for its CVF, comparable to the England-and-Wales force guides
  already cited in the knowledge base? None was located on the recruitment portal
  in this pass [15]; if one exists it would upgrade the Police Scotland questions
  from *(composed)* to *(sourced)*.
- Is there a **Scottish Government numeric scoring scale** published on a
  candidate-facing page, or does it rely on the wider Civil Service 7-point scale
  [12]? The candidate guide describes merit order and overall scores but no point
  scale [6].
- Do any of Scotland's 32 councils publish a **shared competency dictionary** via
  myjobscotland, or is it entirely per-post [29]? A national local-government
  framework, if one exists, would let a future edition cite it.
- Has NHSScotland issued a **single national values-based recruitment framework**
  document (equivalent to the England HEE framework [23]), or does the authority
  chain run only through *Everyone Matters* [18] and board-level frameworks [21]?
