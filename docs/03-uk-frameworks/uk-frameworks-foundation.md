# Executive Summary

This is the foundation document for the UK Frameworks category. It surveys the
main published frameworks that shape competency- and values-based interviews in
UK recruitment: the Civil Service **Success Profiles** framework and its nine
**Behaviours** (the anchor for the whole category, because it is the most
completely documented) [1] [2]; **NHS values-based recruitment**, built on the
six values of the NHS Constitution [4] [6]; the College of Policing
**Competency and Values Framework (CVF)**, refreshed in 2024 around the Code of
Ethics [8]; and the less standardised world of **private-sector and
graduate-scheme employers**, where survey data shows a competency lens still
underpins most early-careers selection [16].

The headline pattern across all four segments is the same: a published (or at
least internally defined) set of behaviours or values, assessed through
structured interviews in which every candidate faces comparable questions
scored against pre-agreed criteria [12] [14]. What differs is the object of
assessment (behaviours, values, strengths or a mix), the vocabulary, and where
the interview sits in the wider selection funnel.

Confidence is **Medium**: the public-sector sections rest on official framework
documents (GOV.UK, HEE/NHS, College of Policing, legislation), which would
support High on their own, but the private-sector and graduate-scheme landscape
rests substantially on careers-service reporting of member-only survey data and
employer careers pages, which involves more synthesis.

# Key Takeaways

- The Civil Service recruits with Success Profiles: five elements —
  Behaviours, Strengths, Ability, Experience, Technical — selected per vacancy;
  no role uses every element automatically. *(documented)* [1]
- There are nine Civil Service Behaviours, each defined at six grade bands from
  AA/AO up to Director/Director General; job adverts name the behaviours being
  tested. *(documented)* [2] [3]
- NHS values-based recruitment requires (for HEE-funded training programmes)
  an individual, face-to-face structured interview that assesses candidates
  against the values of the NHS Constitution; NHS employers are encouraged to
  do the same. *(documented)* [4] [6]
- The College of Policing CVF (2024) defines six competencies at three levels,
  underpinned by three values drawn from the Code of Ethics; it is the basis of
  national police recruitment, promotion and development processes.
  *(documented)* [8]
- Among graduate employers surveyed by the ISE, a competency-based lens still
  underpinned most selection strategies in 2022 (68%), with strengths-based
  (56%) and values-based (35%) approaches increasingly blended in.
  *(documented)* [16]
- Across all frameworks, the interview itself is structured: same or similar
  questions per candidate, scored against pre-agreed criteria — this is the
  common professional-practice core, whatever the branding. *(documented)*
  [12] [14]
- For candidates the practical move is the same everywhere: find the published
  framework for your target employer, and prepare evidence stories mapped to
  its named behaviours or values. *(guidance)*
- Interviewers designing UK processes should treat these public frameworks as
  worked examples of good practice: published criteria, levelled descriptors,
  and structured scoring. *(guidance)*

# Purpose

This document answers two questions. For **candidates**: "which framework will
I actually be interviewed against in the UK, and how do I recognise it from the
job advert?" For **interviewers and HR**: "what do the major published UK
frameworks look like, and what design choices do they embody?" It is the
orientation map for the UK Frameworks category; deep-dives on each framework
are separate documents (see Related Documents).

# Scope

Covered: the UK landscape of competency/behaviour/values frameworks used in
recruitment interviews — Civil Service Success Profiles and Behaviours; NHS
values-based recruitment; the College of Policing CVF; and how private-sector
and graduate-scheme employers structure competency interviews, using published
survey data and one named employer example (Unilever) with public
documentation. For each, it explains where the competency interview sits in the
hiring process.

Not covered (deliberately — this is a Tier-1 breadth document): grade-by-grade
behaviour descriptors, full question banks per framework, EU institutions
(see `eu-frameworks-foundation`), interview answering techniques in depth
(see `techniques-foundation`), and format mechanics such as video interviews
and assessment centres (see `formats-foundation`). Scotland/Wales/Northern
Ireland public-sector variants are out of scope for this edition.

# Definitions

Prefer the shared `GLOSSARY.md`; the following are used in this document's
narrowed sense.

- **Success Profile** — the Civil Service recruitment framework; a per-vacancy
  bundle of up to five assessed elements (Behaviours, Strengths, Ability,
  Experience, Technical) [1].
- **Behaviour (Civil Service sense)** — "actions and activities people do
  which result in effective performance", defined at grade-specific levels [3].
- **Values-based recruitment (VBR)** — recruiting "on the basis that their
  individual values and behaviours align with the values of the NHS
  Constitution" [5].
- **CVF** — the College of Policing Competency and Values Framework: six
  competencies at three levels plus core values, used across police HR
  processes [8].
- **Structured interview** — an interview asking a standardised set of
  predetermined questions, scored against predetermined criteria applied to
  all candidates [6] [14].

# Framework Context

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

The four segments of the UK landscape compare as follows.

| Segment | Framework | Assessed constructs | Levels | Public documentation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Civil Service | Success Profiles [1] | 9 Behaviours + Strengths, Ability, Experience, Technical [1] [2] | 6 grade bands (AA/AO → Director/DG) for Behaviours [2] | Full framework on GOV.UK [1] |
| NHS | Values-based recruitment [5] | 6 NHS Constitution values [4] | Not levelled; same values for all staff [4] | HEE framework + NHS Employers tools [6] [7] |
| Policing | Competency and Values Framework (2024) [8] | 6 competencies + 3 values from the Code of Ethics [8] | 3 levels (practitioner → senior/executive), competencies only [8] | College of Policing framework and guidance [8] [9] |
| Graduate schemes / private sector | Employer-specific | Competencies, strengths and values, typically blended [16] | Employer-specific | Varies; e.g. Unilever publishes its selection stages [18] |

Three context points frame everything below.

First, the Civil Service is the anchor reference: Success Profiles is the
recruitment framework "used within the Civil Service", published in full on
GOV.UK and most recently updated in January 2025 [1] [2]. Because its
behaviours, levels and element definitions are public, it is the most common
vocabulary in UK public-sector interviewing and the best-documented model for
private study.

Second, the public-sector frameworks have been converging on values alongside
competencies: NHS recruitment is explicitly values-based [5], and the 2024 CVF
update rebuilt the police values around the principles of the new Code of
Ethics [8]. The College's guidance describes the CVF interview approach as
continuous with "previous competency-based or values-based interviews" — past
behaviour with probing, plus occasional hypothetical scenarios [8].

Third, in the graduate market the ISE — which describes its annual Student
Recruitment Survey as covering "the largest group of early careers employers
in the UK" [15] — found in 2022 that 68% of employers reported a
competency-based lens underpinning their recruitment strategies, 56% used a
strengths-based perspective and 35% incorporated values-based exercises, as
reported by Prospects' Luminate research site [16]. The three lenses are
complements in practice, not rivals.

# Core Reference

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## Civil Service Success Profiles: the five elements

Success Profiles replace a one-size-fits-all competency model with five
elements a vacancy can draw on: **Behaviours** (actions resulting in effective
job performance), **Strengths** (things done regularly, done well and
motivating), **Ability** (aptitude or potential to meet the required
standard), **Experience** (knowledge or mastery gained through involvement or
exposure), and **Technical** (specific professional skills, knowledge or
qualifications) [1]. Not every element is used for every role; recruiting
managers select the mix per vacancy, and the job advert states which elements
are assessed [1] [3].

## The nine Civil Service Behaviours and their levels

The nine Behaviours, with their official one-line summaries, are [2]:

1. **Seeing the big picture** — understand how your role fits with and
   supports organisational objectives.
2. **Changing and improving** — seek out opportunities to create effective
   change and suggest innovative ideas.
3. **Making effective decisions** — use evidence and knowledge to support
   accurate, expert decisions and advice.
4. **Leadership** — show pride and passion for public service; create and
   engage others in delivering a shared vision.
5. **Communicating and influencing** — communicate purpose and direction with
   clarity, integrity and enthusiasm.
6. **Working together** — form effective partnerships and relationships with
   people both internally and externally.
7. **Developing self and others** — focus on continuous learning and
   development for self, others and the organisation.
8. **Managing a quality service** — deliver service objectives with
   professional excellence, expertise and efficiency.
9. **Delivering at pace** — take responsibility for delivering timely and
   quality results with focus and drive.

Each behaviour carries example statements at six grade bands: AA/AO, EO,
HEO/SEO, Grade 7/Grade 6, Deputy Director, and Director/Director General [2].
Candidates are not asked to demonstrate all nine for one role; the recruiting
manager chooses the behaviours best suited to the job [3].

**Where the interview sits.** A Civil Service campaign may combine a CV and
personal statement sift (with behaviour evidence), online ability tests,
presentations, an interview (face-to-face or video, often before a panel) and
sometimes a full assessment centre; behaviours can be assessed on the
application form, at interview through example-based or situational questions,
or in real-time structured behavioural assessments [3]. GOV.UK notes that
behaviours may be assessed through interviews using situational questions [2].

## NHS values-based recruitment

The NHS Constitution for England sets six values: working together for
patients; respect and dignity; commitment to quality of care; compassion;
improving lives; and everyone counts [4]. Values-based recruitment (VBR) is
the approach of recruiting students, trainees and employees "on the basis that
their individual values and behaviours align with the values of the NHS
Constitution"; the national VBR framework was published in October 2014 and
refreshed in April 2016 by Health Education England (HEE), and from 1 April
2015 all higher-education institutions contracted by HEE to deliver NHS-funded
training programmes must assess students for NHS Constitution values [5] [6].

The framework's evidence review identifies the methods shown to be effective
for recruiting for values: structured interviews, multiple mini interviews
(MMIs), selection centres, and situational judgement tests for screening [6].
Its national core requirement CR4 mandates "an individual, face-to-face
structured interview to include an assessment of values", which may take place
as an MMI, within a selection centre, or in conjunction with SJT screening
[6]. The framework defines a values-based interview as a structured interview
focused on the candidate's values, distinguished by probing questions designed
to reveal what the candidate considers important [6]. NHS Employers describes
VBR delivery for employing organisations through pre-screening assessments,
values-based interviewing techniques (including role play and written
responses to scenarios) and assessment-centre approaches, supported by a
behaviour framework usable as interview scoring criteria [7].

**Where the interview sits.** For NHS-funded student recruitment the values
interview is a required stage (CR4); for NHS employers it typically sits after
screening (which may include SJTs) and alongside or within selection-centre
exercises [6] [7].

## College of Policing Competency and Values Framework

The CVF was introduced in 2016 and updated in 2024 to incorporate the
principles underpinning the new Code of Ethics [8]. The 2024 CVF consists of
six competencies, each at three levels, underpinned by three core values [8]:

- **Competencies:** We are emotionally aware; We take ownership; We
  collaborate; We support and inspire; We analyse critically; We are
  innovative and open-minded [8].
- **Values:** Respect and empathy; Courage; Public service — reflecting the
  Code of Ethics principles; values are not levelled and apply to all roles
  [8].
- **Levels:** level 1 broadly maps to practitioners, level 2 to
  supervisors/managers, level 3 to senior managers/executives; levels reflect
  increasing behavioural complexity and apply to competencies only [8].

The 2016 original differed in two structural ways: its six competencies were
grouped into three clusters (resolute, compassionate and committed; inclusive,
enabling and visionary leadership; intelligent, creative and informed
policing), and it was underpinned by four values — integrity, impartiality,
public service and transparency; its levels were explicitly cumulative, higher
levels building on the behaviours of preceding ones [9]. The 2024 guidance
removed the clusters for simplicity and notes that forces in transition may
still use the 2016 version, so candidates attending College assessment
processes should check which version they are being assessed against [8].

**Where the interview sits.** The CVF is the basis of national HR processes
including national assessments for recruitment and promotion, professional
development review, and leadership development [8]. For recruitment and
selection interviews, the College's guidance states it is good practice to ask
about examples of past behaviour and probe for evidence, sometimes adding
hypothetical future scenarios, with most of the interview focused on past
behaviour; assessment-centre exercises should measure a small number of named
competencies and candidates should be told which [8].

## Graduate schemes and the private sector

There is no single private-sector framework; each employer defines its own
competencies or values, and public documentation varies. The evidence base for
the overall pattern is the ISE's annual Student Recruitment Survey [15], whose
2022 findings — as publicly reported by Luminate (Prospects) — were that 68%
of employers reported a competency-based lens underpinning recruitment
strategy; 56% used a strengths-based perspective; 35% incorporated
values-based exercises; around one in ten had added AI to parts of selection
(most often candidate pre-screening and video-interview analysis); and
personality-test use had fallen from roughly a third of employers in 2014 to
9% in 2022 [16].

Prospects' guidance for graduate candidates describes the standard shape of
the competency interview itself: structured questions each targeting a
specific skill, answers compared against pre-determined criteria, typically
three to six competency questions in a 45–60 minute interview, particularly
favoured by large graduate recruiters and often embedded in assessment centres
[17].

A documented named example: **Unilever's Future Leaders Programme (UK &
Ireland)** publishes a four-stage selection process — online application;
profile assessment (a personality "portrait" plus an online assessment of
cognitive, emotional and social traits); a digital interview of three short
questions followed by a business case solving "real-world problems using
Unilever scenarios"; and a Discovery Centre day including project meetings,
business challenges, a team meeting and an interview [18]. TARGETjobs' guide
to the same programme describes the digital-interview questions as
hypothetical and advises candidates to prepare by studying Unilever's culture,
values and Standards of Leadership [19]. This illustrates the wider
graduate-scheme pattern: a multi-stage funnel in which the interview arrives
late, after online assessment, and blends hypothetical/situational material
with employer-specific values.

# Question Bank

Fifteen questions illustrating the *style* of each framework. Provenance per

## Civil Service Success Profiles style (Behaviours and Strengths)

1. Tell us about a time when you had to use evidence from several sources to make a decision under time pressure. What did you conclude and why? *(composed — in the style of the Behaviour "Making effective decisions" [2])*
2. Describe a time when you built a working relationship with another team or organisation to deliver something neither of you could deliver alone. *(composed — in the style of the Behaviour "Working together" [2])*
3. Give an example of a time you had to deliver a piece of work to a tight deadline while maintaining quality. How did you organise yourself and others? *(composed — in the style of the Behaviour "Delivering at pace" [2])*
4. Tell us about a time you spotted how a change in your organisation's wider priorities affected your own work, and what you did about it. *(composed — in the style of the Behaviour "Seeing the big picture" [2])*
5. What kind of task do you find yourself volunteering for, and what does that tell us about you? *(composed — a strengths-style prompt of the kind Success Profiles pairs with behaviour questions [1] [3])*

## NHS values-based style

1. Describe a time when you noticed that someone — a patient, customer or colleague — was distressed. What did you do, and what were you thinking about as you acted? *(composed — in the style of a values-based interview probing "compassion" [4] [6])*
2. Tell us about a time you worked with people from other teams or professions to improve the experience of a service user. What was your contribution? *(composed — in the style of "working together for patients" [4] [6])*
3. Give an example of a time you saw practice that fell short of the standard you expect. What did you do, and why did it matter to you? *(composed — a values probe in the style of "commitment to quality of care", with follow-up designed to reveal what the candidate considers important [6])*

## College of Policing CVF style

1. Tell us about a time you made a mistake or missed something important. What did you do once you realised, and what changed afterwards? *(composed — in the style of the CVF competency "We take ownership" [8])*
2. Describe a situation where you had to weigh up incomplete or conflicting information before acting. How did you test your thinking? *(composed — in the style of the CVF competency "We analyse critically" [8])*
3. Give an example of a time you did the right thing even though it was uncomfortable or unpopular. What made it difficult? *(composed — a values probe in the style of the CVF value "Courage" [8])*

## Graduate-scheme / general competency style

1. Describe a situation in which you led a team. *(sourced: Prospects, "Competency-based interviews", June 2025)* [17]
2. Give an example of a time you handled conflict in the workplace. *(sourced: Prospects, "Competency-based interviews", June 2025)* [17]
3. Tell me about a time when your communication skills improved a situation. *(sourced: Prospects, "Competency-based interviews", June 2025)* [17]
4. Tell me about a time when you showed integrity and professionalism. *(sourced: Prospects, "Competency-based interviews", June 2025)* [17]

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact.**

- **Map before you draft.** Identify which framework governs your interview:
  the job advert names it in the Civil Service (elements and behaviours are
  listed per vacancy [3]), NHS adverts and person specifications reference the
  Constitution values [6], and police roles are assessed against named CVF
  competencies [8]. Prepare one evidence story per named behaviour/value, not
  a generic set.
- **Use a structured narrative.** Prospects recommends STARR — Situation,
  Task, Action, Result, Reflection — for competency answers [17]; the
  techniques deep-dive (`techniques-foundation`) covers STAR variants in
  detail. Whatever the mnemonic, the load-bearing parts are the *action* (what
  **you** personally did) and the *result*.
- **Match the level, not just the behaviour.** Civil Service behaviours are
  defined differently at each grade band [2]; CVF competencies at each of
  three levels [8]. A Grade 7 answer about "Leadership" should show shaping
  and engaging others, not just personal diligence. Read the descriptor for
  your target level and select evidence at that altitude.
- **Expect values probes to go one layer deeper.** NHS-style values
  interviewing is defined by probing follow-ups that reveal what you consider
  important [6]. Prepare not just what you did but *why* — the reasoning shows
  the value.
- **In strengths portions, answer promptly and honestly.** Civil Service
  guidance to candidates is to give your genuine first response to strengths
  questions, in contrast to the prepared, structured examples expected for
  behaviours [3].

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

- The common documented core is the structured interview: CIPD's selection
  factsheet describes asking all candidates a predefined set of questions in
  the same order and scoring responses against pre-agreed criteria, preferably
  before a panel rather than a sole interviewer, to make responses comparable
  and reduce bias [14]. Acas similarly advises asking each applicant the same
  questions where possible and basing decisions on how each applicant met the
  job description and person specification [12].
- In the Civil Service, the recruiting manager selects which elements and
  which behaviours a vacancy assesses, and the interview may combine
  behaviour, strength, experience and technical questions; panels (e.g. of
  three) are used at interview [1] [3].
- In the NHS model, the interview must be structured and individual, with
  values assessed explicitly; NHS Employers provides a behaviour framework
  that can be used directly as interview scoring criteria, with examples of
  behaviours that do and do not align with the Constitution values [6] [7].
- College of Policing guidance tells assessors that the CVF's example
  behavioural indicators are illustrative, not exhaustive — the framework
  "should not be used as a simple checklist" in selection, and assessors
  should stay open to diverse ways of demonstrating a competency that fit its
  definition [8]. Interviews should focus mostly on past behaviour with
  probing for evidence; assessment exercises should measure a small number of
  competencies, with candidates told which, and assessors trained on what
  evidence to look for [8].
- Graduate recruiters increasingly automate the early funnel — around one in
  ten ISE-surveyed employers used AI, most often for pre-screening and
  video-interview analysis — which repositions the human competency interview
  as a later, higher-stakes stage [16].

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact.**

- **Preparing for the wrong framework.** Bringing Civil-Service-style
  behaviour stories to an NHS values interview misses the point of the
  values probe (the *why*, not just the *what*) [6]. Check the advert's named
  framework first.
- **Ignoring the level descriptors.** Answering a Grade 6 "Leadership"
  question with EO-level evidence under-scores against the published
  grade-band examples [2]; the same applies to CVF levels [8].
- **Treating the framework as a checklist — in either chair.** The College of
  Policing explicitly warns assessors against checklist use of the CVF [8];
  candidates who mechanically name-drop indicator phrases produce answers
  that fit the words but not the definition.
- **Hypothetical answers to past-behaviour questions.** Prospects flags
  "I would usually…" responses as a standard mistake; specific, real events
  with outcomes are wanted [17], and CVF guidance likewise centres the
  interview on past behaviour [8].
- **For interviewers: unstructured drift.** Departing from the planned
  questions and criteria undermines the comparability that is the documented
  rationale of the structured interview [12] [14] and invites the fairness
  risks in the next-but-one section.
- **Assuming the framework version.** Police forces could still be using the
  2016 CVF during transition [8]; candidates and panel members should confirm
  which version applies before preparing or scoring.

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.**

1. **Framework mapping drill (30 min).** Take one live vacancy from Civil
   Service Jobs, one NHS vacancy and one graduate scheme. For each, list the
   named behaviours/values in the advert and match one of your own evidence
   stories to each. Note the gaps — those are your preparation targets.
2. **Level-lift drill (45 min).** Pick one Civil Service Behaviour and read
   its example statements at two adjacent grade bands on GOV.UK [2]. Write a
   STARR answer at the lower band, then rewrite it to evidence the higher
   band. Identify exactly what changed (scope, others involved, time horizon).
3. **Values probe rehearsal (paired, 20 min).** Answer question 6 or 8 from
   the Question Bank while a partner asks only "why?" and "what mattered to
   you there?" follow-ups, in the spirit of the VBI probing style [6].
   Debrief: did your answers reveal values or just actions?
4. **Assessor's seat (30 min).** Using the nine behaviour summaries [2] as
   criteria, score a friend's recorded answer to question 1 on a simple
   1–5 scale with written justification. Notice how much easier scoring is
   when the question targeted a single named behaviour — that is the design
   logic of every framework in this document.
5. **Timed set (25 min).** Answer questions 12–15 aloud, one take each,
   3–4 minutes per answer — the density implied by three to six competency
   questions in a 45–60 minute interview [17].

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

- Employers must follow discrimination law when advertising roles,
  interviewing and deciding whom to appoint; direct and indirect
  discrimination in recruitment are both unlawful under the Equality Act 2010
  [13].
- Under section 20 of the Equality Act 2010, employers owe a duty to make
  reasonable adjustments for disabled people; Acas notes this applies to job
  applicants and interviewees at any stage of recruitment [10] [12]. The
  frameworks operationalise this: Civil Service campaigns offer adjustments
  for any element of the assessment process [3], and CVF guidance tells
  candidates they can request reasonable adjustments to assessments, including
  for neurodiversity [8].
- Section 60 of the Equality Act 2010 restricts asking about a candidate's
  health or disability before a job offer, subject to limited exceptions
  (e.g. establishing the need for adjustments to the assessment itself) [11].
  Interview questions in any framework must be designed with this boundary in
  mind.
- Structured, criteria-based interviewing is itself presented by professional
  bodies as a fairness mechanism: consistent questions and objective scoring
  make it less likely that decisions rest on personal bias [12] [14].

# Risks & Caveats

- **Frameworks move.** The Behaviours page was updated as recently as January
  2025 [2], and the CVF was substantially revised in 2024 with a transition
  period in which both versions circulate [8]. Any deep-dive or coaching built
  on this document must re-verify the current version at time of use.
- **The HEE VBR framework is dated March 2016** and HEE has since been merged
  into NHS England; the framework pages remain live [5] [6], but local trust
  practice may have evolved beyond the national core requirements. Treat
  trust-level detail as unverified until the NHS deep-dive documents it.
- **Private-sector claims are the weakest layer.** The ISE survey itself is
  member-only [15]; the figures here come from Prospects' Luminate reporting
  of it [16] and describe 2022 — AI-in-selection numbers in particular are
  likely to have shifted. Employer processes (e.g. Unilever's stages [18])
  change without notice.
- **Composed questions are illustrative.** They mimic the published style of
  each framework but have no official status; do not present them to
  candidates as real past questions from any employer.
- **This is a breadth document.** Level descriptors, scoring scales and full
  question banks are deliberately summarised; relying on this document alone
  to prepare for a specific Civil Service, NHS or police campaign would
  under-prepare you — use the deep-dives when published.
