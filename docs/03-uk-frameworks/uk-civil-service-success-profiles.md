# Executive Summary

Success Profiles is the recruitment framework used across the UK Civil Service.
It replaced a single competency framework with **five elements** — Behaviours,
Strengths, Ability, Experience and Technical — of which a recruiting manager
selects only those relevant to the vacancy [1] [2]. The behaviour element is
the most visible: nine named Behaviours, each written out as example statements
at six grade bands from Administrative Assistant/Administrative Officer up to
Director and Director General [3]. This document is the end-to-end guide: what
each element is, how each is assessed, how a campaign flows from application to
sift to interview, and how answers are scored.

The document's most consequential finding is a **scoring rule published by the
Cabinet Office, not merely by individual departments**. Cluster 1 of this
knowledge base could not locate a central Civil-Service-wide scoring
publication and fell back on a departmental one. A GOV.UK-hosted Cabinet Office
guidance pack for delegated-grade (EO–Grade 6) recruitment states plainly that
"Experience, technical skills and behaviours should be scored using the 7 point
scale. Strengths should be scored using the 4 point scale" [8]. The identical
sentence appears in the parallel Deputy Director and Director packs [9] [10].
The **1–7 scale is therefore central, not departmental**; the Crown Office and
Procurator Fiscal Service (COPFS) page long used as this KB's scoring source is
best understood as a *departmental publication of a central scale*, and it
remains the only source found that publishes the seven descriptor labels in
full [14]. That distinction is stated explicitly wherever the scale is used
below.

The second thing this guide insists on is the **Behaviours vs Strengths
distinction**, which inverts standard interview advice. Behaviour answers are
prepared, evidenced and structured — GOV.UK points candidates at the STAR
method for evidencing past experience [6] [23]. Strengths answers are the
opposite: GOV.UK's guidance is "Do not rehearse your answers because we are
looking for your initial response", and there are "no right or wrong answers"
[4] [11]. The recruiter-side guidance closes the loop: departments are advised
*not* to name the strengths being assessed in the advert or candidate pack,
precisely so that candidates cannot prepare them [8]. Confidence is **High**:
almost every factual claim here rests on Cabinet Office or GOV.UK primary
publications, with departmental sources used only where they add published
detail the centre does not, and labelled as such.

# Key Takeaways

- Success Profiles has five elements — Behaviours, Strengths, Ability,
  Experience, Technical — and "Not all of the elements are relevant to every
  role"; the job advert states which apply. *(documented)* [1] [2]
- There are nine Behaviours, each with example statements at six grade bands
  (AA/AO, EO, HEO/SEO, Grade 7/Grade 6, Deputy Director, Director/Director
  General). The page publishes **effective-behaviour examples only** — there is
  no published "ineffective" indicator list. *(documented)* [3]
- Cabinet Office guidance recommends **no more than four behaviours** be
  prioritised as critical to a role, so a panel can test them properly.
  *(documented)* [8] [9]
- **Scoring is centrally specified**: experience, technical skills and
  behaviours on a 7-point scale; strengths on a 4-point scale. Sift may use the
  7-point scale, or a 3-point A/B/C rating for high-volume campaigns.
  *(documented)* [8] [9] [10]
- The seven descriptor labels (1 Not Demonstrated → 7 Outstanding
  Demonstration) and the benchmark convention (usually 4, with limited
  tolerance for a single 3) are published by **COPFS, a department** — treat
  the labels as authoritative for COPFS and as strong evidence for the central
  scale they implement. *(documented)* [14]
- **Strengths must not be rehearsed.** GOV.UK tells candidates to give their
  initial response; Cabinet Office tells recruiters not to name the strengths in
  the pack. Preparing a scripted strengths answer works against the design.
  *(documented)* [4] [8] [11]
- The Civil Service Strengths Dictionary defines **36 named strengths** and maps
  each to the Behaviours it most relates to — the single best preparation
  artefact for the strengths element. *(documented)* [11]
- Panels score independently, then reconcile by **arithmetic average** to
  produce a merit list — not by debate to consensus. *(documented)* [8]
- Practically: build a behaviour evidence bank at your target grade band, and
  prepare for strengths by *reflection*, not scripting. *(guidance)*

# Purpose

This document answers, for a **candidate**: what will actually be assessed in a
Civil Service campaign, at what stage, against what scale, and what is the
right preparation for each element — including the counter-intuitive rule that
one whole element should not be rehearsed. For an **interviewer or HR
practitioner**: what the centre actually mandates about element selection,
question design, scoring scales and panel reconciliation, with the primary
sources to justify each design decision.

It is the deep-dive behind the Success Profiles summary in
`uk-frameworks-foundation`, and deliberately does not restate it. The parent
document establishes *that* Success Profiles has five elements and nine
behaviours; this document covers the assessment machinery — grade bands, the
application-to-interview flow, the scoring scales, benchmarks, the strengths
dictionary and the Behaviours/Strengths inversion.

# Scope

**Covered:** the five elements and their official definitions; how each element

# Definitions

Prefer the shared `GLOSSARY.md`. Used here in a narrowed sense:

- **Element** — one of the five assessable components of a Success Profile:
  Behaviours, Strengths, Ability, Experience, Technical [1].
- **Grade band** — one of the six bands at which behaviour example statements
  are published: AA/AO, EO, HEO/SEO, Grade 7/Grade 6, Deputy Director,
  Director/Director General [3].
- **Benchmark** — in COPFS's published definition, "a score that a candidate
  must achieve for a single or combination of elements" [14].
- **Lead criteria** — a sift shortcut permitted in high-volume campaigns, where
  candidates are scored against a nominated subset rather than every criterion
  [8].
- **Blended interview** — the Cabinet Office's term for the standard Success
  Profiles interview, which "will typically assess experience, technical skills,
  behaviours and strengths" in one sitting [8].
- **Strengths Dictionary** — the published set of 36 defined Civil Service
  strengths, which "are not defined by grade" [11].

# Framework Context

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

Success Profiles is the recruitment framework used within the Civil Service,
published as a GOV.UK collection by the Cabinet Office and last updated 29
January 2025 [1]. Its stated rationale is to move recruitment "away from using
a purely competency based system of assessment" toward "a more flexible
framework which assesses candidates against a range of elements using a variety
of selection methods", with the aims of finding the right person for the job,
driving up performance, and improving diversity and inclusivity [11]. All
selection remains governed by the Civil Service Commission's Recruitment
Principles: appointment on merit on the basis of fair and open competition —
a point the Cabinet Office guidance restates at the head of its recruitment
design section [8] [16].

The framework sits alongside, and does not replace, professional competency
frameworks. The Government Statistical Service's departmental guidance is
explicit that "The introduction of Success Profiles does not impact the GSG
competency framework which aligns to the 'technical' element of Success
Profiles" [15]. This is the general pattern: a profession's own technical
framework becomes the Technical element, while Behaviours supply the generic
capability layer [15].

The five elements map onto the wider UK landscape as follows.

| Success Profiles element | Official definition | Nearest analogue elsewhere |
|---|---|---|
| Behaviours | "the actions and activities that people do which result in effective performance in a job" [1] [11] | Competencies (police CVF, EPSO general competencies) |
| Strengths | "the things we do regularly, do well and that motivate us" [1] [11] | Strengths-based interviewing in graduate recruitment |
| Ability | "the aptitude or potential to perform to the required standard" [1] [11] | Psychometric/aptitude testing |
| Experience | "the knowledge or mastery of an activity or subject gained through involvement in or exposure to it" [1] [11] | Person-specification essential criteria |
| Technical | "the demonstration of specific professional skills, knowledge or qualifications" [11] | Professional/technical competency frameworks |

Note the Technical definition: the GOV.UK candidate-overview bullet list has
historically repeated the Behaviours wording against Technical, whereas the
Strengths Dictionary — which reproduces the same five-element list — gives the
correct distinct definition quoted above [2] [11]. Where the two differ, [11]
is the coherent reading.

Behaviour and values expectations are not free-standing: the Civil Service Code
requires civil servants to uphold integrity, honesty, objectivity and
impartiality [17], and the Civil Service blog's candidate guidance tells
applicants to demonstrate alignment with the Code by showing they are "honest,
fair, unbiased, and trustworthy" [22].

# Core Reference

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## The five elements and how each is assessed

**Behaviours** are "the actions and activities that people do which result in
effective performance in a job" [1] [3]. GOV.UK lists the assessment methods:
application forms, Civil Service judgement tests, personality tests,
interviews, assessment centres, and job-related simulations including
presentations, in-tray exercises, group discussions, role-play and oral
briefings [3]. Depending on method, a candidate may be asked to give examples
of behaviours already demonstrated, to say how they would behave in a given
situation (via a situational judgement test or a situational interview
question), or to demonstrate the behaviour live in a structured behavioural
assessment [3].

**Strengths** are "the things that we do regularly, do well and that motivate
us" [4] [11]. Three things determine whether something is a strength:
**Performance** (you can perform the activity to a high level of capability or
proficiency), **Engagement** (you feel motivated, enthused and empowered doing
it), and **Use** (you do it regularly and as often as possible) [4] [11].
Strengths can be assessed by interviews, recorded video interviews, a bespoke
situational strengths test, simulation assessment, or personality test
[4] [11].

**Ability** is "the aptitude or potential to perform to the required standard"
[5] [11]. It is assessed by online psychometric tests — verbal and numerical
reasoning are the standard pair — which are scored automatically, with
candidates told whether they met the pass benchmark; they are commonly placed
at the start of a campaign [5].

**Experience** is "the knowledge or mastery of an activity or subject gained
through involvement in or exposure to it" [6] [11]. It is assessed through the
application form or CV and at interview, looking at previous achievements and
relevant knowledge rather than length of tenure; transferable experience from
volunteering or hobbies counts [6]. GOV.UK's own guidance on this element
directs candidates to the STAR method — set the scene, describe the actions
taken, explain the outcome [6] [23].

**Technical** is "the demonstration of specific professional skills, knowledge
or qualifications" [11]. Assessment methods include application and CV review,
interviews, technical presentations and exercises, technical tests, work
samples, in-tray exercises, job-related simulations, oral briefings, written
exercises and assessment centres, plus evidence of professional qualifications
or memberships where the role requires them [7].

Crucially, **element selection is per vacancy**: "Not all of the elements are
relevant to every role. They will vary depending on the profession, level and
type of role" [2]. The Strengths Dictionary puts the design rationale the same
way: "the makeup of the Success Profile should be different for different types
of job to improve the chances of getting the best person for the role" [11].

## Which elements appear at which grades

There is no single published table of element combinations by grade. What the
Cabinet Office does publish are worked application guides for the HR job family
at three grade tiers, and these agree closely with each other. They are the
best available evidence of the intended pattern; they are HR-specific and
should be read as illustrative rather than as a universal rule.

| Stage | Delegated grades EO–Grade 6 [8] | Deputy Director [9] | Director [10] |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sift (CV / personal statement or application form) | Technical, Behaviours, Experience | Technical, Behaviours, Experience | Technical, Behaviours, Experience |
| Expression of interest | Technical, Behaviours, Experience | Technical, Behaviours, Experience | Technical, Behaviours, Experience |
| Aptitude tests / Civil Service Judgement Test | Ability; Behaviours (CSJT) | — | — |
| Individual Leadership Assessment | — | present, **not scored** | present, **not scored** |
| Staff Engagement Exercise | — | Behaviours, **not scored** | Behaviours, **not scored** |
| Interview | Technical, Behaviours, Strengths, Experience | Technical, Behaviours, Strengths, Experience | Technical, Behaviours, Strengths, Experience |
| Presentation | Technical, Behaviours, Experience | Technical, Behaviours, Experience | Technical, Behaviours, Experience |

Two things follow. First, **Strengths are assessed at interview and essentially
nowhere else** in these matrices — which is exactly why they cannot be sifted
for and, as the guidance argues, should not be pre-announced [8] [9] [10].
Second, at Deputy Director and Director level the leadership assessments are
explicitly **not scored**; instead "The findings will be shared with the
interview panel for the next stage of assessment, along with suggested probing
questions" [9] [10]. Candidates at SCS level are therefore being observed
before the interview in ways that shape the questions they are then asked.

The guidance notes that most HR delegated-grade roles will test technical,
experience, behaviours and strengths, adding ability for some roles and volume
campaigns [8]; at Deputy Director and Director level all five are in play, and
"SCS recruitment already operates in a way that follows the Success Profiles
methodology for blended and role-centered testing" [9] [10].

## The nine Behaviours

The nine Behaviours, with their published summaries, are [3]:

1. **Seeing the big picture** — understand how your role supports
   organisational objectives and Civil Service priorities aligned with the
   national interest.
2. **Changing and improving** — seek out opportunities for effective change,
   suggest innovative ideas, review working methods and provide feedback.
3. **Making effective decisions** — use evidence and knowledge to make accurate
   decisions, considering options, implications and risks.
4. **Leadership** — display pride in public service, create and engage others
   in a shared vision, and value diversity, inclusion and fairness.
5. **Communicating and influencing** — communicate with clarity and integrity,
   respecting others' needs and opinions.
6. **Working together** — form effective partnerships across diverse
   backgrounds, sharing information, resources and support.
7. **Developing self and others** — focus on continuous learning for yourself,
   colleagues and the organisation.
8. **Managing a quality service** — deliver objectives with professional
   excellence, expertise and efficiency, considering diverse customer needs.
9. **Delivering at pace** — take responsibility for timely, quality results
   with focus and drive.

## How the grade-band level descriptors work

Each behaviour is written out at six grade bands: AA/AO; EO; HEO/SEO; Grade 7
and Grade 6; Deputy Director; Director and Director General [3]. The bands are
expressed as lists of example effective-behaviour statements. GOV.UK publishes
**effective-behaviour examples only** — there is no published counterpart list
of ineffective indicators [3]. Any "what not to do" list circulating in
commercial prep material is therefore not an official artefact.

The mechanism is easiest to see by comparing adjacent bands of the same
behaviour. For **Working together**, an EO-level example is to "develop a range
of contacts outside own team and identify opportunities to share knowledge,
information and learning", while at Grade 7/Grade 6 the equivalent is to
"actively build and maintain a network of colleagues and contacts to achieve
progress on shared objectives" [3]. For **Leadership**, the EO example is to
"show pride and passion for your work and positive, inclusive engagement with
your team"; at Grade 7/Grade 6 it becomes to "promote diversity, inclusion and
equality of opportunity, respecting difference and external experience" [3].
The verbs escalate from *identify* and *show* to *actively build* and
*promote*; scope moves from own team to organisational; and the time horizon
lengthens.

Note that grade **bands** and the **scoring scale** are different numbering
systems and are easily confused. Departmental guidance sometimes refers to
behaviour "levels" numerically — the GSS pack, for example, assesses HEO
promotion candidates against "the HEO grade, level 3, of the Civil Service
behaviours" and Grade 7 against "the G7, level 4" [15]. That is a departmental
numbering of the published bands, not a score.

## The application-to-interview flow

**Application and sift.** Candidates apply by CV and personal statement or by
application form. At sift, "Evidence of the candidates' behaviours, technical
skills and experience are considered and scored" against the person
specification [8]. For a single role or a smaller campaign, "candidates should
be scored against the numerical scoring scale for each element"; for
high-volume campaigns they "can be scored using a lead criteria or can be
awarded an overall rating against the 3 point scale" — A (meets all the
criteria), B (borderline/meets some of the criteria), C (does not meet the
criteria) [8]. The sift benchmark "can be flexed in accordance with the quality
of applications and the number of roles available" [8]. At Deputy Director and
Director level the sift is a CV and statement of suitability, and behaviours,
technical skills and experience "are considered together and a single score
given" [9] [10].

**Tests.** A second sift stage may use the Civil Service Judgement Test or
aptitude tests, either for high-volume roles or to test ability in a specific
area; pass marks can be adjusted with advice from Government Recruitment
Service [8]. The Civil Service Judgement Test is an online situational
judgement test measuring "your ability to demonstrate judgement and
decision-making regarding specific Civil Service Behaviours" [12]. Part one
(15% of the score) asks self-assessment questions about ambition, motivation
and flexibility; part two presents workplace scenarios with three scenarios per
behaviour and four actions to rate per scenario, each rated Counterproductive,
Ineffective, Fairly Effective or Effective [12]. Scenarios for three of the
behaviours are presented as short videos, with subtitles and British Sign
Language translations, and the rest in text; the test is untimed and requires
no specialist knowledge [12].

The separate **Work Strengths Test** measures "what you do regularly, what you
do well and what motivates you", assessing five strengths that vary with job
level from Administrative Officer to the Senior Civil Service [13]. It has
three parts: rating agreement with workplace statements; rating four actions
across ten scenarios for effectiveness; and, across five scenarios, choosing
the action you would be most and least likely to take [13]. Results are
reported as a percentile against a comparison group [13].

**Interview.** "The blended interview will typically assess experience,
technical skills, behaviours and strengths. It will also typically include a
5-minute presentation" [8]. At Deputy Director and Director level the
presentation covers "a set topic related to the candidate's leadership
experience" [9] [10]. GOV.UK's candidate overview describes interviews of 30 to
60 minutes in various formats [2]. Live video interviews are explicitly
sanctioned and "this format is compatible with Strengths-based assessment",
with panels advised to pre-agree who leads on which questions and to check
technology accessibility and reasonable adjustments in advance [8].

## Scoring, benchmarks and panel reconciliation

**The central rule.** Cabinet Office guidance states: "Experience, technical
skills and behaviours should be scored using the 7 point scale. Strengths
should be scored using the 4 point scale" [8]. The same sentence appears in the
Deputy Director and Director packs, confirming it as a framework-wide rather
than grade-specific rule [9] [10]. The 7-point scale is noted as the one that
"aligns with the vX application tracking system" [9] [10].

**The descriptors.** The centre does not publish the seven descriptor labels in
any document located for this guide. **COPFS — a department, not the Civil
Service centre — does publish them**, and its scale is the fullest published
articulation of the central 1–7 scale found [14]:

| Score | Label | Descriptor |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Not Demonstrated | No positive evidence and/or substantial negative evidence |
| 2 | Minimal Demonstration | Limited positive evidence and/or mainly negative evidence |
| 3 | Moderate Demonstration | Moderate positive evidence but some negative evidence |
| 4 | Acceptable Demonstration | Adequate positive evidence and any negative evidence would not cause concern |
| 5 | Good Demonstration | Substantial positive evidence of the behaviour |
| 6 | Strong Demonstration | Substantial positive evidence; includes some evidence of exceeding expectations |
| 7 | Outstanding Demonstration | The evidence provided wholly exceeds expectation at this level |

**Benchmarks.** COPFS defines a benchmark as "a score that a candidate must
achieve for a single or combination of elements", gives 4 as the usual
behaviour benchmark (raised to 5 for some campaigns), and describes the
standard convention that a candidate must score 4 or more in all behaviours,
though "a candidate can score a single score of 3 where scores for all other
behaviours are high" [14]. These are COPFS's published rules; other departments
set their own benchmarks. Independent corroboration that a 3 is the failure
threshold comes from GSS, whose temporary-appointment route catches applicants
who "fall short by scoring one '3' in either of the technical competencies or
Civil Service behaviours" [15].

**How many behaviours.** "As a guide, it is recommended that no more than four
behaviours are prioritised as critical to the role. This will allow the panel
to sufficiently test the criteria at interview stage, along with a candidate's
strengths and experience" [8] [9]. GSS's departmental variant recommends the
combined mix of professional competencies and behaviours "does not exceed 6 in
total" [15].

**Panel reconciliation.** "Panel members should observe, record, classify,
evaluate and score each question independently, then calculate an overall score
before jointly agreeing a merit list using the arithmetic method" [8]. Variance
between panellists may be discussed and the underlying evidence examined;
panellists "may decide to change their score(s) or stand by them, but
ultimately individual panel scores should be reconciled by taking an arithmetic
average which will serve as the final scores for the candidate" [8]. This is
not consensus-by-discussion: the arithmetic mean is decisive.

## Behaviours vs Strengths: the inversion

This is the single most consequential distinction in Success Profiles, and it
runs against the grain of ordinary interview coaching.

**Behaviours** are evidenced from prepared past examples. They are named in the
advert, sifted on, and asked about at interview; GOV.UK's own element guidance
points candidates at STAR for structuring evidence of past experience [3] [6].
Preparation is the point.

**Strengths** are the reverse. GOV.UK's Strengths guidance states there are "no
right or wrong answers to these questions" and instructs: "Do not rehearse your
answers because we are looking for your initial response" [4] [11]. The
recommended preparation is reflective, not scripted: "The best way to prepare
is to reflect on what you feel your personal strengths are and your preferred
ways of working" [11]. The qualities required are described in the job
description [11].

The recruiter-side guidance makes the design intent unambiguous. Vacancy
holders are advised that "the specific strengths being assessed are not
explicitly referenced in recruitment materials such as adverts, candidate
information packs and interview invitation letters. A well written person
specification will indicate requirements that relate to the chosen strengths
without being so explicit that candidates prepare their responses prior to the
interview stage" [8]. In other words, the framework deliberately withholds the
strengths list *from* the candidate in order to obtain an unrehearsed response.
Strengths selection is nonetheless principled: vacancy holders "should consider
the strengths that map to their selected behaviours", and may also pick
strengths aligned to behaviours important but not chosen as essential [8].

## The Civil Service Strengths Dictionary

The Strengths Dictionary is the published set of "defined strengths that are
relevant to the culture and type of work that we do", and — unlike Behaviours —
"Civil Service Strengths are not defined by grade" [11]. It contains **36
strengths**, each with a one- or two-sentence definition [11]. A representative
sample:

| Strength | Published definition [11] |
|---|---|
| Adaptable | You can adapt to variations in work or environment and your effectiveness isn't impacted by change. You are flexible and versatile and act as an advocate for change. |
| Analytical | You seek and analyse information to inform decisions based on the best available evidence. |
| Authentic | You are self-aware and true to yourself in all situations, even when under pressure. |
| Catalyst | You are self-motivated to act to achieve a goal. You are confident using your own initiative to take forward actions. |
| Explainer | You communicate thoughts and ideas, verbally or in writing. You simplify complexities and adapt communication so others can understand. |
| Mission | You pursue things which give you a sense of meaning and purpose, working towards a longer term goal. |
| Preventer | You think ahead to anticipate, identify and address any risks or problems before they occur. |
| Resilient | You have inner composure, recover quickly from setbacks and learn from them. |
| Service Focussed | You look for ways to serve customers putting their needs at the heart of everything you do. |
| Visionary | You create and share a clear vision of the future. |

The Dictionary also maps every strength to the Behaviour(s) it most relates to,
noting that "any strength can be assessed if suitable for the role" [11]:

| Behaviour | Mapped strengths [11] |
|---|---|
| Seeing the Big Picture | Challenger, Mission, Strategic, Visionary |
| Changing and Improving | Adaptable, Courageous, Change Agent, Improver, Problem Solver, Resilient |
| Making Effective Decisions | Analytical, Decisive, Preventer, Problem Solver |
| Leadership | Confident, Change Agent, Inclusive, Motivator, Team Leader, Visionary |
| Communicating and Influencing | Authentic, Emotionally Intelligent, Explainer, Inclusive, Influencer |
| Working Together | Challenger, Emotionally Intelligent, Inclusive, Negotiator, Networker, Relationship Builder, Team Player, Mediator |
| Developing Self and Others | Enabler, Explainer, Inclusive, Learner |
| Managing a Quality Service | Disciplined, Efficient, Focussed, Organiser, Precise, Preventer, Service Focussed |
| Delivering at Pace | Adaptable, Disciplined, Catalyst, Focussed, Organiser, Resilient, Responsible |

This mapping is operationally useful in both chairs: it tells a recruiter which
strengths legitimately pair with a chosen behaviour [8], and it tells a
candidate which strengths are *likely* in play given the behaviours named in
the advert — without licensing a rehearsed script, which the guidance forbids
[4] [11].

## Behaviour questions — the nine Behaviours

1. Tell us about a time you had to understand how a piece of your own work connected to a wider organisational objective. How did that change what you did? *(composed — in the style of the Behaviour "Seeing the big picture" [3])*
2. Describe an occasion when you identified an opportunity to improve how something was done, and took it forward. What was the idea and what happened? *(composed — in the style of the Behaviour "Changing and improving" [3])*
3. Give an example of a decision you made where the evidence was incomplete or conflicting. How did you weigh the options, implications and risks? *(composed — in the style of the Behaviour "Making effective decisions" [3])*
4. Tell us about a time you created or communicated a shared sense of direction for a group of people. What did you do to engage them? *(composed — in the style of the Behaviour "Leadership" [3])*
5. Describe a situation where you had to explain something complex to an audience who did not share your background. How did you adapt? *(composed — in the style of the Behaviour "Communicating and influencing" [3])*
6. Give an example of a time you built a working relationship outside your immediate team in order to get something done. *(composed — in the style of the Behaviour "Working together" [3])*
7. Tell us about a time you helped someone else develop a skill or grow in their role. What did you do, and how did you know it worked? *(composed — in the style of the Behaviour "Developing self and others" [3])*
8. Describe a time you improved the quality or efficiency of a service for the people who used it. How did you take account of different users' needs? *(composed — in the style of the Behaviour "Managing a quality service" [3])*
9. Give an example of a time you delivered a demanding piece of work to a deadline without letting quality slip. How did you keep focus? *(composed — in the style of the Behaviour "Delivering at pace" [3])*
10. Tell us about a time you had to change your own approach because organisational priorities shifted around you. *(composed — in the style of the Behaviour "Seeing the big picture" at a senior grade band [3])*
11. Describe an occasion when you influenced a decision you did not have the authority to make. *(composed — in the style of the Behaviour "Communicating and influencing" [3])*
12. Give an example of a time you had to promote inclusion or fairness in how work was allocated or how a team operated. *(composed — in the style of the Behaviour "Leadership" at Grade 7/Grade 6 band, where the published example refers to promoting diversity, inclusion and equality of opportunity [3])*

## Situational behaviour questions

GOV.UK confirms behaviours may be assessed by asking how a candidate would
behave in a given situation, as well as by past example [3].

1. You are asked to deliver a piece of work by a deadline you believe is unachievable without cutting corners. What would you do? *(composed — situational, in the style of "Delivering at pace" and "Managing a quality service" [3])*
2. A colleague in another directorate is blocking a piece of work you need. How would you approach it? *(composed — situational, in the style of "Working together" [3])*
3. You discover an error in analysis that has already been shared with a senior stakeholder. What would you do next? *(composed — situational, in the style of "Making effective decisions" and the Civil Service Code duty of honesty [3] [17])*
4. Your team is asked to adopt a new process that several members think is worse than the current one. How would you handle it? *(composed — situational, in the style of "Changing and improving" and "Leadership" [3])*

## Strengths questions — do not rehearse these

The published guidance is explicit that there are no right or wrong answers and
that candidates should not rehearse a response [4] [11]. These are provided so
you recognise the *form* of a strengths question, not so you can script one.

1. What kind of task do you find yourself volunteering for? *(composed — in the style of the strength "Catalyst" [11])*
2. What sort of work leaves you feeling energised at the end of the day? *(composed — a general strengths prompt in the style of the Strengths Dictionary's "Engagement" criterion [11])*
3. Do you prefer to start things or to finish them? *(composed — in the style of the strengths "Catalyst" and "Focussed" [11])*
4. When something changes at short notice, what is your first instinct? *(composed — in the style of the strength "Adaptable" [11])*
5. What do people most often come to you for help with? *(composed — in the style of the strength "Explainer" [11])*
6. Are you at your best working through detail or working across the big picture? *(composed — in the style of the strengths "Precise" and "Strategic" [11])*
7. What is something you have learned recently, just because you wanted to? *(composed — in the style of the strength "Learner" [11])*
8. How do you feel when a plan you made has to be abandoned? *(composed — in the style of the strengths "Organiser" and "Resilient" [11])*
9. Tell us about something you do regularly that you would do even if no one asked you to. *(composed — in the style of the Strengths Dictionary's "Use" criterion [11])*
10. What part of a project do you find yourself losing track of time in? *(composed — in the style of the Strengths Dictionary's "Engagement" criterion [11])*
11. When a group is stuck, what role do you naturally take? *(composed — in the style of the strengths "Mediator" and "Team Leader" [11])*
12. What are your main strengths? *(sourced: National Careers Service, "How to answer common interview questions")* [24]

## Follow-up and probing questions

At Deputy Director and Director level, panels are supplied with "suggested
probing questions" drawn from the unscored leadership assessments [9] [10].
Probes are also how a panel resolves a thin answer against the 7-point scale
[8] [14].

1. What was your specific contribution, as distinct from the team's? *(composed — probing for individual evidence against the behaviour statements [3])*
2. What options did you consider and reject, and why? *(composed — probing "Making effective decisions" [3])*
3. What would you do differently if you faced that again? *(composed — probing for reflection [3])*
4. How did you know it had worked — what changed? *(composed — probing for the result element of a STAR answer [23])*
5. Who disagreed with you, and how did you handle that? *(composed — probing "Communicating and influencing" [3])*
6. You have described what you did; what were you thinking at the point you decided? *(composed — probing the reasoning behind a decision [3])*
7. Can you tell us about a personal achievement at work? *(sourced: National Careers Service, "How to answer common interview questions")* [24]
8. Have you ever failed at a task? *(sourced: National Careers Service, "How to answer common interview questions")* [24]

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact.**

**Build a Success-Profiles-specific evidence bank, not a generic one.** The
structural feature of this framework is that behaviours are *named in the
advert* and capped at about four [8]. So build the bank around the nine
behaviour names rather than around your CV. For each of the nine, hold two
examples; for the four named in your advert, hold three. Tag each example with:
the behaviour(s) it evidences, the grade band it demonstrates, its date, and
the measurable result. When the advert lands you are then assembling, not
writing.

**Pitch to the band, and know which band.** The example statements escalate in
verb, scope and horizon between bands — *identify* becomes *actively build*,
own team becomes organisation [3]. Read your target band's statements for each
named behaviour before you select which story to tell, not after. If your best
story evidences the band below, either find a different story or extend the one
you have into its wider consequences.

**A worked answer skeleton for a behaviour question.** GOV.UK's own Experience
guidance points to STAR — set the scene, describe the actions taken, explain
the outcome [6] [23]. A serviceable shape for a 7-point-scale audience:

- *Situation (≈15%)* — one or two sentences. Where, when, who, and what made it
  non-trivial. Enough for the panel to judge scope; no more.
- *Task (≈10%)* — your specific responsibility, stated as "I was responsible
  for…". This is where individual attribution is established, and it is the
  most common thing panels have to probe for [8].
- *Action (≈55%)* — the load-bearing part. Sequential, first person, and
  chosen to hit the published example statements for your band. If the
  statement says "actively build and maintain a network", the action must show
  building and maintaining, not attending [3].
- *Result (≈20%)* — quantified where possible, plus what changed afterwards
  and what you learned. Descriptor 6 requires "some evidence of exceeding
  expectations" [14], and it is almost always the result and the reflection
  that supply it.

Worked illustration, against **Working together** at Grade 7/Grade 6, whose
published example is to "actively build and maintain a network of colleagues
and contacts to achieve progress on shared objectives" [3]. A weak answer says
"I worked with three other teams and we delivered the project." A strong one
says who those contacts were, why you sought them out *before* you needed them,
what you did to keep the relationships alive when there was nothing to ask for,
which shared objective this unlocked, and what it delivered — because that is
what "actively build and maintain" and "achieve progress on shared objectives"
literally ask for.

**Prepare for strengths by reflection, not script.** This is the inversion. The
guidance says do not rehearse, because the initial response is what is being
assessed [4] [11]. The legitimate preparation is to read the Strengths
Dictionary definitions [11], identify honestly which five or six describe you,
recall concrete instances so your instinctive answer has substance behind it,
and then stop. Do not draft sentences. A scripted strengths answer is usually
detectable — it arrives too fast, too polished and too general — and it
defeats the purpose of an element designed to test fit and motivation.

**Use the behaviour-to-strength mapping to anticipate, not to script.** If the
advert names "Delivering at pace", the strengths in play are likely to be drawn
from Adaptable, Disciplined, Catalyst, Focussed, Organiser, Resilient and
Responsible [11]. Knowing that lets you reflect in the right territory. It does
not license a prepared paragraph.

**Answer strengths questions in the present tense.** Behaviour answers are
historical ("I did"). Strengths questions ask what you do regularly, do well
and are motivated by [4] [11] — so the natural register is present-tense and
dispositional, followed by a brief real instance if one comes to mind. Notice
when the register of the question changes; that is the panel switching element.

**For interviewers.** Design the interview from the assessment matrix
backwards: decide the elements, cap behaviours at four [8], select strengths
that map to those behaviours [8] [11], and write the strengths questions so
they cannot be answered from the person specification. Score each question
independently before conferring, and reconcile arithmetically [8].

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

- **The scales are prescribed by the centre.** "Experience, technical skills
  and behaviours should be scored using the 7 point scale. Strengths should be
  scored using the 4 point scale" [8], repeated verbatim at Deputy Director and
  Director level [9] [10]. The 7-point scale aligns with the vX application
  tracking system [9] [10].
- **The seven descriptors, as published by COPFS (a department):** 1 Not
  Demonstrated; 2 Minimal; 3 Moderate; 4 Acceptable; 5 Good; 6 Strong; 7
  Outstanding [14]. Score 4 requires "Adequate positive evidence and any
  negative evidence would not cause concern"; score 6 requires evidence of
  exceeding expectations; score 7 requires evidence that "wholly exceeds
  expectation at this level" [14].
- **Benchmarks are set per campaign.** A benchmark is "a score that a candidate
  must achieve for a single or combination of elements"; COPFS gives 4 as usual,
  5 for some campaigns, and permits a single 3 where other behaviour scores are
  high [14]. GSS's departmental route treats a single 3 as the failure point
  that diverts a candidate to a temporary appointment [15].
- **Sift scoring may be simplified for volume.** Numerical per-element scoring
  for smaller campaigns; lead criteria or a 3-point A/B/C rating for
  high-volume campaigns; the progression benchmark "can be flexed in accordance
  with the quality of applications and the number of roles available" [8].
- **Element limits.** No more than four behaviours prioritised as critical [8]
  [9]; GSS caps the combined professional-competency-plus-behaviour mix at six
  [15].
- **Panel process is prescribed.** Observe, record, classify, evaluate, score
  independently; then reconcile by arithmetic average to a merit list; variance
  may be discussed but the mean decides [8].
- **Panels should be trained and diverse.** "It is recommended that all
  interview panels should be diverse, and all panellists should have undertaken
  training on interview skills prior to sitting on an interview panel" [8]. GSS
  additionally requires all panel members to be at least one grade higher than
  the advertised post, and an Independent Assessor at senior boards [15].
- **Strengths are deliberately not signalled.** Recruiters are advised not to
  name the assessed strengths in adverts, candidate packs or invitation
  letters, so that candidates cannot prepare responses [8].
- **Some senior exercises are diagnostic, not scored.** The Individual
  Leadership Assessment and Staff Engagement Exercise at Deputy Director and
  Director level "are not scored"; their findings and suggested probing
  questions go to the interview panel [9] [10].
- **Specialist support exists.** Vacancy holders needing help drafting
  assessment materials are directed to the Cabinet Office Occupational
  Psychology Team, on a chargeable basis, ideally engaged early [8].
- **Fairness was tested.** "Diversity outcomes were analysed, for all groups, to
  monitor impact on protected characteristics" for blended interviews, and
  analysis "indicated that, on the whole, there are no adverse impacts…as long
  as selection methods are delivered in a structured and consistent way, and
  training is provided to and/or official guidance is followed" [8]. The
  fairness claim is explicitly conditional on structured, trained delivery.
- **Merit remains the governing principle.** Selection "must continue to be
  undertaken on the principle of merit on the basis of fair and open
  competition" [8] [16].

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact.**

- **Rehearsing strengths answers.** The most common and most costly error,
  because it is the one thing standard interview advice tells you to do. The
  published instruction is not to rehearse [4] [11], and the framework
  deliberately hides the strengths list to prevent it [8].
- **Preparing all nine behaviours equally.** No more than about four will be
  critical [8]. Effort spread across nine is effort not spent reaching
  descriptor 5 or 6 on the four that count [14].
- **Answering at the wrong band.** The example statements differ by band in
  verb, scope and horizon [3]. An EO-level story told at Grade 7 reads as
  adequate rather than strong, which is the difference between a 4 and a 6
  [14].
- **Team narration instead of individual evidence.** Behaviours are "the
  actions and activities that people do" [3] — singular. Answers narrated as
  "we" force the panel to probe for your contribution [8], and probing time is
  time not spent adding evidence.
- **Confusing grade band with score.** "Level 3" in a departmental pack means
  the HEO band of the behaviour [15]; "3" on the scoring scale means Moderate
  Demonstration and is usually a fail [14]. Two different systems.
- **Treating the sift as a formality.** Behaviours, technical skills and
  experience are scored at sift, and at senior grades condensed into a single
  score [8] [9] [10]. The personal statement is an assessed document.
- **Assuming an "ineffective behaviours" list exists.** GOV.UK publishes
  effective-behaviour examples only [3]. Commercial prep material presenting
  "negative indicators" as official is not citing an official artefact.
- **For interviewers: naming the strengths in the pack.** This directly
  contradicts the guidance and destroys the element's validity by inviting the
  rehearsal it is designed to exclude [8].
- **For interviewers: conferring before scoring.** The prescribed order is
  independent scoring first, then reconciliation by arithmetic average [8].
  Discussing first collapses panel independence.
- **For interviewers: relying on structure alone for fairness.** The published
  diversity finding is conditional on structured, consistent delivery *and*
  training [8]. An untrained panel is outside the conditions under which no
  adverse impact was found.

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.**

1. **Build the evidence bank (90 minutes, once).** Make a nine-row table, one
   row per Behaviour [3]. Fill each with two candidate stories, recording date,
   your specific role, and the measurable result. Mark rows with fewer than two
   stories — those are your gaps.
2. **Band-lift drill (45 minutes).** Open [3] and read one behaviour at your
   current band and at the band above. Write a STAR answer at the lower band,
   then rewrite it for the higher band. List exactly what changed: scope, who
   else was involved, time horizon, verb. Repeat for a second behaviour.
3. **Self-score against the published descriptors (30 minutes).** Record a
   four-minute answer, then score it against the COPFS descriptors [14]. To
   claim 6 you must point to specific evidence of exceeding expectations. Most
   first attempts sit at 4; identify the single addition that would move it
   to 5.
4. **The unrehearsed strengths drill (20 minutes, paired).** Give a partner the
   twelve strengths questions above. They ask eight at random; you answer each
   within five seconds of the question ending, aloud, in the present tense. The
   objective is *not* a good answer — it is discovering which of the 36
   dictionary strengths [11] you reach for under no preparation. Debrief
   against the Dictionary definitions.
5. **Mapping drill (20 minutes).** Take a live Civil Service Jobs advert. List
   the named behaviours. Using the Dictionary's behaviour-to-strength map [11],
   write down the strengths plausibly in play. Reflect on which of those you
   genuinely hold — then deliberately do not draft answers [4].
6. **Probe survival (30 minutes, paired).** Answer one behaviour question, then
   have your partner ask only the six composed probes above, in order. If any
   probe finds nothing, the underlying evidence is thin — that is what a panel
   would find [8].
7. **Assessor's seat (45 minutes).** Score two recorded answers independently
   with a partner using the 7-point descriptors [14], without conferring. Then
   compare, discuss the underlying evidence, and take the arithmetic average as
   the prescribed method requires [8]. Notice how much the discussion does and
   does not move your score.
8. **Test familiarisation (60 minutes).** Work through the GOV.UK practice
   material for the Civil Service Judgement Test [12] and, if your campaign
   uses it, the Work Strengths Test [13], in a quiet place, untimed, as the
   guidance advises [13] [22].

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

- **Merit, fair and open competition.** Civil Service selection must be made on
  merit on the basis of fair and open competition, under the Civil Service
  Commission's Recruitment Principles; the Cabinet Office guidance restates
  this as the governing constraint on Success Profile design [8] [16].
- **Reasonable adjustments.** Section 20 of the Equality Act 2010 imposes a
  duty to make reasonable adjustments [18], and GOV.UK confirms this applies to
  the recruitment process [20]. The framework operationalises it: the Strengths
  Dictionary tells candidates to notify the recruiting manager of any
  reasonable adjustments needed, giving documents in large print or braille,
  extra time for a test or interview, and assistance at an assessment centre as
  examples [11]. For video interviews, panels are told to check the technology
  "is available, accessible and compatible for all panel members and
  candidates" and to consider adjustments arising from access to technology or
  other conditions [8]. The Judgement Test provides subtitles and British Sign
  Language translations for its video scenarios [12].
- **Health and disability enquiries.** Section 60 of the Equality Act 2010
  restricts asking about health or disability before a job offer, with limited
  exceptions including establishing whether adjustments to the assessment are
  needed [19]. Strengths questions, which probe motivation and preferred ways
  of working, must be written so as not to stray across that boundary.
- **Structure as a fairness control, conditionally.** The Cabinet Office
  reports that diversity outcomes for blended interviews were analysed across
  protected-characteristic groups and, on the whole, indicated no adverse
  impacts — but expressly conditional on selection methods being "delivered in
  a structured and consistent way" with training provided and official guidance
  followed [8]. ACAS similarly advises asking each applicant the same questions
  where possible and deciding on how each met the job description and person
  specification [21].
- **Automated scoring.** Ability tests "are scored automatically" and
  candidates are told whether they met the pass benchmark [5]; the Work
  Strengths Test reports a percentile against a comparison group [13]. Where a
  benchmark is a hard gate, candidates should ask about adjustments before
  sitting rather than after.

# Risks & Caveats

- **The element-combination-by-grade table is inferred from HR job-family
  guides.** [8], [9] and [10] are Cabinet Office guidance for HR roles at three
  grade tiers. They agree with each other, which is what makes the pattern
  credible, but no source found states a universal element-by-grade rule. Treat
  the table as a strong illustration, not a specification, and read the actual
  advert.
- **The application guidance packs date from 2019–2020.** [8], [9] and [10] are
  dated April/May 2020 and reference the vX applicant tracking system. The
  behaviour and element guides were refreshed 29 January 2025 [1] [3], but no
  refreshed version of the scoring guidance was located. The scoring rules may
  have been superseded internally; they remain the most recent central
  publication found.
- **The seven descriptor labels rest on one departmental source.** COPFS [14].
  The centre mandates a "7 point scale" [8] but was not found publishing its
  labels. Other departments may word the descriptors differently even while
  using the same scale.
- **Benchmark conventions vary by department.** The "4 usually, 5 sometimes,
  one 3 tolerated" convention is COPFS's [14]. Do not assume it applies to your
  campaign; the pack should state the benchmark.
- **The Strengths Dictionary is hosted by the National Crime Agency.** The
  document is Cabinet Office Success Profiles material and is referenced by name
  in the Cabinet Office guidance's further-guidance list [8], but it was
  located as an NCA-hosted publication [11]. It carries no visible revision
  date, so the 36-strength list could have changed.
- **Composed questions are illustrative.** The behaviour and strengths
  questions here mimic the published framework's style and have no official
  status. They are not real or leaked Civil Service questions and must never be
  presented as such.
- **The Fast Stream and some professions run additional stages** beyond the
  pattern described here; this document does not cover them.
- **Departmental sources are labelled.** COPFS [14] and GSS [15] publish
  departmental applications of the framework. Where this document uses them it
  says so; do not read them as Civil-Service-wide rules.

  Interview methodology", "Success Profiles: Scoring case studies" and
  "Designing Strengths Questions" [8]. None was located in the public domain;
  they appear to sit behind Civil Service Learning. Obtaining them would supply
  the central scoring descriptors and, potentially, an official question set.
  *(Would resolve: a GOV.UK publication or FOI release of these three
  documents.)*
- **Has the 2020 scoring guidance been superseded?** The behaviour guides were
  updated in January 2025 [1] [3] but the scoring packs are 2020 [8] [9] [10].
  A current central statement of the 7-point and 4-point rules would close this.
- **What are the four points of the strengths scale?** The centre mandates a
  4-point scale for strengths [8] but no official descriptor set for it was
  found. Practitioner accounts describe it but no primary source was located.
- **Does the Civil Service publish official example behaviour or strengths
  interview questions?** Carried forward from `uk-frameworks-foundation` and
  still unresolved: none found on GOV.UK, in the element guides, or in the
  Cabinet Office application packs [1] [3] [4] [8].
- **Is the 36-strength Dictionary current?** [11] is undated. A dated central
  republication would confirm the list.
- **How do departments other than COPFS word the 1–7 descriptors?** A second
  departmental publication of the scale would establish whether the wording is
  centrally supplied or locally drafted [14].
