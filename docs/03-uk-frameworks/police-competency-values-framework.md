# Executive Summary

This is the deep-dive on the College of Policing **Competency and Values
Framework (CVF)** — the behavioural standard that underpins recruitment,
promotion, appraisal and leadership development across policing in England and
Wales [1] [2]. It sets out the **six competencies at three levels** and the
**three values** in their official 2024 wording, records how the framework is
actually used in the national police-constable selection process, and gives a
question bank plus coaching for candidates and interviewers.

The single most important fact in this document is a **currency warning**. The
CVF was rewritten in 2024. The 2016 "clusters" were removed, the four 2016
values (integrity, impartiality, public service, transparency) were replaced by
three values drawn from the new Code of Ethics (**courage; respect and empathy;
public service**), and two competencies were renamed — "We are collaborative"
became **"We collaborate"** and "We deliver, support and inspire" became **"We
support and inspire"** [1] [2] [4]. Forces had until **1 May 2025** to
transition [1], and the College's own national sift exercises switched to the
2024 CVF on **10 March 2025** [7]. A great deal of published preparation
material still teaches the 2016 model; a candidate who prepares against it will
be naming values that no longer exist.

Confidence is **High**. Every factual claim rests on College of Policing
primary documents — the framework itself, its guidance, the Code of Ethics
principles, and the College's own candidate guidance for the national sift and
online assessment process — supplemented by statute for the fairness section.
The coaching layer and the composed questions are clearly separated and carry
no evidential weight.

# Key Takeaways

- The 2024 CVF is **six competencies** (We are emotionally aware; We take
  ownership; We collaborate; We support and inspire; We analyse critically; We
  are innovative and open-minded) and **three values** (courage; respect and
  empathy; public service). *(documented)* [1]
- The 2016 clusters were abolished and the four 2016 values replaced; two
  competency names changed. Prep material using "We are collaborative",
  "We deliver, support and inspire", "integrity", "impartiality" or
  "transparency" as current CVF labels is **out of date**. *(documented)*
  [1] [2] [4]
- Competencies have **three levels of increasing complexity** — level 1
  practitioner, level 2 supervisor/middle manager, level 3 senior
  manager/executive — and the levels are **cumulative**. Values are **not**
  levelled: the same value behaviours are expected of everyone regardless of
  rank. *(documented)* [1] [2]
- **Initial police recruitment assesses at level 1**, in both the national sift
  and the online assessment process. *(documented)* [5] [6]
- The national selection process is documented in detail: a **national sift**
  (situational judgement test plus behavioural styles questionnaire) followed
  by an **online assessment process** of a competency-based interview (five
  questions, 60s thinking / 300s answer each), a written exercise and a
  briefing exercise (12 questions) — each exercise assessing a named subset of
  CVF competencies and values. *(documented)* [5] [6]
- The College tells interviewers that **most of the interview should focus on
  past behaviour**, with probing for evidence, and that hypothetical questions
  are a supplement, not the core. *(documented)* [2]
- The guidance is explicit that the behavioural indicators are examples only
  and that **"the CVF itself should not be used as a simple checklist, whether
  as part of selection or assessment, or professional development review"**.
  *(documented)* [2]
- For candidates the practical consequence is that indicator-phrase
  name-dropping is a weak strategy: assessors are told to judge against the
  competency **description**, not the bullet list. *(guidance)*

# Purpose

This document answers three questions.

For **candidates** (police officer, police staff, special constable, PCSO, and
serving officers seeking promotion or specialist roles): what exactly is the
CVF in its current 2024 form, which competencies and values will be assessed at
which stage of the process, at what level should I pitch an answer, and what
does a strong CVF answer look like?

For **interviewers, assessors and police HR**: what does the College actually
require of a CVF-based selection process, what does its own assessment-design
guidance say about levels, rating scales, assessor training and bias, and where
are the documented boundaries (no checklists; reasonable adjustments)?

For **readers of the wider knowledge base**: how the CVF compares with the
other UK frameworks covered in `uk-frameworks-foundation`, which this document
deepens rather than restates.

# Scope

**Covered:** the CVF (2024) in full — the six competencies with their official
descriptions and level structure, the three values and their derivation from
the Code of Ethics ethical policing principles; the documented 2016→2024
change; how the CVF is used across recruitment, promotion, professional
development review and talent development; the national police-constable
selection process (national sift and online assessment process) as published by
the College; the special constable route; where the CVF sits in the National
Police Promotion Framework; a question bank; coaching; and the assessor-side
evidence on scoring and bias.

**Not covered:** the full text of every behavioural indicator at every level

fitness and medical stages of police recruitment; force-specific frameworks
that some forces run instead of the CVF [2]; Police Scotland and (except in
passing) Police Service of Northern Ireland processes, which are separate
jurisdictions; the legal syllabus of the NPPF step two examination; and general
answering technique, which lives in `techniques-foundation`.

# Definitions

Prefer the shared `GLOSSARY.md`. Used here in this narrowed sense:

- **CVF** — the College of Policing Competency and Values Framework: three
  values and six competencies describing the behaviours required of police
  officers and staff to be effective and to uphold the Code of Ethics [1].
- **Value (CVF sense)** — "beliefs which are important to an individual and
  which guide and motivate particular behaviours and actions"; in the CVF the
  three values are derived from the ethical policing principles and are **not**
  split into levels [1] [2].
- **Competency (CVF sense)** — "behaviours (knowledge, skills or abilities)
  related to effective job performance"; each of the six has three levels [2].
- **Level** — one of three descriptions of a competency reflecting increasing
  complexity of behaviour: level 1 practitioner, level 2 supervisor/middle
  manager, level 3 senior manager/executive. Levels are cumulative and are not
  assigned to specific ranks [1] [2].
- **Example behavioural indicator** — an illustrative behaviour listed under a
  competency or value; explicitly not an exhaustive list and not a checklist
  [2].
- **National sift** — the College's first-stage online screening for police
  constable, PCSO and special constable recruitment: a situational judgement
  test and a behavioural styles questionnaire [6] [8].
- **Online assessment process (OAP)** — the College's second-stage national
  assessment for initial police recruitment: competency-based interview,
  written exercise and briefing exercise [5].
- **Policing professional profiles (PPP)** — national role profiles describing
  purpose, accountabilities, behaviours, skills, education and experience for
  common policing roles, including the CVF competency levels for the role [1].

# Framework Context

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## Where the CVF sits

The CVF "provides clear expectations for everyone working in policing",
describing "the behaviours required by you, as a police officer or member of
staff, to be effective in your role and uphold the Code of Ethics", and it
"plays a significant role in the recruitment, assessment and development of
officers and staff at every level" [1]. It is the basis of several national HR
processes — "national assessments for recruitment and promotion, PDR,
leadership development and continuing professional development (CPD)" [2].

Use is not universal or mandatory. Some forces have adopted the CVF entire,
others have amalgamated it with an existing force framework, and it "is not
mandatory to use the CVF as a force framework" — though the College recommends
that forces using a different framework conduct a mapping exercise so that the
CVF behaviours are appropriately represented [2].

The CVF is the *how*; the *what* remains defined by organisational strategies,
standards and objectives, the policing professional profiles, supervision
guidelines and leadership standards. The College's own diagram of "effective
performance" places the Code of Ethics and the CVF on the "how we do it" side
[2].

## The 2016 → 2024 change (read this before using any older material)

| Aspect | CVF 2016 [4] | CVF 2024 [1] [2] |
|---|---|---|
| Clusters | Six competencies grouped in three clusters: resolute, compassionate and committed; inclusive, enabling and visionary leadership; intelligent, creative and informed policing | **Clusters removed** for simplicity, "to create a simpler model without losing any of the benefits of the model itself" |
| Values | **Four**: impartiality, integrity, public service, transparency | **Three**: courage; respect and empathy; public service — derived from the ethical policing principles of the 2024 Code of Ethics |
| Competency names | We are emotionally aware; We take ownership; **We are collaborative**; **We deliver, support and inspire**; We analyse critically; We are innovative and open-minded | We are emotionally aware; We take ownership; **We collaborate**; **We support and inspire**; We analyse critically; We are innovative and open-minded |
| Levels | Three levels, cumulative, mapped broadly to practitioner / supervisor / senior manager | Unchanged in structure: three levels, cumulative, level 1 practitioner, level 2 supervisor/middle manager, level 3 senior manager/executive; explicitly "not assigned to specific ranks" |
| Structure of competencies | — | "the basic structure of the competencies has remained the same" |

The College states the reasons for the change: significant shifts since 2016 in
the profile of the policing workforce, the operational context and the national
landscape, practical learning from using the CVF, and above all "changes to the
policy landscape — especially a new Code of Ethics" [2]. The stated design
goals were less cluttered content and removal of duplication; "simpler and
clearer wording ... more specific, behavioural and measurable"; and removal of
the clusters [2].

**Transition dates.** "Your force has until 1 May 2025 to transition to this
revised version" [1]; the 2016 version remained available on the College
website until May 2025 [2]. For the College's own assessments, a February 2025
notice set out which CVF version applied where: the initial-recruitment
**national sift** exercises were updated to CVF 2024 on **10 March 2025** (2016
before that date); the **online assessment process**, the **police leadership
programme stage 5 executive leaders development centre**, and the **Fast Track
national assessment centres** (both serving-constable and substantive-inspector
routes) already used CVF 2024 [7]. The policing professional profiles were
updated on a rolling basis, with core rank profiles updated in April 2024 and
other profiles by April 2025 [2].

## The values and the Code of Ethics

The three CVF values are the three **ethical policing principles** of the Code
of Ethics, summarised into the framework [2]. The Code's own statement of the
principles — which is also the CVF's definition of each value — is [3]:

| Principle / value | "As policing professionals, we commit to…" |
|---|---|
| **Courage** | "making, communicating and being accountable for decisions, and standing against anything that could bring our profession into disrepute" |
| **Respect and empathy** | "encouraging, listening to and understanding the views of others, and seeking to recognise and respond to the physical, mental and emotional challenges that we and other people may face" |
| **Public service** | "working in the public interest, fostering public trust and confidence, and taking pride in providing an excellent service to the public" |

The Code of Ethics and the CVF are described as complementary documents, both
to be regarded as guides to ethical behaviour in policing; the Code's strapline
is "doing the right things, in the right way, for the right reasons" [2] [3].
Where behaviour falls short, assessment moves out of the CVF and into the
conduct regulations (for officers) or terms of employment (for staff) [2].

## The six competencies, in official wording

Each competency below is given with the College's own definition of what it
means and why it matters, plus an indicative level-1 and level-3 behaviour so
the reader can see the change in altitude. The full indicator lists are in the
framework PDF [1].

### We are emotionally aware

"We make the effort to understand ourselves, our colleagues and all those we
serve so that we can manage our own emotions and understand the emotions of
those around us." It covers managing emotions in stressful situations,
understanding the underlying reasons for one's own behaviour, seeking
appropriate support "so that we do not just cope but thrive at work", and being
sensitive to cultural and individual differences. It matters because "the way
in which we conduct ourselves is just as important as what we do", and because
understanding ourselves improves resilience [1].

- Level 1: "I seek to understand and manage my own emotions to remain
  effective, even under pressure" [1].
- Level 3: "I create a supportive and compassionate organisational culture that
  recognises and values all people" [1].

### We take ownership

"We take personal responsibility for what we do ourselves and for making
policing better and our force more effective." It covers delivering tasks in a
timely and effective way, making decisions appropriate to one's level and area
of work "being clear why we do so (for example, by using decision-making
models)", seeking feedback without being defensive, and recognising where
limitations in one's own knowledge may affect decision-making. It matters
because "our work is safety-critical and of huge public interest", and because
"not all decisions need senior leader approval" [1].

- Level 1: "I take responsibility for my own actions and decisions" [1].
- Level 3: "I promote a culture that responds to mistakes with learning rather
  than with blame" [1].

### We collaborate

"We can only deliver effective policing by working with others — not just with
the colleagues we see daily, but across our whole force, with other
organisations and with the public." It covers knowing stakeholders, building
sustainable partnerships, establishing effective ways of working with new
people "sometimes at great speed and under pressure", and sharing skills,
knowledge and insight to reduce "silo working". It matters because demands on
the police "are not defined by organisational and geographical boundaries" [1].

- Level 1: "I work cooperatively with others to get things done, willingly
  giving help and support to colleagues" [1].
- Level 3: "I am politically astute and understand how national policy and
  politics affects our partners" [1].

### We support and inspire

"We understand the vision for the organisation. We apply our organisation's
values in our day-to-day activities to provide inspiration and clarity to
others." The framework is explicit that "this behaviour is not restricted to
those who are in formal or senior management positions" — everyone contributes
by operating at their best and helping others, including through coaching and
mentoring [1].

- Level 1: "I take steps to understand how my work contributes to the purpose
  of policing and the priorities of my force" [1].
- Level 3: "I demonstrate long-term strategic thinking, going beyond personal
  goals and considering how the police service operates in the broader societal
  and economic environment" [1].

### We analyse critically

"We analyse diverse information and the best available evidence to make
decisions and understand the root causes of issues that arise in complex
situations." It covers drawing on experience, knowledge and wide-ranging
evidence, and combining insight with evidence-based approaches "accepting that
we will not have all the answers". It matters because policing must "accept
that ambiguity is part of contemporary working life" while still identifying
interrelationships between factors and implementing preventative solutions [1].

- Level 1: "I recognise the need to think critically about issues and challenge
  my assumptions" [1].
- Level 3: "I identify root causes, even in ambiguous or unclear situations, and
  ensure my organisation goes beyond treating only the symptoms" [1].

### We are innovative and open-minded

"We have an inquisitive and outward-looking nature, searching for new
information to understand alternative sources of good practice and implement
creative working methods." It includes looking at "relevant standards outside
policing in other organisations and sectors". It matters because "new and
emerging threats mean that our required response will not always be obvious"
[1].

- Level 1: "I share suggestions with colleagues, speaking up to help improve
  existing working methods and practices" [1].
- Level 3: "I lead, implement and evaluate new, complex and creative initiatives
  from inception to impact" [1].

## The three levels, and how they map

Each competency is described at three levels "which reflect increasing
complexity of behaviours", because "behaviours tend to become more complex at
more senior levels in the organisation" [2]. The mapping is **broad, not
binding**: "although the levels will broadly relate to role functions across
the organisation ... a key feature of the model is that levels can be flexibly
applied to specific professional profiles as required", and the levels "are not
assigned to specific ranks" [2].

| Level | Broad role band [1] [2] | Police leadership programme stage and ranks [2] |
|---|---|---|
| Level 1 | Practitioner | Stage 1 (initial entry routes; leadership foundations) — constable and equivalent, including equivalent police staff grades |
| Level 2 | Supervisor / middle manager | Stages 2 and 3 (first-line and mid-level leaders; Fast Track constable to inspector) — police sergeant, inspector and equivalent police staff grades |
| Level 3 | Senior manager / executive | Stages 4 and 5 (Fast Track inspector to superintendent; senior and executive leadership programmes) — superintendent, chief superintendent, chief officer ranks and equivalent police staff grades |

Two structural points candidates routinely miss. First, **the levels are
cumulative**: "those working at higher levels should have built on their
behaviours from the preceding levels" [1]. A level-2 answer does not abandon
level-1 behaviour; it adds to it. Second, **values are not levelled**: levels
apply to competencies only, and the same value behaviours are expected of
everyone regardless of rank or seniority [1] [2] [9].

The CVF is aligned to the national **policing professional profiles**, which
describe the purpose, key accountabilities, behaviours, skills, education and
experience for each role "as well as the competency levels" — so the level to
assess for a given vacancy is normally specified by the profile [1] [9].

## How the CVF is used across the employee lifecycle

**Recruitment, assessment and selection.** "Values now play an important part in
recruitment, assessment and selection. During recruitment interviews and
assessments for selection, candidates should demonstrate how they apply the
three values in their work, as well as how they demonstrate the competencies"
[2]. For interviews specifically: "As with previous competency-based or
values-based interviews, it is good practice to ask about examples of past
behaviour, probing for evidence. It is also sometimes helpful to ask about how
candidates would deal with hypothetical future situations. However, most of the
interview should focus on past behaviour" [2]. For assessment centres:
exercises "should be carefully designed to measure competencies and values",
usually measuring more than one competency "but not more than three or four in
a large exercise", and "candidates should be clearly told which competencies
and values are being assessed" [2].

**Appraisal / professional development review (PDR).** The CVF supplies the
behavioural half of a rounded appraisal. Individuals are advised that "it is
not necessary to demonstrate evidence of every single indicator of a competency
or value", but to be ready to give examples of the competency as a whole [2].

**Talent development.** The CVF is "an enabler in the talent system", used to
identify potential for leadership roles, lateral or specialist development, and
development needs — with the caveat that it "is only part of the picture" since
it looks only at behaviour, not skills, knowledge and practical experience [2].
The College also suggests 360-degree feedback against the CVF for
self-development [2]. Where candidates are tested at a level above their
current role, "care should be taken to ensure that the context of the
assessment is appropriate and fair given the experience and opportunities of
the participant pool" [2].

## The national police constable selection process

The national process for initial police recruitment has two published stages
after the force's own application and eligibility checks: the **sift** (either
an in-force sift or the College's national sift, depending on the force) and
then the **online assessment process** [5]. Throughout, "you will be assessed
against the competencies and values that are important for effective police
constables ... For initial police recruitment, the competencies review criteria
is set at level 1" [5]. Candidates "do not need any prior knowledge of policing
or the police role" [5], and for the OAP specifically "you do not need to know
about the law or procedures relating to police work to be successful" [5].

### Stage 1 — the national sift

Two untimed online exercises [6]:

| Exercise | Format | Expected duration | CVF assessed |
|---|---|---|---|
| Situational judgement test (SJT) | Scenarios reflecting challenging situations in the role; four possible actions per scenario, each rated independently on a five-point effectiveness scale (counterproductive / ineffective / slightly effective / effective / very effective) | ~45 minutes | We analyse critically; We are innovative and open-minded; We take ownership; We are emotionally aware; We collaborate; We support and inspire |
| Behavioural styles questionnaire (BSQ) | Statements about typical workplace behaviour rated on a seven-point agree–disagree scale; some responses trigger a ranking task ("select the one that is most like you") | ~20 minutes | All six competencies plus all three values (respect and empathy; courage; public service) |

The College publishes two worked example SJT scenarios in the candidate
guidance — one concerning a group of young people congregating outside a
community centre where residents want them moved on but no offence appears to
have been committed, and one concerning a forgetful new team member who has not
completed paperwork on a case a week after attendance [6]. Candidates are told
to rate each action independently, that the same rating may be given to more
than one action, and that the full range of the scale need not be used [6].

### Stage 2 — the online assessment process

Three exercises delivered on the Tazio platform, each assessing a named subset
of the CVF [5]:

| Exercise | Format | Timing | CVF assessed |
|---|---|---|---|
| Competency-based interview (CBI) | Five questions about how you have dealt with specific situations in the past; each read aloud by a pre-recorded assessor video and shown on screen with prompts; examples may come from work or personal life | ~40 minutes total; **60 seconds** thinking time and **300 seconds** (five minutes) to record per question | Courage; public service; respect and empathy; We take ownership; We are innovative and open-minded |
| Written exercise | You take the role of a police constable and complete an urgent written task for your line manager, using four supplied items of information and adding nothing invented | ~10 minutes to read instructions; ~40 minutes expected, 120-minute limit | Respect and empathy; We analyse critically; We support and inspire; We collaborate |
| Briefing exercise | You take the role of a police constable dealing with issues presented to you, giving verbal responses to **12 questions** in three parts of four, with new information introduced at parts two and three | ~60 minutes: ~10 minutes untimed, unassessed preparation, then ~50 minutes; **60 seconds** thinking and **180 seconds** answering per question | Public service; We are emotionally aware; We take ownership; We are innovative and open-minded |

The national recruitment site aimed at applicants describes the same three
exercises in candidate-facing terms — a video interview in which each question
appears on screen with about a minute to prepare, a ~40-minute written task, and
a ~60-minute briefing exercise of 12 questions — and refers applicants to the
College's CVF for the competencies and values being assessed [16].

Additional documented process facts: spelling and grammar are **not** assessed
in the written exercise, explicitly for fairness across devices [5];
**pre-prepared notes are prohibited** and evidence of them "may result in an
automatic failure", though notes made during an exercise may be used [5]; an
automatic fail for contravening the Code of Ethics, behaving inappropriately or
seeking unfair advantage is decided by an independent quality-assurance team
and **cannot be appealed** [5]; appeals on other grounds must be submitted in
writing to the recruiting force **within 24 hours** of taking the assessment and
are only considered if the candidate did not meet the standard [5]; a resit is
possible from three months after the start of the assessment window, limited to
two attempts in 12 months, and all exercises must be retaken [5]; and a pass may
be transferable between forces at the receiving force's discretion [5].

### Special constables and police staff

Special constable applications are made directly to a force; if progressed, the
candidate completes "a national sift and an in-force interview", a process that
"tests the key competencies and values that are important for the role of a
special constable and form the national standard for special constable
recruitment", with the competencies review criteria again "set at level 1" [8].
In the in-force interview, "the panel will ask you a series of questions based
on the CVF", which "may be followed by additional questions to further explore
your initial answer"; the panel may interrupt to seek more information and
takes notes throughout to capture evidence [8]. The CVF applies to police staff
as well as officers — it describes the behaviours required "by you, as a police
officer or member of staff" — and the leadership-level mapping explicitly
includes "equivalent police staff grades" at every level [1] [2].

### Promotion

Promotion to sergeant and inspector runs through the **National Police
Promotion Framework**. Step one confirms eligibility, including "demonstrated
competence in their current rank through their professional development review
(PDR), their student officer learning and assessment portfolio (SOLAP) or an
acceptable equivalent", plus conduct, performance and attendance criteria [10].
Step two is the legal examination — "a single, three-hour examination containing
150 multiple-choice questions", with a pass mark of 55% (77 correct answers out
of 140 scored questions) for the sergeants' paper [10]. A step two pass is valid
for five years less one day, within which the candidate must be successful at
step three [10]. The CVF is the framework behind the behavioural side of
promotion: the College describes it as the basis of "national assessments for
recruitment and promotion" [2], and its own Fast Track national assessment
centres (constable-to-inspector and inspector-to-superintendent) use CVF 2024
[7].

# Question Bank

follow-up probes. Provenance per the house rule: *(sourced …)* questions are

## Values — Courage

1. Tell us about a time you challenged behaviour you felt was wrong. *(sourced: PolicePay, "Police CVF Interview Questions and Answers")* [20]
2. Tell us about a time you spoke up even though it was uncomfortable. *(sourced: PolicePay, "Police CVF Interview Questions and Answers")* [20]
3. Tell us about a time you admitted a mistake. *(sourced: PolicePay, "Police CVF Interview Questions and Answers")* [20]

## Values — Respect and empathy

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact. It points back to the
evidence above and introduces no new claims.**

## Pitch the answer at the right level

The level is the thing most candidates get wrong, and a force's own published
guidance lists it among the common mistakes: "Not the right CVF level — be
mindful of the level you are answering at. Review the Framework and check the
level" [17]. The documented anchors are: level 1 is practitioner, level 2
supervisor/middle manager, level 3 senior manager/executive, and the levels are
cumulative [1] [2].

A practical way to hear the difference in your own answer:

- **Level 1 sounds like:** *I* noticed, *I* decided, *I* asked for advice, *I*
  kept people updated. The unit of analysis is your own actions and your own
  task. This is the level assessed in initial police recruitment [5] [6].
- **Level 2 sounds like:** I set the direction for others, I monitored the
  team's progress, I did not leave the problem for someone else, I created the
  conditions in which other people worked well. The unit is a team.
- **Level 3 sounds like:** I changed how the organisation works, I acted on the
  external environment, I took a service-wide view. The unit is the
  organisation and its context.

If you are applying for a level-2 role, do not drop level-1 behaviour out of the
story — the levels build on each other [1]. Add the supervisory layer on top of
demonstrably sound personal practice.

## Use STAR, because the College tells you to

The College's own candidate guidance names STAR and defines each element:
Situation ("briefly describe the specific event or situation ... a short
description to set the context"); Task ("briefly explain what you had to do ...
what were the success criteria?"); Action ("the main part of your answer ...
What did you do? How did you do it? Why did you do it that way?"); Result
("What was the outcome? What did you learn?") [5]. It also advises that "it may
be beneficial to provide one example for each question and describe this in
detail, rather than using different examples" [5]. Some forces additionally
offer CARL as an alternative structure [17]. Deeper treatment of STAR variants
lives in `techniques-foundation`.

Budget your time against the documented clock. In the OAP competency-based
interview you get 60 seconds to think and 300 seconds to speak [5]; in the
briefing exercise, 60 seconds to think and only 180 seconds to answer [5]. A
five-minute answer that spends two minutes on Situation has thrown away the
part that scores.

## Worked skeleton — "We take ownership", level 1

Target question: *"Tell us about a time you took responsibility for something
that went wrong."*

| Element | What goes here | Roughly how long (of 5 minutes) |
|---|---|---|
| **Situation** | One or two sentences. Where, when, who, and the single fact that makes the story matter. "I was the duty supervisor on a Saturday shift; a delivery of donated stock arrived and I had logged it to the wrong account." | 30–40s |
| **Task** | What you had to achieve and what "good" would have looked like [5]. "I needed to correct the record before the weekly reconciliation, and to tell my manager before she found it herself." | 20–30s |
| **Action** | The bulk. Each action a separate, concrete thing you personally did, with the *reason* attached. Owning the error; telling the manager unprompted; correcting the record; checking whether anyone else had relied on the wrong figure; proposing a check so it could not recur. The reasons are what evidence the value underneath [9]. | 2.5–3 min |
| **Result** | Outcome plus learning [5]. "Reconciliation was clean; the double-check is still in use; and I now say the awkward thing first rather than last." | 40–60s |

Why this scores against the framework: the competency description covers
accepting responsibility for one's judgements and seeking feedback "without
being defensive so that we can learn from our mistakes" [1], and the level-1
indicators include taking responsibility for one's own actions and decisions
and regularly updating people who are relying on you [1]. Notice that the
answer never says the words "I take ownership" — it evidences them.

## Prepare for values as well as competencies

Values are assessed alongside competencies at every stage: the OAP interview
assesses courage, public service and respect and empathy explicitly, alongside
two competencies [5], and the BSQ covers all three values [6]. Because the
values are not levelled [1], the same value behaviour is expected of a special
constable applicant and a chief officer — what changes is only the scale of the
situation. Prepare at least one story per value, and make sure each one can
answer "why did that matter to you?", because that is where a values probe goes
[9].

## Do not walk in with a script

The College's rules are explicit and carry a hard penalty: pre-prepared notes or
scripts are prohibited in the online assessments and evidence of them "may
result in an automatic failure", which cannot be appealed [5]. Preparation
therefore means having a **stock of real experiences you can find quickly**, not
memorised paragraphs. Build a grid — six competencies plus three values down one
side, your genuine experiences across the top — and rehearse retrieving them,
not reciting them.

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## The College's central instruction to assessors

The behavioural indicators "are just that — they are indicators of the sorts of
behaviours that could demonstrate the competency"; "the list of behavioural
indicators is not the totality of the competency", and therefore "**the CVF
itself should not be used as a simple checklist, whether as part of selection or
assessment, or professional development review (PDR)**. Instead, it should be
used as a guide to what the competency or value is and how it may be expressed
in the workplace" [2]. Those assessing others "should retain flexibility and be
open to diverse ways of demonstrating the behaviours, as long as they are in
line with the definition of the competency" [2]. The framework itself repeats
the instruction and adds that assessment must take account of individual
circumstances, the specific requirements of the role, and "competency
description (not just the behavioural examples)" [1]. The same warning is
reproduced verbatim at the top of at least one force's published interview
marking guide [18].

## Designing a CVF assessment

The College's guidance for using the CVF in recruitment and selection sets out
the design chain [9]:

- **Choose the level from the role, not the rank.** "Most roles will have the
  level specified for each competency as part of the professional profile and
  this will normally be the level that should be assessed against", though
  recruiters may assess higher when selecting onto a development programme
  aimed at a higher rank; assessment content and performance expectations
  "should be relevant to the specific job being recruited for" [9]. The same
  indicator can imply different expected behaviour for, say, a sergeant and an
  inspector [9].
- **Define what good looks like in advance.** In an interview, assessors can use
  the indicators to judge the evidence; for a richer exercise such as a roleplay
  or written exercise "the indicators in the CVF will not be sufficient on their
  own" and exercise-specific assessor guidance must be generated, often by
  trialling the exercise with good performers already in the role. Crucially,
  "there will often be several different ways a candidate could demonstrate the
  competency in any given scenario — this is about creating supportive guidance
  for assessors, not a 'model answer'" [9].
- **Use a described rating scale.** The published example scale is four bands,
  A–D, defined by the quality and quantity of evidence: A "substantial positive
  evidence of the competency observed ... and few or no areas for development";
  B "positive evidence ... and some areas for development but, on balance, more
  positive evidence"; C "some positive evidence ... but, on balance, more areas
  for development"; D "substantial areas for development identified and little
  or no positive evidence" [9]. Scales must carry clear descriptors and
  assessors must be calibrated in advance — "benchmarking or frame of reference
  training" [9].
- **Assess with ORCE.** Trained assessors should follow observe–record–classify–
  evaluate: observe and record contemporaneously and as close to verbatim as
  possible with "no assuming or interpreting"; classify only after the candidate
  has finished, classifying each behaviour once and not double-counting;
  evaluate on the quality and quantity of classified evidence, independently of
  other assessors before discussion [9].
- **Train and quality-assure assessors.** Minimum training content is specified:
  understanding the role being assessed for, a shared understanding of the
  criteria, familiarisation with questions or exercise materials, and
  familiarisation with the competencies and values; volume processes need a
  quality-assurance process monitoring assessors for consistency [9].
- **Watch the documented biases.** The guidance names expectancy effects,
  confirmatory information seeking ("interviewers tend to ask questions designed
  to elicit information confirming their initial impressions"), personal liking,
  primacy, stereotyping, prototyping and halo/horns — countered by ORCE,
  restricting pre-assessment information and comprehensive assessor training
  [9].

The College also notes that values assessment differs from competency
assessment in practice: assessors "may need to probe behaviours to identify the
underlying values — asking candidates to explain their thought processes and
feelings and why they took a particular course of action"; values questions can
be emotive, so assessors should be alert to candidates becoming upset, treat
disclosed personal information sensitively, and not reproduce sensitive personal
detail in scoring sheets or feedback reports [9].

## What the national process shows about scoring in practice

The College's OAP assessors are "a mixture of police officers, police staff and
people who have been specifically chosen from the community", all specifically
trained per exercise and continually monitored; they see only the candidate's
name and email address and do not know whether the candidate has applied before
[5]. Candidates receive a feedback report giving the overall score for each
exercise and describing "the kinds of performance that distinguishes
better-performing candidates from those who did not perform as effectively"
[5]. In the in-force special constable interview, the panel probes initial
answers with additional questions and takes notes throughout "to capture all
evidence" [8] — the interview-room form of ORCE.

Force-published guidance describes what panels count as good evidence: "clear
and detailed sentences, providing evidence rather than statements", the
candidate "using I instead of we/us/them", a suitable detailed example with a
clear "beginning, middle and end", and talking openly "about reflecting and
learning the lessons" [18]. One force runs its constable and special constable
force interviews with a two-person panel drawn from police officers, special
constables and police staff, face to face or over Teams, with six questions,
questions shown to the candidate before being asked, and a commitment to verbal
feedback within three weeks for unsuccessful candidates [17].

## Why structure matters at all

The College's own guidance states that interviews "are best when they are
standardised so that each candidate gets the same opportunity to demonstrate
their suitability to the role", that "highly structured interviews have been
shown to be the most valid", and that unstructured interviews "have very poor
validity" [9]. Acas likewise advises employers to ask each applicant the same
questions where possible and to base decisions on how each applicant met the job
description and person specification [14]; CIPD describes structured interviews
as a predefined question set scored against pre-agreed criteria [15]. The
underlying occupational-psychology evidence base — including recent
re-estimation of predictor validities — is treated in `best-practice-foundation`
[23].

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact.**

- **Preparing against the 2016 framework.** The most consequential error
  available to a 2026 candidate. If your notes contain "integrity",
  "impartiality" or "transparency" as CVF *values*, or "We are collaborative" /
  "We deliver, support and inspire" as competencies, they describe a framework
  that was withdrawn from the College website in May 2025 [1] [2] [4].
  Re-download the framework PDF before you prepare.
- **"CVF bingo".** A force's published guidance names this explicitly: "buzz
  words don't score — please don't try and get as many buzz words in as
  possible, it is about you answering the question and being authentic in your
  response, not repeating buzz words from the CVF Framework" [19]. It is the
  candidate-side mirror of the checklist error the College warns assessors
  against [2].
- **Answering the competency instead of the question.** Listed as a common
  mistake by a recruiting force: "didn't answer the question — answered the
  competency and not the question" [17]. Reciting everything you know about "We
  collaborate" is not an answer to the question that was actually asked.
- **The wrong level.** Covered above; it is on the same force's list of common
  mistakes [17]. Under-pitching (level-1 evidence for a supervisory role) and
  over-pitching (strategic language with no personal action underneath) both
  cost marks.
- **"We" instead of "I".** Force marking guidance treats first-person specificity
  as a marker of good evidence [18]; a panel that cannot tell what you
  personally did has nothing to classify under ORCE [9].
- **Hypothetical answers to past-behaviour questions.** The College directs that
  most of the interview focus on past behaviour with probing for evidence [2];
  "I would usually…" gives the assessor nothing to record.
- **Missing the *why*.** Values are probed by asking for thought processes,
  feelings and reasons [9]. An answer that reports actions but never reasoning
  can evidence a competency while failing a value.
- **Bringing a script.** Prohibited, and the penalty is an unappealable
  automatic fail [5]. This is a rule, not a style preference.
- **For interviewers: turning the indicators into a tick-list.** Explicitly
  warned against [1] [2], and it will systematically disadvantage candidates who
  demonstrate the competency in a way the bullet list did not anticipate — which
  the College says is normal and expected [2].
- **For interviewers: assuming which framework version your process uses.**
  Applicability varied by assessment and by date through 2024–25 [7], and some
  forces run their own framework entirely [2]. Confirm before designing or
  scoring.

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.**

1. **Framework re-download (10 minutes).** Download the 2024 framework PDF [1]
   and search it for the words "collaborative", "integrity", "impartiality" and
   "transparency" as value headings. Finding none is the point: it inoculates
   you against the stale material that dominates search results.
2. **Evidence grid (60 minutes).** Build a 9-row grid — six competencies, three
   values — and fill each row with two genuine experiences, one from work and
   one from elsewhere (the College explicitly allows both [5]). Any row you
   cannot fill twice is your preparation target.
3. **Clock drill (30 minutes).** Record yourself answering three questions from
   the bank under the real OAP constraints: 60 seconds thinking, then 300
   seconds speaking [5]. Then repeat under briefing-exercise constraints: 60
   seconds thinking, 180 seconds speaking [5]. Notice what you cut. It should be
   Situation, never Action.
4. **Level-lift drill (45 minutes).** Take one competency and read its level-1
   and level-2 indicator lists in the framework [1]. Write a level-1 answer to
   the matching question in the bank, then rewrite it at level 2 without
   changing the underlying story — only the scope of what you took
   responsibility for. Name exactly what changed.
5. **Self-score with the published scale (30 minutes).** Score a recorded answer
   A–D using the College's example rating descriptors [9], writing the evidence
   you would have recorded verbatim under ORCE [9] before you decide the grade.
   Doing observe–record–classify–evaluate in that order on your own answer is
   the fastest way to see how thin "I'm a good team player" looks as evidence.
6. **Probe rehearsal (paired, 20 minutes).** Have a partner ask one bank
   question and then only follow-up probes from the last group — "what did *you*
   do?", "why that rather than the alternative?", "how do you know?" — for five
   minutes. This mirrors the documented in-force panel behaviour of probing
   initial answers [8].
7. **SJT calibration (20 minutes).** Work the two published example scenarios in
   the national sift guidance [6], rating all four actions independently on the
   five-point scale, then articulate which CVF competency each action engages.
   The habit of asking "what is the main priority, and who is affected?" is the
   preparation the College itself recommends [6].
8. **For interviewers — question audit (45 minutes).** Take your force's current
   interview question set and check each question against three tests: does it
   target one named competency or value at a defined level [9]; does it ask for
   past behaviour rather than a hypothetical [2]; and does the accompanying
   assessor guidance describe evidence rather than a model answer [9]?

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

- **Reasonable adjustments are built into the framework itself.** The CVF
  "should be used in ways that allow for differing abilities. This includes
  those that stem from disabilities or neurodiversity", and "reasonable
  adjustments should always be made for people who have disabilities that impact
  on their ability to demonstrate the competencies. This is in line with
  equality legislation" [1]. The guidance repeats this with the statutory
  reference: adjustments "should be provided for those who need them under the
  Equality Act (2010)", and candidates with a disability under the Act "can ask
  for reasonable adjustments to the assessment", with a diagnostic report or
  professional workplace assessment helping the assessment team [2]. The
  underlying duty is section 20 of the Equality Act 2010 [11].
- **Accommodations extend beyond disability.** The College states that
  accommodations can be made for "pregnancy or breastfeeding", and for an injury
  or illness making the assessment more difficult [2]; the OAP guidance lists
  "pregnancy, injury or disability, including neurodiverse conditions" [5], and
  the special constable guidance adds dyslexia and autism as examples [8].
  Adjustments are based on supporting information from an appropriately
  qualified professional and are arranged through the recruiting force [8].
  Pregnancy and maternity, disability, race, sex, religion or belief and the
  other characteristics listed at section 4 of the Equality Act 2010 are the
  protected characteristics engaged here [13].
- **Assessment-timing adjustments change the published timings.** The College
  notes that documented exercise timings "may vary if you have a timings-related
  reasonable adjustment", and that an accessibility toolbar is available to all
  candidates regardless of whether an adjustment was granted [5].
- **Adjustments must not change the standard.** The NPPF examination handbook
  states the requirement that an accommodation "does not change the standard
  that the candidate" is assessed against [10] — the standard-preserving
  principle that distinguishes a lawful adjustment from an advantage.
- **Health and disability enquiries before an offer are restricted.** Section 60
  of the Equality Act 2010 limits questions about health or disability before a
  job offer, subject to exceptions including establishing whether adjustments to
  the assessment are needed [12]. Routing adjustment requests through the
  recruiting force rather than the interview panel, as the College's process
  does [8], keeps that boundary intact.
- **Adverse impact must be monitored.** The College's recruitment guidance
  states that selection processes must comply with the Equality Act 2010 and not
  unfairly discriminate against groups defined by protected characteristics, and
  describes the four-fifths rule — a method is said to cause adverse impact where
  the minority-group pass rate is under 80% of the majority-group rate — while
  noting explicitly that the rule "is a useful guide for evaluation and is
  commonly used in practice" but "does not have a legal footing" [9]. It also
  advises removing identifying information before assessing competency or
  value-based question responses, and warns that ability tests "have a tendency
  to cause adverse impact" [9].
- **Structure as a fairness mechanism.** Standardisation is presented by the
  College as the fairness rationale for the process design — the OAP interview
  "will follow a structured format with a set list of questions. These are
  designed to make the process as fair and objective as possible" [5] — and
  professional bodies make the same argument for structured, criteria-based
  interviewing generally [14] [15].
- **Candidate data.** Assessment records, scores and feedback reports are
  personal data; the College's own examination handbook carries a data-protection
  section governing candidate information [10]. Interviewers should treat notes
  taken under ORCE as disclosable records and, per the College, keep sensitive
  personal disclosures out of scoring sheets and feedback reports [9].

# Risks & Caveats

- **The 2018 recruitment-and-selection guidance predates the 2024 framework.**
  The College's *Values-based recruitment and selection* guidance [9] is dated
  2018 and its worked examples quote 2016 competency wording and the four 2016
  values. Its **process** advice (levels, rating scales, ORCE, assessor training,
  bias, adverse impact) is framework-agnostic and is what this document draws on;
  its **content** examples should not be used as current CVF text. Treat any
  successor edition as superseding it.
- **Two live sources of stale labels.** Older College candidate guides published
  before the March 2025 sift update list 2016 competency and value names for
  sift exercises; the current national sift guidance (May 2026) lists the 2024
  set [6] [7]. If you find a College PDF naming "integrity", "transparency" or
  "impartiality" as assessed values, check its date.
- **Force-level variation is real and undocumented here.** Forces may use the
  CVF entire, a mapped local framework, or their own [2]. In-force sifts and
  in-force interviews are designed by forces [5] [8], so panel size, question
  count and feedback commitments vary — the two-panel, six-question format cited
  above is one force's published practice [17], not a national standard.
- **Process mechanics change without notice.** Platform names, timings, resit
  windows and appeal deadlines are taken from the September 2024 OAP guidance
  and May 2026 sift guidance [5] [6]; verify against the guidance issued with
  your own invitation.
- **Question-bank provenance is uneven.** The *(sourced)* questions come from
  commercial preparation sites [20] [21] and a graduate careers site [22]; the
  College publishes no official CVF interview question list. One of those
  sources is undated [20]. They are cited as evidence that a question is
  *published*, never as evidence of a fact about the College's process, and no
  claim in the evidence layer rests on them.
- **Composed questions are illustrative.** They are written in the style of the
  cited competency descriptions and indicators and have no official status. Do
  not present them to candidates as real past questions from the College or any
  force.
- **Scope of jurisdiction.** The College of Policing is the professional body
  for the police service in England and Wales [1]. Other services may adopt the
  CVF — the Police Service of Northern Ireland publishes a CVF overview — but
  their processes are outside this document.
- **This document deepens, it does not replace, the framework.** The framework
  PDF is 27 pages, free and definitive; read it [1].

  step one eligibility and the step two legal examination in detail [10], and
  states that the CVF underpins national assessments for promotion [2], but the
  step three selection process is force-run and its CVF question sets and scoring
  are not published nationally. *(Would resolve: a force-published step three
  promotion board pack or the College's promotion process guidance.)*
- **Is a revised values-based recruitment and selection guidance planned?** The
  2018 edition [9] is the only detailed public assessment-design guidance and its
  examples are pre-2024. *(Would resolve: a successor publication or a College
  statement.)*
- **What proportion of forces had completed the transition by the 1 May 2025
  deadline [1], and how many run a non-CVF force framework [2]?** No national
  count was found. This matters directly to candidates deciding which framework
  to prepare against.
- **Are the OAP pass thresholds or score bands published anywhere?** Candidates
  receive an overall score per exercise and comparative feedback [5], but the
  standard required is not stated publicly. The A–D scale cited here is the 2018
  guidance's *example* scale [9], not confirmed as the OAP's live scale.
- **Do published adverse-impact statistics exist for the national sift and OAP?**
  The College sets out the four-fifths method [9]; whether it publishes the
  resulting monitoring data for its national exercises was not established.
