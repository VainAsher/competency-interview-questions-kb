# Executive Summary

Most candidates experience the follow-up question as an interruption — a sign
that something has gone wrong. The documentary record says the opposite. In the
NHS values-based interview, the probing questions *are* the instrument: Health
Education England's guidance on structured interviews for Values Based
Recruitment states that a values-based interview may look like any other
interview in format, but "it is the probing questions, designed as part of a
VBI, that are utilised to elicit detailed evidence in relation to learning and
reflection that differentiate it" [1]. Probes are not improvised. Published
guidance instructs organisations to build question banks in which each entry is
"a lead question followed by a series of possible relevant probe questions, as
well as a set of defined behavioural indicators" [1], and university
interviewer-training materials teach the same lead-plus-probe shape [17].

The reason probes exist is structural. Trained assessors are told to follow
ORCE — observe, record, classify, evaluate — recording behavioural evidence "as
close to verbatim as possible", with "no assuming or interpreting", and only
then classifying it against competencies and values [4]. An assessor working
that way cannot score what a candidate did not say. A vague answer therefore
produces a probe, because the alternative — inferring the missing detail — is
exactly what the model forbids. The College of Policing makes the values case
explicit: assessors "may need to probe behaviours to identify the underlying
values — asking candidates to explain their thought processes and feelings and
why they took a particular course of action", and notes this is "clearly easier
to do in an interview or other exercise where the assessors have a chance to
interact with the candidate and ask questions" [4].

That last clause defines the boundary of this document. Where interaction is
impossible, probing is impossible. The University of Oxford Careers Service
states plainly that in pre-recorded video interviews "there is no opportunity
for the interviewer to ask for clarification" [19], and the College of
Policing's own online assessment substitutes on-screen written prompts
alongside each question in place of a live assessor's follow-ups [5]. EPSO's
current generalist competition goes further still and contains no oral test at
all [9]. Confidence is **High**: every evidence-layer claim here is quoted
from an official framework document, a professional body, or a named,
dated practitioner publication, and the probe families are labelled analysis.

# Key Takeaways

- **Probing is the defining feature of a values-based interview**, not an
  optional extra: HEE's guidance says the probes are what differentiate a VBI
  from any other structured interview, and that they focus on "how and why an
  applicant makes particular choices in the workplace" [1]. *(documented)*
- **Probes are pre-agreed, not improvised.** Good-practice structured
  interviews use standardised questions "accompanied by a range of pre-agreed
  probing questions which can be used to elicit further information" [1], and
  interview question banks are built as lead question plus probe set plus
  behavioural indicators [1]. *(documented)*
- **A probe usually signals missing evidence, not disbelief.** ORCE requires
  assessors to record behaviour verbatim and classify only what was actually
  said, with "no assuming or interpreting" [4] — so an unevidenced claim must
  be probed or it cannot be scored. *(documented)*
- **Published probe sets are short and repetitive.** Across four independent
  UK public-sector question banks the same handful recurs: what was the
  outcome, what did you learn, what was your role, what would you do
  differently, how did you feel [1] [6] [7] [18]. *(documented)*
- **Consistency is a fairness requirement.** ACAS tells employers to "ask each
  applicant the same questions where possible… to ensure you're treating
  applicants in the same way" [14], and CIPD defines the structured interview
  as a predefined question set asked "in the same order to all candidates"
  with responses scored against objective criteria [16] — which is why probes
  belong in the design, not the moment. *(documented)*
- **Asynchronous formats remove probing entirely.** Pre-recorded video offers
  "no opportunity for the interviewer to ask for clarification" [19]; the
  police online interview compensates with on-screen prompts presented with
  each question [5]; EPSO's current AD5 competition has no oral test [9].
  *(documented)*
- **Six probe families cover almost everything you will be asked**:
  ownership, rationale, outcome, reflection, feeling and challenge — plus
  silence, which is a probe without words. *(guidance)*
- **The recovery move for a probe you cannot answer is candour plus the
  nearest true detail**, never invention: assessors classify only behavioural
  evidence, so a fabricated specific is both unscoreable and fragile under the
  next probe [4]. *(guidance)*

# Purpose

This document answers, for candidates: *"Why is the interviewer digging, what
are they actually missing, and how do I answer a probe without contradicting
what I just said?"* And for interviewers and HR: *"How do I design probe sets
that gather classifiable evidence while staying consistent enough across
candidates to be defensible?"*

Its parent, **best-practice-foundation**, established that competency
interviews are structured, criteria-scored events and noted in passing that
scripted answers "collapse under follow-up probes". This document takes that
one sentence and gives it the documentary treatment it deserves: what published
guidance actually says probes are for, what published probe sets look like, and
what happens to the whole method in formats where probing cannot occur.

# Definitions

- **Lead question** — the opening, standardised question for a criterion, asked
  identically of every candidate. HEE's guidance uses the term for the top line
  of each question-bank entry [1].
- **Probe question** (or *probing question*) — a pre-agreed follow-up used "to
  elicit further information from applicants" [1] against the same criterion as
  the lead question.
- **Probe ladder** — this knowledge base's term for a lead question plus its
  ordered set of probes, escalating from clarification to reflection. The
  structure is documented [1] [17]; the phrase "probe ladder" is ours.
- **ORCE** — observe, record, classify, evaluate: the assessment sequence the
  College of Policing directs trained assessors to follow [4].
- **FORCE** — familiarise, observe, record, classify, evaluate: HEE's
  interviewer-side variant, adding a preparation step [1].
- **Behavioural indicator** — the positive or negative behaviour statement an
  assessor classifies recorded evidence against; published question-bank
  entries pair every lead-and-probe set with one [1].
- **Question funnel** — the documented questioning sequence of open question,
  then narrowing, then probes for specific detail, then closed questions to
  confirm understanding [17].

# Framework Context

**Evidence layer — cited.** Probing appears in the UK and EU frameworks in four
distinct roles, and the difference matters because it changes what a probe is
allowed to do.

| Framework / body | Role probing plays | Source |
|---|---|---|
| NHS Values Based Recruitment | The **defining feature** of a values-based interview; probes carry the assessment, not the lead question | HEE structured interviews guidance [1]; VBR programme page [2] |
| College of Policing | A **corrective mechanism** within ORCE — probing surfaces the underlying values behind observed behaviour so they can be classified | Values-based recruitment and selection guidance [4] |
| ACAS / CIPD | A **fairness constraint** — consistency of questioning across candidates is the point of structure | ACAS interviewing guidance [14]; CIPD selection methods [16] |
| EPSO (current model) | **Absent** — the current generalist competition has no oral test, so no probing occurs at EPSO stage | EPSO Graduates AD5 test list [9] |
| EU agencies (e.g. EMA) | **Retained** — agency selection committees interview against vacancy-notice criteria and expect substantiation | EMA application process [10] |

NHS Values Based Recruitment "attracts and recruits students, trainees and
employees on the basis that their individual values and behaviours align with
the values of the NHS Constitution" [2]. Within it, HEE distinguishes the VBI
from other structured interviews solely on the probing: a VBI "may appear
similar to other types of interviews in that the format will involve asking
applicants to provide examples of behaviour they have previously demonstrated
or to explain how they would respond to a particular situation. However, it is
the probing questions, designed as part of a VBI, that are utilised to elicit
detailed evidence in relation to learning and reflection that differentiate
it" [1]. The same guidance states that "it is these probing questions that
provide insight into an applicant's values or what they consider to be
important", and that the probes following a lead question "focus on how and why
an applicant makes particular choices in the workplace, providing insight into
the reasons and drivers for their behaviour" [1]. Oxford University Hospitals
describes the candidate-facing consequence in the same terms: a VBI explores
"how and why people do things in any given situation", with a panel asking
"between two and four questions depending on the job role" [8] — a small number
of leads, which implies depth is reached by probing rather than by breadth.

**Currency caution.** The HEE guidance is the anchor source for this document
and its publisher no longer exists: Health Education England merged into NHS
England, which "has assumed all activities previously undertaken by HEE" [3].
No successor structured-interview guidance has been reissued under NHS England
branding, and VBR remains operative in practice [2]. Treat the guidance as
still operative but unreissued (see Risks & Caveats).

In policing, the framing is procedural rather than definitional. Because police
values "are behaviourally defined, they can be assessed in the same way as
competencies, ie assessors rate the behavioural evidence they observe" [4] —
and it is precisely because values sit below observable behaviour that probing
becomes necessary: assessors "may need to probe behaviours to identify the
underlying values — asking candidates to explain their thought processes and
feelings and why they took a particular course of action" [4].

# Core Reference

**Evidence layer — cited.**

## Why probing exists: ORCE forbids inference

The College of Policing directs that "trained assessors should follow a
structured process such as ORCE (observe, record, classify, evaluate), which
breaks down the assessing process into component parts and helps to avoid bias"
[4]. Each stage carries explicit instructions [4]:

- **Observe** — "watching, listening or reading a candidate's assessment",
  carried out "independent of any other assessor(s)".
- **Record** — "take detailed notes of behavioural evidence (as close to
  verbatim as possible)", with "**no assuming or interpreting**" and "record
  just behavioural evidence".
- **Classify** — "after the candidate has left the room", "only classify
  behavioural evidence", "only classify behaviour from that assessment", and
  "classify each behaviour once — do not double count".
- **Evaluate** — "based on quality and quantity of the classified evidence",
  considering "what the candidate missed or if anything detracted from their
  performance".

The consequence is mechanical rather than attitudinal. An assessor who may not
assume or interpret, and who scores on the quantity as well as the quality of
classified evidence, has only one legitimate route to the missing detail:
asking for it. This is why the guidance lists ORCE as the remedy against a long
table of assessment barriers — expectancy effect, confirmatory information,
personal liking, primacy effect and stereotyping are each answered with "using
ORCE model of assessment" [4]. A probe is the instrument that keeps evidence
gathering separate from judgement.

HEE's interviewer-side sequence, FORCE, is the same model with a preparation
step in front, and its wording on observation is equally restrictive: "the key
skill in observing is not to make judgements, but to be aware of what the
applicant is saying or doing", followed by "make written notes of the
applicant's responses and behaviour during the interview", then classification
in which "some comments will include evidence — these should be identified.
Some comments may not be directly relevant to the target criteria" [1].

## The published lead-question-plus-probe structure

HEE instructs that "interview templates or questioning frameworks should
contain a set of standardised lead questions and associated probe questions for
each of the criteria assessed as part of the interview for a particular role",
aligned to the person specification [1]. Where an organisation builds a
question bank, "an interview question bank should contain questions structured
as follows; a lead question followed by a series of possible relevant probe
questions, as well as a set of defined behavioural indicators" [1]. For
values-based questions specifically, "the questions should be designed to elicit
evidence in relation to an applicant's learning and reflection in a given
situation and focus on how or why they made particular choices" [1].

Two worked templates are published in full. The Collaboration template, mapped
to the NHS Constitution value "Working together for patients", pairs the lead
question "Describe a situation where it was important for you to
establish/develop an effective working relationship with someone…" with four
probes — "Why was this relationship significant?", "What did you learn from
this experience?", "What was the outcome?", "How did you react/feel about
developing this relationship?" — and with positive indicators including
"considers the needs/perspectives of others" and negative indicators including
"is dismissive of the views/perspectives of others" [1]. The Integrity
template, mapped to "Commitment to quality of care", pairs "Tell me about a
time when you had to address a difficult situation with a colleague…" with
"Why was it important to address this situation?", "What was the outcome?",
"How did you feel about addressing the situation and the outcome?" and "How did
you perceive your colleague's reaction?", against positive indicators including
"is proactive in identifying and addressing issues" and negative indicators
including "defers responsibility to others" and "seeks to blame others" [1].

Note the shape both templates share: **one behavioural lead, then probes that
are almost entirely about reasoning, outcome and feeling** — not about further
facts of the situation. The evidence the indicators require is not in the story;
it is in the account of why the story went the way it did.

University interviewer training teaches the same architecture. The University
of Nottingham's training deck for interview chairs and panel members sets out a
five-step STAR-with-Reflection sequence in which the interviewer establishes
context without elaborating, then, at the Action step, "most of the time should
be spent exploring actions and seeking to understand how the candidate has
carried out a task", adding that "probing is essential, remember you need to
know what they did and more importantly how they went about it" [17]. Its
published probe sets are grouped by STAR element: for Task, "What was your role
in the situation?" and "Specifically what were you asked to do?"; for Action,
"What did you actually do to be successful?", "What responsibility did you
take?", "Who did you engage / consult with?" and "What prompted you to take
that action?"; for Result, "What did you achieve?" and "How did you know that
your approach was successful?"; and for Reflection, "Would you approach it
different next time?" [17]. The deck also documents an explicit **question
funnel**: "start with an open question", "narrow down to a specific area", "use
probe questions to find out specific details (how, what, who, when, where,
why)", "use closed questions to clarify your understanding", then "move on to a
new area" [17]. It records why structure is worth the effort: it "requires
deeper exploration of candidate answers", "elicits in depth evidence making it
less likely that a candidate is able to fake it", and "provides the evidence to
justify a decision and/or give feedback to a candidate" [17].

## The recurring published probe set

Independent UK public-sector question banks converge on a very short list.
Gloucestershire Hospitals NHS Foundation Trust's recruiting-manager toolkit
tells interviewers that "after the candidate has answered you may also want to
ask probing questions", and publishes them as: "What did you learn?", "What
would you do different next time?", "How did it make you feel?", "Who was
involved?", "What was the outcome?" [6]. In its equality and diversity
interview pack the same toolkit lists a slightly different set — "Can you tell
me a bit more about that?", "Can you provide a specific example?", "What was
the outcome of this?", "What did you learn from this?", "How were you involved
in this?/ what was your role in this?" — introduced with a fairness rationale:
"please remember that you may need to ask some candidates 'probing' questions
to evaluate their performance fairly" [6]. That sentence is worth dwelling on:
the toolkit frames probing as something owed to candidates who have
under-evidenced, not as a penalty imposed on them.

The same toolkit sets out the questioning technique probes sit inside: open
questions "with the intent of finding out things you do not know already",
closed questions "to check facts you are uncertain about", avoidance of leading
questions and of multiple questions bundled into one, and a target in which
"ideally the candidate should spend about 70-80% of the interview talking" [6].
Interviewers are told to "use supplementary questions to elicit further
information and to probe for more in-depth answers" [6].

Wirral University Teaching Hospital's published values question bank embeds
probes directly into its entries rather than listing them separately — for
example "Please give an example of a situation where you've spoken up because
you had concerns. What was the outcome? How did this make you feel?", and a
paired construction in which question 9 probes question 8: "Considering the
outcome of the above, if it was successful, why do you think it was successful?
If it wasn't successful, why do you think this was the case and what would you
do differently?" [7]. The University of Oxford Careers Service's published
example-question document uses the same layout, printing a line of probes under
each competency's lead questions — "How did you make a contribution? How do you
help build good team spirit? What makes a successful team?" under
Teamwork/Collaboration, and "What did you do to resolve things? What did you
learn from the experience? What would you do differently in future?" under
Initiative and problem-solving [18].

## What happens where probing is impossible

Probing depends on interaction, and the College of Policing says so directly:
probing to identify underlying values "is clearly easier to do in an interview
or other exercise where the assessors have a chance to interact with the
candidate and ask questions" [4]. Three documented cases show what the method
does when that interaction is removed.

**Pre-recorded video.** The University of Oxford Careers Service states that in
pre-recorded interviews candidates are "asked a set no. of questions and being
given a specific amount of time to respond to each question", and that "there
is no opportunity for the interviewer to ask for clarification" [19]. The
answer recorded is the entire evidence base for that criterion.

**Prompt substitution.** The College of Policing's online assessment process
replaces the live probe with a written one. Candidates face five questions;
"each question will provide some prompts for you to consider when answering",
which "will be presented as text on screen" alongside a pre-recorded video of
an assessor reading the question, with 60 seconds of unrecorded thinking time
and 300 seconds to record an answer [5]. The published STAR guidance embedded
in the same document reads as a probe set in disguise — under Action: "What did
you do? How did you do it? Why did you do it that way? What skills did you
use?"; under Result: "What was the outcome? What did you learn?" [5]. The
guidance also tells candidates "we can only make an assessment based on what
you tell us in relation to the relevant competencies and values" [5], which is
the ORCE constraint restated for a format with no follow-up.

**Removal of the oral stage altogether.** EPSO's current competition for
Graduates (Administrators AD5) lists reasoning tests, an EU Knowledge test, a
digital skills test and a written Free-Text Essay on EU Matters — and no
interview or oral test [9]. Probing therefore plays no part in the current
EPSO generalist selection. It survives at EU agency level: the European
Medicines Agency runs in-person interviews conducted by a selection committee
of three to five people, assessing candidates "against all of the criteria
listed in the vacancy notice", typically over 30 to 60 minutes, and recommends
the STAR technique for structuring substantiated answers [10].

**Telephone and online interviews** occupy a middle position: probing is
retained, but HEE cautions that "if this form of interview is being used for
some applicants and not others it is particularly important that there is
consistency between the two approaches to ensure the process is fair for all
applicants", giving the worked example that a two-interviewer face-to-face
process should be matched by a two-interviewer remote one [1].

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact. It points back to the
evidence above and adds no new facts.**

## Read the probe as a request for a specific missing thing

Every published probe maps to a slot the assessor could not fill from the
answer already given. Diagnosing which slot is the whole skill.

| Probe family | Typical wording | What the assessor is missing |
|---|---|---|
| **Ownership** | "How were you involved in this — what was your role?" [6]; "What responsibility did you take?" [17] | Your individual behaviour, separable from the team's |
| **Rationale** | "What prompted you to take that action?" [17]; "Why was it important to address this situation?" [1] | The reasoning that reveals the underlying value [4] |
| **Outcome** | "What was the outcome?" [1] [6] [7] | Result evidence to weigh the action against |
| **Reflection** | "What did you learn?" [6]; "What would you do differently in future?" [18] | Learning and reflection — the VBI's target evidence [1] |
| **Feeling** | "How did it make you feel?" [6]; "How did you react/feel?" [1] | Motivation and drivers behind the behaviour [1] |
| **Challenge** | Counterfactual and pushback probes | Whether your account survives contact with an alternative reading |

Two practical consequences. First, a probe about your *feelings* is not small
talk — HEE's indicator sets score exactly this territory, and the College of
Policing warns assessors that values questions "may be emotive" and that
values-based interviews "can quickly get into territory that is personal to the
individual" [4]. Answer it as evidence, not as chat. Second, an outcome probe
following an outcome you already stated usually means your outcome was
unquantified; give the number, the sign-off, or the named consequence.

## Answer a probe without contradicting yourself

- **Add, do not revise.** The assessor's notes are contemporaneous and as
  verbatim as possible [4]. A probe answer that quietly changes the facts of
  the answer above it produces two recorded versions and forces the panel to
  discount both. Extend the account; do not re-cut it.
- **Stay inside the same example** unless invited out of it. The funnel model
  keeps one area open until the interviewer moves on [17]; jumping to a
  stronger, different example reads as evading the probe and abandons the
  evidence already banked.
- **Answer the actual question first, in one sentence, then evidence it.**
  Probes are short and specific by design [1] [6]; a two-minute re-run of the
  original story answers nothing.
- **Do not treat a challenge probe as an accusation.** Concede the accurate
  part, hold the part you can evidence, and say which is which. Assessors are
  scoring "quality and quantity of the classified evidence" [4], and a
  defensible partial concession is evidence; a defensive re-assertion is not.
- **Use the silence.** An interviewer who stops writing and waits is running
  the cheapest probe there is. Silence after an incomplete answer is an
  invitation to add the missing slot from the table above, not a cue to fill
  air with a new anecdote. *(This inference about silence is ours; the
  documented material is the note-taking and probe design in [4] and [17].)*

## When you genuinely lack the detail

Say so, then give the nearest true thing. Assessors may classify only
behavioural evidence, with "no assuming or interpreting" [4] — which means an
invented specific is not merely risky, it is worthless as soon as the next
probe tests it. A workable three-part move:

1. **Name the gap honestly.** "I don't have the exact figure to hand."
2. **Give the nearest verifiable substitute.** The order of magnitude, the
   sign-off you got, the person who confirmed it, the observable change.
3. **Offer the evidence you do hold.** "What I can tell you precisely is what I
   changed in the process, and why."

The same move works for a probe about a part of the story you were not present
for: state the boundary of your knowledge, then return to your own actions.
This keeps every recorded sentence classifiable.

## Recovering when a probe exposes a weak example

If two probes in it becomes clear the example is thin — your role was
peripheral, or the outcome never landed — the recovery is not to inflate it.

- **Finish the ladder truthfully**, using the reflection probe as the exit. The
  published banks reward learning explicitly: "if it wasn't successful, why do
  you think this was the case and what would you do differently?" [7]. An
  honest weak example with strong reflection still generates classifiable
  evidence against reflection-focused indicators [1].
- **Then ask to add.** "I've got a stronger example of the same behaviour if
  that would be useful" is a legitimate offer; substituting an example
  mid-probe without saying so is not.
- **Do not argue the score.** The evidence deficit is factual, not a
  disagreement. The College of Policing frames feedback in exactly this way:
  decisions rest on what was observed, so "it is not to say they don't hold the
  values, but that they have not evidenced them" [4].

## Preparing for probes rather than for questions

Because probe sets are short, repetitive and published, they are the most
predictable part of an interview. Prepare each evidence-bank example by
pre-answering the recurring six — role, reasoning, obstacles, outcome, feeling,
learning [1] [6] [17] [18] — rather than by scripting the lead answer. This is
also the reason careers guidance warns against memorised scripts: the National
Careers Service's own definition of the "R" in STAR already includes "what you
learned from the experience" [20], and a script that stops at the result has
nothing left when the reflection probe arrives.

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — cited.** What interviewers are instructed to do when
designing and delivering probes.

## Design the probes before the interview, not during it

Good-practice structured interviews use standardised questions that "should be
accompanied by a range of pre-agreed probing questions which can be used to
elicit further information from applicants" [1]. HEE puts probe design in the
interview-preparation checklist as explicit questions for the designer: "How
many questions (lead and probes) are required to effectively assess the
applicant against the criteria within the time allocated for the interview?",
"What probe questions are required to support the lead questions?" and "How do
existing interview questions need adapting to meet the level and complexity of
the role?" [1]. Every question in the bank must carry its behavioural
indicators: "ensure that the positive and negative behavioural indicators or
key criteria have been identified for each of the questions incorporated in the
interview as part of the interview scoring framework. This will be used to
assist with objective evaluation of an applicant's performance" [1]. HEE also
recommends consulting stakeholders "including staff and service users" when
developing and refining interview questions [1].

## Keep probing consistent across candidates

This is the fairness constraint that distinguishes a designed probe from an
improvised one. ACAS advises employers that "when interviewing it's a good idea
to ask each applicant the same questions where possible. This is to ensure
you're treating applicants in the same way", and that decisions should be based
"on how each applicant met each point in the job description and person
specification" [14]. The CIPD defines the structured interview as one that
involves "asking a predefined set of questions, in the same order, to all
candidates", and directs employers to "ask all candidates the same questions in
the same order and score responses according to the objective criteria" [16].
HEE's rationale is the same: standardised documentation "enables the consistent
delivery of the interview across different interviewers" and predetermined
scoring criteria and questions "enhance reliability and fairness across
applicants and interviewers" [1].

The reconciling principle in the published guidance is that probes are drawn
from a pre-agreed pool available to every candidate, and are deployed where
evidence is missing — Gloucestershire's toolkit frames this as probing "to
evaluate their performance fairly" [6]. Probing one candidate hard on rationale
while accepting another's unexplained assertion is precisely the inconsistency
ACAS and CIPD guidance exists to prevent [14] [16].

## Deliver the probe cleanly

Published interviewer guidance is specific about technique [6] [17]:

- **Open, then narrow, then probe, then confirm.** The documented funnel is
  open question, narrow to a specific area, "use probe questions to find out
  specific details (how, what, who, when, where, why)", then "use closed
  questions to clarify your understanding", then move on [17].
- **One question at a time.** Multiple questions bundled together "are often
  confusing and a candidate might easily forget the second part", and "give the
  candidate a way of ignoring something that they don't want to discuss by
  talking at length to only one part of the question. It is better to ask each
  question individually" [6].
- **Never probe with a leading question.** Leading questions "inform or suggest
  to the candidate the answer which will most help their application"; ask
  "tell us about the figure work in your present job" rather than "there is a
  lot of figure work in this job, is this something you are good at?" [6].
- **Protect the talking ratio.** "Ideally the candidate should spend about
  70-80% of the interview talking" [6].
- **Do not elaborate during the scene-setting.** At the Situation step,
  "don't elaborate/interrupt if answer is too broad"; focus the candidate and
  "if necessary probe for missing information" [17]. Interrupting is reserved
  for when "it is clear that s/he is not answering the question asked" [6].
- **Record while you probe, and finalise notes immediately.** HEE directs that
  an observation/recording sheet be used and that time be allowed "immediately
  after the interview for interviewers to finalise their notes as leaving this
  until later can result in distorted information" [1].
- **Tell the candidate you are taking notes.** "Before the interview begins,
  advise the applicant whether you will be writing notes as they speak and
  inform them if audio recordings will be used"; consent to recording "should
  be obtained prior to commencement" [1].

## Probe values sensitively, and close the interview with care

Because values-based probing reaches motivations, the College of Policing tells
assessors to be "alert to signs that candidates are becoming upset or emotional
when describing events", to "treat this information sensitively and in
particular not reveal sensitive personal information in scoring sheets or
feedback reports" [4]. HEE flags the same risk from the candidate's side: "there
is a risk, particularly in VBIs where the questions will have explored the
applicant's motivations and drivers, that an applicant could feel demotivated
or that they have been 'exposed' in some way. Interviewers should be trained in
appropriately closing an interview in a supportive and reassuring manner" [1].

## Review whether the probes worked

HEE makes probe effectiveness an explicit evaluation criterion: reviewers should
consider "whether the interview questions and follow up probing questions
provided sufficient opportunity for applicants to demonstrate the intended
competencies or values", whether "there were any difficulties obtaining
information to allow effective assessment of any particular competencies or
values", and whether "there was enough time during the interview for
interviewers to ask all of the questions expected", noting that "some questions
may also need to be refreshed periodically" [1]. Differentiating between
candidates "should only be on the basis of demonstrated attributes", with
awareness of "differential performance associated with membership of a
particular ethnic group, gender or other protected characteristic" [1].

## Assessor training is part of the instrument

Interviewers "should be trained to deliver selection and assessment activities
according to best practice principles" and "specifically trained in the
techniques and approach for values based interviewing", with training "regularly
refreshed" [1]. Reliability and validity of structured interviews "is improved
through ensuring interviewers are trained appropriately" [1], and where
resources allow "it is good practice to use multiple interviewers as this helps
to minimise bias and enhance fairness" [1] — a point ACAS states as a
recommendation that "each interview should be carried out by more than 1 person
to reduce the risk of discriminating" [14]. In assessment-centre settings ORCE
"should be used with paired assessment or in groups with assessors first
carrying out each stage independently and then meeting to discuss their grades
and evidence and then agreeing on a final grade" [4].

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact.** Each pitfall names
the documented criterion it violates.

## Candidate-side

- **Hearing the probe as hostility and defending.** The published rationale for
  probing is evidence gathering and fair evaluation [1] [6], not challenge for
  its own sake. A defensive answer supplies no new classifiable evidence [4].
- **Changing the story under the probe.** Notes are contemporaneous and near
  verbatim [4]; a revised fact creates a contradiction the panel must discount.
- **Answering the ownership probe in the plural.** "How were you involved in
  this — what was your role in this?" [6] is asked precisely because "we" is
  unclassifiable to an individual [4].
- **Reciting more Situation when probed for Action.** Interviewer training
  directs that most interview time belong to the Action step [17]; extra
  scene-setting answers a question nobody asked.
- **Inventing a specific under pressure.** Fabricated detail cannot survive the
  next probe in the ladder, and the ladders are published — reflection and
  outcome probes routinely follow rationale probes [1] [7] [18].
- **Stopping at the result.** Both the reflection probes in the published sets
  [7] [17] [18] and the National Careers Service's own definition of "Result"
  include learning [20]; an answer with no reflection forfeits the evidence a
  VBI is specifically designed to collect [1].
- **Rehearsing lead answers instead of probe answers.** Because probe sets are
  short and repeat across employers [1] [6] [18], the predictable part of the
  interview is the part most candidates leave unprepared.
- **Treating an asynchronous video answer as an opening statement.** With "no
  opportunity for the interviewer to ask for clarification" [19], an answer
  that leaves the probe slots open simply never gets them filled.

## Interviewer-side

- **Improvising probes.** Guidance requires pre-agreed probe sets attached to
  each lead question with defined indicators [1]; probes invented in the moment
  are neither consistent nor scoreable against agreed criteria [14] [16].
- **Probing some candidates harder than others.** The direct opposite of ACAS's
  same-questions guidance [14] and CIPD's same-order structured definition
  [16], and the most common route by which bias re-enters a structured process
  [4].
- **Leading the witness.** A probe phrased as a suggestion supplies the answer
  and destroys the evidence [6].
- **Stacking probes into one utterance.** Bundled questions let candidates
  answer only the easy half [6].
- **Probing without recording.** ORCE separates recording from classification
  [4]; a probe whose answer is not written down produces evidence that exists
  only in memory, which is what the barriers table exists to prevent [4].
- **Classifying inference rather than evidence.** "No assuming or interpreting"
  [4] — if the detail was not probed for, it cannot be scored.
- **Leaving no time for the ladders.** HEE makes "was there enough time during
  the interview for interviewers to ask all of the questions expected" a review
  question for a reason [1]; a five-lead interview with no probing time assesses
  breadth and nothing else.
- **Recording sensitive personal disclosure in scoring sheets.** Explicitly
  warned against where values probing has reached personal territory [4].

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.** Drills a reader can run this week.

1. **Probe-proof one example (45 minutes).** Take a single evidence-bank entry
   and write a one-sentence answer to each of the six recurring probes — role,
   reasoning, obstacles, outcome, feeling, learning [1] [6] [17] [18]. Any slot
   you cannot fill in one honest sentence is the slot a real probe will find.
   Fix the example, not the wording.
2. **Run the published ladders cold.** Have a partner read Ladders 1, 2 and 5
   above verbatim, giving you no preparation and no pause between the lead and
   the probes. Time the whole ladder; published NHS VBIs use only two to four
   lead questions per interview [8], so a ladder that runs dry in ninety seconds
   is a warning.
3. **The contradiction audit.** Record a ladder run, transcribe it, and mark
   every factual assertion. Highlight any fact that changed between the lead
   answer and the probe answers. Assessors record near verbatim [4] — treat
   every highlight as a lost mark.
4. **The gap drill.** Have a partner invent probes you genuinely cannot answer
   ("what was the percentage improvement?", "what did your manager say
   afterwards?"). Practise the three-part move — name the gap, give the nearest
   verifiable substitute, redirect to evidence you hold — until it is automatic.
5. **The challenge drill.** Ask a partner to push back once per ladder with a
   plausible alternative reading of your actions. Practise conceding the
   accurate part explicitly before defending the rest.
6. **The self-contained rewrite.** Take one answer and rewrite it for a
   pre-recorded video where no probing is possible [19]: fold role, reasoning,
   outcome and learning into a single answer inside the time limit. Then compare
   it with the police online assessment's on-screen prompt set — "What did you
   do? How did you do it? Why did you do it that way? What skills did you use?…
   What was the outcome? What did you learn?" [5] — and check nothing is
   missing.
7. **Interviewer drill.** Take one live vacancy criterion. Write one lead
   question, four probes and a positive/negative indicator pair, following the
   published template shape [1]. Then have two colleagues score the same
   recorded answer independently and reconcile — the paired-assessment practice
   ORCE prescribes [4].
8. **The consistency audit (for panels).** After a round of interviews, list
   which probes each candidate actually received. Any probe given to some
   candidates and not others is a fairness exposure under the same-questions
   guidance [14] [16].

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — cited. Signpost only; the fairness-bias-legal document
treats these in full.**

- **Consistency as discrimination control.** ACAS advises that "each interview
  should be carried out by more than 1 person to reduce the risk of
  discriminating" and that applicants be asked the same questions where
  possible to ensure equal treatment [14]. Where probing is discretionary, it
  is the point at which unequal treatment most easily enters a nominally
  structured process; the CIPD's same-questions, same-order, objective-criteria
  definition of the structured interview is the countermeasure [16].
- **Probes must not stray into protected territory.** ACAS states that
  employers "must not ask a job applicant about their health or any disability
  at any stage in the application or interview process, unless an exception
  applies", and lists the nine protected characteristics that must not
  influence selection [15]. In statute, enquiries about disability and health
  before offer are restricted by s.60 of the Equality Act 2010 [11]. This bites
  hardest on probing: a probe asking *why* a candidate acted as they did can
  drift into health, caring responsibilities, religion or belief without the
  interviewer intending it. Probe the behaviour and the reasoning, not the
  personal circumstance.
- **Reasonable adjustments apply to the probing itself.** The duty to make
  reasonable adjustments is set out at s.20 of the Equality Act 2010 [12], and
  ACAS gives recruitment examples including alternative formats and accessible
  interview rooms [15]. Adjustments relevant to probing include giving
  questions in writing, allowing extra processing time before a follow-up, and
  avoiding rapid-fire probe stacking — which published guidance discourages for
  all candidates in any case [6].
- **Sensitive disclosure.** The College of Policing directs assessors not to
  reveal sensitive personal information in scoring sheets or feedback reports,
  and to treat emotionally charged values territory with care [4].
- **Probe answers are personal data.** Interview notes, recordings and scores
  are candidate personal data; the ICO's draft employment-practices guidance on
  recruitment and selection sets out employers' UK GDPR and Data Protection Act
  2018 obligations across shortlisting, testing, interviewing and record-keeping
  [13], and ACAS requires notes to be stored securely and confidentially, with
  recording only by prior permission [14]. HEE likewise requires that
  applicants be told notes are being taken and that consent be obtained before
  any audio recording [1].
- **Evidence-based differentiation only.** HEE states that differentiating
  between applicants "should only be on the basis of demonstrated attributes",
  with explicit attention to differential performance by protected
  characteristic [1]; the College of Policing directs that decisions be based
  only on information from "a well-designed, valid, objective and standardised
  assessment process" and that it "must be the same process for all candidates"
  [4].

# Risks & Caveats

- **The anchor source's publisher no longer exists.** The HEE structured
  interview guidance [1] is the single richest published source on probe design
  located for this document, but Health Education England merged into NHS
  England [3] and the guidance has not been reissued under NHS England
  branding. VBR remains operative in practice [2]. Treat it as still operative
  but unreissued, and re-verify at review.
- **Legacy-domain risk.** References [1], [2] and [3] resolve to `hee.nhs.uk`,
  a retirement candidate. All were live on 2026-07-20; capture Internet Archive
  snapshots before relying on them long-term.
- **The College of Policing VBR guidance predates the current CVF.** [4] was
  published in 2018 and predates the 2024 Competency and Values Framework. Only
  its framework-agnostic process content — ORCE, assessment barriers, decision
  making, feedback — is used here; no competency or value names are taken from
  it.
- **The Nottingham training deck is illustrative university practice.** [17]
  was last modified in 2019 and is a recruitment-training presentation, not
  statutory or professional-body guidance. It is cited for the lead-plus-probe
  structure and question funnel it teaches, corroborated independently by [1]
  and [6], not as an authority in its own right.
- **The Oxford example-question document is dated May 2016** [18]. Its probe
  lines remain live on the Careers Service site and are corroborated by the NHS
  banks, but the underlying Civil Service material it draws on predates current
  Success Profiles wording.
- **Trust question banks are local, not national.** [6], [7] and [8] are
  individual NHS trusts' published practice. They agree with each other and with
  HEE's national guidance [1], which is why they are used, but no single trust's
  wording is national policy.
- **The probe-family taxonomy is our analysis.** The six families in Model
  Answer Guidance are a labelled synthesis of the published probe sets, not a
  classification any cited source publishes.
- **The "silence probe" is inference.** No cited source documents deliberate
  silence as a probing technique; the guidance offered here is explicitly
  labelled as ours.
- **CIPD pages bot-block automated link checkers.** [16] is a real URL and its
  content was read for this document, but a human should eyeball it
  periodically.
- **The ICO recruitment guidance cited is a draft** published for consultation
  [13]; check for the final version at review.
- **EPSO's position may change.** [9] records the current AD5 test list with no
  oral component. EPSO has revised its competition model more than once; do not
  treat the absence of interviews as permanent.

- Do any published UK frameworks specify a **maximum** number of probes per lead

- Is there published UK guidance on whether an interviewer may lawfully probe
