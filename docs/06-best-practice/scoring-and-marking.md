# Executive Summary

This document assembles, in one place, the published machinery by which
competency answers are turned into numbers — and by which those numbers are
turned into a hiring decision. It is the deep-dive child of
`best-practice-foundation`, which sketched the Civil Service 1–7 scale in
outline; here the scales are set out in full, with their sources, their
benchmarks, their reconciliation rules and their limits.

Four published systems are covered. The **UK Civil Service** scores experience,
technical skills and behaviours on a **7-point scale** and strengths on a
**4-point scale** — a *central* Cabinet Office instruction, stated verbatim in
three independent grade-tier packs [1] [2] [3] — with a **3-point A/B/C** scale
available at sift for high-volume campaigns [1] [2] [3], and a rule that panel
members score **independently** before reconciling by **arithmetic average** [1].
**Policing** uses an **A–D** rating scale defined by the balance of positive
evidence against areas for development, applied through the **ORCE** sequence,
and the College of Policing states plainly that **arithmetic decision making is
the most valid method** while discussion-based ("rational") decision making is
"less effective" and "difficult to justify from a fairness point of view" [8].
**EPSO** marks against published **anchors**, with per-competency marks and
combined pass thresholds set in each competition notice — and its **current**
model marks no general competencies at interview at all, because it has no
interview [12]. **NHS** values-based interviews score against paired
positive/negative **behavioural indicators** using the **FORCE** sequence [15].

The shared logic underneath all four is **evidence balance**: the assessor
weighs positive evidence against negative evidence and against what was
missing, rather than forming an impression. Every published descriptor examined
for this document — the COPFS 1–7 wording [4], the policing A–D wording [8], the
five-point scheme reproduced by Oxford [23] — is phrased in exactly those terms.

Confidence is **High**: the core claims rest on primary Cabinet Office, College
of Policing, EPSO/EUR-Lex, NHS and departmental publications, each read in full
rather than summarised second-hand. The honest gaps are named in Risks &
Caveats: **no published descriptors exist for the Civil Service 4-point
strengths scale**, the central Cabinet Office packs date from **2020** with no
located refresh, COPFS remains the only located publisher of the seven
descriptor labels, and benchmarks vary campaign by campaign.

# Key Takeaways

- The 7-point/4-point split is **central, not departmental**: "Experience,
  technical skills and behaviours should be scored using the 7 point scale.
  Strengths should be scored using the 4 point scale" appears verbatim in the
  Cabinet Office EO–Grade 6, HR Deputy Director and HR Director packs [1] [2]
  [3]. *(documented)*
- The seven descriptors run **1 Not Demonstrated → 7 Outstanding
  Demonstration**, and each is defined by an explicit balance of positive
  against negative evidence — 4 is "Adequate positive evidence and any negative
  evidence would not cause concern" [4]. *(documented)*
- A **benchmark** is campaign-specific. The common standard is **4 or more in
  all behaviours**, with a single 3 tolerable where other scores are high;
  boards may set higher bars, such as a 5 on lead behaviours or a total of 20
  across four behaviours, where there is a job and business need [4].
  *(documented)*
- At sift, single-role and smaller campaigns score each element on the numeric
  scale; **high-volume campaigns may use a lead criterion or the 3-point A/B/C
  scale**, and the progression benchmark "can be flexed" against application
  quality and the number of roles [1]. *(documented)*
- **Panels score independently, then average.** Civil Service guidance:
  observe, record, classify, evaluate and score each question independently,
  then agree a merit list "using the arithmetic method"; variances may be
  discussed against the underlying evidence, but scores are "reconciled by
  taking an arithmetic average" [1]. *(documented)*
- Policing supplies the *evidence for why*: arithmetic decision making "has
  been shown to be the most valid decision-making method", while discussion-led
  "rational" methods are "less effective in selecting the best candidates and
  are difficult to justify from a fairness point of view" [8]. *(documented)*
- **EPSO's marking generations differ and the differences matter.**
  EPSO/AD/389/21 set pass marks of "3/10 per competency and 40/80 in total"
  [10]; EPSO/AD/399/22 kept 40/80 but stated "There is no pass mark per
  competency" [11]; EPSO/AD/427/26 — the current model — has **no interview and
  no general-competency marking at all**, retaining anchor-based marking only
  in the written essay, scored 0 to 10 with a 5/10 pass [12]. *(documented)*
- The single most useful calibration fact for a candidate: on every published
  scale, moving up a band is achieved by **adding positive evidence and
  removing negative evidence and gaps**, not by adding fluency. *(guidance)*

# Purpose

This document answers one question from two sides.

For a **candidate**: *"My answer scored a 4. What specifically would have made
it a 6?"* Published descriptors make that answerable rather than mystical — the
gap between bands is defined in the source documents in terms of quantity and
quality of evidence, negative evidence, and things the answer missed.

For an **interviewer, panel chair or HR designer**: *"What is the defensible
way to turn five answers into a score, and five scorers into a decision?"* The
published instruments and the published reconciliation rules are set out here
with their citations, so a process can be built on the record rather than on
custom.

# Scope

**Covered:** the published scoring instruments and marking processes of the UK
Civil Service (Success Profiles), UK policing (Competency and Values
Framework), EPSO/EU institutions, and NHS values-based recruitment; scale
descriptors where they are published; benchmarks and pass thresholds; sift-stage
versus interview-stage scoring; the sequence assessors are told to follow
(ORCE/FORCE); independent scoring and reconciliation; calibration and assessor
training; the evidence-balance logic common to all of them; and the legal
constraints that attach to scores as records and decisions.

**Not covered:** the content of the competency frameworks themselves — the nine
Civil Service Behaviours, the six CVF competencies and three values, EPSO's
eight general competencies, the NHS Constitution values — which belong to
`uk-civil-service-success-profiles`, `police-competency-values-framework` and
`epso-competency-framework`. Question *design* belongs to
`interviewer-question-design`; probing technique to
`follow-up-probing-questions`; panel composition and dynamics to
`panel-interviews`; diagnosis of specific answer failures to
`common-mistakes-recovery`. Psychometric test scoring (ability tests, judgement
tests, situational judgement tests) is out of scope except where a competency
score depends on it. Private-sector scoring is largely unpublished and is
therefore treated only where a professional body documents the method.

# Definitions

- **Rating scale** — the ordered set of scores an assessor may award, with a
  written descriptor attached to each point. The College of Policing states the
  requirement directly: "the rating scale must have descriptors alongside each
  score and these should be clear and unambiguous" [8].
- **Descriptor** — the published wording defining what a given score point
  means (e.g. "5 Good Demonstration — Substantial positive evidence of the
  behaviour" [4]).
- **Anchor** — EPSO's term for the observable elements a marker considers when
  evaluating a competency: "Anchors refer to the elements considered by test
  markers—i.e., assessors—when evaluating a given competency" [13].
- **Behavioural indicator** — the NHS/VBR term for the paired positive and
  negative observable behaviours listed against a criterion in the interview
  scoring framework [15].
- **Benchmark** — a minimum score, for a single element or a combination of
  elements, that a candidate must achieve to progress or be appointed [4]. Also
  used in policing for pre-assessment calibration of assessors ("often referred
  to as benchmarking or frame of reference training") [8]; this document uses
  "benchmark" for the threshold sense and "calibration" for the training sense.
- **Pass mark / pass score** — EPSO's term for the threshold on a test or a
  combined set of tests, fixed in the competition notice [10] [11] [12].
- **ORCE** — observe, record, classify, evaluate: the structured assessment
  sequence used in policing [8] and, in the same five verbs plus "score", in
  Civil Service panel guidance [1].
- **FORCE** — familiarise, observe, record, classify, evaluate: the NHS VBR
  variant, which adds a preparatory step [15].
- **Arithmetic decision making** — ranking candidates by their combined scores
  [8]; the Civil Service equivalent is reconciliation "by taking an arithmetic
  average" [1].
- **Rational decision making** — the College of Policing's term for the
  alternative, in which "groups of assessors discuss the candidates'
  performances and decide who should be successful" [8].

# Framework Context

**Evidence layer — cited.** Four published systems, four different instruments,
one common logic.

| System | Instrument | Points | Where the descriptors are published | Reconciliation rule |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| UK Civil Service — experience, technical, behaviours | Numeric rating scale | **7** | Labels and definitions published by COPFS [4]; the *instruction* to use it is central [1] [2] [3] | Independent scoring, then arithmetic average [1] |
| UK Civil Service — strengths | Numeric rating scale | **4** | **Not published** — see Risks & Caveats [1] [5] | As above [1] |
| UK Civil Service — sift, high volume | A/B/C rating | **3** | In the Cabinet Office packs themselves [1] [2] [3] | Benchmark flexed to campaign [1] |
| UK policing | A–D rating scale | **4** | College of Policing example scale [8] | Arithmetic; discussion only for borderline/tied cases [8] |
| EPSO — general competencies (pre-2023 model) | Marks per competency | **1 to 10** | Anchors per competency [14]; thresholds per notice [10] [11] | Combined pass mark in the notice [10] [11] |
| EPSO — written test (current model) | Marks | **0 to 10** | Five published written-test anchors [13]; pass score in the notice [12] | Weighted combined score [12] |
| NHS values-based interview | Scoring framework against indicators | Not centrally fixed | Positive/negative behavioural indicators per criterion [15] | Combined with other assessment outputs [15] |

Two structural points follow from the table.

First, **the scale and the standard are different objects.** The Civil Service
publishes grade-banded behaviour descriptors — the *Civil Service Behaviours*
document sets out "Examples of behaviours at this level" for six levels from
Level 1 (AA and AO or equivalent) to Level 6 (Director and Director General)
[6]. Those tell a panel what good looks like *at that grade*. The 7-point scale
tells the panel how far a given answer fell short of, met or exceeded that
standard [4]. Policing separates the same two objects: the CVF's three
cumulative levels — "Level 1: Practitioner, Level 2: Supervisor/middle manager,
Level 3: Senior manager/executive", where "The levels are cumulative" — set the
standard [9], and the A–D scale measures performance against it [8].

Second, **EPSO's design point is different in kind.** Where the UK systems
publish a scale and leave the standard to the role, EPSO publishes the
*standard* — the anchors — and fixes the scale and thresholds separately in each
competition notice. The anchors leaflet defines eight general competencies, each
"composed of various observable elements, called anchors" [14]; the notice then
says what each is worth. This is why an EPSO marking claim is only ever true of
a named notice, never of "EPSO" in general.

# Core Reference

**Evidence layer — cited.**

## The Civil Service instruction, in its own words

The Cabinet Office guidance for delegated grades (EO–Grade 6, May 2020) states,
under "Interview assessment": "Experience, technical skills and behaviours
should be scored using the 7 point scale. Strengths should be scored using the
4 point scale" [1]. The identical sentence appears in the HR Deputy Director
pack [2] and the HR Director pack [3]. Three independent grade tiers carrying
the same wording is what makes this a central rule rather than a local one.

The same delegated-grades pack describes the blended interview it applies to:
it "will typically assess experience, technical skills, behaviours and
strengths" and "will also typically include a 5-minute presentation" [1]. The
Deputy Director and Director packs describe a blended interview assessing
experience, technical skills, behaviours and strengths, with a five-minute
presentation on a set topic related to the candidate's leadership experience
[2] [3].

## The seven descriptors

COPFS — a UK government department recruiting under Success Profiles — is the
only located publisher of the seven labels *with* their definitions. Applications
are "scored using the 7 point scale noted below", and at interview "Behaviours,
experience and technical skills are scored on the same 1-7 rating scale used at
Sift" [4]:

| Score | Label | Published definition |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Not Demonstrated | "No positive evidence and/ or substantial negative evidence demonstrated" |
| 2 | Minimal Demonstration | "Limited positive evidence and/ or mainly negative evidence demonstrated" |
| 3 | Moderate Demonstration | "Moderate positive evidence but some negative evidence demonstrated" |
| 4 | Acceptable Demonstration | "Adequate positive evidence and any negative evidence would not cause concern" |
| 5 | Good Demonstration | "Substantial positive evidence of the behaviour" |
| 6 | Strong Demonstration | "Substantial positive evidence; includes some evidence of exceeding expectations at this level" |
| 7 | Outstanding Demonstration | "The evidence provided wholly exceeds expectation at this level" |

All wording quoted from [4].

Read the column carefully and the scale's internal logic is visible. Points 1–3
are governed by **negative** evidence. Point 4 is the point at which negative
evidence stops mattering ("would not cause concern"). Points 5–7 are governed
purely by the **quantity and reach** of positive evidence — 5 is substantial
evidence *at* the level, 6 adds "some evidence of exceeding expectations at this
level", 7 is evidence that "wholly exceeds" it. The 4→6 gap, in other words, is
not a fluency gap; it is a gap of substantiveness plus demonstrated stretch
beyond the grade standard.

## Benchmarks: the part that varies

COPFS publishes the benchmark mechanics: "A board may set a benchmark for
interview where there is a job and business need. A benchmark is a score that a
candidate must achieve for a single or combination of elements. I.e. Candidates
must score a 5 on the lead behaviours noted in the pack or an overall score of
20 must be scored across the 4 behaviours." The standing floor is separate:
"we set a standard benchmark for all their assessments; candidates must
demonstrate the minimum essential criteria for all roles, this includes scoring
4 or more in all behaviours. However, a candidate can score a single score of 3
where scores for all other behaviours are high." Any additional benchmark "will
be detailed in the application pack and invite to interview letter so candidates
are aware during their preparation" [4].

Departmental variation is real and documented. The Government Statistical
Service's Success Profiles guidance sets its standard by *framework level* —
Statistical Officer and Statistical Data Scientist candidates must show "the
potential to achieve Level 1 of the GSG Competency Framework within their first
year; and Level 2 of the Civil Service behaviours", while higher grades must
demonstrate "Level 2 GSG competencies and Level 3 of the Civil Service
behaviours" — and it operationalises the near-miss explicitly: candidates who
"fail to meet the required standard and fall short by scoring one '3' in either
of the technical competencies or Civil Service behaviours" are routed to a
temporary Statistical Officer pipeline rather than simply rejected [7].

## Sift scoring and the 3-point scale

At sift, "candidates submit a CV and personal statement or application form
which will be considered against the person specification. Evidence of the
candidates' behaviours, technical skills and experience are considered and
scored. For single role or smaller campaigns, candidates should be scored
against the numerical scoring scale for each element. For high volume campaigns,
candidates can be scored using a lead criteria or can be awarded an overall
rating against the 3 point scale" — A "candidate meets all the criteria", B
"borderline/candidate meets some of the criteria", C "candidate does not meet
the criteria". Crucially: "The benchmark of those who will progress to the next
stage of the assessment process can be flexed in accordance with the quality of
applications and the number of roles available" [1].

At Deputy Director and Director level the sift is scored as a single combined
judgement — "Evidence of the candidates' behaviours, technical skills and
experience are considered together and a single score given. It is acceptable to
award a score using either the 7 point scale, which aligns with the vX
application tracking system, or the following 3 point scale" [2] [3] — and
progression is capacity-bound: "Those who clearly meet the criteria will progress
to the next stage. Those who meet some of the criteria may progress to the next
stage of assessment depending on the places available" [2].

## Policing: the A–D scale

The College of Policing publishes an example rating scale defined entirely by
evidence balance [8]:

| Grade | Published descriptor |
|---|---|
| A | "Substantial positive evidence of the competency observed with regard to the quality and quantity of the evidence and few or no areas for development observed" |
| B | "Positive evidence of the competency observed and some areas for development but, on balance, more positive evidence with regard to the quality and quantity of the evidence" |
| C | "Some positive evidence of the competency observed but, on balance, more areas for development with regard to the quality and quantity of the evidence" |
| D | "Substantial areas for development identified and little or no positive evidence of the competency observed with regard to the quality and quantity of the evidence" |

The pivot between B and C is the phrase "on balance" — B and C describe the same
mixture of positive evidence and development areas, differing only in which side
weighs more. That is the clearest published statement anywhere in these four
systems that marking is a weighing exercise, not a threshold checklist.

## EPSO: three generations of marking, in three notices

EPSO's marking rules live in competition notices, and they have changed
materially. All three notices below were opened and read directly on EUR-Lex for
this document.

**EPSO/AD/389/21** (OJ C 39A, 4 February 2021) assessed eight general
competencies — including Leadership — across a situational competency-based
interview and a general competency-based interview, and set "Pass marks: 3/10
per competency and 40/80 in total" [10]. Successful candidates received a
"competency passport showing your overall marks for each competency and the
Selection Board's comment providing quantitative and qualitative feedback" [10].

**EPSO/AD/399/22** (OJ C 114A, 10 March 2022) assessed the same eight general
competencies, splitting them between a competency-based interview and a
situational competency-based interview, each marked "1 to 10", with the
threshold stated as a single combined figure: "Each competency will be marked
out of 10. There is no pass mark per competency. However, candidates must reach
a pass mark of at least 40/80 in total for all competencies combined" [11]. The
reserve list was drawn from candidates with "the required pass marks in all the
tests as well as the highest overall marks, out of 280, in the tests aimed at
assessing general and field-related competencies" [11].

The difference between the two is the single most instructive fact in EPSO
marking: **within roughly a year, EPSO removed the per-competency floor and kept
only the aggregate.** Under AD/389/21 a candidate scoring 2 on one competency
was eliminated regardless of the total; under AD/399/22 the same candidate
passed if the eight marks summed to 40.

**EPSO/AD/427/26** (OJ C/2026/711, 5 February 2026) is the current model, and it
marks no general competencies at interview because it holds no interview. The
tests are reasoning tests, an EU knowledge test, a digital skills test and a
free-text essay on EU matters ("EUFTE") [12]. The word "interview" does not
appear in the notice's test provisions at all. The EUFTE is scored "0 to 10"
with a pass score of "5/10", lasts 40 minutes, and is marked against published
anchors: "The EUFTE is neither a language test nor a test of factual knowledge.
The assessment will be based on the specific anchors published on the EPSO
website" [12]. Scores are combined by weighting: verbal reasoning 40% and the EU
knowledge and digital skills tests 30% each in the preliminary combined score;
in the final combined score, verbal reasoning 35%, EU knowledge 25%, digital
skills 25% and the EUFTE 15% [12]. Sequencing is strictly gated — the
multiple-choice tests are "scored in the following order: the reasoning tests,
the EU knowledge test, and the digital skills test. Each time, a test will be
scored only for those candidates who obtained at least the required pass score(s)
in the previous test", and "Should a candidate fail to obtain the required pass
score(s) in one of the tests, their participation in the competition will come
to an end" [12].

## EPSO's anchors: the published marking standard

Anchor-based marking survives the abolition of the oral tests. EPSO publishes
the five anchors used to mark the written test formats (WT, FRWT and EUFTE),
stating that "The assessment will be based on the specific 'anchors' listed
below" and defining the term: "Anchors refer to the elements considered by test
markers—i.e., assessors—when evaluating a given competency" [13]. The five are
[13]:

1. "Structures written communication with a logical flow of ideas."
2. "Writes concisely, avoiding unnecessary words and sentences."
3. "Presents subject matter in a clear and understandable way."
4. "Adapts and tailors writing to suit the intended audience and purpose."
5. "Uses the information provided effectively to address the assignment."

For the general competencies, the anchors leaflet remains the published marking
standard: the framework "defines the 8 general competencies that EU officials
are expected to demonstrate throughout their career. Each general competency is
composed of various observable elements, called anchors" [14]. Under Critical
thinking, analysing and creative problem-solving, for example, the published
anchors include "Differentiates the essential from less crucial elements and
manages to integrate information coming from different sources" and "Identifies
possible ways forward taking a range of points of view and positions into
consideration" [14].

## NHS: indicators rather than a scale

NHS values-based recruitment publishes no central numeric scale. Its instrument
is the paired positive/negative behavioural indicator set inside a structured
interview template. The framework instructs designers to develop criteria "into
specific indicators describing positive and negative" behaviours, and to ensure
"that the positive and negative behavioural indicators or key criteria have been
… [built into an] interview scoring framework. This will be used to assist with
objective evaluation" [15].

The published worked template for the value **Integrity** (mapped to the NHS
Constitution value "Commitment to quality of care"), defined as "Is open, honest
and transparent in all actions. Takes responsibility for challenging
inappropriate behaviour and is receptive to feedback from others", pairs the
lead question "Tell me about a time when you had to address a difficult
situation with a colleague…" with these indicators [15]:

| Positive indicators | Negative indicators |
|---|---|
| "Is proactive in identifying and addressing issues" | "Defers responsibility to others" |
| "Is sensitive and tactful when raising difficult issues" | "Jumps to conclusions" |
| "Is aware of the risks associated with inaction" | "Seeks to blame others" |

A second published template, for working with others, pairs positives such as
"Considers the needs/perspectives of others" and "Has strategies for working
effectively alongside others (for example, through compromise)" against
negatives including "Is dismissive of the views/perspectives of others" and
"Makes inappropriate assumptions about others" [15].

The negative column is the part candidates never anticipate. In this instrument
a blaming aside does not merely fail to earn credit — it is a listed, tickable
negative indicator.

## Evidence balance: the shared logic

Set the four descriptor sets side by side and the same sentence structure
recurs. COPFS 3: "Moderate positive evidence but some negative evidence" [4].
Policing C: "Some positive evidence … but, on balance, more areas for
development" [8]. The five-point competency evaluation scheme reproduced by the
University of Oxford Careers Service from Civil Service resourcing
best-practice guidance runs from 5, "positive evidence of a high level of
competence with no substantial negative evidence", down to 1, "little or no
positive evidence of competence, along with considerable negative evidence"
[23]. NHS scoring frameworks are built from explicitly paired positive and
negative indicators [15].

None of these instruments asks the assessor whether the candidate was good. All
four ask what evidence was present, what evidence was absent, and which way the
balance falls.

# Question Bank

Two audiences, two kinds of question. The candidate set is calibration practice:
each question is one you should be able to answer *about your own answer* before
a panel scores it. The interviewer set is scoring-decision practice: each
describes a marking situation with a documented right answer. Sourced entries
quote or closely paraphrase a named publication; composed entries are written
for this knowledge base in the style of the cited instruments and are not
attributable to any employer.

## Candidate calibration — moving a 4 towards a 6

1. Reading the published descriptors, what "substantial positive evidence of the behaviour" did that answer actually contain, and how much of it? *(composed — in the style of the published descriptor for score 5)* [4]
2. What in that answer would a panel record as negative evidence, and would it "cause concern"? *(composed — in the style of the published descriptor for score 4)* [4]
3. Where in that answer is the "evidence of exceeding expectations at this level" that separates a 6 from a 5? *(composed — in the style of the published descriptor for score 6)* [4]
4. If the standard is the behaviour descriptor for the advertised grade, which sentence of your answer met it and which merely described the context? *(composed — in the style of the grade-banded behaviour descriptors)* [6]
5. On balance, did that answer contain more positive evidence or more areas for development? *(composed — in the style of the A–D rating scale)* [8]
6. What did the answer miss, and did anything in it detract from your performance? *(sourced: College of Policing — the evaluate step of ORCE instructs assessors to "Consider what the candidate missed or if anything detracted from their performance")* [8]
7. Which competency would an assessor classify each part of that answer under, and did you accidentally give the same evidence twice for two competencies? *(sourced: College of Policing — assessors "Classify each behaviour once – do not double count")* [8]
8. Would a benchmark of "a 5 on the lead behaviours" have been met by that answer, or only a 4? *(composed — in the style of the published benchmark example)* [4]
9. If the campaign requires "an overall score of 20 across the 4 behaviours", which of your four answers is carrying the total and which is dragging it down? *(composed — in the style of the published benchmark example)* [4]
10. If this were sifted on the 3-point scale, does your application evidence meet all the criteria, some of them, or none? *(composed — in the style of the published A/B/C sift scale)* [1]
11. Did you structure that answer with a logical flow of ideas? *(sourced: EPSO — published written-test anchor)* [13]
12. Did you write or speak concisely, avoiding unnecessary words and sentences? *(sourced: EPSO — published written-test anchor)* [13]
13. Did you present the subject matter in a clear and understandable way? *(sourced: EPSO — published written-test anchor)* [13]
14. Did you adapt and tailor what you produced to suit the intended audience and purpose? *(sourced: EPSO — published written-test anchor)* [13]
15. Did you use the information provided effectively to address the assignment? *(sourced: EPSO — published written-test anchor)* [13]
16. Did your answer differentiate the essential from less crucial elements and integrate information from different sources? *(sourced: EPSO — published anchor under Critical thinking, analysing and creative problem-solving)* [14]
17. Did your answer identify possible ways forward taking a range of points of view and positions into consideration? *(sourced: EPSO — published anchor under Critical thinking, analysing and creative problem-solving)* [14]
18. Reviewing that answer against a negative-indicator list, did you at any point defer responsibility to others, jump to conclusions, or seek to blame others? *(sourced: NHS Values Based Recruitment — published negative indicators for Integrity)* [15]
19. Did that answer show you were proactive in identifying and addressing the issue, and aware of the risks of inaction? *(sourced: NHS Values Based Recruitment — published positive indicators for Integrity)* [15]

21. Did you provide evidence rather than statements, with a clear beginning, middle and end? *(sourced: Dyfed-Powys Police interview marking guide — published description of what good evidence looks like)* [22]

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact. It points back to the
descriptors cited above and adds no new claims.**

- **Write to the band you want, using the band's own words.** If you are
  targeting 5–6 on the Civil Service scale, the descriptors ask for
  "substantial positive evidence" plus "some evidence of exceeding expectations
  at this level" [4]. Operationally that is: more than one distinct piece of
  evidence per behaviour, and at least one thing you did that the grade
  descriptor for the role [6] would not have required. A single competent
  example, cleanly told, is a 4.
- **Audit for negative evidence before you audit for polish.** Points 1–3 of the
  7-point scale are driven by negative evidence, and 4 is defined as the point
  where it "would not cause concern" [4]. On the NHS instrument, negatives are
  literally listed — deferring responsibility, jumping to conclusions, blaming
  [15]. Removing one blaming clause can be worth more than rewriting the whole
  answer.
- **Answer in classifiable units.** Assessors classify evidence into
  competencies after you have finished, and are told to classify each behaviour
  once without double counting [8]. An answer that blends three competencies
  into one narrative gives the assessor an allocation problem; an answer that
  moves through distinct actions gives them clean units to file.
- **Give them something to record.** The record step requires notes "as close to
  verbatim as possible", with "No assuming or interpreting" [8]. Concrete
  clauses — what you did, to whom, with what number attached — survive that
  process. Adjectives about yourself do not.
- **Say what you missed, once.** The evaluate step directs assessors to
  "Consider what the candidate missed or if anything detracted from their
  performance" [8]. A brief, specific reflection closes that gap on your terms
  rather than leaving it to inference.
- **Where anchors are published, treat them as the marking scheme, because they
  are.** EPSO says the assessment "will be based on the specific 'anchors'"
  [13]. For a written test that means: structure, concision, clarity, audience
  fit, and use of the provided material [13] — five things to check against
  before submitting, in that order.
- **Worked calibration, question 3 above.** Take a real answer about resolving a
  delivery risk. *At 4*: you identified the risk, told your manager, and the
  deadline was met. Adequate positive evidence; nothing concerning [4]. *At 5*:
  add substance — you quantified the slippage, put two options to your manager
  with trade-offs, and re-planned the sequence yourself. *At 6*: add stretch
  beyond the grade standard — you also changed how the team logged risks
  afterwards, so the same failure could not recur, and one other team adopted
  it. The facts are the same event; what moved is the quantity of positive
  evidence and the presence of something exceeding what the level required [4].
- **For interviewers: score first, talk second.** The documented sequence is
  independent scoring, then reconciliation [1] [8]. Discussing before scoring
  converts an arithmetic process into a rational one, which the published
  evidence rates as less valid and harder to defend [8].

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — cited.** This section is the core of the document: how a
score is actually produced, and how several scores become one decision.

## The sequence: ORCE and FORCE

The College of Policing prescribes ORCE for values and competency assessment:
"Trained assessors should follow a structured process such as ORCE (observe,
record, classify, evaluate), which breaks down the assessing process into
component parts and helps to avoid bias" [8]. The published expansion is
specific about independence at every stage [8]:

| Step | What the guidance requires |
|---|---|
| **Observe** | "Watching, listening or reading a candidate's assessment"; carried out at the same time as record; "Independent of any other assessor(s)" |
| **Record** | "Take detailed notes of behavioural evidence (as close to verbatim as possible)"; "No assuming or interpreting"; "Record just behavioural evidence"; independent of other assessors |
| **Classify** | "After candidate has left the room"; "Only classify behavioural evidence"; "Only classify behaviour from that assessment"; "Classify each behaviour once – do not double count"; independent of other assessors |
| **Evaluate** | "After classifying"; "Based on quality and quantity of the classified evidence"; "Consider the level of role and aim of the assessment process (benchmarking)"; "Consider what the candidate missed or if anything detracted from their performance"; independent of other assessors |

The model is explicitly designed for group use with deferred discussion: "ORCE
should be used with paired assessment or in groups with assessors first carrying
out each stage independently and then meeting to discuss their grades and
evidence and then agreeing on a final grade" [8].

The Civil Service uses the same five verbs plus scoring: "Panel members should
observe, record, classify, evaluate and score each question independently, then
calculate an overall score before jointly agreeing a merit list using the
arithmetic method" [1]. At Deputy Director and Director level the wording is
"observe, record, classify, evaluate and score each question independently
before jointly calculating and agreeing an overall score based on all of the
candidate information, including the pre-interview leadership assessment
reports" [2] [3].

NHS VBR adds a preparatory step and drops scoring from the acronym, giving
**FORCE** [15]:

- **F — Familiarise:** "Ensure that you are familiar with the interview
  materials to be used as well as the entry requirements or job description and
  person specification and values of importance for the role."
- **O — Observe:** "The key skill in observing is not to make judgements, but to
  be aware of what the applicant is saying or doing. Be aware of possible
  assessment bias."
- **R — Record:** "Make written notes of the applicant's responses and behaviour
  during the interview. An observation/recording sheet should be used."
- **C — Classify:** "Evidence of the required competencies and values should be
  identified through examining the information provided in the applicant's
  responses. Some comments will include evidence - these should be identified.
  Some comments may not be directly relevant to the target criteria."
- **E — Evaluate:** "The information identified in the response now needs to be
  evaluated. Look at the scoring indicators to decide on the quality of the
  evidence relevant to each criterion."

The framework also requires notes to be finalised immediately: "Allow some time
immediately after the interview for interviewers to finalise their notes as
leaving this until later can result in distorted information" [15].

## Reconciliation: the arithmetic rule and the evidence for it

The Civil Service rule is unambiguous. After independent scoring, "Variances in
scoring amongst panel members can be discussed; it may be useful to discuss
evidence underlying scores when these variances are particularly marked. Panel
members may decide to change their score(s) or stand by them, but ultimately
individual panel scores should be reconciled by taking an arithmetic average
which will serve as the final scores for the candidate" [1].

Three things are worth drawing out. Discussion is permitted but **optional and
evidence-anchored** — the trigger is a marked variance, and the subject is the
evidence, not the candidate. Panel members are explicitly allowed to **stand by
their scores**; there is no requirement to converge. And the arithmetic average
is **decisive**, so a dissenting scorer's number still counts in full.

The College of Policing supplies the empirical case for that design: "Arithmetic
decision making, where the candidates' scores are used to rank candidates
according to their performance, has been shown to be the most valid
decision-making method. This can incorporate the application of thresholds for
certain competencies, values or exercises that have been identified as most
important in advance of the process." The alternative is rejected on two
grounds at once: "Rational decision-making methods, where groups of assessors
discuss the candidates' performances and decide who should be successful, have
been shown to be less effective in selecting the best candidates and are
difficult to justify from a fairness point of view" [8].

The exception is narrow and named: "Assessor discussions can, however, be useful
where candidates are borderline or where there are a number of candidates tied
with the same score or score band, and qualitative information can be used to
supplement the decision-making process" [8]. Otherwise, "All decisions should be
evidence based", based on information "collected only from a well-designed,
valid, objective and standardised assessment process", with "the same process
for all candidates" and a process "clearly documented and explained" [8]. Full
records are required for both purposes: "Full and accurate records of scores for
each candidate should be made throughout the assessment process so that there is
a final verified record to be used in decision making and also forming an audit
trail" [8].

The wider evidence base points the same way. Sackett and colleagues' revision of
meta-analytic validity estimates in personnel selection — which reduced most
estimates by .10–.20 points after correcting systematic overcorrection for range
restriction — nonetheless found that "Structured interviews emerged as the
top-ranked selection procedure" [24]. The CIPD documents the structured
interview as a predefined question set asked in the same order with responses
scored against pre-agreed objective criteria, on the rationale that this makes
responses comparable and reduces the role of personal bias [21]. ACAS
operationalises the same principles for employers: "If possible, each interview
should be carried out by more than 1 person to reduce the risk of
discriminating"; "When interviewing it's a good idea to ask each applicant the
same questions where possible. This is to ensure you're treating applicants in
the same way"; and "check your decisions are based on how each applicant met
each point in the job description and person specification" [20].

## Calibration before the first candidate

Descriptors alone do not produce consistency. The College of Policing is
explicit: "It is important that rating scales are understood by everyone
involved in the process and that everyone has a shared understanding of the
grades and what they mean, to ensure consistency of scoring. To this end, the
rating scale must have descriptors alongside each score and these should be
clear and unambiguous. Steps should be taken to calibrate multiple assessors'
ratings, for example, by conducting a mock assessment or having a discussion
about expected levels of performance at each grade, in advance of the first live
candidate assessment. This is often referred to as benchmarking or frame of
reference training" [8].

Assessor training is specified as a minimum content list covering the role being
assessed for, a shared understanding of the criteria, familiarisation with
questions and materials, familiarisation with competencies and values,
"understanding the rating scale and calibration with other assessors", barriers
to accurate assessment, unconscious bias, and assessing and interviewing skills
[8]. Volume processes additionally "should also have a quality assurance process
to ensure that candidates are being assessed fairly and consistently" [8]. The
Civil Service position is shorter but consistent: "It is recommended that all
interview panels should be diverse, and all panellists should have undertaken
training on interview skills prior to sitting on an interview panel" [1].

Setting standards is a design decision made before assessment, not during it.
The CVF instructs: "When setting standards for assessment, consider the
importance of each competency and value for the particular role. Assessments
should be designed accordingly. When assessing all competencies and values,
allow for candidates to demonstrate strengths in different areas" [9]. It also
warns against mechanical use of the framework: "The behaviours in the CVF should
not be used in isolation or as a checklist. This is because the behaviours are
just examples, and in any role there will be many other ways in which an
individual might demonstrate the competencies and values", and assessment must
take account of "individual circumstances", "the specific requirements of a
role" and the "competency description (not just the behavioural examples)" [9] —
wording reproduced at the head of at least one force's published interview
marking guide [22].

## Named barriers to accurate scoring

The College of Policing publishes a barriers table with prescribed counters [8].
The expectancy effect — "Tendency for assessors to generate either a positive or
negative expectation from pre-assessment information (eg application form,
word-of-mouth opinion, appraisal document) and for their evaluation and
decisions to follow these expectations" — is countered by "Using ORCE model of
assessment" and by giving assessors "only … information they truly need in
advance of the assessment". Confirmatory information bias — assessors "actively
seek information to confirm their initial impressions" — is countered by
comprehensive assessor training. Central tendency bias, where "Assessors
consistently score candidates in the middle of a rating scale, avoiding the
extreme ends", is countered by training in how the rating scale is used, with
assessors "encouraged to use the full length of" it; leniency and severity are
the same failure displaced towards one end [8]. Halo and horns effects — where an
assessor concludes the candidate performed uniformly well or poorly and scores
accordingly — are likewise countered by ORCE [8].

## Sensitivity when scoring values

Values-based assessment carries a specific handling instruction. Because values
"are often tied to individuals' deeply held views and motivations", assessors
"may need to probe behaviours to identify the underlying values"; questions "may
be emotive and assessors should be alert to signs that candidates are becoming
upset or emotional"; and assessors "should treat this information sensitively
and in particular not reveal sensitive personal information in scoring sheets or
feedback reports" [8]. The NHS framework makes the equivalent point about
closing the interview, warning that in values-based interviews "an applicant
could feel demotivated or that they have been 'exposed' in some way" [15].

## Feedback derived from the score

Both systems tie feedback back to the instrument. Policing: feedback should "be
constructive, highlighting what was done well and what can be done to improve",
"be based on their actual performances at the assessment centre or interviews",
and "be given in a timely manner"; and it should be framed by explaining "that
decisions are based on what was observed during an assessment, which is only a
snapshot of their performance. Therefore, it is not to say they don't hold the
values, but that they have not evidenced them" [8]. NHS: "Be sure to describe the
applicant's performance based on the behavioural indicators when providing
feedback on the interview" [15]. COPFS offers candidates feedback "in the form of
a Candidate Rating form" [4]. EPSO's pre-2023 model gave successful candidates a
competency passport with per-competency marks and Selection Board comment [10]
[11].

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact.** Each pitfall names
the documented criterion it violates.

## Candidate-side

- **Optimising for fluency instead of evidence.** Nothing in any published
  descriptor rewards delivery. Scores 5–7 are defined by quantity and reach of
  positive evidence [4]; A and B by the balance of evidence against development
  areas [8]. A polished answer with one thin example is a 4.
- **Leaving negative evidence in.** A blamed colleague, an unacknowledged
  consequence, a decision defended rather than reflected on — these are listed
  negative indicators on the NHS instrument [15] and the definitional driver of
  scores 1–3 on the Civil Service scale [4].
- **Reusing the same evidence across two competencies.** Assessors "classify
  each behaviour once – do not double count" [8], so the second use scores
  nothing while consuming your time.
- **Aiming at the wrong level.** Where levels are cumulative [9], evidence at a
  lower level does not satisfy a higher one; and the Civil Service standard is
  the behaviour descriptor for the advertised grade [6].
- **Assuming a strong average carries a weak element.** True under some
  benchmarks and fatal under others: the standard COPFS floor tolerates a single
  3, but a board may require a 5 on lead behaviours [4], and EPSO/AD/389/21 set
  a hard 3/10 per-competency floor [10]. Read the pack.
- **Preparing scripted "strengths" answers.** Cabinet Office guidance
  deliberately keeps strengths out of recruitment materials so that candidates
  do not prepare responses in advance [1]; a rehearsed strengths answer is
  answering a question the panel was told not to signal.
- **Never self-scoring.** The descriptors are public [4] [8] [13] [14] [15]. A
  candidate who has never applied them to their own recording is guessing at the
  standard they are being measured against.

## Interviewer-side

- **Discussing before scoring.** Converts arithmetic decision making into
  rational decision making — documented as less effective at selecting the best
  candidates and hard to justify on fairness [8]; the Civil Service sequence
  puts independent scoring first [1].
- **Chasing consensus.** Panel members "may decide to change their score(s) or
  stand by them"; the average is what settles it [1]. Pressure to converge is
  not required by the guidance and quietly re-weights the loudest scorer.
- **Reading the application form for atmosphere.** The named expectancy effect,
  countered by restricting pre-assessment information to what assessors truly
  need [8].
- **Interpretive notes.** "No assuming or interpreting" [8]; "seemed confident"
  is not behavioural evidence and cannot be classified.
- **Classifying while the candidate is still in the room.** The sequence places
  classification after the candidate has left [8], for good reason: classifying
  live biases the questions you ask next.
- **Skipping calibration.** Descriptors without frame-of-reference training
  produce four private scales wearing the same labels [8].
- **Living in the middle of the scale.** Central tendency bias; assessors should
  be "encouraged to use the full length of" the scale [8].
- **Setting a benchmark after seeing the scores.** Thresholds are to be
  identified "in advance of the process" [8] and communicated to candidates
  before assessment [8]; retrofitted benchmarks are the fastest route to an
  indefensible decision.
- **Rubber-stamping an automated score.** Covered under Legal & Fairness
  Considerations — the regulator treats it as solely automated decision-making
  [19].

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.** Drills that can be run this week.

1. **Self-score three recorded answers (45 minutes).** Record three answers to
   questions from `best-practice-foundation` or a sibling document. Score each
   1–7 using the published COPFS descriptors [4], writing the descriptor wording
   next to your score. Then, separately, list every piece of negative evidence
   the answer contains. Revise the weakest answer only by removing negatives and
   adding one further piece of positive evidence — do not re-record for polish.
   Re-score. The delta is your calibration.
2. **The 4-to-6 rewrite (30 minutes).** Take one answer you scored 4. Using the
   descriptors, add (a) a second distinct piece of positive evidence and (b) one
   thing you did that went beyond what the advertised grade descriptor [6]
   required. Re-score against "some evidence of exceeding expectations at this
   level" [4]. If you cannot find (b) in the real event, the answer is a 4 and
   you need a different example — which is a finding, not a failure.
3. **Run ORCE on yourself (30 minutes).** Play back a recorded answer. Take
   verbatim notes only, with no interpretation [8]. Stop. Classify each noted
   behaviour into exactly one competency, without double counting [8]. Only then
   evaluate against the descriptors, and finish by writing down what the answer
   missed [8]. Most candidates discover that the classify step, not the evaluate
   step, is where their answer thins out.
4. **Anchor check for written assessments (15 minutes).** Before submitting any
   written exercise, check it against EPSO's five published written-test anchors
   in order: structure, concision, clarity, audience fit, and use of the
   provided information [13]. Fix in that order; structure problems make the
   other four unfixable.
5. **Negative-indicator sweep (15 minutes).** Read a written-up example against
   the published NHS negative indicators — deferring responsibility, jumping to
   conclusions, blaming, dismissiveness, inappropriate assumptions about others
   [15]. Strike or reframe each hit.
6. **Paired scoring drill for panels (60 minutes).** Two assessors watch the
   same recorded or mock answer. Score independently, in silence, using the
   published descriptors [4] or [8]. Reveal both scores. Where the variance
   exceeds one point, discuss only the underlying evidence [1], then each decide
   whether to change or stand by. Take the arithmetic average as final [1].
   Repeat across three answers and track whether your variances shrink — that is
   frame-of-reference training in miniature [8].
7. **Benchmark rehearsal for designers (30 minutes).** For a live vacancy, write
   the benchmark down *before* any assessment: the floor per behaviour, any lead
   behaviour requiring a higher score, and any combined total [4]. Then state
   where candidates will be told [4]. If you cannot articulate the business need
   for a raised benchmark, it does not meet the published condition [4].
8. **Barrier audit (20 minutes).** After a real panel, go through the published
   barriers list — expectancy, confirmatory information, primacy, stereotyping,
   halo/horns, central tendency, leniency, severity [8] — and name honestly
   which one most affected your scoring. Assign one counter-measure for the next
   panel.

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — cited.** Scores are simultaneously a decision, a record and,
increasingly, an output of automated processing. Each attracts obligations.

- **Adjustments change the assessment, not the standard.** Under the Equality
  Act 2010 an employer must take reasonable steps to avoid the substantial
  disadvantage caused to a disabled person by a provision, criterion or practice
  [17]. The CVF applies this directly to scoring design: the framework "should be
  used in ways that allow for differing abilities", including those stemming
  from disabilities or neurodiversity, and "Reasonable adjustments should always
  be made for people who have disabilities that impact on their ability to
  demonstrate the competencies. This is in line with equality legislation" [9].
- **Public bodies must monitor the outcomes of their scoring.** The public sector
  equality duty requires listed authorities to have due regard to eliminating
  discrimination and advancing equality of opportunity in the exercise of their
  functions [18]. The College of Policing operationalises this for selection:
  adverse impact is assessed using the "80 per cent or four-fifths rule", and
  "Where adverse impact is detected, further analysis" is required [8]. Cabinet
  Office guidance reports that diversity outcomes were analysed for all groups
  across blended interviews and that "on the whole, there are no adverse impacts
  indicated with outcomes based on protected characteristics — as long as
  selection methods are delivered in a structured and consistent way, and
  training is provided to and/or official guidance is followed" [1]. The
  conditional clause is the substance of that finding: the fairness claim is
  contingent on the structure being followed.
- **Scores are records, and must be kept as such.** ACAS requires that
  recordings and notes made in interviews are handled in line with
  data-protection obligations [20]; the College of Policing requires "Full and
  accurate records of scores for each candidate … throughout the assessment
  process" forming "an audit trail" [8]; the NHS framework requires applicant
  consent before audio recording is used to assist recall [15]. Sensitive
  personal information disclosed during values questioning should not be
  reproduced in scoring sheets or feedback reports [8].
- **Automated scoring has a human-involvement test.** The ICO's *Recruitment
  rewired* findings apply UK GDPR article 22A, which provides that "a decision is
  based solely on automated processing if there is no meaningful human
  involvement in the taking of the decision". The regulator found that where a
  hiring manager reviewed high scorers but "'rubber-stamped' the 'red'
  candidates' rejections", that "constitutes solely ADM within the scope of
  article 22" — and that employers' belief that their tools were decision
  *support* was frequently not borne out in practice [19]. A panel that treats a
  machine-generated score as the decision has not made a human decision, whatever
  its process documentation says.
- **Blind sifting is a scoring control.** COPFS removes name, personal
  information, biographical data and education dates before the sift panel sees
  an application, so that candidates can "be judged only on merit and not on
  … background, race, gender, or other protected characteristic" [4].
- **Threshold flexing has fairness limits.** COPFS applies a higher progression
  threshold in high-volume campaigns while requiring that Disability Confident
  and veteran scheme applicants meet that same revised standard, stating that
  the approach is applied "consistently and transparently" [4]. Flexing a
  benchmark is permitted [1]; flexing it differently for different candidates is
  not.

# Risks & Caveats

- **No published descriptors exist for the 4-point strengths scale.** The
  instruction to use it is unambiguous and central [1] [2] [3], but no source
  located for this document publishes what its four points mean. The GOV.UK
  Success Profiles: Strengths page describes why and how strengths are assessed
  and lists assessment methods, but publishes no scale, no descriptors and no
  benchmark [5]. Candidates cannot self-score strengths answers against a
  published standard, and this document does not invent one.
- **The central Cabinet Office packs date from 2020.** The delegated-grades
  guidance is dated May 2020 and the Deputy Director and Director packs April
  2020 [1] [2] [3]. No refresh was located as of 2026-07-20. They remain
  published on GOV.UK, but a six-year-old process document should be treated as
  the best available record of central policy rather than as proof of current
  practice in any given department. The internal reference to the "vX
  application tracking system" [2] [3] is a dating marker worth noting.
- **COPFS is a departmental publication of a central scale.** The 7-point
  instruction is central [1]; the seven *labels and definitions* are published by
  one department [4]. Other departments applying the same central instruction may
  word their descriptors differently. Treat the wording as authoritative for
  COPFS and indicative elsewhere.
- **Benchmarks vary by campaign, and the published examples are examples.** "4 or
  more in all behaviours" is COPFS's standard benchmark, not a Civil Service
  rule; the 5-on-lead-behaviours and total-of-20 figures are illustrations given
  in that department's guidance [4]. GSS sets its bar by framework level instead
  [7]. Always read the pack.
- **The policing A–D scale is published as "an example rating scale"** [8], not
  as a mandated national instrument, and the guidance carrying it dates from 2018
  — predating the 2024 CVF. Its process content (ORCE, calibration, decision
  making, barriers) is framework-agnostic and remains applicable; its competency
  and value *names* do not reflect the 2024 framework and are not reproduced
  here.
- **EPSO marking claims are notice-specific and perishable.** The three notices
  cited [10] [11] [12] disagree with each other by design. Any general statement
  about "how EPSO scores competencies" is unsafe; the current model marks no
  general competencies at interview because it holds no interview [12]. Legacy
  EPSO test pages describing competency-based and situational interviews remain
  live on the EPSO site and are archive material, not current practice.
- **The NHS framework is dated March 2016 and its publisher no longer exists.**
  Health Education England merged into NHS England, which "has assumed all
  activities previously undertaken by HEE" [16]. The structured-interview
  framework [15] has not been reissued under NHS England branding. It remains
  operative in practice but is unreissued; several NHS citations also resolve to
  the legacy `hee.nhs.uk` domain, a retirement candidate.
- **The five-point Oxford scheme is illustrative, not current.** It is reproduced
  in a May 2016 careers-service document from Civil Service resourcing
  best-practice guidance [23] and is cited here only as corroboration of the
  evidence-balance logic, never as a live Civil Service instrument.
- **CIPD pages bot-block automated link checkers.** The selection-methods
  factsheet URL is real and allowlisted [21]; a human should eyeball it
  periodically.
- **Private-sector scoring is largely unpublished.** Nothing in this document
  should be read as describing how a named private employer marks. Where a
  private process is scored at all, the professional-body method [21] is the
  best available proxy, not evidence.

# Open Questions

- **What are the four points of the strengths scale?** A published descriptor set
  would let candidates self-score strengths answers and would close the largest
  single gap in this document. Cabinet Office "Designing Strengths Questions" and
  "Success Profiles: Scoring case studies" are named as further guidance in the
  delegated-grades pack [1] but were not located as public documents.
- **Has the 2020 central guidance been refreshed?** A post-2020 Cabinet Office
  statement of the 7-point/4-point rule would move several claims here from
  "best available record" to "current".
- **Is there a centrally published Civil Service interview evaluation form?**
  Carried forward from `best-practice-foundation`; still unresolved as of
  2026-07-20.
- **Are the five EPSO written-test anchors derived from the Communication
  competency's anchors?** The similarity is visible, but no EPSO source states
  it, and this document does not assert it [13] [14].
- **Does any UK police force publish its live A–D descriptors under the 2024
  CVF?** Force marking guides publish "what good evidence looks like" [22] but
  the located scale descriptors remain those of the 2018 guidance [8].
- **What proportion of scores are now produced or pre-filtered by automated
  tools, and how often does the human review meet the article 22A test?** The ICO
  found employers' self-assessment unreliable [19]; sector-level data on
  competency scoring specifically was not located.
