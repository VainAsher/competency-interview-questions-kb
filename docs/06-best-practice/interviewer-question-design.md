# Executive Summary

This document is the interviewer- and HR-facing flagship of the Best Practice
category. Where its parent, **best-practice-foundation**, surveys what good
interviewing looks like from both sides of the table, this one answers a single
practical question: *how do I actually build a defensible competency interview —
the criteria, the questions, the rubric, the panel process and the monitoring —
from published UK and EU guidance rather than from instinct?*

The method it documents is remarkably consistent across four independent UK
public-sector frameworks. Start from job analysis and cap the criteria: Civil
Service HR recommends **no more than four behaviours** prioritised as critical
to a role [1] [2] [3] [4], and the Government Statistical Service caps the mix
of competencies and behaviours at **six in total** [5]. Write **four to six
questions** that map to the essential criteria you advertised [6]. Build each
question as the canonical three-part unit — a **lead question, a set of
pre-agreed probes, and positive and negative behavioural indicators** — a
structure published verbatim in Health Education England's structured-interview
design guidance with worked templates [7]. Turn the indicators into
exercise-specific assessor guidance, ideally piloted with existing good
performers in the role, and be clear that this is **"supportive guidance for
assessors, not a 'model answer'"** [8]. Score against a published scale with
descriptors — the central Civil Service 7-point and 4-point scales [1], the
College of Policing A–D scale [8], or EPSO's published anchors [11] [12] —
with panel members observing, recording, classifying, evaluating and scoring
**independently** before reconciling by arithmetic average [1] [8].

Confidence is **High**. Every core claim rests on an official framework
document, a regulator, a statute or a professional body, and the central
design pattern is corroborated independently by Civil Service HR, the NHS VBR
programme, the College of Policing and ACAS. The two acknowledged soft spots
are that the College of Policing guidance predates the 2024 CVF (only its
framework-agnostic process content is used here) and that one rich source of
worked panel practice — a University of Nottingham training deck — is
illustrative university practice, not statutory guidance.

# Key Takeaways

- **Cap what you assess before you write a single question.** Civil Service HR
  recommends "no more than four behaviours are prioritised as critical to the
  role", explicitly so that the panel can *sufficiently test* the criteria at
  interview alongside strengths and experience [1] [2] [3] [4]; the GSS caps
  the total mix of competencies and behaviours at six [5]. *(documented)*
- **Questions must map to the criteria you advertised.** DWP Digital tells
  candidates the panel will ask "four to six questions on your skills,
  knowledge and experience, based on the topics listed under 'essential
  criteria' in the advert", and that they are scored on those criteria [6].
  *(documented)*
- **The canonical design unit is lead question + probes + behavioural
  indicators.** HEE's guidance specifies that a question bank "should contain
  questions structured as follows; a lead question followed by a series of
  possible relevant probe questions, as well as a set of defined behavioural
  indicators", and publishes two worked templates with paired positive and
  negative indicators [7]. *(documented)*
- **A rubric is assessor guidance, not a model answer.** The College of
  Policing states that exercise-specific guidance is generated by having "a
  sample of good performers already in the role complete the exercise as part
  of a trial or pilot" and that "there will often be several different ways a
  candidate could demonstrate the competency… this is about creating supportive
  guidance for assessors, not a 'model answer'" [8]. *(documented)*
- **Score independently first, reconcile arithmetically second.** Cabinet
  Office guidance instructs panel members to "observe, record, classify,
  evaluate and score each question independently", then reconcile "by taking an
  arithmetic average which will serve as the final scores" [1]; the College of
  Policing describes the same ORCE sequence used independently before a
  moderation discussion [8]. *(documented)*
- **Panels should be diverse and trained.** The same Cabinet Office guidance
  recommends "that all interview panels should be diverse, and all panellists
  should have undertaken training on interview skills prior to sitting on an
  interview panel" [1]; ACAS advises more than one interviewer to reduce
  discrimination risk [21]. *(documented)*
- **Your notes are disclosable.** Interview notes are personal data subject to
  subject access; in a public body they also sit alongside Freedom of
  Information duties, and at Employment Tribunal it is the interview notes —
  "especially Chair's notes" — that are looked for, with the chair
  representing the panel [22]. *(documented)*
- **Monitor for adverse impact, but know the limits of the four-fifths rule.**
  The College of Policing documents the 80 per cent / four-fifths rule as
  "a useful guide for evaluation and… commonly used in practice", while
  stating plainly that "it does not have a legal footing" [8]. *(documented)*
- **Cabinet Office diversity analysis of blended interviews found no adverse
  impacts indicated — conditionally.** The finding holds "as long as selection
  methods are delivered in a structured and consistent way, and training is
  provided to and/or official guidance is followed" [1]. The condition is the
  whole point: structure is what makes the finding true. *(documented)*
- **If any part of your process is automated, the ICO's 2026 expectations
  apply on top of everything above** — meaningful human involvement,
  transparency at three trigger points, and the article 22C safeguards
  [16] [17] [18]. *(documented)*

# Purpose

This document exists for the person who has to *build* the interview: a hiring
manager, a panel chair, an HR business partner or an assessment designer. It
answers: how many things should I assess, how do I write questions that will
survive scrutiny, what does a defensible rubric look like, how should the panel
score, and what legal and data-protection duties bear on the design itself.

Candidates are a secondary audience. Understanding how a well-designed
interview is constructed — that the probes are pre-agreed, that the indicators
are written down before you speak, that there is no model answer — is among the
most useful things a candidate can know.

# Scope

**Covered:** selecting and capping assessment criteria from job analysis;
writing lead questions against advertised essential criteria; designing probe
sets; writing positive and negative behavioural indicators; converting
indicators into exercise-specific assessor guidance and rating scales;
published UK and EU scales usable as rubric spines; panel composition,
training, calibration and score reconciliation; note-taking and record
standards; the legal duties that constrain question content; adverse-impact
monitoring and validation of a question set.

**Not covered:** the full taxonomy of published scoring scales and worked
score calculations (**scoring-and-marking**); the technique and psychology of
probing in the live interview (**follow-up-probing-questions**); the complete
legal analysis of discrimination, adjustments and data protection
(**fairness-bias-legal**); panel dynamics and logistics
(**panel-interviews**); and the framework definitions themselves
(**uk-civil-service-success-profiles**, **nhs-values-based-interviews**).
Assessment-centre exercise design beyond the interview is out of scope except
where the College of Policing's rubric guidance is framework-agnostic.

# Definitions

- **Lead question** — the single opening question for a criterion, which
  establishes the context and invites the candidate's example. In HEE's
  template, one lead question sits per criterion, followed by its probes [7].
- **Probe** — a pre-agreed follow-up question attached to a lead question to
  elicit further evidence. HEE requires lead questions to be "accompanied by a
  range of pre-agreed probing questions" [7].
- **Behavioural indicator** — a written description of what a positive or
  negative demonstration of a criterion looks like, used to evaluate the
  answer. HEE's templates pair positive and negative indicator lists per
  criterion [7].
- **Assessor guidance** — exercise-specific worked examples of what good
  performance looks like in *this* exercise, derived from the framework
  indicators, and explicitly not a model answer [8].
- **Anchor** — EPSO's term for the observable elements a marker considers when
  evaluating a competency; each of EPSO's eight general competencies is
  "composed of various observable elements, called anchors" [11].
- **ORCE** — Observe, Record, Classify, Evaluate: the structured assessing
  sequence documented by the College of Policing [8] and echoed in the Cabinet
  Office instruction to "observe, record, classify, evaluate and score" [1].
- **FORCE** — HEE's variant, adding Familiarise to the front of the ORCE
  sequence [7].
- **Frame-of-reference training** — calibrating multiple assessors' ratings
  before live candidates, "for example, by conducting a mock assessment or
  having a discussion about expected levels of performance at each grade";
  also called benchmarking [8].

# Framework Context

**Evidence layer — cited.** Four UK public-sector frameworks and one EU
institution publish interviewer-side design guidance. They differ in
vocabulary and converge almost completely in method.

| Framework | Design instruction it publishes | Rubric instrument |
|---|---|---|
| Civil Service Success Profiles | No more than four behaviours prioritised as critical; questions designed to let candidates give the required level of evidence; diverse, trained panels [1] [2] [3] [4] | 7-point scale for experience, technical skills and behaviours; 4-point for strengths; 3-point A/B/C at sift [1] |
| Government Statistical Service | Mix of GSG competencies and Civil Service behaviours "does not exceed 6 in total"; level expectations set by grade [5] | Civil Service scales, applied with a documented failure threshold on a single low score [5] |
| NHS Values Based Recruitment | Criteria from role analysis; standardised documentation; lead question + probes + positive/negative indicators; interviewer training refreshed regularly [7] | Interview scoring framework built from the behavioural indicators [7] |
| College of Policing | Contextualise the assessment to the role and level; identify what good — and ideally poor — performance looks like; pilot with good performers [8] | A–D rating scale with descriptors; ORCE; assessor calibration [8] |
| EPSO | Competencies decomposed into published observable anchors; written-test assignments marked against five published anchors [11] [12] | Anchor-based marking [11] [12] |

The Civil Service position is the most explicitly quantified. The Cabinet
Office's delegated-grade guidance sets the sequence: "After undertaking a
thorough job analysis of the vacancy to define the role requirements, the
vacancy holder should refer to the relevant delegated grade Success Profile
Guide(s) and select relevant elements to define the Success Profile for the
specific role… Once defined, the elements will form the person specification in
the recruitment materials" [1]. The same guidance stresses that the Success
Profile Guides "are not designed to be used prescriptively; they have been
produced as starting guides and do not replace the requirement for job analysis
to be undertaken prior to recruiting" [1].

NHS VBR arrives at the same place from a different direction. HEE's guidance
states that "the effectiveness of a structured interview is based on a thorough
and accurate identification of the relevant knowledge, skills, attributes and
values associated with successful performance in the role and in the
organisation. A role analysis should be used to accurately identify the
criteria to be assessed at the interview", and that the criteria "should be
reflected in entry requirements, person specifications, job descriptions and
attraction materials so that applicants are aware of what they will be assessed
against" [7].

The College of Policing adds the level dimension: assessment content
"(questions/exercises) and the expectations of performance should be relevant
to the specific job being recruited for", and it publishes a worked example
showing how the same competency indicator — "I actively encourage and support
learning within my teams and colleagues" — carries different expected
behaviours for a sergeant and for an inspector [8].

At EU level, EPSO publishes the observable elements rather than the questions.
The competency framework leaflet defines eight general competencies, each
"composed of various observable elements, called anchors" [11]. Under the
current competition model the marking anchors that remain published are those
for the **written** test — five anchors covering logical structure, concision,
clarity, audience adaptation and effective use of the material provided [12].

# Core Reference

**Evidence layer — cited.**

## 1. Deciding what to assess, and how much

Civil Service HR's guidance is explicit and repeated verbatim across all three
grade-tier Success Profile Guides: "As a guide, it is recommended that no more
than four behaviours are prioritised as critical to the role. This will allow
the panel to sufficiently test the criteria at interview stage, along with a
candidate's strengths and experience" [1] [2] [3] [4]. The stated rationale
matters for design: the cap exists to protect *depth of testing*, not to save
time.

Two related central rules constrain criterion selection. On experience, vacancy
holders "should adhere to the central CSEP guidance that, where experience can
be gained within six months of commencing a role it should not be considered as
essential experience at the point of recruitment" [1]. On strengths, it is
"recommended that the specific strengths being assessed are not explicitly
referenced in recruitment materials such as adverts, candidate information
packs and interview invitation letters. A well written person specification
will indicate requirements that relate to the chosen strengths without being so
explicit that candidates prepare their responses prior to the interview stage"
[1]. Note the asymmetry this creates: behaviours are advertised and prepared
for; strengths deliberately are not.

The Government Statistical Service applies a departmental cap on the combined
total: "The number of GSG competencies and CS behaviours required will depend on
the requirements for the post(s) and can be decided by Recruiting Managers. The
recommemdation is that the mix of competencies and behaviours does not exceed 6
in total" [5]. The GSS guidance also fixes level expectations by grade — for
example that Statistical Officer candidates should be "demonstrating the
potential to achieve Level 1 of the GSG Competency Framework within their first
year; and Level 2 of the Civil Service behaviours", while higher grades are
assessed at GSG Level 2 and Civil Service behaviour Level 3 [5] — and records a
failure threshold expressed as falling "short by scoring one '3'" against the
required standard [5].

HEE frames the same decision as a set of planning questions to answer before
writing anything: how many criteria are being assessed; how many questions,
lead and probes, are required to assess the applicant against those criteria
within the time allocated; what probe questions are required; and how existing
questions need adapting to the level and complexity of the role [7].

## 2. Writing the questions

The Cabinet Office instruction on question design is short and load-bearing:
"Interview questions should be designed to allow the candidate ample
opportunity to provide the required level of evidence to demonstrate their
capability for the role" [1]. The same guidance directs vacancy holders needing
help drafting assessment materials to the Cabinet Office Occupational
Psychology Team, and notes that this is a chargeable bespoke service best
engaged during early planning [1].

The number and anchoring of questions is documented on the delivery side. DWP
Digital's published account of its standard Civil Service interview format
records a two-or-three-person panel with "diverse representation", an unmarked
warm-up question, and then "four to six questions on your skills, knowledge and
experience, based on the topics listed under 'essential criteria' in the
advert", with follow-up questions used "to delve deeper into your answers" and
scoring done on the essential criteria [6]. The advertised criteria are
therefore the contract: the question set is bounded by what was published.

HEE specifies the internal structure. Interview templates or questioning
frameworks "should contain a set of standardised lead questions and associated
probe questions for each of the criteria assessed… These questions should be
aligned to the person specification" [7]. Where an organisation maintains a
question bank, it "should be designed to be used flexibly by recruiting
managers developing interviews for roles of different levels and complexity",
and "should contain questions structured as follows; a lead question followed
by a series of possible relevant probe questions, as well as a set of defined
behavioural indicators" [7]. HEE further recommends that "stakeholders,
including staff and service users, are consulted to develop and refine
interview questions", noting that service users in particular "may be able to
provide valuable insight into what is important in terms of demonstration of
skills or values as behaviours in practice" [7].

HEE also documents the two question types available and what each buys you.
Situational interviews ask candidates "to imagine a set of circumstances and
then indicate how they would respond", so questions are future-oriented ("what
would you do if…"); behavioural interviews "use past-oriented questions"
("tell me about a time when…") on the premise "that past behaviour is the best
predictor for future performance in similar situations". A single interview may
combine both [7]. What differentiates a values-based interview is not the lead
question but the probes: "it is the probing questions, designed as part of a
VBI, that are utilised to elicit detailed evidence in relation to learning and
reflection", focusing "on how and why an applicant makes particular choices in
the workplace" [7].

## 3. The worked unit: lead question, probes, indicators

HEE publishes two complete worked templates. The first, for a Collaboration
criterion mapped to the NHS Constitution value *Working together for patients*,
defines the criterion as "Works effectively with others, showing consideration
to their needs and perspectives. Recognises the benefits that others can bring
to a situation and seeks to learn from others"; sets the lead question
"Describe a situation where it was important for you to establish/develop an
effective working relationship with someone…"; lists four probes ("Why was this
relationship significant?", "What did you learn from this experience?", "What
was the outcome?", "How did you react/feel about developing this
relationship?"); and pairs positive indicators (considers the
needs/perspectives of others; articulates the benefits of working together;
shows willingness to cooperate; has strategies for working effectively
alongside others, for example through compromise) with negative indicators (is
dismissive of the views/perspectives of others; makes inappropriate assumptions
about others; demonstrates a negative attitude towards working with others) [7].

The second template, for Integrity mapped to *Commitment to quality of care*,
follows the identical shape: lead question "Tell me about a time when you had
to address a difficult situation with a colleague…"; probes on why it mattered,
the outcome, how the candidate felt, and how they perceived the colleague's
reaction; positive indicators (proactive in identifying and addressing issues;
sensitive and tactful when raising difficult issues; aware of the risks
associated with inaction) against negative indicators (defers responsibility to
others; jumps to conclusions; seeks to blame others) [7].

A university HR training deck published by the University of Nottingham
Recruitment Services shows the same unit in a non-NHS setting, alongside a
five-step STARR model (Situation, Task, Action, Result, Reflection) used to
generate the probe set. Its worked example pairs the lead question "Tell me
about an occasion when you needed to gain buy-in to a new process" with four
follow-up probes: "Who did you need to influence?", "How did you approach
this?", "What were the challenges and how did you overcome these?", "What was
the outcome?" [22]. The deck also publishes a probe library organised by STARR
step — task probes ("What was your role in the situation?", "Specifically what
were you asked to do?"), action probes ("What did you actually do to be
successful?", "What responsibility did you take?", "Who did you engage /
consult with?", "What prompted you to take that action?"), result probes ("What
did you achieve?", "How did you know that your approach was successful?") and
reflection probes ("Would you approach it different next time?") — and
describes a *question funnel*: start with an open question, narrow to a
specific area, use probes for specific details, then closed questions to
clarify understanding before moving to a new area [22]. Reflection is presented
as an optional step, used "if the candidate has described something that didn't
achieve its objectives" or if the answer has not yet produced enough evidence
[22].

## 4. From indicators to a rubric

The College of Policing draws the line between a framework indicator and a
usable rubric. In an interview, "where questions are designed to elicit
evidence of the competency or value, assessors can use the indicators to help
them decide how well the candidate has demonstrated evidence of the competency
at the appropriate level, in the context of the role being applied for" [8].
For anything more complex — a role play, a written exercise — "the indicators
in the CVF will not be sufficient on their own. The exercise designers will
need to identify what good demonstration of the competency looks like in the
context of the exercise" [8].

The documented method for producing that guidance is a pilot: "This is usually
done by having a sample of good performers already in the role complete the
exercise as part of a trial or pilot, and using their responses to help
generate guidance for assessors" [8]. And the documented limit on it is
explicit: "There will often be several different ways a candidate could
demonstrate the competency in any given scenario — this is about creating
supportive guidance for assessors, not a 'model answer'" [8].

The College publishes a fictional worked example: for the competency
*We are Emotionally Aware* at level 2, framework behaviours such as "I consider
the perspectives of people from a wide range of backgrounds before taking
action" are translated into exercise-specific assessor guidance such as
"Candidate specifically asks the role actor to challenge their thinking" and
"Candidate communicates sensitively and warmly but changes style to challenge
role actor's inappropriate behaviour immediately and firmly when required" [8].
The guidance summarises the design step as: "Identifying what good performance
looks like (and, ideally, what poor performance looks like) whether in the form
of brief bullet points for an interview or detailed assessor guidance, is an
essential step. Where there are multiple assessors, it is even more critical to
ensure that everyone has the same shared understanding of what is being sought"
[8].

## 5. Published scales you can use as a rubric spine

Three families of published scale are available to a designer.

**The central Civil Service scales.** Cabinet Office guidance states that
"Experience, technical skills and behaviours should be scored using the 7 point
scale. Strengths should be scored using the 4 point scale", with a 3-point
A/B/C rating available for high-volume sift: "A - candidate meets all the
criteria; B - borderline/candidate meets some of the criteria; C - candidate
does not meet the criteria" [1]. The seven descriptor labels are published
departmentally by COPFS as "1 Not Demonstrated, 2 Minimal Demonstration, 3
Moderate Demonstration, 4 Acceptable Demonstration, 5 Good Demonstration, 6
Strong Demonstration, 7 Outstanding Demonstration" [9].

**The College of Policing A–D scale.** Published in full with descriptors: A =
"Substantial positive evidence of the competency observed with regard to the
quality and quantity of the evidence and few or no areas for development
observed"; B = "Positive evidence of the competency observed and some areas for
development but, on balance, more positive evidence"; C = "Some positive
evidence of the competency observed but, on balance, more areas for
development"; D = "Substantial areas for development identified and little or no
positive evidence of the competency observed" [8]. The guidance adds the design
rule that "the rating scale must have descriptors alongside each score and these
should be clear and unambiguous" [8].

**EPSO anchors.** Rather than a numeric scale, EPSO publishes the observable
elements a marker considers. For the current written tests the five published
anchors are: structures written communication with a logical flow of ideas;
writes concisely, avoiding unnecessary words and sentences; presents subject
matter in a clear and understandable way; adapts and tailors writing to suit
the intended audience and purpose; uses the information provided effectively to
address the assignment [12]. The competency framework leaflet publishes the
anchors for all eight general competencies [11].

The behaviour level descriptors themselves — the Civil Service's six levels
across nine behaviours — supply the raw material for indicators when the role
is a Civil Service one [10].

## 6. Running it: panels, calibration, reconciliation, records

**Panel composition and training.** Cabinet Office guidance: "It is recommended
that all interview panels should be diverse, and all panellists should have
undertaken training on interview skills prior to sitting on an interview panel"
[1]. ACAS advises that "each interview should be carried out by more than 1
person to reduce the risk of discriminating" [21]. HEE agrees on the
reliability grounds: "Where resources allow, it is good practice to use
multiple interviewers as this helps to minimise bias and enhance fairness", and
adds that interviewers should be trained specifically in the interviewing
approach being used, with training "regularly refreshed" [7].

**Calibration before live candidates.** The College of Policing: "Steps should
be taken to calibrate multiple assessors' ratings, for example, by conducting a
mock assessment or having a discussion about expected levels of performance at
each grade, in advance of the first live candidate assessment. This is often
referred to as benchmarking or frame of reference training" [8]. Its minimum
assessor briefing covers understanding the role being assessed for, a shared
understanding of the criteria, familiarisation with the interview questions or
exercise materials, and familiarisation with the competencies and values [8].
HEE requires interviewers, after formal training, to familiarise themselves
with the standardised documentation, "in particular the behavioural indicators
and key criteria differentiating effective and ineffective performance", and
with the scoring framework [7].

**Independent scoring, then arithmetic reconciliation.** The Cabinet Office
sequence is precise: "Panel members should observe, record, classify, evaluate
and score each question independently, then calculate an overall score before
jointly agreeing a merit list using the arithmetic method. Variances in scoring
amongst panel members can be discussed; it may be useful to discuss evidence
underlying scores when these variances are particularly marked. Panel members
may decide to change their score(s) or stand by them, but ultimately individual
panel scores should be reconciled by taking an arithmetic average which will
serve as the final scores for the candidate" [1]. The College of Policing
describes the same discipline through ORCE, which "should be used with paired
assessment or in groups with assessors first carrying out each stage
independently and then meeting to discuss their grades and evidence and then
agreeing on a final grade" [8]; classification of behaviours into competencies
is done "after the candidate has finished the assessment" [8]. HEE's FORCE adds
Familiarise, and defines Observe as "not to make judgements, but to be aware of
what the applicant is saying or doing" [7].

**Records.** The College of Policing requires "Full and accurate records of
scores for each candidate… throughout the assessment process so that there is a
final verified record to be used in decision making and also forming an audit
trail" [8], and cautions specifically that in values-based interviewing
assessors should "not reveal sensitive personal information in scoring sheets
or feedback reports" [8]. HEE advises allowing time immediately after the
interview to finalise notes, "as leaving this until later can result in
distorted information", and requires that candidates be told in advance if
notes will be taken, with consent obtained before any recording [7].

## 7. Validating the design

HEE requires that "planning for monitoring, reviewing and validating the
effectiveness of a structured interview should form part of the initial project
planning phase", with an initial review examining "whether the questions are
reflective of aspects of the role; are the indicators for competencies and
values clearly defined for assessment; are interviewers operating in accordance
with best practice principles"; whether the questions and probes "provided
sufficient opportunity for applicants to demonstrate the intended competencies
or values"; and whether there was enough time to ask all planned questions.
Questions "may also need to be refreshed periodically" [7]. Evaluation "should
be rigorous and scientific as far as possible", potentially including
qualitative content analysis, statistical analysis, applicant and assessor
reactions, and validation studies matching interview outcomes to work
performance [7].

The College of Policing sets the adverse-impact half: "It is important to
monitor selection processes for adverse impact on under-represented groups.
This will require collecting monitoring data, such as age, gender or ethnicity…
All data should be collected, used and stored in line with data protection
laws. Where adverse impact is detected, further analysis should be conducted to
verify if the difference is statistically significant and the possible reasons
for this" [8]. It defines the test and immediately qualifies it: "Commonly the
80 per cent or four-fifths rule is used… If the minority group pass rate is
less than 80 per cent or four-fifths of the majority group pass rate, the
method is said to cause adverse impact… Although the four fifths rule is a
useful guide for evaluation and is commonly used in practice, **it does not
have a legal footing**" [8].

Finally, the Cabinet Office records a conditional diversity finding on the
Success Profiles blended interview: "Diversity outcomes were analysed, for all
groups, to monitor impact on protected characteristics… Analysis indicated
that, on the whole, there are no adverse impacts indicated with outcomes based
on protected characteristics - as long as selection methods are delivered in a
structured and consistent way, and training is provided to and/or official
guidance is followed by those who are involved in delivering and
assessing/making selection decisions" [1].

# Question Bank

## Scoping — deciding what to assess

1. How many criteria are being assessed? *(sourced: Health Education England with Work Psychology Group)* [7]
2. How many questions, lead and probes, are required to effectively assess the applicant against the criteria within the time allocated for the interview? *(sourced: Health Education England with Work Psychology Group)* [7]
3. How do existing interview questions need adapting to meet the level and complexity of the role? *(sourced: Health Education England with Work Psychology Group)* [7]
4. Examine the role profile provided, including the main responsibilities, person specification and the expectations and behaviours for the role: what competencies and behaviours do you want to assess at interview? Choose 2 areas to assess. *(sourced: University of Nottingham Recruitment Services)* [22]
5. Take a live vacancy and list every behaviour and technical criterion you are tempted to assess. Now cut the list to four behaviours, and write one sentence per cut criterion explaining why the panel can live without testing it. *(composed)*
6. For each essential criterion you have kept, state which assessment stage will carry it — sift, test, interview or presentation — so that no criterion is assessed twice by accident and none is left unassessed. *(composed)*
7. Apply the six-month test to your essential experience requirements: for each one, could a capable appointee acquire it within six months in post? If yes, move it to desirable. *(composed)*
8. Write the person specification wording for a strength you intend to assess, without naming the strength anywhere in the advert or candidate pack. *(composed)*

## Writing lead questions

1. Write an interview question for each of your chosen areas that will elicit evidence of the candidate's ability to deliver the main responsibilities, and of how the candidate will meet the role requirements through their skills and behaviours. *(sourced: University of Nottingham Recruitment Services)* [22]
2. For one criterion, write the same lead question twice — once as a past-oriented behavioural question and once as a future-oriented situational question — and note in one line what each version would let you score that the other would not. *(composed)*
3. Take an advertised essential criterion verbatim and write a lead question whose wording a candidate could trace back to it without guessing. *(composed)*
4. Draft a four-to-six question set for a real vacancy and map each question to exactly one advertised essential criterion. Any question that maps to none, or to three, is redesigned. *(composed)*
5. Rewrite a lead question that currently contains two demands — for example "a time you led a team through change and delivered to deadline" — as two separate questions, or choose which single demand you are actually scoring. *(composed)*
6. Draft the same criterion's lead question at two role levels, and write the differing performance expectation for each, following the published pattern of one indicator producing different expected behaviour at two ranks. *(composed)*

## Designing probe sets

1. What probe questions are required to support the lead questions? *(sourced: Health Education England with Work Psychology Group)* [7]
2. For one lead question, write three probes that ask *why* and *how* the candidate made particular choices, rather than what happened next. *(composed)*
3. Build a probe set on the funnel pattern: one open question, one narrowing question, three detail probes using how, what, who, when, where or why, and one closed question to confirm your understanding. *(composed)*
4. Write one reflection probe per criterion for use only when an answer describes something that did not achieve its objective, and agree with the panel when it may be deployed. *(composed)*

## Writing indicators and rubrics

1. How will you measure the candidate responses — what does good look like? *(sourced: University of Nottingham Recruitment Services)* [22]
2. Identify what good demonstration of the competency looks like in the context of the exercise, and ideally what poor performance looks like, in the form of brief bullet points for an interview or detailed assessor guidance. *(sourced: College of Policing)* [8]
3. Have a sample of good performers already in the role complete the exercise as part of a trial or pilot, and use their responses to help generate guidance for assessors. *(sourced: College of Policing)* [8]
4. For one criterion, write four positive and three negative behavioural indicators, following the paired positive/negative pattern in the published NHS templates. *(composed)*
5. Take a framework indicator written in the first person and translate it into two exercise-specific observations a panel member could tick during your actual interview. *(composed)*
6. Check your draft rubric against the "not a model answer" test: for each criterion, name two genuinely different answers that would both score well. If you cannot, your rubric is a model answer and needs widening. *(composed)*
7. Choose your rating scale and write a descriptor beside every point on it, then have a panel member who did not write them explain each point back to you in their own words. *(composed)*
8. Map your rubric to a published scale spine — the 7-point Civil Service scale, the A–D policing scale, or an anchor list — and state the minimum score required per criterion before any candidate is seen. *(composed)*
9. Write the anchors for one competency as observable elements rather than traits, in the style of a published anchor list. *(composed)*

## Calibrating and quality-assuring the set

1. Are the questions reflective of aspects of the role, and are the indicators for competencies and values clearly defined for assessment? *(sourced: Health Education England with Work Psychology Group)* [7]
2. Did the interview questions and follow-up probing questions provide sufficient opportunity for applicants to demonstrate the intended competencies or values? *(sourced: Health Education England with Work Psychology Group)* [7]
3. Was there enough time during the interview for interviewers to ask all of the questions expected? *(sourced: Health Education England with Work Psychology Group)* [7]
4. Are interviewers operating in accordance with best practice principles? *(sourced: Health Education England with Work Psychology Group)* [7]
5. Run a frame-of-reference session: score the same mock answer independently as a panel, then compare and discuss the evidence behind any divergence before the first live candidate. *(composed)*
6. Timebox your draft: read the full set aloud with plausible answers and probes, and cut questions until the interview fits its scheduled length with slack. *(composed)*

## Legal, records and automated-tool checks

1. What demographic groups was the model trained on? *(sourced: Information Commissioner's Office)* [19]
2. Has any underlying bias been detected, or may any emerge? *(sourced: Information Commissioner's Office)* [19]
3. What algorithmic fairness testing has been conducted? *(sourced: Information Commissioner's Office)* [19]
4. Read every question in your set and strike any that would elicit information about a protected characteristic without a lawful basis — in particular any health or disability enquiry made before offer or shortlisting. *(composed)*
5. For each stage of your process, write down how a candidate would request an adjustment, who decides, and what the alternative assessment route is. *(composed)*
6. Draft the note-taking standard your panel will follow, then reread your own last set of interview notes as if they had been disclosed under a subject access request. *(composed)*
7. If any stage of your process is automated, write down for each candidate group whether a human with authority to overturn the outcome actually reviews it — and how you would evidence that. *(composed)*

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact.** It assembles the
cited evidence above into a working order of operations.

## A nine-step design workflow

1. **Do the job analysis first.** Both the Cabinet Office and HEE put role
   analysis before criterion selection, and the Cabinet Office explicitly says
   the Success Profile Guides "do not replace the requirement for job analysis"
   [1] [7]. Skipping it means you are assessing a job title, not a job.
2. **Select and cap the criteria.** Four behaviours maximum [1] [2] [3] [4];
   six criteria in total if you are also carrying technical or professional
   competencies [5]. Apply the six-month test to experience requirements [1].
3. **Publish the criteria in the advert.** They become the contract your
   question set must honour [6], and HEE requires candidates to be aware of
   what they will be assessed against [7]. Keep strengths out of the advert if
   your framework treats them as unrehearsed [1].
4. **Decide the question type per criterion.** Past-oriented behavioural for
   things the candidate has plausibly done; situational for things the role
   will require that few candidates will have faced [7].
5. **Write one lead question per criterion**, then the probe set, then the
   indicators — in that order [7]. Writing indicators last is a common
   sequencing error: if you cannot write the indicators, the lead question is
   probably too vague.
6. **Convert indicators into exercise-specific assessor guidance.** Pilot with
   good performers if you can [8]. Where you cannot run a pilot, at minimum
   have two experienced post-holders answer the question and mine their
   answers — and say in your documentation that this was a substitute for a
   pilot, not a pilot.
7. **Choose the scale and write descriptors for every point** [8], and set the
   minimum score per criterion and any overall benchmark **before** the first
   interview [1] [9].
8. **Assemble and rehearse the panel.** Diverse, trained, briefed on the
   materials, calibrated on a mock answer [1] [7] [8].
9. **Plan the evaluation before you run the campaign** — monitoring data,
   adverse-impact analysis, a review of whether the questions did their job
   [7] [8].

## Worked example: turning a behaviour into a scorable unit

Suppose the advertised criterion is a collaboration behaviour. The published
templates give the shape [7]:

- **Criterion definition** — one or two sentences, lifted from the framework
  and contextualised to the role's level.
- **Lead question** — one sentence, one demand, open. HEE's is "Describe a
  situation where it was important for you to establish/develop an effective
  working relationship with someone…" [7].
- **Probes** — four is the published number in both HEE templates [7], and
  four in the Nottingham worked example [22]. Cover significance, action,
  outcome and learning.
- **Positive indicators** — three to five, each an observable thing the
  candidate says or describes doing.
- **Negative indicators** — three to four, and genuinely the opposite of the
  positives rather than their absence. HEE's negatives ("is dismissive of the
  views/perspectives of others") describe behaviour, not silence [7].
- **Exercise-specific guidance** — for an interview, brief bullets; for a role
  play or written exercise, the fuller assessor guidance the College of
  Policing describes [8].

Run this before the pack goes to the panel. It restates cited requirements as
checks; the citation beside each is what the check enforces.

- [ ] Every question maps to exactly one advertised essential criterion [6].
- [ ] No more than four behaviours, and no more than six criteria in total,

- [ ] Every lead question has a written probe set [7].
- [ ] Every criterion has positive **and** negative indicators [7].
- [ ] The rating scale has a descriptor at every point [8].
- [ ] The minimum score and any benchmark are written down and dated before

- [ ] The adjustment route and its owner are documented for every stage

- [ ] The panel is diverse, trained and calibrated [1] [7] [8].
- [ ] Scoring is independent first, reconciled arithmetically second, and the

- [ ] A note-taking standard and a retention position exist in writing [7]

- [ ] Monitoring data collection and an adverse-impact review are planned

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — cited.** This section records what the published guidance
tells assessors to *do* with a well-designed question set, which is also the
strongest argument for designing one.

The structured interview earns its place on evidence. HEE summarises the
literature: "Research consistently shows that interviews that are structured,
ask relevant and standardised questions based on a thorough role analysis,
utilise a panel of interviewers trained in best practice interview techniques
and use a validated scoring criteria achieve the highest levels of validity"
[7]. The College of Policing states the same hierarchy and its downside:
"Highly structured interviews have been shown to be the most valid, but can be
seen as rigid. In practice, many organisations use a semi-structured interview
in which candidates are asked the same questions but assessors have freedom to
ask different follow-up questions and probe the candidate's responses."
Unstructured interviews, "where the interviewer asks different questions of
each candidate and where the interview is not timed and controlled, have very
poor validity" [8]. The current meta-analytic picture supports the ranking
while cautioning against complacency: Sackett and colleagues report structured
interviews as "the predictor with the highest mean validity" at a mean of .42,
but with an 80% credibility interval running from .18 to .66 — "the validity of
structured interviews should really be viewed as '.42, plus or minus .24'"
[23]. Design quality, in other words, is where that variance lives.

Assessors are instructed to work the evidence, not the impression. The College
of Policing's ORCE model has assessors observe and listen to everything the
candidate does and says, "take contemporaneous notes, as verbatim as possible,
whilst observing", classify the observed behaviours into competencies and
values *after* the candidate has finished, and only then "decide how well the
candidate demonstrated the competencies and values, and award a rating" [8].
Its appendix catalogues the biases the sequence is designed to defeat —
including halo and horns, primacy and recency, contrast, and central-tendency,
leniency and severity errors in scale use, for which the stated remedy is that
"assessors should be trained in how any rating scales are used and should be
encouraged to use the full length of the scale" [8]. The Nottingham deck
catalogues the same family for panel training — like-me effect, personal
liking, non-verbal bias, halo, horns, primacy/recency, contrast, confirmation
bias — and identifies deliberation and the appointment decision as the key
area where bias enters [22].

Decision rules are documented. The Nottingham deck instructs panels to "compare
candidate responses / performance against the selection criteria — do not
compare candidates to other candidates" [22]; the College of Policing requires
that "all decisions should be evidence based" and based on "information
collected only from a well-designed, valid, objective and standardised
assessment process", unless candidates were told in advance that other
information may be used [8]. The Cabinet Office fixes the merit-list mechanic
at an arithmetic average of independently reached scores [1].

Records carry weight beyond the campaign. The Nottingham deck sets a
note-taking standard — notes should be "a summary of what the candidate said",
sustained, "complete but succinct", done openly, clear and legible, and
compliant with data protection — because notes are needed "for providing
feedback", for "an objective review to eliminate bias", as "evidence to justify
decisions", and possibly "for appeals"; it states that "accurate note taking is
as important as questioning and listening" [22]. It records that candidates can
make a subject access request for information held about them, that Freedom of
Information duties apply to public authorities, and that an Employment Tribunal
will look for the job description, person specification, advert, application
forms, references, short-listing decisions, "Interview Notes (especially
Chair's notes)" and email exchanges — with the chair listed as the person who
"represents the panel at a Tribunal" [22]. The deck also publishes a retention
schedule, including interview notes for unsuccessful applicants retained six
months from interview date and then destroyed [22]. Retention periods are
organisation-specific; the principle that one must exist is not.

Feedback closes the loop. HEE requires feedback to "describe the applicant's
performance based on the behavioural indicators" [7]; the College of Policing
requires it to be "constructive, highlighting what was done well and what can be
done to improve" and "based on their actual performances" [8]; Nottingham warns
panels not to compare with other candidates' performance and to "refer to role
criteria" [22]. A rubric written to the standard above is what makes defensible
feedback cheap to produce.

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact.** Each pitfall is
tied to the documented requirement it violates.

- **Assessing everything.** Six behaviours plus three technical criteria plus
  strengths cannot be tested to depth in one interview; the four-behaviour cap
  exists precisely so the panel "can sufficiently test the criteria" [1]. The
  practical symptom is a 45-minute interview with nine questions and no probes.
- **Questions that drifted from the advert.** If the panel asks about something
  not in the advertised essential criteria, candidates could not have prepared
  for it and the scoring is hard to defend against the published contract [6].
- **Double-barrelled lead questions.** A question demanding leadership *and*
  delivery under pressure produces an answer you must split before you can
  score it — and different panel members will split it differently, which is
  exactly what independent-then-reconcile scoring cannot absorb [1].
- **Indicators written after the interviews.** Both HEE and the College of
  Policing put indicator definition in the *preparation* phase [7] [8].
  Indicators reverse-engineered from what candidates actually said are a record
  of the panel's impressions wearing a rubric's costume.
- **A rubric that is secretly a model answer.** The College of Policing warns
  that there are usually several ways to demonstrate a competency [8]. A rubric
  listing one route penalises the candidate who took another and rewards the
  one who guessed your script.
- **Negative indicators that are just absences.** "Does not mention
  collaboration" is not evidence of a negative behaviour; the published
  negative indicators describe things a candidate does, such as being
  "dismissive of the views/perspectives of others" [7]. Absence of evidence
  belongs in the scale, not the indicator list.
- **A scale without descriptors, or a scale nobody agreed on.** The requirement
  that "the rating scale must have descriptors alongside each score and these
  should be clear and unambiguous" exists because undescribed scales collapse
  into personal calibration [8] — and then into central tendency or leniency
  [8].
- **Scoring by conversation.** Discussing scores before writing them down
  inverts the documented sequence and lets the most senior or most confident
  voice set the anchor. The instruction is to score independently, then
  reconcile arithmetically [1] [8].
- **Comparing candidates to each other.** Documented practice is to compare
  each candidate to the criteria [22]; ranking by relative impression
  reintroduces contrast bias, one of the errors ORCE is built to suppress [8].
- **Notes as verdicts.** "Weak", "not a fit", "nervous" are impressions.
  Contemporaneous, close-to-verbatim behavioural notes are what the models
  require [8] [22] — and what a subject access request or Tribunal will
  surface [22].
- **Adjustments treated as an exception process.** HEE and the Cabinet Office
  both fold accessibility into design — including checking that video
  interview technology "is available, accessible and compatible for all panel
  members and candidates" and considering reasonable adjustments arising from
  technology access or other conditions [1]. Designing the adjustment route in
  advance is cheaper than improvising it on the day.
- **Treating "no adverse impact was indicated" as a property of the
  framework.** It is a conditional finding, conditional on structured,
  consistent delivery by trained assessors [1]. An unstructured interview
  running under a Success Profiles banner inherits none of it.
- **Treating a four-fifths pass as legal cover.** The rule "does not have a
  legal footing" in the UK [8]; passing it is not a defence, and failing it is
  a trigger for statistical analysis, not an automatic finding of
  discrimination [8].
- **Assuming a human in the loop is meaningful involvement.** The ICO's finding
  is that hiring managers who prioritise green-rated candidates and glance at
  red ones before rejecting them have "rubber-stamped" those rejections, which
  "constitutes solely ADM" [17].

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.** Drills for an interviewer, panel chair or assessment
designer.

1. **Criterion cull (30 minutes).** Take a real vacancy you are recruiting.
   List every criterion you would like to assess. Cut to four behaviours [1]
   and no more than six criteria in total [5]. Write one line per cut
   explaining where else, if anywhere, that criterion is assessed. If the
   answer is "nowhere", decide honestly whether it was ever essential.
2. **Build one complete unit (45 minutes).** For a single criterion, produce
   the full HEE template: definition, lead question, four probes, four positive
   and three negative indicators [7]. Then write the two exercise-specific
   bullets a panel member would actually tick [8].
3. **The two-good-answers test (15 minutes).** For each criterion in your set,
   write two genuinely different answers that should both score highly. If you
   cannot, widen the indicators — you have written a model answer, which the
   published guidance rejects [8].
4. **Substitute pilot (60 minutes).** Ask two strong current post-holders your
   draft lead question and probes. Note every behaviour they produced that your
   indicators do not cover, and add them. This approximates the good-performer
   trial the College of Policing describes [8]; record in your documentation
   that it was an approximation.
5. **Frame-of-reference session (60 minutes, whole panel).** Score the same
   recorded or role-played answer independently against your scale. Reveal
   scores simultaneously. Discuss only the evidence behind divergences, then
   re-score. Repeat until spread is one scale point or less [8].
6. **Reverse the tribunal (30 minutes).** Take your last completed campaign's
   pack and assemble what a Tribunal would ask for: job description, person
   specification, advert, applications, shortlisting decisions, interview notes
   — especially the chair's — and emails [22]. Anything missing or
   embarrassing is a design task, not a filing task.
7. **Timebox rehearsal (30 minutes).** Read the whole set aloud with a
   colleague giving plausible two-minute answers, and probe as you would live.
   If you overrun the scheduled slot, cut questions rather than probes: the
   probes are where the differentiating evidence comes from [7].
8. **Adverse-impact dry run (45 minutes).** For a past campaign, compute pass
   rates by the monitoring categories you hold, and apply the four-fifths
   comparison [8]. Whatever the result, write the sentence you would say to a
   sceptical stakeholder about what the number does and does not mean —
   including that it has no UK legal footing [8].
9. **Automation audit (30 minutes).** For each automated or semi-automated
   stage, answer the ICO's procurement questions [19] and the meaningful
   human involvement test: could your reviewer alter the outcome, and do they
   in practice, for low-scoring candidates as well as high [17]?

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — cited.** This section covers the duties that bear on
*design*; **fairness-bias-legal** treats the law in full.

**Question content and protected characteristics.** ACAS is direct: employers
must not discriminate against an applicant because of any of the protected
characteristics, and in recruitment discrimination "could happen through: the
arrangements you make for deciding who to offer the job; the terms under which
you offer the job; not offering someone the job" [20]. A question set is one of
those arrangements. Questions about protected characteristics are permissible
only in defined circumstances — equality and diversity monitoring, making
reasonable adjustments, positive action, a disability exception, an
occupational requirement, or a statutory age requirement — and monitoring data
must be kept separate, with "anyone involved in interviewing or deciding who to
hire" having no access to it [20].

**Health and disability enquiries before offer.** Equality Act 2010 s.60
provides that a person to whom an application for work is made "must not ask
about the health of the applicant… before offering work to B, or… before
including B in a pool of applicants from whom A intends… to select a person to
whom to offer work" [13]. Statutory exceptions include asking so far as
necessary for "establishing whether B will be able to comply with a requirement
to undergo an assessment or establishing whether a duty to make reasonable
adjustments is or will be imposed" [13]. ACAS operationalises this: "You must
not ask a job applicant about their health or any disability at any stage in
the application or interview process, unless an exception applies", the
exceptions being adjustments to the assessment process, whether the person
could do the essential parts of the role after adjustments, confirming
disability for the disability exception, the monitoring form, and positive
action [20]. This is a **design** constraint, not merely a delivery one: a
question set containing a fitness or attendance question is unlawful before
anyone asks it.

**Adjustments.** Equality Act 2010 s.20 sets the three requirements of the duty
to make reasonable adjustments — for a provision, criterion or practice, for a
physical feature, and for auxiliary aids — each triggered where a disabled
person is put at a substantial disadvantage, and expressly including steps to
provide information "in an accessible format" [14]. ACAS confirms the duty
"can be relevant at any stage of the recruitment process" and gives examples
including alternative application formats and wheelchair-accessible interview
rooms; where someone has not asked, "you can ask them if reasonable adjustments
might be needed" [20]. Cabinet Office guidance builds the same consideration
into video-interview design, requiring checks that the technology "is
available, accessible and compatible for all panel members and candidates
taking part", with the panel considering adjustments "arising either from
access to technology or a range of other conditions" [1].

**Public sector equality duty.** Equality Act 2010 s.149 requires a public
authority, in the exercise of its functions, to have due regard to the need to
eliminate discrimination, harassment and victimisation; to advance equality of
opportunity between those who share a relevant protected characteristic and
those who do not; and to foster good relations — with sub-section (2) extending
the duty to non-public bodies exercising public functions [15]. Designing an
assessment is the exercise of a function. The Nottingham deck lists PSED
awareness and the handling of adjustment requests among the chair's explicit
responsibilities [22].

**Candidate records.** Interview notes and scores are personal data.
Contemporaneous notes are required by the assessment models [8], subject to
access rights [22], and — in public authorities — sit alongside Freedom of
Information duties [22]. HEE requires candidates to be told in advance that
notes will be taken and consent to be obtained before any recording [7]; the
College of Policing requires monitoring data to be "collected, used and stored
in line with data protection laws" [8] and warns against recording sensitive
personal information disclosed in values-based interviews in scoring sheets or
feedback reports [8].

**Automated tools in the design.** The ICO's *Recruitment rewired* report,
based on evidence from over 30 employers engaged between March 2025 and January
2026, finds that "many employers engaging in automated recruitment are likely
relying on solely automated decisions", using "solely automated systems without
meaningful human involvement" for decisions with legal or similarly significant
effects — bringing them within the UK GDPR ADM provisions and requiring "a
greater range of safeguards… than our evidence suggests are currently in place"
[16]. Article 22A of the UK GDPR, as introduced by the DUAA, provides that "a
decision is based solely on automated processing if there is no meaningful
human involvement in the taking of the decision"; the critical question is
whether a human "can exercise real influence over decision before it is applied
and has the authority, discretion and competence to alter it", or is "simply
directly applying the outcome of an automated system" [17]. The ICO's worked
case study — a manager who prioritises green-scored candidates and takes "only
a glance at 'red' candidates before rejecting them" — concludes that the
manager "has 'rubber-stamped' the 'red' candidates' rejections", constituting
solely automated decision-making within article 22 [17].

Transparency obligations bite at three points in time: when information is
first collected, when a person makes a subject access request, and when the ADM
itself occurs [18]. Employers must provide "meaningful information about the
logic involved and the likely consequences", which "does not need to be complex
or include material that could allow candidates to 'game the system'" —
appropriate content being how the tool is used to make decisions, how accurate
it is, and what safeguards are in place [18]. The article 22C safeguards give a
person the right to be informed about decisions made about them, the
opportunity to make representations, the opportunity to obtain human
intervention, and the ability to contest a decision [18]. On fairness, the ICO
states that organisations "are responsible for the algorithms and tools they
use… even if they didn't build the software", and identifies as good practice
conducting trials to limit bias and requesting from developers information on
the demographic groups a model was trained on, whether underlying bias has been
detected or may emerge, and any algorithmic fairness testing conducted [19]. It
also records that employers typically provided reasonable adjustments — often a
phone interview — for people unable to access automated methods [19].

# Risks & Caveats

- **The College of Policing guidance predates the 2024 CVF.** *Values-based
  recruitment and selection* was published in 2018 [8], before the 2024
  Competency and Values Framework abolished the 2016 clusters and renamed
  competencies. Only its framework-agnostic process content — rubric
  construction, rating scales, ORCE, assessor training, adverse-impact
  monitoring — is used here. Its example competency names are 2016 names and
  should not be treated as current CVF wording.
- **The Nottingham deck is illustrative, not statutory.** It is a University of
  Nottingham Recruitment Services training deck last modified in 2019 [22].
  Its worked probes, note-taking standard, chair duties and Tribunal-evidence
  list are documented university practice and are cited as such; the retention
  periods in particular are organisation-specific and must not be copied as a
  general standard.
- **The four-behaviour cap is a recommendation, not a rule.** The wording is
  "As a guide, it is recommended" [1] [2] [3] [4], and the six-criterion cap is
  a *departmental* GSS recommendation [5], not a Civil-Service-wide one.
- **The GSS guidance is dated October 2019** [5] and departmental; its grade
  and level expectations are specific to statistical professions.
- **The NHS VBR anchor documents are unreissued.** HEE's structured-interview
  guidance [7] remains the fullest published UK design template located, but
  HEE has merged into NHS England and no successor has been reissued; the
  guidance is operative in practice but not freshly current. Its URLs sit on
  the legacy `hee.nhs.uk` domain, a retirement candidate.
- **DWP Digital's account is one department's published practice** [6]. The
  four-to-six question range is documented for that context, not mandated
  centrally.
- **Structured-interview validity is high on average and highly variable.**
  The mean of .42 carries an 80% credibility interval of .18 to .66 [23]; a
  structured format is a necessary but not sufficient condition for a valid
  interview, which is the argument for the design discipline in this document
  rather than a guarantee delivered by it.
- **EPSO's current competition model contains no competency interview.** The
  anchors cited here [11] [12] are cited as published rubric *instruments*;
  readers should not infer that EPSO currently runs a competency interview.
- **ICO obligations are evolving.** *Recruitment rewired* is based on voluntary
  engagement rather than audit or investigation [16], and the underlying
  recruitment and selection guidance was published in draft; article 22A/22C
  wording derives from recent reform. Re-check before relying on specifics.

# Open Questions

- Is the Cabinet Office's "Success Profiles: Interview methodology" document —
  listed in the further-guidance index of the delegated-grade pack [1] —
  publicly retrievable? It is the most likely home of central question-design
  instruction and would move several claims here from departmental
  corroboration to single central authority.
- Likewise "Success Profiles: Scoring case studies" and "Designing Strengths
  Questions", both listed in the same index [1] but not located as public
  documents during research.
- Does any central Civil Service source publish behavioural indicators at
  question level, as distinct from behaviour level descriptors [10]? No such
  publication was located, which is why the worked indicator templates in this
  document are NHS-sourced.
- Has NHS England reissued or replaced the HEE structured-interview design
  guidance [7] under its own branding? None located as at 2026-07-20.
- What proportion of UK employers actually pilot exercises with existing good
  performers, as the College of Policing describes [8]? No adoption data was
  located; this determines whether the practice is a realistic default or an
  aspiration.
- Are there published UK adverse-impact benchmarks for competency interviews
  specifically, as opposed to the general four-fifths convention [8]?
