# Executive Summary

This is the foundation document for the Best Practice category. It gathers what
authoritative UK and EU sources actually advise about preparing for, conducting
and scoring competency-based interviews — for **both** sides of the table. For
candidates, it covers the documented preparation guidance published by GOV.UK,
the National Careers Service, ACAS and named university careers services; how
to build a personal evidence bank mapped to the target framework; and what
published scoring guidance (such as the Civil Service 1–7 rating scale) reveals
about how answers earn marks. For interviewers, it covers the documented best
practice on structured questioning, consistent scoring, note-taking and legal
compliance published by ACAS, the CIPD and the ICO.

The document gives visible weight to private-sector and graduate-scheme
practice: the CIPD's guidance on structured selection applies across sectors,
and Institute of Student Employers (ISE) survey data shows why disciplined
preparation matters in a graduate market that now averages 140 applications per
vacancy. As a Tier-1 foundation it favours breadth over depth; dedicated
deep-dives (evidence bank construction, scoring and marking, mistakes and
recovery, interviewer question design, fairness and legal) are planned as
children of this document.

Confidence is **Medium**: the core claims rest on official and
professional-body sources (GOV.UK, COPFS, ACAS, CIPD, EPSO, legislation), but
the synthesis across sectors involves some inference, one key scoring source is
a single department's published articulation of the Success Profiles scale, and
the ICO recruitment guidance cited was published in draft for consultation.

# Key Takeaways

- UK official guidance tells candidates to prepare **examples mapped to the
  advertised behaviours**: read the job advert, identify the behaviours or
  competencies listed, and prepare a concrete example for each. *(documented)*
- The **STAR structure** (Situation, Task, Action, Result) is the answer
  structure recommended by the National Careers Service and GOV.UK Success
  Profiles guidance, with CARL (Context, Actions, Results, Learning) a
  documented alternative. *(documented)*
- Where scoring guidance is published, competency answers are scored on
  **numeric scales against pre-agreed criteria** — e.g. the Success Profiles
  1–7 scale from "Not Demonstrated" to "Outstanding Demonstration", with 4
  commonly required as a minimum. *(documented)*
- Documented interviewer best practice: **more than one interviewer**, the
  **same questions for every applicant** where possible, decisions scored
  against the job description and person specification, and notes stored
  securely under data-protection law. *(documented)*
- Structured interviews — same questions, same order, responses scored against
  objective criteria — are professional-body best practice because they make
  candidates comparable and reduce bias. *(documented)*
- Graduate-scheme competition is at a record high (140 applications per
  vacancy on average across ISE employers), which is why careers services
  stress preparation and a varied evidence bank rather than improvisation.
  *(documented)*
- The most common failure mode on the candidate side is narrating the team's
  story instead of your own actions; on the interviewer side it is
  inconsistent questions and unscored, impressionistic judgements.
  *(guidance)*
- A reusable **evidence bank** of 6–10 STAR examples, each mapped to two or
  three competencies of the target framework, covers most first interviews and
  is the single highest-leverage preparation asset. *(guidance)*

# Purpose

This document answers two questions. For candidates: *"What do authoritative
sources actually tell me to do to prepare for a competency-based interview,
and how will my answers be scored?"* For interviewers and HR: *"What do ACAS,
the CIPD and the regulators say a well-run, defensible competency interview
looks like?"* It is the entry point to the Best Practice category and the
anchor for its planned deep-dive children.

# Scope

**Covered:** documented preparation guidance from UK official careers sources
and named university careers services; the evidence-bank method of mapping
personal examples to a target framework; published scoring guidance
(Success Profiles-style numeric scales) and what it implies for answer
construction; documented interviewer best practice (structure, consistency,
note-taking, record-keeping); an overview of common pitfalls on both sides;
and the UK/EU legal guardrails at signpost level. UK Civil Service, EU
institution (EPSO), private-sector and graduate-scheme contexts are all in
scope, with the latter two given visible weight.

**Not covered (planned deep-dives):** step-by-step evidence-bank construction
(building-evidence-bank), detailed scoring and marking schemes across
employers (scoring-and-marking), mistake diagnosis and in-interview recovery
(common-mistakes-recovery), question-writing for interviewers
(interviewer-question-design), and full legal analysis
(fairness-bias-legal). Framework definitions themselves belong to the
Competencies, UK Frameworks and EU Frameworks categories; interview formats
(video, panel, assessment centre) belong to Formats; answer techniques in
depth belong to Techniques.

# Definitions

- **Evidence bank** — this knowledge base's term for a prepared, reusable set
  of real examples from work, study, volunteering or life, each written up in
  STAR form and mapped to the competencies of a target framework. The term is
  ours; the underlying practice is documented in the sources cited in Core
  Reference.
- **Structured interview** — an interview in which all candidates are asked a
  predefined set of questions, in the same order, with responses scored
  against pre-agreed criteria (see Assessor Perspective).
- **STAR** — Situation, Task, Action, Result: the answer structure recommended
  by the National Careers Service and Success Profiles guidance.
- **CARL** — Context, Actions, Results, Learning: an alternative structure
  documented by the University of Edinburgh Careers Service.
- **Benchmark** — a minimum score (per element or in total) that a recruiting
  panel sets for progression or appointment under Success Profiles-style
  scoring.

# Framework Context

**Evidence layer — cited.** Best-practice guidance for competency interviews
comes from four distinct source families, each authoritative for a different
part of the picture:

| Source family | Authority for | Key sources |
|---|---|---|
| UK official (GOV.UK, Civil Service) | How the largest UK competency framework tells candidates to prepare and how it assesses | Success Profiles candidate overview [1]; Civil Service Behaviours [2]; COPFS assessment guidance [3] |
| UK public careers services | General candidate preparation advice | National Careers Service interview advice [4], STAR method [5], common questions [6] |
| EU official | EU-institution competency assessment | EPSO competency framework [7] |
| Professional & advisory bodies | Interviewer-side best practice, fairness, private-sector selection | ACAS interviewing guidance [11], ACAS discrimination guidance [12], CIPD selection methods [13] |

The UK Civil Service Success Profiles framework defines behaviours as "the
actions and activities that people do which result in effective performance in
a job", and lists nine of them (Seeing the Big Picture, Changing and
Improving, Making Effective Decisions, Leadership, Communicating and
Influencing, Working Together, Developing Self and Others, Managing a Quality
Service, Delivering at Pace); recruiting managers choose the subset best
suited to the role, so candidates never face all nine at once [2]. EPSO's
competency framework plays the equivalent role for EU institutions: it
"defines the competencies that are considered essential to perform effectively
in any given job" for the EU institutions and bodies EPSO serves, with each
competency assessed against defined observable elements called anchors — some
assessed by EPSO at selection, others by the recruiting institution [7].

For the private sector there is no single mandated framework, but the CIPD's
selection guidance documents the same structured, criteria-scored approach as
cross-sector professional best practice [13]. In the graduate market, ISE
survey data (155 employers, over 1.8 million applications handled) records
the competitive context this guidance operates in: applications per graduate
vacancy have risen from 38 in 2002/03 to 140 in each of the last two years,
with 79% of employers redesigning or reviewing recruitment processes in
response to AI developments [14]. University careers services (Oxford,
Edinburgh) and graduate careers platforms (Prospects) publish the most
detailed candidate-side preparation guidance and are treated here as named
practitioner sources [15] [16] [17] [18].

# Core Reference

**Evidence layer — cited.**

## What official sources advise candidates to do

The Success Profiles candidate overview tells applicants to study the job
advert and prepare framework-mapped examples: "Think about examples you can
give of times when you have shown the behaviours outlined in the job advert"
[1]. It also advises reflecting in advance on "what you feel are your personal
strengths and preferred ways of working" for strengths-based elements, and
notes that interviews typically last 30–60 minutes and may be face-to-face,
by phone, by video or recorded [1]. Behaviours may be assessed via
application examples, tests, interviews, assessment centres or job
simulations, and candidates may draw examples from work, volunteering or
hobbies [2].

The National Careers Service's interview advice breaks preparation into
before, during and after. Before: check the interview format, "read the job
description carefully", research the employer's website, review your CV,
"prepare some examples from your past experience to refer to in the
interview", practise answers, and prepare your own questions. During: arrive
early, be ready 5–10 minutes before the start, ask the interviewer to repeat
or explain a question you do not understand, and structure answers with the
STAR method. After: ask when you will hear back, follow up if no timeline was
given, and ask for feedback if unsuccessful [4].

The STAR method itself is documented by the National Careers Service as:
Situation — "the situation you had to deal with"; Task — "the task you were
given to do"; Action — "the action you took"; Result — "what happened as a
result of your action and what you learned" [5]. The University of Edinburgh
Careers Service documents CARL as an alternative: "Context: Briefly set the
scene; Actions: What did you do?; Results: Briefly outline the outcome;
Learning: what did you learn from the experience" [17].

## Building a personal evidence bank mapped to the target framework

The mapping practice is documented consistently across sources. Prospects
advises candidates to "read and understand the job advert", then "from the
job description or person specification pick out the main competencies that
the employer is looking for and think of examples of when and how you've
demonstrated each of these", drawing "on a variety of experiences from your
studies, previous employment or any work experience", and warns against
overusing one example across a whole interview [18]. The University of Oxford
Careers Service advises preparing "a series of points in line with the job
description / person specification, stressing those aspects of your
experience, qualifications and skills that match the employer's requirements
most closely" rather than memorising scripted answers [16]. Edinburgh notes
that "Your application may form the basis of your interview so the
interviewer may ask you to elaborate or provide further examples", that
employers "expect interviewees to show evidence of commercial or business
awareness", and recommends practising aloud while avoiding over-rehearsal
[17]. The Success Profiles guidance anchors the same practice for Civil
Service applications: examples of times you have shown the advertised
behaviours, structured using STAR [1].

## The competitive context for graduates and career changers

ISE's Student Recruitment Survey 2025 (155 employers, 1.8 million
applications, 31,000+ hires) records: graduate hiring down 8% year-on-year;
an average of 140 applications per graduate vacancy for the second year
running (up from 38 in 2002/03), peaking at 290 for retail, FMCG and tourism
roles; 49% of employers permitting candidate AI use (mainly for CVs and cover
letters); and 61% of employers reporting undisclosed candidate AI use at
interview — driving process redesign at 79% of employers [14]. Despite the
volumes, 93% of employers report they can almost always or often recruit the
quality of graduate they need [14].

# Question Bank

General and opening competency questions suitable for a first practice
session. Sourced questions are quoted from the named publication; composed
questions are written for this knowledge base in the style of the cited
frameworks and are not attributable to any employer.

## Opening questions from public careers guidance

1. When have you faced a challenging situation? *(sourced: National Careers Service)* [6]
2. Can you tell us about a personal achievement at work? *(sourced: National Careers Service)* [6]
3. Have you ever taken the initiative? *(sourced: National Careers Service)* [6]
4. Have you ever failed at a task? *(sourced: National Careers Service)* [6]
5. What are your main strengths? *(sourced: National Careers Service)* [6]

## Core competency starters from named careers services

1. Tell me about a time when you were part of an effective team. *(sourced: University of Oxford Careers Service)* [15]
2. Describe a situation when you assumed the role of leader. *(sourced: University of Oxford Careers Service)* [15]
3. Give an example of a time when you had to cope under pressure. *(sourced: University of Oxford Careers Service)* [15]
4. Tell me about a time when you have had to balance or juggle a number of conflicting priorities. *(sourced: University of Oxford Careers Service)* [15]
5. Give me an example of a time when you were particularly successful, or delivered over and above expectations. *(sourced: University of Oxford Careers Service)* [15]
6. Describe a situation in which you led a team. *(sourced: Prospects)* [18]
7. Give an example of a time you handled conflict in the workplace. *(sourced: Prospects)* [18]

## Composed first-session questions

 1. Tell me about a time you had to build a productive working relationship with someone whose priorities differed from your own. What did you do to make it work? *(composed)*
 2. Describe a time you had to make a decision without all the information you would have liked. How did you decide, and what was the outcome? *(composed)*
 3. Give an example of a time you explained something complicated to someone outside your specialism. How did you check they had understood? *(composed)*

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact.**

- **Build the evidence bank first, not answers to individual questions.**
  Write up 6–10 real examples in STAR form [5], each two to three sentences
  per letter, and map each example to two or three competencies of the target
  framework (the nine Civil Service Behaviours [2], EPSO's general
  competencies [7], or the employer's person specification [18]). Most
  first-round questions — including all fifteen above — can then be answered
  by selecting and adapting, not inventing under pressure.
- **Spend most of the answer on Action, in the first person singular.**
  Scoring guidance rewards demonstrated behaviour (see Assessor Perspective):
  an answer that is mostly Situation, or that says "we" throughout, gives the
  assessor little positive evidence of *your* competence to score against the
  published scales [3]. A workable time split for a 2–3 minute answer:
  roughly 15% Situation and Task, 60–70% Action, the remainder Result and
  learning.
- **End with a result and, where natural, what you learned.** The National
  Careers Service's own definition of the R in STAR includes "what you
  learned" [5], and CARL makes learning an explicit fourth step [17] — a
  closing sentence on outcome plus reflection matches both documented
  structures.
- **Worked skeleton for question 6** (effective team, Oxford [15]):
  *Situation/Task* — one sentence naming the team, your role, and the goal at
  stake. *Action* — three or four first-person steps: how you took on your
  part, coordinated with others, handled a friction point. *Result* — what
  the team delivered, plus one thing you now do differently in teams.
- **Vary your sources of evidence.** Careers services advise drawing examples
  from study, work, volunteering and life, and warn that reusing one example
  across an interview reads as thin experience [18]; official guidance
  likewise accepts examples from work, volunteering or hobbies [2].
- **Practise aloud, but do not script.** Practising with another person while
  avoiding memorised answers is the documented advice from both Oxford and
  Edinburgh [16] [17]; scripts collapse under follow-up probes, which the
  published question sets explicitly include ("How did you make a
  contribution?", "What would you do differently?") [15].

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — cited.**

## Published scoring guidance

Where Success Profiles scoring guidance is published, behaviours are scored on
a 7-point scale. The Crown Office and Procurator Fiscal Service (a UK
government department recruiting under Success Profiles) publishes the scale
in full: "1 Not Demonstrated, 2 Minimal Demonstration, 3 Moderate
Demonstration, 4 Acceptable Demonstration, 5 Good Demonstration, 6 Strong
Demonstration, 7 Outstanding Demonstration", and states that at interview
"Behaviours, experience and technical skills are scored on the same 1-7
rating scale used at Sift" [3]. The same source documents pass and benchmark
mechanics: candidates "must demonstrate the minimum essential criteria for
all roles, this includes scoring 4 or more in all behaviours", with a single
3 tolerable where other scores are high, and boards may set higher
benchmarks — for example a 5 on lead behaviours or a total of 20 across
several behaviours — "where there is a job and business need" [3].

An example five-point competency evaluation scheme, reproduced by the
University of Oxford Careers Service from Civil Service resourcing
best-practice guidance, shows the underlying logic of evidence-based marking:
5 = "positive evidence of a high level of competence with no substantial
negative evidence" down to 1 = "little or no positive evidence of competence,
along with considerable negative evidence" [15]. In both schemes the assessor
scores the *evidence of behaviour in the answer* — positive and negative —
not the candidate's fluency or likeability.

## Documented interviewer best practice

ACAS guidance to employers on interviewing states that "each interview should
be carried out by more than 1 person to reduce the risk of discriminating",
that employers should "ask each applicant the same questions where possible…
to ensure you're treating applicants in the same way", that hiring decisions
should be based on how each applicant met each point in the job description
and person specification, and that interview notes or recordings must be
stored securely and confidentially in line with data-protection law (with
recording requiring the candidate's advance permission) [11]. The CIPD's
selection guidance defines the structured interview — a predefined set of
questions, asked in the same order to all candidates, with responses scored
against pre-agreed objective criteria — and documents its rationale: it makes
responses comparable and less likely that decisions rest on personal biases
[13]. The ICO's draft recruitment and selection guidance sets out employers'
UK GDPR / Data Protection Act 2018 obligations across the pipeline, including
sections on shortlisting, testing and interviewing candidates and on keeping
recruitment records [10]. At EU institutions, assessment against the EPSO
competency framework is anchored to defined observable elements per
competency, split between EPSO's selection phase and the recruiting
institution's own assessment [7].

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact.** Each pitfall is
tied to the documented criterion it violates.

## Candidate-side

- **Narrating the team, not yourself.** Scales score demonstrated individual
  behaviour [3] [15]; an all-"we" answer leaves nothing to mark.
- **All situation, no action.** Long scene-setting starves the part of the
  answer where the published schemes look for positive evidence [15].
- **One example stretched across the whole interview** — explicitly warned
  against by Prospects as reading like limited experience [18].
- **Ignoring the advertised framework.** Official guidance says the behaviours
  to prepare for are listed in the job advert [1]; preparing generic answers
  instead of mapping to those behaviours misses the marking criteria the
  panel is scoring against.
- **Memorised scripts.** Documented follow-up probes ("What would you do
  differently?") [15] break scripted answers; careers services advise
  practising aloud without over-rehearsing [17].
- **No result, no learning.** Both documented structures end on outcome and
  learning [5] [17]; answers that trail off after the action forfeit that
  evidence.
- **Undisclosed AI assistance.** 61% of ISE employers report candidates using
  AI at interview without disclosure, and employers are actively redesigning
  processes in response [14] — treating AI as a drafting aid for preparation
  is different from relying on it live.

## Interviewer-side

- **Different questions for different candidates**, or scoring by overall
  impression rather than against pre-agreed criteria — the exact practices
  ACAS and CIPD guidance exist to prevent, because they make candidates
  incomparable and let bias in [11] [13].
- **Solo interviewing** where avoidable, against ACAS's more-than-one-person
  recommendation [11].
- **Missing, judgemental or over-retained notes.** Notes are disclosable
  personal data and must be stored securely with a records policy [10] [11];
  writing down evidence, not impressions, is the practice the scoring
  schemes assume [3].
- **Health or disability questions before offer**, or questions touching
  protected characteristics — prohibited territory covered in Legal &
  Fairness Considerations [8] [12].

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.** Drills a reader can run this week.

1. **Evidence-bank sprint (60–90 minutes).** List every substantial project,
   job, course, volunteering role and crisis of the last five years. Pick the
   eight richest. Write each up in four STAR sentences [5]. Map each to the
   competencies of one real target: the behaviours in a live Civil Service
   advert [1], EPSO's general competencies [7], or a private-sector person
   specification [18]. Gaps in the map tell you what experience to seek or
   which examples to develop next.
2. **Timed first-fifteen run.** Answer questions 1–15 above aloud, two to
   three minutes each, over two or three sittings. Record yourself; count the
   "we"-to-"I" ratio in each Action section and re-run any answer where "we"
   wins.
3. **Self-score against a published scale.** Re-listen to three answers and
   score each 1–7 using the published descriptors ("Acceptable
   Demonstration" = 4) [3], or the five-point positive/negative-evidence
   scheme [15]. Write one sentence per answer on what evidence was missing;
   revise the underlying evidence-bank entry, not just the answer.
4. **Mock panel with follow-ups.** Give a friend three sourced questions plus
   the documented probes ("How did you make a contribution?", "What would you
   do differently in future?") [15] and have them interrupt your scripted
   flow. Careers services document mock interviews and practising aloud as
   standard preparation [16] [17].
5. **Interviewer drill (for HR readers).** Take a live vacancy, draft five
   questions mapped to its person specification, agree scoring criteria per
   question before any interview, and run a paired mock scoring of one
   candidate answer — then compare scores and reconcile against the criteria,
   per ACAS and CIPD structured practice [11] [13].

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — cited. Signpost only; the fairness-bias-legal deep-dive
will treat these in full.**

- **UK — Equality Act 2010.** Discrimination against applicants in
  recruitment arrangements and hiring decisions is prohibited (s.39); the
  duty to make reasonable adjustments for disabled people is set out at
  ss.20–21; enquiries about disability and health before offer are restricted
  by s.60 [8]. ACAS's recruitment guidance operationalises this for
  interviews: the nine protected characteristics must not influence
  selection, "You must not ask a job applicant about their health or any
  disability at any stage in the application or interview process, unless an
  exception applies", and employers must make reasonable adjustments for
  disabled applicants and interviewees — for example accessible rooms,
  extended test time or alternative formats [12].
- **EU — equal treatment in recruitment.** Council Directive 2000/78/EC
  establishes the general framework for equal treatment in employment and
  occupation, and applies expressly to "conditions for access to employment…
  including selection criteria and recruitment conditions, whatever the
  branch of activity" (Art. 3(1)(a)), covering religion or belief,
  disability, age and sexual orientation [9].
- **Candidate data.** Interview notes, scores and recordings are personal
  data. The ICO's draft employment-practices guidance on recruitment and
  selection sets out employers' obligations under the UK GDPR and the Data
  Protection Act 2018, including for shortlisting, testing and interviewing
  and for keeping recruitment records [10]; ACAS likewise requires notes and
  recordings to be stored securely and confidentially, with recording only by
  advance permission [11].

# Risks & Caveats

- **The 1–7 scale citation is one department's published articulation.** COPFS
  publishes the Success Profiles 7-point scale and benchmark rules openly
  [3], and it matches practice described across Civil Service recruitment,
  but departments vary in benchmarks and in which elements they score; a
  central, GOV.UK-hosted scoring publication has not yet been verified for
  this document (see Open Questions).
- **The Oxford example-questions document is dated May 2016** [15]. Its
  questions and evaluation scheme remain live on the Careers Service site,
  but the Civil Service guidance it excerpts predates the current Success
  Profiles wording; treat its five-point scheme as illustrative of
  evidence-based marking logic, not as the current Civil Service instrument.
- **The ICO recruitment guidance cited is a draft** published for
  consultation (the page notes the final version is to follow) and references
  law amended by the Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 [10]; check for the final
  version at review.
- **CIPD pages bot-block automated link checkers**; the selection-methods
  factsheet URL is real and was verified in-browser terms via search excerpts
  [13], but a human should eyeball it periodically.
- **ISE full survey reports are member-gated.** All ISE figures cited here
  come from ISE's openly published summary of the 2025 Student Recruitment
  Survey [14], not the gated report.
- Private-sector practice varies far more than public-sector practice; CIPD
  guidance is best practice, not a mandate, and individual employers may run
  unstructured interviews that reward different behaviour.
