# Executive Summary

Most published lists of "interview mistakes" are folklore: plausible advice with
nothing behind it. This document is built the other way round. It starts from
mistakes that a **named, dated, official source actually names as a mistake**,
or that are **structurally guaranteed to lose marks by a documented scoring
rule**, and works back to what the candidate should do instead. Two UK police
forces publish an explicit "common mistakes" slide in their candidate guidance
[9] [10]; the College of Policing publishes the assessment model (ORCE) whose
mechanics make certain answer habits unscoreable [6]; the Cabinet Office and
COPFS publish the scoring scales whose descriptors show exactly which part of
an answer earns marks [4] [5]. Where a mistake cannot be tied to a documented
criterion, it is either omitted or flagged as guidance.

The second half is the part almost nobody writes down: **recovery**. What do
you do after a blank, after realising 40 seconds in that you picked the wrong
example, after a probe exposes a detail you got wrong, or after your camera
freezes in a recorded interview? Here the documented process facts are decisive
and mildly counter-intuitive. Under ORCE, assessors record behavioural evidence
contemporaneously, classify it *after* the candidate has finished, classify each
behaviour **once** without double counting, and — in Civil Service delegated-grade
practice — score each question **independently** before scores are reconciled
by arithmetic average [4] [6]. That has a precise consequence: **you cannot
delete evidence a panellist has already written down, but you can still add
classifiable evidence, and the classification step has not happened yet.**
Recovery is therefore additive, not corrective — and several sources explicitly
invite the additions (COPFS: candidates "can also ask questions, seek
clarification or add extra information at any time" [5]; Hampshire: "If you need
'thinking time' please ask. If you need the question repeated please ask" [9]).

Confidence is **High**, with one honest qualification. Every evidence-layer
claim here is cited to an official framework document, Cabinet Office or
College of Policing guidance, a published force or departmental assessment
document, or a statute — and the two central mistake lists are independently
corroborated across two police forces [9] [10]. The recovery half is reasoned
synthesis from those documented process mechanics, because **no located source
publishes a mid-interview recovery protocol for candidates**; it is therefore
confined to the coaching layer and labelled as guidance throughout, per the
house convention. Format-specific parameters (video timers, retake policies)
rest partly on careers-service sources and vary by platform — see Risks &
Caveats.

# Key Takeaways

- Two police forces publish a named **"common mistakes"** list for competency
  interviews. Between them they name six: not answering the question (answering
  the competency instead), **telling a story** rather than explaining what you
  did, answering at the **wrong CVF level**, **pre-empting** what the panel
  wants to hear, **"CVF bingo"** (buzzwords), and unexplained **abbreviations**
  [9] [10]. *(documented)*
- **"CVF bingo" is documented as scoreless**: "buzz words don't score… it is
  about you answering the question and being authentic in your response, not
  repeating buzz words from the CVF Framework" [9] [10]. *(documented)*
- **Answering at the wrong framework level is a marking error, not a style
  error.** CVF competencies have three cumulative levels tied to role seniority
  [7], and Civil Service Behaviours are "specific to the grade level of the job
  role" across six levels from AA/AO to Director General [1]. Evidence pitched
  at the wrong level is evidence against a different descriptor. *(documented)*
- **"We" answers are unclassifiable.** ORCE requires assessors to classify
  *observed behaviour* into competencies and values, recording "just behavioural
  evidence" with "no assuming or interpreting" [6]; COPFS tells candidates
  directly to "use 'I' and not 'WE' so that the selection panel obtains a full
  understanding of what you did" [5]; Dyfed-Powys' marking guide lists "using I
  instead of we/us/them" under *what good evidence looks like* [11].
  *(documented)*
- **Reusing one example caps your score by rule, not by taste.** ORCE instructs
  assessors to "classify each behaviour once — do not double count" [6], so a
  second airing of the same evidence adds nothing; targetjobs advises aiming
  "to use a different example for each competency you are asked to discuss"
  [21]. *(documented)*
- **Rehearsing a strengths answer is a mistake caused by over-applying STAR.**
  GOV.UK instructs candidates: "Do not rehearse your answers - because we are
  looking for your initial response" [2], repeated verbatim in the Strengths
  Dictionary [3]. *(documented)*
- **Scoring descriptors are balance sheets.** COPFS publishes the 7-point
  Success Profiles descriptors as positive-versus-negative evidence ("Moderate
  positive evidence but some negative evidence demonstrated") [5]; the College's
  A–D scale is worded the same way [6]. Long scene-setting is not neutral — it
  displaces the Action content that generates positive evidence. *(documented)*
- **Recovery is additive.** Because evidence is recorded contemporaneously,
  classified only after you finish, and scored per question independently by
  each panellist [4] [6], you cannot erase a weak first minute — but nothing has
  been classified yet, and adding better evidence before the question closes is
  the only lever you have. *(guidance)*
- **For technical failure, there is a documented protocol, and "carry on
  regardless" is the wrong move.** The College's online assessment guidance:
  "please contact the assessment platform immediately. Please do not finish the
  exercise if you are experiencing technical issues", with appeals due within
  **24 hours** of taking the assessment [8]. The National Careers Service says
  of live video: "if you have any technical problems, let the interviewer know.
  It's best to stop and restart if you need to" [19]. *(documented)*
- **Interviewers make mistakes too, and they are catalogued.** The College of
  Policing publishes a *Barriers to effective assessment* table naming eighteen
  named assessor biases with counter-measures [6]. *(documented)*

# Purpose

This document answers two questions that the Best Practice foundation raises
but does not resolve.

For candidates: *"Which interview mistakes are actually documented as mistakes
by the people doing the marking — and if I make one, is there anything I can
do about it while the interview is still running?"*

For interviewers and HR: *"What does the published guidance say goes wrong on
my side of the table, and what counter-measures are documented?"* The assessor
half of this document is deliberately not an afterthought: the single richest
located catalogue of interview failure modes in UK public-sector guidance is a
catalogue of **assessor** failure modes, not candidate ones [6].

# Scope

**Covered.** Candidate-side mistakes that can be tied to a named published
source or to a documented scoring/assessment rule; the mechanics that make
those mistakes costly (ORCE, evidence-balance descriptors, independent
per-question scoring, level descriptors); recovery options after a blank, a
wrong-example start, a contradicted detail, or a technical failure; the
realistic ceiling on recovery given how scoring works; format-specific errors
in asynchronous video and group exercises; and the documented assessor-bias
catalogue.

**Not covered.** The scoring scales themselves in full (see
**scoring-and-marking**); how probes are constructed and what they are testing
(**follow-up-probing-questions**); STAR mechanics (**star-technique**); how to
write good questions (**interviewer-question-design**); framework definitions
(**uk-civil-service-behaviour-questions**,
**police-competency-values-framework**); and full format guidance
(**video-asynchronous-interviews**). General preparation advice belongs to the
parent, **best-practice-foundation**, and is not restated here.

**Deliberate exclusion.** Undocumented folklore — dress codes, handshake
advice, "power poses", the claim that panels decide in the first thirty seconds
— is excluded. Where a widely repeated belief is *contradicted* by a published
source, it is recorded in Common Pitfalls as a myth with the citation.

# Definitions

- **Classifiable evidence** — this knowledge base's shorthand for a statement
  in an answer that an assessor following ORCE could record and then assign to
  a named competency, value or behaviour. Derived from the ORCE "classify" step
  as published by the College of Policing [6]; the term is ours.
- **Evidence balance** — the positive-versus-negative structure of published
  rating descriptors (e.g. "Moderate positive evidence but some negative
  evidence demonstrated") [5] [6]. Scoring is not a percentage of airtime; it
  is a judgement on the balance of classified evidence.
- **Level mismatch** — an answer whose content sits at a different framework
  level from the one the role is assessed at (CVF Levels 1–3 [7]; Civil Service
  Behaviour Levels 1–6 [1]).
- **Recovery** — anything a candidate does *after* a mistake and *before* the
  assessment closes that adds classifiable evidence. It does not mean removing
  evidence already recorded.
- **Automatic fail** — in the College of Policing online assessment process, a
  process-level failure "regardless of your performance in the individual
  exercises", awarded by an independent Quality Assurance team and not
  appealable [8].

The two framework families this document draws on define levels differently,
and the difference matters for level mismatch. The **CVF 2024** gives each
competency "three levels, which can be used flexibly", labelled Level 1
Practitioner, Level 2 Supervisor/middle manager, Level 3 Senior
manager/executive, and states: "The levels are cumulative. This means that
those working at higher levels should have built on their behaviours from the
preceding levels" [7]. **Civil Service Behaviours** run to six levels mapped to
grades — Level 1 AA and AO, Level 2 EO, Level 3 HEO and SEO, Level 4 Grade 7
and 6, Level 5 Deputy Director, Level 6 Director and Director General — and the
framework states plainly that "Civil Service Behaviours are specific to the
grade level of the job role" [1].

# Core Reference

**Evidence layer — cited.**

## The published "common mistakes" lists

Two forces publish an explicit common-mistakes list in candidate-facing
guidance. Hampshire and Isle of Wight Constabulary's transferee guidance names
six [9]:

1. "Not answering the question. Answering the competency and not the question."
2. "Telling a story. Tell us the situation but use your time explaining what
   you did and why."
3. "Not the right CVF level - Be mindful of the level you are answering at.
   Review the Framework and check the level."
4. "Pre-empting the interview board — Do not pre-empt what you think the panel
   want to hear. The Panel want to hear about you and your behaviours."
5. "CVF 'Bingo' — buzz words don't score - Please don't try and get as many
   buzz words in as possible, it is about you answering the question and being
   authentic in your response, not repeating buzz words from the CVF
   Framework."
6. "Abbreviations — All constabulary's have acronyms and team names — please
   explain the full details initially."

Devon & Cornwall Police's force-interview guidance publishes four of the same
items — "Didn't answer the question - Answered the competency and not the
question", "Not the right CVF level", "Pre-empting the interview board", and
"CVF 'Bingo' — buzz words don't score" — independently corroborating them
across two forces [10].

Both forces also publish a **myths** section that contradicts two widely held
candidate beliefs: "There is a pre-agreed set number who will pass/fail - We do
want you all to be successful there is no set pass or fail numbers", and
"There are trick questions - There are no trick questions" [9] [10]. Hampshire
adds a third: the interviewers "will be making notes during the interview.
Please do not read into how much the interviewers are writing down — it is not
an indicator of score and any pass or fail" [9].

## Why "we" and reused examples fail: the ORCE mechanics

The College of Policing's values-based recruitment and selection guidance
publishes the ORCE model — observe, record, classify, evaluate — as the
structured assessment process trained assessors should follow, because it
"breaks down the assessing process into component parts and helps to avoid
bias" [6]. Its published sequence is precise:

- **Observe** — "watching, listening or reading a candidate's assessment",
  "independent of any other assessor(s)".
- **Record** — "Take detailed notes of behavioural evidence (as close to
  verbatim as possible)", "No assuming or interpreting", "Record just
  behavioural evidence".
- **Classify** — "After candidate has left the room", "Only classify
  behavioural evidence", "Only classify behaviour from that assessment",
  "**Classify each behaviour once — do not double count**", classified
  "according to competencies or values and assessor guidance".
- **Evaluate** — "After classifying", "Based on quality and quantity of the
  classified evidence", "Consider the level of role and aim of the assessment
  process (benchmarking)", "Consider what the candidate missed or if anything
  detracted from their performance" [6].

Three consequences follow directly from that published sequence. First, an
assessor is instructed **not** to interpret: a sentence beginning "we decided"
records a group behaviour, and nothing in the model licenses the assessor to
infer that the candidate personally decided. COPFS states the candidate-side
corollary outright: "Candidates are reminded to use 'I' and not 'WE' so that
the selection panel obtains a full understanding of what you did within a
specific example" [5]. Dyfed-Powys' published interview marking guide lists,
under "What does good evidence look like?", "The candidate is using I instead
of we/us/them" [11].

Second, the no-double-counting rule caps repetition: a behaviour already
classified from example A cannot be classified again when example A is retold
under a later question. targetjobs' guidance gives the candidate-side version:
"aim to use a different example for each competency you are asked to discuss"
[21].

Third — and this is the load-bearing fact for recovery — **classification
happens after the candidate has finished**, and evaluation happens after
classification [6]. Nothing is scored while you are still speaking.

## What the scoring descriptors actually reward

Published descriptors are written as an **evidence balance**, not as an
impression. The College's example rating scale runs A to D [6]:

| Grade | Published descriptor |
|---|---|
| A | "Substantial positive evidence of the competency observed with regard to the quality and quantity of the evidence and few or no areas for development observed" |
| B | "Positive evidence of the competency observed and some areas for development but, on balance, more positive evidence with regard to the quality and quantity of the evidence" |
| C | "Some positive evidence of the competency observed but, on balance, more areas for development with regard to the quality and quantity of the evidence" |
| D | "Substantial areas for development identified and little or no positive evidence of the competency observed with regard to the quality and quantity of the evidence" |

COPFS publishes the Success Profiles 7-point scale in the same idiom: 1 Not
Demonstrated = "No positive evidence and/or substantial negative evidence
demonstrated"; 3 Moderate Demonstration = "Moderate positive evidence but some
negative evidence demonstrated"; 4 Acceptable Demonstration = "Adequate
positive evidence and any negative evidence would not cause concern"; 6 Strong
Demonstration = "Substantial positive evidence; includes some evidence of
exceeding expectations at this level" [5]. The Cabinet Office's delegated-grade
guidance confirms the scale is the central rule, not a departmental one:
"Experience, technical skills and behaviours should be scored using the 7 point
scale. Strengths should be scored using the 4 point scale" [4].

Read together, these descriptors explain why the "no measurable Result" and
"all Situation" mistakes cost marks. A minute of scene-setting produces no
classifiable behavioural evidence; it neither adds to the positive column nor
subtracts from it, but it consumes the finite question window in which positive
evidence could have been generated. And the Evaluate step explicitly directs
assessors to "consider what the candidate missed" [6].

## What the panel is instructed to do with your answer

The Cabinet Office's delegated-grade guidance sets out the interview mechanics
for EO to Grade 6 recruitment: "Panel members should observe, record, classify,
evaluate and score each question independently, then calculate an overall score
before jointly agreeing a merit list using the arithmetic method. Variances in
scoring amongst panel members can be discussed… Panel members may decide to
change their score(s) or stand by them, but ultimately individual panel scores
should be reconciled by taking an arithmetic average" [4]. It also recommends
"that no more than four behaviours are" tested [4], which is why a candidate
who burns two of four behaviours on one example has few chances left.

COPFS documents the same structure from the candidate's side: the panel
comprises at least two members; "all candidates will be asked the same core
questions. Any follow-up questions will depend on the answers you give and
won't necessarily be the same as those for other candidates"; and the board
"will ask you probing questions to ensure they fully understand the situation
you are describing" [5].

## The documented invitations to recover

Four separate official sources publish candidate permissions that function as
recovery levers:

- COPFS: "You can also ask questions, seek clarification or add extra
  information at any time and will be given a specific opportunity to do so at
  the end" [5].
- Hampshire: "If you need 'thinking time' please ask. If you need the question
  repeated please ask" [9].
- Devon & Cornwall: "Don't be afraid to ask for clarification if you do not
  understand the question"; and "You won't be told to stop mid sentence,
  however if the Chair of the interview believes you are not answering they may
  gently remind you" [10] — the same wording appears in Hampshire's guidance
  [9].
- National Careers Service: "ask the interviewer to repeat the question or
  explain further if you do not understand"; "write down the question or refer
  to your notes if you need to"; "think about your prepared examples and choose
  the best example to give" [17].

COPFS additionally documents that "You may bring personal notes to your
interview. However it is good to remember that the interview is a conversation
with a purpose and therefore the board are not looking for answers by rote.
Notes should only be used as reference material" [5]. **Note the sharp
contrast with the police online assessment**, where the opposite rule applies:
"Do not use notes or scripts that have been prepared prior to your assessment
to support your response. Any evidence of this may result in an automatic
failure" — though within the exercise, "after receiving the question, you can
make notes to prepare your answer" [8]. Recovery tactics are not portable
between processes.

## Format-specific mistakes and their documented parameters

**Asynchronous / recorded video.** The College's online assessment process
gives, per question, "one minute (60 seconds) of thinking time to consider the
question and prepare your answer" — during which "Nothing that you say during
the thinking time will be recorded" — followed by "five minutes (300 seconds)
to record your answer", across five questions in total [8]. Commercial
platforms are tighter: targetjobs reports "You are usually given a short time
of between one and three minutes to answer each question, with a timer on
screen indicating how long you have left", a completion deadline "usually
within a week", and that retake policy varies — "Some employers allow you
multiple retakes, others only a single attempt" — so candidates should "check
whether you are able to re-record responses and, if so, how many retakes you
get" before starting [20]. Oxford's careers service records the structural
consequence: in a pre-recorded interview "There is no opportunity for the
interviewer to ask for clarification" [23]. The National Careers Service adds
that in a pre-recorded interview "you will not be speaking to anyone. You will
not have any visual clues from an interviewer to encourage you", and advises
using "any practice questions to check out if you can stop or re-record any of
your answers if something goes wrong" [19].

**Technical failure.** The College publishes an explicit protocol: "If you
encounter technical difficulties during the online assessment, please contact
the assessment platform immediately. Please do not finish the exercise if you
are experiencing technical issues", supplying full name, recruiting force,
exercise name, device and browser, and the nature of the issue; live chat runs
08:00–18:00 Monday to Friday with "a response within 15 minutes" [8]. Appeals
"must" be submitted "within 24 hours of taking the assessment. Appeals received
after this date will not be considered. You therefore cannot appeal after
receiving your final result" [8]. Separately, extenuating circumstances are
defined as "a serious event or series of events, such as illness, accident or
injury, or the death or serious illness of a family member" — and explicitly
**not** "lack of preparation or a heavy workload" — and must be raised
*before* the assessment, since "These will not be grounds for appeal after the
assessment" [8]. For live video, the National Careers Service says: "if you
have any technical problems, let the interviewer know. It's best to stop and
restart if you need to" [19]. targetjobs notes that on live calls "recruiters
will be in the same boat and will most likely forgive any unavoidable technical
glitches" and "may be willing to have conversations over phone or to rearrange
the call" [20].

**Group exercises.** The University of York publishes the observed criterion:
"collaboration - working with the group, contributing without trying to
dominate", alongside a warning that "you may be assessed throughout the time
you are with the employer" and that assessors commonly evaluate candidates'
behaviour "towards staff who are not involved in the assessment centre, such as
reception staff" [22]. It also records that "you are not competing against the
others in the group - the employer may choose to appoint some, all or none of
you" [22].

**Strengths elements.** GOV.UK's Success Profiles strengths guidance states
that at interview "there are no right or wrong answers to these questions. Do
not rehearse your answers - because we are looking for your initial response",
and that "The best way to prepare is to reflect on what you feel your personal
strengths are and your preferred ways of working" [2]. The Civil Service
Strengths Dictionary repeats the instruction verbatim and lists the assessment
methods that may carry strengths, including "Recorded video interviews" [3].

# Question Bank

Diagnostic and recovery drills. Sourced items are published questions or
published assessment prompts quoted from the named publication; composed items
are written for this knowledge base in the style of the cited frameworks and
are **not** real or leaked questions from any employer. Several composed items
are *diagnostic prompts* — you answer them about a flawed answer rather than
about yourself.

## Weak-answer diagnostics — attribution and the "we" problem

1. Record a two-minute answer to a teamwork question, then count every sentence in your Action section: how many begin with "I" and how many with "we"? *(composed)*
2. Take one of your recorded answers and strike out every sentence an assessor following ORCE could not attribute to you personally. What percentage of the answer survives? *(composed)*
3. Read your answer against the marking-guide criterion "The candidate is using I instead of we/us/them" — does it pass? *(sourced: Dyfed-Powys Police interview marking guide)* [11]
4. Rewrite the weakest "we" sentence in your answer three ways, so that each version makes your own contribution explicit without erasing the team. *(composed)*
5. Where you genuinely acted as part of a pair, how would you phrase your contribution without either over-claiming or disappearing? *(composed)*

## Weak-answer diagnostics — level, question and relevance

1. Tell me about a time when you worked in a team. *(sourced: targetjobs)* [21]
2. For your answer to the previous question, identify the framework level your evidence actually sits at, then compare it to the level the role is assessed at. *(composed)*
3. CVF Level 1 includes "I take responsibility for my own actions and decisions"; Level 2 includes "I delegate appropriately by giving clear direction". Which of these does your answer evidence, and is that the level you are being assessed at? *(sourced: College of Policing, Competency and values framework 2024)* [7]
4. Reread the question you were asked, then reread your answer: did you answer the question, or did you answer the competency? *(composed)*
5. Highlight every acronym, team name or internal system in your answer that an outside assessor would not recognise. Rewrite the first mention of each in full. *(composed)*
6. Which sentence in your answer exists only because you were guessing what the panel wanted to hear? Cut it and check whether the answer got worse. *(composed)*

## Weak-answer diagnostics — evidence balance and buzzwords

1. Count the words in your Situation and Task versus your Action. Does "most of your response" sit in the Action part? *(sourced: targetjobs)* [21]
2. Delete every framework buzzword from your answer. Is there any classifiable behaviour left underneath? *(composed)*
3. Score your own answer against the published descriptor "Some positive evidence of the competency observed but, on balance, more areas for development" — can you justify a higher grade with the words you actually said? *(sourced: College of Policing, example rating scale)* [6]
4. Score the same answer against the Success Profiles descriptor "Adequate positive evidence and any negative evidence would not cause concern" — what is the negative evidence in your answer? *(sourced: COPFS Success Profiles assessment guidance)* [5]
5. State the Result of your example as a number, a date, a decision taken, or a named change adopted. If you cannot, what would you need to find out? *(composed)*
6. Apply the Evaluate-step question to your own answer: what did you miss, and did anything you said detract from your performance? *(sourced: College of Policing, ORCE sequence)* [6]
7. Which two of the four behaviours advertised for your target role does your favourite example evidence — and which two are you leaving uncovered? *(composed)*

## Recovery drills — mid-answer

1. You are 40 seconds into an answer and realise you have chosen an example that evidences the wrong behaviour. Script, out loud, the two sentences you would use to pivot without apologising at length. *(composed)*
2. You go blank at the start of a question. Practise the documented move: ask for the question to be repeated, or ask for thinking time. Time how long the pause actually is. *(sourced: Hampshire and Isle of Wight Constabulary CVF guidance)* [9]
3. You do not understand what the question is driving at. Draft the clarifying question you would ask. *(sourced: National Careers Service interview tips)* [17]
4. A probe exposes a detail you stated wrongly earlier in the same answer. Script a correction that is explicit, brief, and adds new classifiable evidence rather than only retracting. *(composed)*
5. You have finished an answer and immediately realise you omitted your most important action. Practise using the documented permission to "add extra information" — and decide whether to use it now or at the end. *(sourced: COPFS Success Profiles assessment guidance)* [5]
6. The chair "gently reminds" you that you are not answering the question. Rehearse the fifteen-second reset that gets you back onto the question without restarting the whole example. *(sourced: Devon & Cornwall Police force interview guidance)* [10]
7. You used your strongest example under question two and question four now suits it perfectly. Choose a second-best example and practise making it land — you cannot bank the same behaviour twice. *(composed)*
8. Your last answer ended with no Result because the project was cancelled. Practise closing on what you learned instead, and check it against the STAR definition of R. *(sourced: National Careers Service, The STAR method)* [18]

## Recovery drills — format-specific

1. In a recorded interview you have one minute of unrecorded thinking time before a five-minute answer. Practise using the full minute to choose the example and fix the Result before you start speaking. *(sourced: College of Policing online assessment process guidance)* [8]
2. Your recording timer cuts you off mid-Result. Re-answer the same question in 80% of the time available, front-loading the Action. *(composed)*
3. Before your next recorded interview, find and write down the answer to: how many retakes am I allowed, and what is the completion deadline? *(sourced: targetjobs video interview tips)* [20]
4. Your camera or connection fails during an online assessment exercise. Rehearse the documented protocol — contact the platform immediately and do not finish the exercise — and note the 24-hour appeal window. *(sourced: College of Policing online assessment process guidance)* [8]
5. Your connection drops in a live video interview. Practise the documented move of telling the interviewer and stopping and restarting. *(sourced: National Careers Service video interview tips)* [19]
6. In a group exercise you have spoken for four of the last five minutes. Practise the two interventions that restore "contributing without trying to dominate" — bringing in a quiet member, and summarising rather than adding. *(sourced: University of York assessment centres guidance)* [22]

## Interviewer-side self-audit prompts

1. Before this candidate spoke, what expectation had you formed from their application form — and can you show that it did not shape your questions? *(sourced: College of Policing barriers to effective assessment)* [6]
2. Are you scoring this candidate against the criteria, or against the previous candidate? *(sourced: College of Policing barriers to effective assessment)* [6]
3. Did a strong first answer carry your scores across the remaining behaviours? *(sourced: College of Policing barriers to effective assessment)* [6]
4. How many candidates have you assessed today without a break, and at what point did your notes get shorter? *(sourced: College of Policing barriers to effective assessment)* [6]
5. Are your scores clustered in the middle of the scale across every candidate this week? *(composed)*
6. Did you record behavioural evidence as close to verbatim as possible, or did you record your interpretation of it? *(sourced: College of Policing, ORCE sequence)* [6]
7. Did you classify before you evaluated, and did you do both independently of the other panellist before reconciling? *(sourced: Cabinet Office delegated-grade Success Profiles guidance)* [4]
8. Have you asked the same core questions of every candidate, with follow-ups driven only by their answers? *(sourced: COPFS Success Profiles assessment guidance)* [5]

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact. It points back to the
citations above and adds no new claims.**

## The repair order: fix the biggest scoring leak first

Not all mistakes cost the same. Ranked by how directly they hit the published
descriptors:

1. **Unattributed action ("we")** — costs the most, because it converts
   potentially strong evidence into evidence the assessor is instructed not to
   interpret [6] and the candidate is explicitly told to avoid [5]. Fix this
   first; it is a rewrite of pronouns and verbs, not of content.
2. **Level mismatch** — costs almost as much, because correct evidence at the
   wrong level is evidence against a different descriptor [1] [7]. Fix by
   re-selecting examples, not by inflating language.
3. **No Result** — forfeits the closing evidence that the descriptors and the
   Evaluate step both look for [6] [18].
4. **Scene-setting overrun** — does not directly create negative evidence, but
   consumes the window in which positive evidence could be generated [21].
5. **Buzzwords** — documented as scoreless [9] [10], so at best neutral; the
   real cost is the airtime.
6. **Unexplained abbreviations** — a comprehension tax on the assessor [9].

## Worked repair: one answer, three passes

Take a genuine two-minute answer to a teamwork question and run three passes.

- **Pass one — attribution.** Convert every "we did" into either "I did" or "my
  colleague did, and I…". Where the honest answer is that the team decided,
  say what *your* input into the decision was. Target: every sentence in the
  Action section attributable to a named actor, per ORCE's no-interpretation
  rule [6].
- **Pass two — level.** Put the level descriptors for your target role beside
  the answer [1] [7]. If you are being assessed at CVF Level 1 and every
  sentence is about delegating and monitoring a team, you have evidenced Level 2
  behaviour and left Level 1 unevidenced — and vice versa. Because CVF levels
  are cumulative [7], a senior candidate evidencing only Level 1 behaviour is
  the more common and more damaging error of the two.
- **Pass three — balance and close.** Cut Situation and Task to the minimum
  that makes the Action intelligible, and end on a stated outcome plus, where
  natural, what you learned [18]. Then read the answer against the C descriptor
  — "Some positive evidence… but, on balance, more areas for development" [6] —
  and ask what a sceptical assessor would put in the second column.

## What "recovery" can and cannot do

This is the part where candidates most often act on a false model. The
documented mechanics say:

- **You cannot un-say it.** Assessors record contemporaneously, "as close to
  verbatim as possible" [6]. A weak first minute is on the page.
- **But nothing has been classified yet.** Classification happens "after
  candidate has left the room", and evaluation after classification [6]. Your
  weak minute has not yet been turned into a grade.
- **So recovery means adding classifiable evidence**, not persuading anyone to
  forget. Time spent apologising, explaining that you are nervous, or narrating
  your own failure is time not spent producing evidence.
- **The additions are explicitly permitted.** Asking for the question again,
  asking for thinking time, seeking clarification, and adding extra information
  at any time — including the specific opportunity at the end — are all
  documented candidate permissions [5] [9] [10] [17].
- **Panel scoring is independent then averaged** [4], so a second panellist may
  weight your recovery differently from the first. This is an argument for
  making the recovery *explicit and audible*, not subtle.

A workable four-move recovery script, in order: (1) name the correction in one
short sentence; (2) state the corrected fact; (3) add the strongest piece of
new classifiable evidence you have; (4) stop. Example shape for a
wrong-example pivot: *"Can I redirect — that example shows how I planned, but
you asked how I handled the disagreement. The clearer example is X. What I did
was…"*

## Recovery by failure type

- **Blank.** Use the documented ask: request the question again, or ask for
  thinking time [9] [17]. Write the question down if you have notes with you
  and the process allows them [5] [17]. In a recorded assessment with unrecorded
  thinking time, that is what the thinking time is for [8].
- **Wrong example, spotted early.** Pivot immediately; the sunk cost is 30–40
  seconds and the alternative is spending the whole window on unscoreable
  content.
- **Wrong example, spotted late.** Do not restart. Finish the Action and
  Result, then use the "add extra information" permission to offer a second,
  better-fitting example briefly [5].
- **Contradicted detail under probing.** Correct it plainly and immediately.
  Probing exists to make the panel "fully understand the situation you are
  describing" [5]; a clean correction is compatible with that purpose, while
  defending an error invites more probing on the same weak ground.
- **Ran out of time.** Prioritise the Result over the remaining Actions — the
  descriptors and the Evaluate step both weigh what was missed [6].
- **Technical failure.** Follow the documented protocol for the process you are
  in: for the police online assessment, contact the platform immediately and do
  **not** finish the exercise, and note the 24-hour appeal deadline [8]; for a
  live video interview, tell the interviewer and stop and restart if needed
  [19].
- **Something genuinely serious happened before the assessment.** Where the
  process publishes an extenuating-circumstances route, use it *before* you
  sit the assessment — the police guidance is explicit that after-the-fact
  claims "will not be grounds for appeal" and that lack of preparation does not
  qualify [8].

## What not to do

- Do not read the panel's note-taking as feedback; one force states directly
  that the volume of notes "is not an indicator of score" [9].
- Do not treat a probing follow-up as evidence you are failing; follow-ups are
  documented as answer-driven and candidate-specific by design [5].
- Do not rehearse strengths answers to repair a weak behaviour answer — the
  strengths element is scored on an initial response and GOV.UK tells you not
  to rehearse [2] [3].
- Do not import notes into a process that forbids them; in the police online
  assessment, prepared notes "may result in an automatic failure" [8], while at
  a COPFS interview personal notes are permitted as reference material [5].

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — cited.** Interviewers make documented mistakes too, and the
fullest published catalogue in UK selection guidance is the College of
Policing's *Barriers to effective assessment* table, which names eighteen
barriers with descriptions and counter-measures [6].

| Barrier | Published description (abridged) | Published counter-measure |
|---|---|---|
| Expectancy effect | Assessors generate a positive or negative expectation from pre-assessment information (application form, word-of-mouth, appraisal) and their decisions follow it | Use ORCE; "Only give assessors information they truly need in advance" |
| Confirmatory information | Assessors "actively seek information to confirm their initial impressions"; ask questions designed to elicit confirming information or ignore contrary evidence | Comprehensive assessor training; unconscious-bias training |
| Personal liking | Evaluation influenced by liking or disliking the candidate | As above |
| Primacy effect | "Assessors are more influenced by information provided early in the assessment process" | Use ORCE |
| Stereotyping | Characteristics ascribed to all individuals in a group | Assessor training |
| Prototyping | Assessors believe there is an ideal "type" for a role and are biased for or against candidates matching it | Assessor training |
| Halo / horns effect | A good showing in one exercise is assumed to predict good performance throughout (halo); a poor showing likewise (horns) | Assessor training; use ORCE |
| Quota effect | Decisions affected by pre-set employment quotas | Assessor training |
| Contrast effect | "Candidate's performance is compared to and evaluated against previous candidates' performance" | Assessor training |
| Negative information bias | Evaluation "influenced more by negative information than positive" | Assessor training |
| Similar to me effect | Assessor favours candidates similar to them | Assessor training |
| Non-verbal communication | Assessors misinterpret non-verbal communication when rating | Bias training; use ORCE |
| Fundamental attribution error | Causes of behaviour attributed to personality rather than situational factors | Bias training; use ORCE |
| Information overload / selective attention | Assessors "have a lot of information to contend with and may consider a fraction of the available evidence" | Design in breaks; do not over-schedule one assessor |
| Fatigue | Where mental demands outweigh available attention, assessors attend only to what they consider salient | As above; use ORCE |
| Rushing | "Assessors may cut corners due to rushing to meet logistical arrangements" | As above |
| Central tendency bias | Scores consistently clustered mid-scale, avoiding the extremes | Train assessors in scale use; "encouraged to use the full length of the scale" |
| Leniency / stringency | Scores consistently at the higher end (leniency) or lower end (stringency) of the scale | As above; use ORCE |

Two of these bear directly on candidate recovery. **Primacy effect** means a
weak opening is disproportionately influential — which is precisely why the
guidance prescribes ORCE, a model that defers classification and evaluation
until after the candidate has finished [6]. **Confirmatory information** means
a panellist who forms an early view may probe to confirm it; a candidate whose
recovery is explicit and evidence-bearing gives the counter-evidence a
disciplined assessor is trained to record.

The wider assessor-side apparatus is documented: ORCE "should be used with
paired assessment or in groups with assessors first carrying out each stage
independently and then meeting to discuss their grades and evidence and then
agreeing on a final grade" [6]; assessor training or briefing should as a
minimum cover the role being assessed for, a shared understanding of the
criteria, and familiarisation with the questions and with the competencies and
values [6]; and calibration ("benchmarking or frame of reference training")
should happen "in advance of the first live candidate assessment" [6]. Cabinet
Office guidance mandates the equivalent for Civil Service delegated grades:
independent per-question scoring, discussion of marked variances, and
reconciliation "by taking an arithmetic average" [4]. ACAS adds the baseline
that "each interview should be carried out by more than 1 person to reduce the
risk of discriminating" and that employers should "ask each applicant the same
questions where possible" [16].

Finally, the College's online assessment process documents structural
de-biasing that candidates rarely know about: "The assessors will only know
your name and email address. They will not have seen any other information
about you and they will not know whether you have applied before" [8] — a
direct operationalisation of the expectancy-effect counter-measure.

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact. Each pitfall names
the documented criterion it violates.**

## Candidate-side

- **Treating buzzwords as evidence.** Violates the explicit force guidance that
  "buzz words don't score" [9] [10] and the ORCE requirement that assessors
  classify *behaviour*, not vocabulary [6]. Symptom: the answer names the
  competency more often than it describes an action.
- **Answering the competency instead of the question.** The single mistake both
  published force lists put first [9] [10]. Symptom: you could have given the
  same answer to any of three different questions.
- **Level mismatch.** Violates the grade-specific structure of Civil Service
  Behaviours [1] and the three-level CVF structure [7]. Two directions: a
  senior candidate evidencing only practitioner behaviours, and a junior
  candidate claiming strategic scope that the descriptor does not ask for.
- **"We" throughout.** Violates the no-interpretation rule in ORCE Record [6]
  and the explicit COPFS instruction [5]; contradicts the published marker of
  good evidence [11].
- **Reusing one example.** Capped by the no-double-counting rule [6] and
  advised against directly [21]. Especially costly where no more than four
  behaviours are assessed [4].
- **Rehearsing strengths answers.** Violates the explicit GOV.UK instruction
  [2] [3]. This is a mistake *caused* by good STAR discipline over-applied:
  the technique that helps for behaviours is the wrong tool for strengths.
- **Reciting notes.** COPFS permits notes but states the board "are not looking
  for answers by rote" and that notes "should only be used as reference
  material" [5]; in the police online assessment, prepared notes may trigger an
  automatic failure [8].
- **All Situation, no Action.** Contradicts "dedicate most of your response to
  the Action part" [21] and starves the positive-evidence column the
  descriptors weigh [5] [6].
- **No measurable Result.** Forfeits the evidence the STAR definition [18] and
  the Evaluate step [6] both expect.
- **Unexplained abbreviations.** Named as a mistake [9]; the assessor cannot
  classify what they cannot decode.
- **Talking past the timer in recorded video.** Windows are fixed and visible
  [8] [20]; there is no interviewer to invite you to finish [23].
- **Not checking the retake policy before starting a recorded interview.**
  Retakes "vary" and the deadline is typically about a week [20]; the National
  Careers Service advises using practice questions to find out whether you can
  stop or re-record "if something goes wrong" [19].
- **Dominating a group exercise.** Directly contradicts the published
  criterion, "contributing without trying to dominate" [22]; compounded by
  forgetting that the employer "may choose to appoint some, all or none of you"
  [22].
- **Switching off outside the assessed exercise.** York warns candidates "may
  be assessed throughout the time you are with the employer", including
  behaviour towards non-assessing staff [22].
- **Believing the myths.** Three are contradicted in published force guidance:
  that there is a fixed pass/fail quota, that there are trick questions, and
  that the volume of panel note-taking signals your score [9] [10].
- **Spending recovery time on apology.** Not named in any source as a mistake,
  so this is analysis only: given that scoring rests on classified behavioural
  evidence [6], apology produces none.

## Interviewer-side

- **Reading the application form for atmosphere rather than need.** Feeds the
  expectancy effect; the published counter-measure is to give assessors only
  information they truly need in advance [6].
- **Probing to confirm rather than to understand.** The confirmatory-information
  barrier names exactly this: "Interviewers tend to ask questions designed to
  elicit information confirming their initial impressions" [6].
- **Recording interpretation instead of behaviour.** ORCE Record requires "No
  assuming or interpreting" and notes "as close to verbatim as possible" [6].
- **Classifying while the candidate is still speaking**, or classifying the
  same behaviour twice — both contrary to the published Classify step [6].
- **Conferring before scoring independently.** Cabinet Office guidance requires
  independent per-question scoring first, then reconciliation by arithmetic
  average [4]; ORCE likewise requires each stage independently before assessors
  meet [6].
- **Back-to-back scheduling.** Named under information overload, fatigue and
  rushing, with the published fix being to "factor in breaks and not schedule
  the assessment of too many candidates by one assessor" [6].
- **Living in the middle of the scale.** Central tendency is a named barrier;
  assessors "should be encouraged to use the full length of the scale" [6].
- **Penalising a candidate for using a documented permission.** Asking for a
  question to be repeated, for thinking time, or to add information later is
  published candidate guidance [5] [9] [10] [17]; treating it as weakness
  scores the candidate on something other than the criteria.
- **Solo interviewing.** Contrary to the ACAS recommendation that each
  interview be carried out by more than one person [16], and to the COPFS
  practice of a panel of at least two [5].

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.** Drills a reader can run this week.

1. **The strike-through audit (20 minutes).** Record one answer. Transcribe it.
   Strike every sentence that an assessor following the ORCE Record rule — "no
   assuming or interpreting" [6] — could not attribute to you. Rewrite so that
   at least 80% survives.
2. **Level calibration (30 minutes).** Print the level descriptors for your
   target role — CVF Level 1/2/3 [7] or the Civil Service Behaviour level for
   your grade [1]. Beside each of your top four examples, write which level its
   evidence sits at. Reassign or re-scope any example that lands off-level.
3. **The four-behaviour budget (20 minutes).** Take a real advert. List the
   behaviours assessed — Cabinet Office guidance recommends no more than four
   [4]. Now map your examples so that no example is doing more than one job.
   Where two behaviours share an example, find a replacement, because a
   behaviour is classified once and never double-counted [6].
4. **Descriptor self-scoring (30 minutes).** Score three of your recorded
   answers against the published A–D descriptors [6] and again against the
   Success Profiles 7-point wording [5]. For each, write the sentence a
   sceptical assessor would put in the "areas for development" column.
5. **Recovery rehearsal (30 minutes, with a partner).** Ask your partner to
   interrupt you three times: once to say your example does not answer the
   question, once to probe a detail you will have to correct, and once to
   signal that time is nearly up. Rehearse the four-move script — name the
   correction, state the corrected fact, add new evidence, stop — until it
   takes under fifteen seconds.
6. **Timed-window drill (20 minutes).** Answer three questions under recorded
   conditions: one minute of silent thinking, then a hard cut at your platform's
   limit [8] [20]. Practise landing the Result before the cut, not after it.
7. **Process-rules checklist (10 minutes, before every assessment).** Write down
   the answers to: are notes permitted? [5] [8] how many retakes? [20] what is
   the completion deadline? [20] who do I contact for technical failure, and
   what is the appeal window? [8] What is the adjustments route? [13] [14]
8. **Group-exercise self-observation (one exercise).** In your next group task,
   track your own airtime and count how many times you brought in someone
   quieter — the published criterion is "contributing without trying to
   dominate" [22].
9. **Interviewer bias audit (for HR readers, 45 minutes).** After your next
   panel, take the barriers table [6] and mark honestly which three most
   plausibly affected your scoring. Check your process against the published
   counter-measures: information given in advance, breaks scheduled,
   independent scoring before discussion [4] [6].
10. **Feedback harvest.** Where feedback is offered, take it. Two forces commit
    to providing feedback within three weeks of an unsuccessful force interview
    and describe it as "pivotal to learning" [9] [10]; the National Careers
    Service likewise advises asking the interviewer "for feedback on how it went
    and where you could have done better" [17].

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — cited. Signpost only; the fairness-bias-legal deep-dive
treats these in full.**

- **Adjustments are the correct route for a foreseeable "mistake" caused by a
  disability or condition.** Under the Equality Act 2010 s.20, an employer is
  under a duty to make reasonable adjustments where a provision, criterion or
  practice puts a disabled person at a substantial disadvantage [12]. GOV.UK's
  candidate-facing guidance sets out that disabled applicants can ask for
  reasonable adjustments to the recruitment process, giving examples such as
  changes to the interview format and extra time [13]. The practical point for
  this document: a timed video window or a rapid-fire panel is a provision,
  criterion or practice — the remedy is to request an adjustment in advance,
  not to attempt recovery in the moment. Published processes reflect this:
  Devon & Cornwall and Hampshire both direct candidates to the recruitment team
  for adjustments before interview [9] [10], the College notes that assessment
  timings "may vary if you have a timings-related reasonable adjustment" [8],
  and COPFS invites adjustment requests within the application form [5].
- **Pre-offer health and disability enquiries are restricted.** Equality Act
  2010 s.60 restricts enquiries about disability and health before a job offer,
  subject to specified exceptions [15]. A candidate volunteering a health
  explanation for a poor answer, and a panel probing it, both stray into
  territory the section governs; the adjustments route exists precisely so that
  the interview itself does not have to become that conversation.
- **EU institutions.** EPSO publishes a specific-adjustments route for
  selection tests, assessed case by case by its Accessibility Team, requested
  through the published procedure rather than raised at the test itself [14].
- **Fairness of the assessment is a documented employer obligation, not a
  favour.** The College's guidance frames its bias catalogue and ORCE model as
  the means of delivering "objective assessment decisions" [6]; ACAS ties
  multi-person interviewing and consistent questioning directly to reducing the
  risk of discriminating [16]. A candidate who believes an assessment was
  procedurally unfair has a published route in some processes — the College's
  written appeal to the recruiting force within 24 hours [8].

# Risks & Caveats

- **The two published "common mistakes" lists are force-level candidate
  guidance, not national standards.** Hampshire's document is aimed at
  transferees and re-joiners [9]; Devon & Cornwall's at force interviews [10].
  Their overlap is strong evidence that the mistakes are real and recognised,
  but neither is a College of Policing national publication, and neither is
  dated on its face. Treat them as named, attributable practitioner-official
  sources rather than as framework text.
- **`cop-vbr-selection-2018` predates the 2024 CVF.** Only its
  framework-agnostic process content — ORCE, the barriers table, the example
  rating scale, assessor training and calibration — is used here [6]. Its
  competency *names* are 2016-era and are not reproduced.
- **The A–D scale is published as "an example rating scale"** [6], not as a
  mandated national instrument. Do not present a specific force's grade
  boundaries as universal.
- **Recovery guidance is synthesis.** No located source publishes a
  "what to do after a mistake" protocol for candidates. Everything in Model
  Answer Guidance is inference from documented process mechanics (ORCE
  sequencing [6], independent per-question scoring [4], published candidate
  permissions [5] [9] [10] [17]), and is labelled as coaching. It has not been
  validated against outcome data.
- **Process rules are not portable.** The clearest example in this document is
  notes: permitted as reference material at a COPFS interview [5], potentially
  an automatic failure in the police online assessment [8]. Any recovery tactic
  must be checked against the specific process.
- **Video-interview parameters vary by platform and are practitioner-sourced.**
  The one-to-three-minute window, the roughly one-week deadline and the varying
  retake policy come from a careers-service article [20]; the College's 60/300
  second structure is official but specific to the police online assessment [8].
- **EU coverage is thin by necessity.** EPSO's current competition model has no
  competency interview, so no EU-official source publishes candidate mistakes
  at interview; the EU material here is limited to the adjustments route [14].
  UK sources carry the analytical weight, and readers preparing for
  EU-institution selection should treat the mechanics as transferable analysis,
  not as EPSO-published guidance.
- **Assessor-bias counter-measures are prescriptions, not evidence of
  efficacy.** The barriers table lists what should be done [6]; it does not
  publish evidence that ORCE or unconscious-bias training measurably reduces
  the biases named.

# Open Questions

- Does the College of Policing publish a national candidate-facing
  "common mistakes" list, or is the force-level guidance [9] [10] the only
  published articulation? Resolving this would raise the mistake catalogue
  towards High confidence.
- Is there any published UK guidance on what a candidate should do *after* a
  mistake mid-interview, beyond the permissions to ask for repetition,
  clarification and additional information [5] [9] [17]? Nothing was located as
  of 2026-07-20.
- Do panels in practice re-score a question after a candidate returns to it at
  the end under the COPFS "add extra information" permission [5], or is the
  addition recorded against the closing section? The published guidance does not
  say.
- What proportion of the 18 named assessor barriers [6] are actually addressed
  in current force and departmental assessor training? Training content was not
  located.
- Are the Hampshire and Devon & Cornwall guidance documents dated, versioned or
