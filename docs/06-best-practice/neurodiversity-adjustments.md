# Executive Summary

This document is **signposting and practical guidance for both candidates and
interviewers — it is not legal or medical advice.** It does not diagnose any
condition, and it does not tell anyone whether or not to disclose a disability
or neurodivergence. It sets out what UK and EU law, regulators, official
recruiters and professional bodies actually publish about adjustments (UK) and
reasonable accommodations (EU) in competency-based selection, and it reasons
about *why* competency interviews can disadvantage some neurodivergent
candidates strictly from **documented assessment mechanics**, not assertion.

The legal spine is settled and citable. In the UK the Equality Act 2010 imposes
a three-part duty to make reasonable adjustments and forbids charging the
disabled person for them (s.20) [1]; makes discrimination bite on the
*arrangements* an employer makes for deciding whom to offer employment — which
is what makes interview *design* actionable (s.39) [2]; restricts pre-offer
health and disability enquiries, with the important nuance that asking is not
itself the contravention but *relying on the answer* may be (s.60) [3]; and
places a public sector equality duty on public authorities (s.149) [4]. In the
EU, Council Directive 2000/78/EC requires reasonable accommodation for disabled
persons (art. 5) and expressly covers "selection criteria and recruitment
conditions" (art. 3(1)(a)) [14]. Named neurodiversity-specific recruitment
guidance of adequate quality **does exist** — ACAS [15] [16] and the CIPD [17]
both publish it — so this document reports that as a positive finding, not a
verified negative.

Confidence is **Medium.** Every evidence-layer claim rests on a statute,
regulator, official recruiter or professional body. But the document's central
analytical move — connecting documented interview mechanics to disadvantage for
specific neurodivergent profiles — is labelled synthesis, and the Question Bank
is almost entirely composed because no authoritative body publishes an
adjustment-request question set. Those are honest Medium markers, not failures.

# Purpose

This document answers two practical questions. For a **candidate**: *"What
adjustments can I ask for in a competency interview, how do I ask, and what are
the documented consequences of disclosing or not?"* For an **interviewer or HR
designer**: *"What does the law, and what do the regulators and professional
bodies, tell me a fair, defensible competency process looks like for
neurodivergent and disabled candidates, and how do I audit my own process?"*

A backlog review found neurodiversity mentioned in nine of this knowledge
base's documents but treated properly in none. This is the dedicated treatment.
It goes deeper than the Best Practice foundation's signpost-level Legal &
Fairness note and the fairness-bias-legal deep-dive, focusing specifically on
adjustments and neurodivergent candidates rather than discrimination law in
general.

# Scope

**Covered:** the UK and EU legal spine for adjustments/accommodations in
selection; what adjustments look like per competency format (interview, panel,
asynchronous video, assessment centre); the documented mechanics by which
competency assessment can disadvantage some neurodivergent candidates;
Disability Confident and guaranteed-interview schemes; assessor-side duties
(bias, structure, automated-decision safeguards); and named
neurodiversity-specific recruitment guidance.

**Not covered:** medical descriptions of any condition; whether an individual
"counts" as disabled in a specific case (that is a legal test on the facts);
advice on whether to disclose (presented as options, never a recommendation);
full discrimination-law analysis (see fairness-bias-legal); and framework
mechanics in general (see the UK/EU framework documents). This document is
UK-and-EU in market and does not cover non-EU jurisdictions.

**This is not legal or medical advice.** Individuals facing a specific decision
should seek advice from a qualified adviser (for example a solicitor, ACAS, or
an occupational-health professional).

# Definitions

- **Neurodivergent / neurodiversity** — ACAS describes neurodiversity as the
  natural range of difference in human brain function; "some well-known types of
  neurodivergence are ADHD (attention deficit hyperactivity disorder), autism,
  dyslexia and dyspraxia" [16]. Used here descriptively, not as a clinical or
  legal category.
- **Reasonable adjustment (UK)** — the Equality Act 2010 s.20 duty: where a
  provision, criterion or practice, a physical feature, or the absence of an
  auxiliary aid puts a disabled person at a substantial disadvantage, to take
  such steps as it is reasonable to take to avoid it [1].
- **Reasonable accommodation (EU)** — the Directive 2000/78/EC art. 5 duty:
  "appropriate measures, where needed in a particular case, to enable a person
  with a disability to have access to, participate in, or advance in
  employment", short of a disproportionate burden [14]. EPSO uses the term
  "Reasonable Accommodations" [12].
- **Disclosure** — telling an employer about a disability or neurodivergence.
  This document treats disclosure as the candidate's decision and never advises
  for or against it.
- **Disability Confident** — a voluntary UK government scheme (three levels)
  whose Level 1 commitments include offering an interview to disabled people and
  providing reasonable adjustments [6].

from four authoritative families, each definitive for a different part.

| Source family | Authority for | Key sources |
|---|---|---|
| UK statute | The reasonable-adjustments duty; reach of discrimination law into interview design; pre-offer enquiry limits; the public sector duty | Equality Act 2010 ss.20 [1], 39 [2], 60 [3], 149 [4] |
| UK official recruiters & regulators | Practical adjustment examples; Disability Confident; automated-decision duties | GOV.UK adjustments [5], Disability Confident [6]; ICO Recruitment rewired [10] [11] |
| EU official | The EU accommodation duty and EPSO practice | Directive 2000/78/EC [14]; EPSO Reasonable Accommodations [12] [13] |
| Professional & advisory bodies | Neurodiversity-specific recruitment guidance | ACAS [15] [16]; CIPD [17] |

The **UK duty** is triggered by "a substantial disadvantage… in comparison with
persons who are not disabled", and its three requirements cover a provision,
criterion or practice; a physical feature; and the provision of an auxiliary aid
or service (s.20(2)–(5)) [1]. Crucially for recruitment, **s.39 makes that duty
apply to employers and forbids discrimination "in the arrangements A makes for
deciding to whom to offer employment"** (s.39(1)(a), (5)) [2] — the interview,
its format and its scoring are all "arrangements".

The **EU duty** is materially parallel: art. 5 requires reasonable accommodation
for disabled persons short of a disproportionate burden, and art. 3(1)(a) states
the Directive applies to "conditions for access to employment… including
selection criteria and recruitment conditions" [14]. EPSO — the EU institutions'
selection office — publishes a standing **Reasonable Accommodations** process:
candidates request accommodations in the application form with supporting
documentation, and "each request is assessed individually by the EPSO
Accessibility Team" on a case-by-case basis [12] [13].

Named **neurodiversity-specific** recruitment guidance of adequate quality
exists on both the advisory side (ACAS's neurodiversity-at-work guidance,
updated 2025 [15] [16]; the CIPD's *Neuroinclusion at work* guide, 2024 [17])
and the official side (GOV.UK adjustments [5]; Disability Confident [6]). This
is recorded as a **positive finding** — a verified negative was *not* required.

# Core Reference

**Evidence layer — cited.**

## The three-part UK duty and the "no charge" rule

Equality Act 2010 s.20 sets out a duty "in three requirements": (1) where a
**provision, criterion or practice** puts a disabled person at a substantial
disadvantage, to take reasonable steps to avoid it; (2) the same where a
**physical feature** causes the disadvantage; (3) where a disabled person would
be disadvantaged **but for the provision of an auxiliary aid**, to take
reasonable steps to provide it [1]. Where a requirement relates to information,
reasonable steps "include steps for ensuring that… the information is provided
in an accessible format" (s.20(6)) [1]. And the disabled person may **not** be
made to pay: a person subject to the duty "is not… entitled to require a
disabled person… to pay to any extent A's costs of complying with the duty"
(s.20(7)) [1].

## Interview design is an "arrangement"

Section 39(1) prohibits an employer discriminating against an applicant "(a) in
the arrangements A makes for deciding to whom to offer employment", and s.39(5)
states "A duty to make reasonable adjustments applies to an employer" [2]. This
is the provision that makes competency-interview *design* — question format,
timing, medium, scoring — reviewable, not just the final hiring decision.

## Pre-offer enquiries: the s.60 nuance

Under s.60(1) a person to whom an application is made "must not ask about the
health of the applicant… before offering work" [3]. But the statute is careful:
s.60(3) says "A does not contravene a relevant disability provision merely by
asking about B's health; but A's conduct **in reliance on information given in
response** may be a contravention" [3]. There are explicit exceptions in
s.60(6), including asking so far as necessary for "establishing whether a duty
to make reasonable adjustments is or will be imposed… in connection with a
requirement to undergo an assessment", to establish whether the applicant can
carry out a function "intrinsic to the work", and for diversity monitoring [3].
In plain terms: an employer **may** ask whether you need an adjustment to the
selection process; what it may not do is use disclosed health information to
discriminate.

## The public sector equality duty

Section 149 requires public authorities, in exercising their functions, to have
"due regard" to the need to eliminate discrimination, advance equality of
opportunity, and foster good relations across protected characteristics,
including disability [4]. Advancing equality of opportunity "involves having due
regard, in particular, to the need to… remove or minimise disadvantages" and to
"take steps to meet the needs" of people sharing a characteristic that differ
from others' needs (s.149(3)), and s.149(4) specifies that meeting disabled
persons' needs includes "steps to take account of disabled persons'
disabilities" [4]. For a public-sector competency panel, that is a positive,
anticipatory duty — not merely a duty to respond to a request.

## What adjustments actually look like, by format

The examples below are all published by official recruiters, regulators or
advisory bodies. They are non-exhaustive; the duty is always to the individual.

- **Questions or task outline in advance.** ACAS advises that, to make interviews
  inclusive, employers should "where possible, allow applicants to see the
  questions before the interview" [15]; the CIPD guide recommends "providing the
  outline of any tasks or questions in advance" [17]. ACAS's worked example: an
  autistic applicant asks to see the interview questions in advance and the
  employer agrees to share most of them, holding back one question that is
  specifically designed to test how someone deals with unexpected questions [15].
- **Extra time / adjusted timing.** The CIPD lists "longer answering time" as an
  interview adjustment [17]; ACAS lists "extra time to read information and
  complete tasks" among written-communication adjustments [16]; GOV.UK's
  recruitment-adjustments guidance illustrates the wider duty with examples such
  as completing a written test on a computer [5].
- **A written or phone alternative to asynchronous video.** The ICO found that
  employers "typically provided reasonable adjustments for people with
  disabilities who weren't able to access automated recruitment methods", and
  that "this often involved a phone interview" [10]. ACAS advises employers to
  "offer different ways to complete the application" and to "consider
  alternatives to interviews, such as short paid work trials or practical
  assessments" [15].
- **A quiet room / adjusted environment.** ACAS advises holding "interviews or
  assessments in suitable, quiet spaces away from other distractions", and lists
  environmental adjustments such as "a quiet place to work away from
  distractions" and noise-reducing equipment [15] [16]. The CIPD advises choosing
  "a suitable, quiet space free from distractions" [17].
- **Breaks, support workers, accessible formats, physical adjustments.** The s.20
  auxiliary-aid and accessible-format duties cover support at interview and
  materials in an accessible format [1]; GOV.UK's example of a reasonable
  recruitment adjustment is "allowing wheelchair users to have their interview on
  the ground floor" and "candidates to complete a written test using a computer"
  [5]. EPSO's accommodations cover scheduling, special equipment, test formats
  and layouts, personal devices, and assistance "to neurodiverse candidates" [12].
- **Alternative assessment-centre exercises.** ACAS's advice to "consider
  alternatives to interviews, such as short paid work trials or practical
  assessments" [15], and the CIPD's suggestion of "work trials, practical
  assessments… and mini apprenticeships" [17], map onto the assessment-centre
  formats covered elsewhere in this knowledge base (see assessment-centre-exercises).

## No diagnosis required (UK advisory position)

ACAS states plainly that "a worker does not need a diagnosis to be considered
disabled under the Equality Act 2010" and that an employer "should offer workers
support whether or not they have a diagnosis… This includes making reasonable
adjustments" [16]. It records real reasons a candidate may lack a diagnosis —
long NHS waiting times, cost of private diagnosis, or personal choice not to
share one [16]. (Note the contrast with EU practice: EPSO operates an explicit
"No certificates no adjustments" documentary policy for its accommodations
process [13] — a genuine UK/EU divergence, not a sourcing error.)

# Question Bank

These are **process and preparation prompts**, not competency questions to be
scored. Group A helps a **candidate** think through requesting adjustments and
what to ask the employer; Group B is a **process-audit set for interviewers and
HR**. Almost every entry is *(composed)*: no authoritative body publishes an
"adjustment-request question bank", so these are written for this knowledge base
in the style of the cited guidance and must not be read as leaked or official
questions. A few are *(sourced)* where a named body poses the point directly.

## Group A — For candidates: requesting adjustments

1. What specifically about the interview format — timing, medium, environment, question style — creates a substantial disadvantage for me? *(composed)*
2. Do I want to request an adjustment to the recruitment process, and if so which of the documented examples fits my needs? *(composed)*
3. Would seeing the questions or a task outline in advance help me, and is that something I can ask for? *(sourced: ACAS — allow applicants to see the questions before the interview)* [15]
4. Would I benefit from longer answering time or extra reading time? *(sourced: CIPD — longer answering time)* [17]
5. If this stage is an asynchronous or automated video, can I ask for a phone or written alternative? *(sourced: ICO Recruitment rewired — often a phone interview)* [10]
6. Would a quiet room, away from distractions, reduce sensory load for me? *(sourced: ACAS — quiet spaces away from distractions)* [15]
7. Do I need breaks during a long assessment day, and how would I ask for them? *(composed)*
8. Would an alternative exercise — a work trial or practical task — assess me more fairly than a conventional interview? *(sourced: ACAS — alternatives to interviews such as short paid work trials or practical assessments)* [15]
9. Do I want to request interview materials in an accessible format? *(composed, in the style of Equality Act 2010 s.20 subsection 6)* [1]
10. How and when do I want to make the request — at application, at invitation, or later — and to whom? *(composed)*
11. Do I understand that the employer may ask whether I need an adjustment, and that this is permitted even before an offer? *(composed, in the style of Equality Act 2010 s.60)* [3]
12. Am I clear that I cannot be charged for a reasonable adjustment? *(composed, in the style of Equality Act 2010 s.20 subsection 7)* [1]
13. Do I know whether this employer is Disability Confident, and what its published interview commitment is? *(composed, in the style of GOV.UK Disability Confident)* [6]
14. Have I decided, on my own terms, whether to disclose a diagnosis — knowing a diagnosis is not legally required to receive support in the UK? *(sourced: ACAS — a worker does not need a diagnosis)* [16]
15. If I am applying to an EU institution, have I read the EPSO Reasonable Accommodations process and its documentation requirements? *(composed, in the style of EPSO Reasonable Accommodations)* [12]
16. If an adjustment I asked for is refused or offered in a different form, do I know who to ask for the reasons? *(composed)*
17. Have I confirmed whether an asynchronous video stage allows re-records, and how many? *(composed, in the style of the video-asynchronous-interviews guidance)* [18]
18. What do I need in place to be able to demonstrate my competencies "on an equal basis with other candidates"? *(sourced: EPSO — compete on an equal basis)* [13]

## Group B — For interviewers and HR: a process audit

1. Do our job adverts state clearly that applicants can request adjustments and do not need a diagnosis to do so? *(sourced: ACAS — make it clear applicants can ask for reasonable adjustments and do not need a diagnosis)* [15]
2. Have we reviewed the whole recruitment process to check it does not exclude neurodivergent applicants? *(sourced: ACAS — review your recruitment process)* [15]
3. Are interviewers trained not to penalise unconventional body language or an apparent lack of social interaction? *(sourced: CIPD — avoid penalising unconventional body language)* [17]
4. Have we asked whether each candidate needs an adjustment, rather than waiting to be told? *(sourced: GOV.UK — you can ask if a candidate needs an adjustment)* [5]
5. Where we ask about health or adjustments before offer, are we sure we are not acting in reliance on the answer to discriminate? *(composed, in the style of Equality Act 2010 s.60 subsection 3)* [3]
6. Is our interview a test of the ability to do the job, or has it become a test of social competence? *(sourced: CIPD — interviews are generally more a test of social competence)* [17]
7. Can we offer questions or a task outline in advance, holding back only questions whose purpose is genuinely to test the unexpected? *(sourced: ACAS worked example)* [15]
8. Do we offer a quiet, low-distraction room and check lighting and sensory factors? *(sourced: ACAS and CIPD — quiet space free from distractions)* [15] [17]
9. For any asynchronous or automated stage, do we offer an accessible alternative such as a phone interview? *(sourced: ICO Recruitment rewired)* [10]
10. If an automated tool scores candidates, is there meaningful human involvement in every decision, not rubber-stamping? *(sourced: ICO — rubber-stamping is not meaningful human involvement)* [11]
11. As a public authority, have we had due regard to removing disadvantage and meeting disabled candidates' different needs? *(composed, in the style of Equality Act 2010 s.149)* [4]
12. Do our scorers understand they classify only the behavioural evidence a candidate actually states, and cannot infer the unstated? *(sourced: GOV.UK brief guide — the panel cannot infer what is not included)* [8]
13. Have we checked our assessor guidance against a documented bias catalogue? *(composed, in the style of the College of Policing bias catalogue)* [9]
14. Is the same structured question set used for every candidate, with adjustments layered on rather than a different, easier interview? *(composed, in the style of ACAS and CIPD structured-interview guidance)* [15]
15. Do we record adjustment requests and outcomes so we can show a fair, consistent process? *(composed)*
16. If we are Disability Confident, are we actually delivering the interview commitment we signed up to? *(sourced: GOV.UK Disability Confident commitments)* [6]
17. Do we review adjustments with the individual and try things out, recognising the same condition affects people differently? *(sourced: ACAS — adjustments that suit one person might not help another)* [16]
18. When we design a strengths element that expects an unrehearsed response, have we considered its impact on candidates who prepare to manage anxiety, and offered an alternative if needed? *(composed, in the style of GOV.UK Success Profiles: Strengths)* [7]

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact, and it is not legal
advice.** It points back to the evidence above.

- **Requesting an adjustment (candidate).** Keep it about the *process barrier*
  and the *adjustment*, not a medical account you do not wish to give. A workable
  shape: name the stage, name the barrier, name the adjustment, offer a time to
  discuss. For example: "For the video stage I'd work best with a phone or live
  alternative — is that possible?" This mirrors the ICO's documented practice of
  a phone alternative to automated methods [10] and ACAS's advice that employers
  offer different formats [15]. In the UK you are not required to justify it with
  a diagnosis [16], though EU/EPSO processes do require documentation [13].
- **Timing the request.** GOV.UK is explicit that an employer *may* ask about
  adjustment needs and *may* wait to be told [5]; either way, requesting early
  gives the employer time to arrange a quiet room, extra time or a support
  worker. There is no single "right" moment — the documented options are at
  application, at invitation, or once a specific stage is known.
- **Deciding on disclosure.** This document does not advise for or against
  disclosure. What is *documented* is: (a) in the UK a diagnosis is not required
  to receive support [16]; (b) an employer may not use disclosed health
  information to discriminate [3]; and (c) an employer can only adjust for a
  barrier it is aware of or could reasonably be expected to know about. Weigh
  these documented points yourself.
- **Interviewer preparation (HR).** The strongest documented single move is to
  **separate the competency being assessed from the social performance of the
  interview**. The CIPD frames the risk directly: interviews can become "more a
  test of social competence than ability to perform specific job tasks" [17].
  Offering questions in advance [15] [17] and training assessors not to read
  meaning into body language [17] both attack that specific failure.
- **Layer adjustments onto the same structure, don't build a weaker interview.**
  Structured, consistent questioning is the documented fairness mechanism
  (Assessor Perspective, below); an adjustment changes *access* to the assessment
  (more time, a quiet room, a different medium), not the *standard* being
  assessed. ACAS's worked example holds back exactly the one question whose
  purpose is "dealing with the unexpected", precisely because sharing it would
  change what is measured [15].

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — cited.** This section explains, from documented assessment
mechanics, both *how competency interviews are marked* and *why that can
disadvantage some neurodivergent candidates*. The mechanics are cited facts; the
inference to disadvantage is labelled as reasoning in Common Pitfalls.

## Assessors score stated evidence — and cannot infer the unstated

Official Civil Service guidance tells candidates that "the sift panel cannot
infer what is not included in the example and can only assess what you have
actually written" [8], and advises "Use I not we" so the assessor can see the
candidate's own contribution [8]. The College of Policing's values-based
recruitment guidance sets out the **ORCE** model — assessors observe, record,
classify, evaluate and score each answer independently — and, in its CVF
context, instructs assessors to "classify each behaviour once" and to evaluate on
"the quality and quantity of the classified evidence" [9]. The consequence is
structural: **an assessor marks the behavioural evidence a candidate makes
explicit, not the competence a candidate holds but does not narrate.**

## Strengths elements expect unrehearsed answers

The Civil Service Success Profiles *Strengths* guidance tells candidates "Do not
rehearse your answers - because we are looking for your initial response", and
that there are "no right or wrong answers to these questions" [7]. The design
intent is a spontaneous reaction.

## Asynchronous and automated formats remove clarification and human nuance

The University of Oxford Careers Service records that in recorded video
interviews "there is no opportunity for the interviewer to ask for
clarification", with a set time to answer per question [18]. The ICO's
*Recruitment rewired* work documents automated tools that score video-interview
transcriptions and evaluate language, tone and content, and warns that where a
human simply "rubber-stamps" a red-rated candidate there is no meaningful human
involvement — the decision is solely automated [11].

## Structure and trained, diverse panels are the documented fairness controls

The bias catalogue an assessor is measured against is real and enumerated: the
College of Policing's 2018 guidance lists **nineteen** named assessor biases —
including the expectancy effect, halo/horns, similar-to-me, stereotyping,
prototyping, contrast, non-verbal-communication bias and the fundamental
attribution error — and prescribes counter-measures in groups rather than one
per bias [9]. The documented controls are structured, consistent questioning
scored against pre-agreed criteria (Best Practice foundation), independent
per-question scoring under ORCE [9], and, for automated stages, meaningful human
involvement applied to *every* candidate, not only high scorers [11].

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — this is reasoned analysis, clearly labelled, not cited fact.**
Each pitfall connects a documented mechanic (above) to a plausible disadvantage.

## How the mechanics can disadvantage neurodivergent candidates

- **"They cannot infer the unstated" meets literal or concise answering.** A
  candidate who answers precisely and briefly, or who assumes shared context, may
  leave behavioural evidence unstated — which the assessor is documented as
  unable to credit [8] [9]. This is an *access* problem (how the format elicits
  evidence), not a competence problem. *(reasoning)*
- **"Do not rehearse" meets anxiety management.** A strengths element that
  rewards an unrehearsed, spontaneous response [7] can disadvantage a candidate
  whose way of managing interview anxiety is thorough preparation — the very
  strategy the format is designed to bypass. *(reasoning)*
- **"No clarification" meets literal interpretation.** Asynchronous video removes
  the chance to ask what a question means [18]; a candidate who interprets a
  question literally, or differently from the assessor's intent, has no route to
  recover. *(reasoning)*
- **Reading meaning into body language.** Eye contact, facial expression and
  social-interaction style are exactly what the CIPD warns interviewers not to
  penalise [17]; assessor biases such as non-verbal-communication bias and
  similar-to-me are named risks [9]. *(reasoning, tied to [9] [17])*

## Interviewer-side process failures

- **Waiting to be told, then treating a late request as a problem.** GOV.UK is
  explicit that an employer *can* ask about adjustment needs [5]; a process that
  never asks, then treats a request as disruptive, misses the anticipatory
  posture s.149 requires of public bodies [4]. *(reasoning)*
- **Using disclosed information against the candidate.** The s.60 line is fine:
  asking is permitted, acting in reliance on the answer to discriminate is the
  contravention [3]. *(reasoning)*
- **Building a weaker interview instead of an accessible one.** Swapping in easier
  questions changes the standard; the documented practice is to change access
  (time, medium, environment) while holding the construct — as in ACAS's
  hold-back-one-question example [15]. *(reasoning)*
- **Rubber-stamping automated scores.** Treated by the ICO as solely automated
  decision-making without meaningful human involvement [11]. *(reasoning)*

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.** Concrete drills for both audiences.

1. **Candidate — barrier map (20 minutes).** For each stage of a real process
   (application, any online test, video or phone screen, panel, assessment
   centre), write one line naming the specific barrier and the documented
   adjustment that would address it [5] [15] [16] [17]. Take the two
   highest-impact into a request.
2. **Candidate — draft the ask (10 minutes).** Write a three-sentence adjustment
   request: stage, barrier, adjustment. Keep it about the process, not a medical
   account. Check it against the documented examples [10] [15] [17].
3. **Candidate — disclosure decision (private).** List, for your situation, the
   documented facts only: diagnosis not required in the UK [16]; employer may not
   act in reliance on disclosed health information [3]; adjustment must be free to
   you [1]. Decide on your own terms; this document takes no side.
4. **Interviewer — advert audit.** Take a live advert. Does it name that
   adjustments can be requested and that no diagnosis is needed [15]? Does it
   separate essential from desirable criteria in clear language [15]? Fix what is
   missing.
5. **Interviewer — construct check.** For each interview question, write one line:
   *what competency does this measure?* Flag any question that in fact measures
   social fluency rather than the job competency [17]; redesign or add an
   alternative route to the evidence.
6. **Interviewer — adjustment dry run.** Take one common request (questions in
   advance) and script how you would honour it while protecting any genuine
   test-the-unexpected item, following the ACAS worked example [15].
7. **Interviewer — automation check (if used).** For any automated scoring stage,
   confirm a documented accessible alternative exists [10] and that a human with
   authority reviews every candidate's outcome, not only the top scorers [11].

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — cited. This is signposting, not legal advice.**

- **UK — the adjustments duty (Equality Act 2010, s.20).** Three requirements
  (provision/criterion/practice; physical feature; auxiliary aid), an
  accessible-format duty for information, and a rule that the disabled person may
  not be charged for compliance [1].
- **UK — reach into interview design (s.39).** Discrimination is prohibited "in
  the arrangements A makes for deciding to whom to offer employment", and the
  adjustments duty applies to employers [2].
- **UK — pre-offer enquiries (s.60).** Asking about health before offer is
  generally prohibited, but "A does not contravene… merely by asking… conduct in
  reliance on information given in response may be a contravention"; exceptions
  include establishing adjustment needs for an assessment and intrinsic-function
  and diversity-monitoring purposes [3].
- **UK — public sector equality duty (s.149).** Public authorities must have due
  regard to eliminating discrimination and advancing equality of opportunity for
  disabled people, including taking steps to meet needs that differ from those of
  non-disabled people [4].
- **UK — automated decisions (ICO).** Employers must ensure meaningful human
  involvement in each decision; rubber-stamping an automated score is solely
  automated decision-making [11]; and adjustments (often a phone interview) should
  be available where a candidate cannot access an automated method [10].
- **EU — reasonable accommodation (Directive 2000/78/EC).** Article 5 requires
  reasonable accommodation for disabled persons short of a disproportionate
  burden; article 3(1)(a) applies the Directive to "selection criteria and
  recruitment conditions" [14]. EPSO operates a standing, case-by-case
  Reasonable Accommodations process [12] [13].
- **Disclosure and data.** Adjustment requests and any disclosed health
  information are sensitive personal data; the general obligations sit with the
  fairness-bias-legal and video/automation documents. This document does not
  advise whether to disclose.

# Risks & Caveats

- **Not legal or medical advice, and no diagnosis is offered or endorsed.** Every
  individual case turns on its facts; a qualified adviser (solicitor, ACAS,
  occupational health) should be consulted for a specific decision.
- **"Disability" is a legal test, not a label this document applies.** Whether a
  given condition amounts to a disability under the Equality Act, or under
  Directive 2000/78/EC, is decided on the facts; ACAS notes neurodivergence "will
  often" but not always meet the threshold [16].
- **UK/EU divergence on documentation is real.** The UK advisory position is that
  no diagnosis is required for support [16]; EPSO applies an explicit "No
  certificates no adjustments" policy [13]. Do not generalise one to the other.
- **Disability Confident is a voluntary self-declared scheme.** Membership signals
  commitments (including an interview commitment) [6]; it is not a regulator's
  certification that any given process is compliant, and the precise interview
  guarantee — for example, whether it applies to applicants meeting minimum
  criteria — is set in each employer's implementation.
- **The disadvantage analysis is reasoning, not measurement.** The mechanics are
  cited [7] [8] [9] [18]; the inference that they disadvantage specific
  neurodivergent profiles is labelled synthesis, individual and contested, and
  must not be read as a finding that any format discriminates as a matter of law.
- **Source currency.** The College of Policing 2018 guidance predates the 2024
  CVF — only its framework-agnostic process content (ORCE, the nineteen biases)
  is used here [9]; the ICO *Recruitment rewired* findings (2026) reference law
  amended by the Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 [10] [11]; the brief guide to
  competencies is cited for answer-mechanics only, not framework content [8].
