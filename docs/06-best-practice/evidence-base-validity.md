# Executive Summary

This document supplies the academic-evidence layer the rest of the knowledge
base was built without. The knowledge base is assembled almost entirely from
official and grey-literature sources; a backlog review found several open
questions that could only be answered by the occupational / industrial-
organisational (I-O) psychology research literature. This reference reads that
literature honestly and reports what it establishes — and, where it establishes
nothing, says so plainly.

The headline finding is robust and current. Across roughly a century of research
and more than a dozen meta-analyses, **structured interviews predict job
performance substantially better than unstructured interviews**, and in the most
recent re-analysis of the field's validity estimates they emerge as the single
highest-ranked selection procedure [1] [3]. That same re-analysis (Sackett,
Zhang, Berry & Lievens, 2022–2023) **lowered** many long-quoted figures:
correcting a systematic error in how range-restriction corrections had been
applied, it puts the operational validity of structured interviews at a mean of
**.42 with an 80% credibility interval of .18 to .66** — down from the .51 figure
widely cited from Schmidt & Hunter (1998), and best read as ".42, plus or minus
.24" rather than as a fixed number [1] [3]. Structure earns its advantage through
concrete design features — job-analysed questions, the same questions for every
candidate, anchored rating scales, rating each answer, multiple trained raters,
note-taking — not through any answer mnemonic [2].

On the knowledge base's specific open questions the answers are mixed, and two
are deliberately negative. There **is** strong evidence that structure raises
validity and reduces group differences, that anchored rating scales lift both
validity and inter-rater reliability, and that accent / fluency bias in hiring
evaluations is real and measurable [2] [4]. There is **no research located** that
any particular answer *structure* (STAR versus another) raises interview scores,
nor that *Action-heavy* answers score higher — these are coaching inferences, not
findings. Confidence is **Medium**: the core validity claims rest on high-quality
open-access peer-reviewed sources, but they draw on a small number of meta-
analyses conducted largely on US samples, one supporting source was read at
abstract level only, and applying these findings to UK/EU public-sector
competency interviewing is reasoned synthesis rather than direct evidence.

# Key Takeaways

- **Structured interviews work, and the best current estimate is lower than the
  famous one.** Sackett et al. (2022) place structured-interview operational
  validity at a mean **.42** (80% credibility interval **.18–.66**), the top-
  ranked predictor in their revised table, down from Schmidt & Hunter's .51.
  *(documented)*
- **The reductions were a methods correction, not new pessimism.** Prior meta-
  analyses over-corrected for range restriction; removing that inflation lowered
  most predictors by **.10–.20** while largely preserving their rank order [1] [3].
  *(documented)*
- **Structure, not scripting, is the mechanism.** The research attributes the gain
  to job-analysed questions, identical questions per candidate, anchored rating
  scales, rating each answer, multiple raters, note-taking and interviewer
  training — a 15-component taxonomy [2]. *(documented)*
- **Past-behaviour and situational questions both predict well; past-behaviour has
  a slight edge for complex jobs**, and the literature recommends using both
  rather than choosing [2]. *(documented)*
- **Anchored rating scales measurably help.** Interviews using them show higher
  validity (.35 vs .26) and higher inter-rater reliability (.77 vs .73) than those
  without [2]. *(documented)*
- **Structure narrows group differences.** High-structure interviews show smaller
  demographic score gaps than low-structure ones, and large modern field studies
  find negligible gaps in well-designed structured interviews [2]. *(documented)*
- **Accent / fluency bias is evidenced.** A meta-analysis of 139 effect sizes
  finds standard-accented candidates rated more hireable (d = 0.47), an effect
  better explained by prejudice than by genuine comprehension difficulty [4].
  *(documented — abstract-level reading; see Risks & Caveats)*
- **Two of the knowledge base's questions return an honest "no research located":**
  whether a specific answer structure raises scores, and whether Action-heavy
  answers score higher. No peer-reviewed test of either was found. *(guidance)*

# Purpose

This document answers a single question the rest of the knowledge base could not:
*what does the research actually establish about whether competency / structured
interviewing works, and where does the evidence run out?* It exists because a
backlog review found five open questions across the knowledge base resting on
inference for want of an academic layer — including whether Action-heavy answers
score higher, whether answer structure affects scores, and whether accent and
fluency bias operate in structured communication scoring. It serves interviewers
who want to know whether their process is defensible on evidence, and candidates
who want to know what the research implies for how they prepare. It also lets
every other document stop hedging: a well-evidenced "no research located" is a
result other documents can cite.

# Scope

**Covered:** the meta-analytic evidence on structured-versus-unstructured
interview validity and the 2022–2023 revision of the field's validity estimates;
what the literature attributes structure's advantage to; past-behaviour versus
situational questions; inter-rater reliability, rater bias and whether training
and design counter-measures have empirical support; adverse impact and the
validity–diversity tradeoff; accent / fluency bias; and the honest limits
(publication and range-restriction issues, US-sample generalisability, and the
gap between what research measures and what candidates care about). Explicit
verdicts are given for the knowledge base's open questions.

**Not covered:** framework definitions (see the Competencies, UK Frameworks and

`alternative-answer-frameworks`); the mechanics of scoring scales (see

sources were read in full text; one was read at abstract level, which is flagged
wherever it is used.

# Definitions

- **Validity (criterion-related / operational).** The correlation between a
  selection procedure's scores and a measure of later job performance, corrected
  where appropriate for criterion unreliability and range restriction.
  "Operational validity" is the value expected in an unrestricted applicant pool
  [3].
- **Range restriction.** Under-representation of the full range of ability in a
  validation sample (e.g. because only hired people are studied), which
  *depresses* an observed correlation; corrections estimate what the correlation
  would be in the full applicant pool. Over-correcting inflates validity
  estimates — the core of the 2022 re-analysis [1] [3].
- **Credibility interval.** The band (conventionally the 10th–90th percentiles, an
  "80% credibility interval") within which the true validity is expected to fall
  across settings — a statement about real variation between jobs and designs, not
  merely sampling error [3].
- **Structured interview.** An interview standardised on question content and on
  response scoring; the research operationalises "structure" as a set of discrete
  components (see Core Reference) rather than a single on/off property [2].
- **Situational question (SQ) / past-behaviour question (PBQ).** SQs ask what a
  candidate *would* do in a hypothetical scenario; PBQs ask what they *did* do in
  a real past situation [2].
- **Adverse impact / group differences.** A substantially different selection
  rate, or mean score difference (reported as a standardised mean difference,
  Cohen's *d*), between demographic groups [2].

# Framework Context

**Evidence layer — cited.** The UK and EU competency-interview frameworks the
knowledge base documents are, in research terms, *applications* of the structured
interview. None of them cites the primary validity literature, so this section
maps the connection rather than restating framework content (for which see the
parent, `best-practice-foundation`).

| Framework artefact | What it prescribes | Which evidenced structure component it is |
|---|---|---|
| Civil Service Success Profiles 7-point scale, scored per behaviour [5] | Anchored numeric rating; rating evidence against criteria | Anchored rating scales; rate each answer [2] |
| GOV.UK brief guide to competencies [11] | Offers STAR or CAR; requires no mnemonic | Answer aid — *not* a scored structure component (see verdicts) |
| EPSO competency framework anchors [6] | Defined observable "anchors" per competency | Anchored rating scales [2] |
| College of Policing CVF assessor guidance: ORCE model, 19 named biases, four counter-measure groups [7] | Observe–record–classify–evaluate–score; rater-bias controls | Multiple trained raters; note-taking; anchored evaluation [2] |
| CIPD structured-selection guidance [8] | Same questions, same order, pre-agreed criteria | Same questions; standardised scoring [2] |

The point of the mapping is that the frameworks' defining features — anchored
scales, identical questions, multiple trained assessors, evidence-based scoring —
are precisely the structure components the I-O literature has tested and found to
raise validity and reliability [2] [3]. The frameworks are, in effect, structure
delivered under official branding. That is the strongest thing the evidence base
says in their favour, and it is said without any framework having to prove itself
individually.

# Core Reference

**Evidence layer — cited.** This section reports what the literature documents.
Its two full-text anchors are Sackett, Zhang, Berry & Lievens (2023, open access
in *Industrial and Organizational Psychology*), which draws out the applied
implications of Sackett et al. (2022, *Journal of Applied Psychology*); and
Levashina, Hartwell, Morgeson & Campion (2014, *Personnel Psychology*), a
narrative-plus-quantitative review of the structured interview.

## The headline: structured interviews are the top-ranked predictor — at a revised, lower value

Levashina et al. open by recording the field's most durable result: "One of the
most consistent findings in the history of research on the employment interview is
that structured interviews are much more reliable and valid than unstructured
interviews," supported by twelve meta-analyses [2]. Sackett et al. quantify it on
current, corrected numbers. In their revised table, **structured interviews have
the highest mean operational validity of any procedure examined, ρ = .42**, above
job-knowledge tests (.40), empirically keyed biodata (.38), work-sample tests
(.33) and general cognitive ability (.31); unstructured interviews sit far lower
at .19 [1] [3].

Crucially, these figures are *lower* than the ones the knowledge base and the
wider world inherited from Schmidt & Hunter (1998), where structured interviews
were quoted at .51 and cognitive ability at .51. Sackett et al. found that the
range-restriction corrections used in prior meta-analyses had been applied in a
way that "often result[s] in substantial overcorrection," because large correction
factors derived from predictive studies were applied to the roughly 80% of studies
that are concurrent, where restriction is usually small [1] [3]. Removing that
inflation reduced most predictors by **.10 to .20** (for example ".21 lower for
work sample tests, .20 lower for cognitive ability, .19 lower for unstructured
interviews") while largely preserving rank order [3]. The authors are explicit
that this is not a refutation of selection: composites of several predictors still
reach validities near the old single-predictor figures (a mean of about .47 on
revised values), and "our selection procedures remain useful, but selection
predictor-criterion relationships are considerably lower than previously thought"
[1] [3].

## Read the interval, not just the mean

Sackett et al. stress a point the knowledge base should adopt: the mean hides real
variation. For structured interviews the standard deviation of validity across
studies is .19, giving an **80% credibility interval of .18 to .66**. In their
words, "the validity of structured interviews should really be viewed as '.42,
plus or minus .24'" [3]. The variation is not noise: it reflects that "structured
interview" covers a wide range of quality, and that better-designed instruments
land nearer the top of the interval [3]. This is why the knowledge base's earlier
statement of "around .42 with a wide credibility interval" is **verified and
correct** — and why a single point estimate should never be quoted bare.

## Why structure helps: the 15 components

The literature does not treat structure as one thing. Levashina et al. use the
Campion, Palmer & Campion (1997) taxonomy of **15 components**, grouped into a
*content* dimension — (1) basing questions on a job analysis; (2) asking the same
questions of each applicant; (3) limiting prompting and follow-up; (4) using
better types of questions; (5) longer interviews / more questions; (6) controlling
ancillary information; (7) no questions from the applicant until the end — and an
*evaluation* dimension — (8) rating each answer or using multiple scales; (9)
using anchored rating scales; (10) taking detailed notes; (11) multiple
interviewers; (12) the same interviewers across applicants; (13) no discussion
between interviews; (14) interviewer training; (15) statistical rather than
clinical combination of ratings [2]. A survey of expert raters confirmed 14 of the
15 load onto their intended dimension, and a content analysis found studies use on
average about six components, most often job analysis, same questions, better
questions, anchored rating scales, rating each question, and training [2]. Anchored
rating scales earn a specific evidential mention: Taylor & Small's (2002) meta-
analysis, as reported in Levashina et al., found interviews with anchored scales
reached higher validity (rxy = .35 vs .26) and higher inter-rater reliability (rxx
= .77 vs .73) than those without [2].

Sackett et al. add a conceptual reason the interview-as-work-sample does well:
job-specific "sample" methods (structured interviews, job-knowledge tests, work
samples, empirically keyed biodata) out-predict general "sign" measures of
constructs such as cognitive ability and personality, supporting a strategy of
sampling job behaviour — including "tell me about a time when…" approaches — over
inferring from abstract traits [3].

## Past-behaviour vs situational questions

Both formats predict performance well. Levashina et al. conclude that "SQs have
slightly lower criterion-validity than PBQs, especially for high-complexity jobs,"
but caution that "this finding does not necessarily mean that only PBQs should be
used, as both SQs and PBQs have good validity for most jobs," and note the common
recommendation to use both because they tend to measure somewhat different
constructs and may complement each other [2]. Two nuances matter for the knowledge
base's UK-behaviour / EPSO-situational split: SQs may measure job knowledge ("what
you *should* do") more than genuine intention ("what you *would* do"), and past-
behaviour questions elicit more self-promotion while situational questions elicit
more other-focused impression management [2].

## Reliability and rater bias

Structure raises inter-rater reliability directly — anchored scales alone lift it
from .73 to .77 [2] — and Levashina et al. report that interviewer *training*
(component 14) has been found to increase the reliability of interviewer ratings
(citing Conway, Jako & Goodman, 1995) [2]. On bias, the review's meta-analysis of
group differences (below) and its discussion conclude that "structure reduces the
impact of different biasing factors on interview ratings," with well-designed
structured interviews in high-stakes settings showing "little to no effect of
demographic variables and demographic similarity" [2].

## Adverse impact and group differences

Handled neutrally: the evidence is that **structure narrows demographic score
gaps**. Huffcutt & Roth's (1998) meta-analysis, as reported in Levashina et al.,
found White–Black rating differences of about d = .25, but smaller gaps in high-
structure than low-structure interviews (d = .23 vs .32), and smaller gaps for
past-behaviour than situational questions (d = .10 vs .20) [2]. Levashina et al.'s
own updated meta-analysis found pre-1996 field studies showed a gap disadvantaging
minority and/or female candidates (d = .28 for real interviews), but **post-1996
studies showed negligible differences** (overall d = -.02, with confidence
intervals including zero) in large, rigorously designed structured interviews —
while cautioning that the number of studies is small [2]. Separately, Sackett et
al. report that structured interviews carry a **small Black–White mean difference
(d = 0.23)** — far below cognitive ability's (d = 0.79) — which is why they figure
prominently in designs that seek validity with less adverse impact [3]. On the
wider validity–diversity tradeoff, the 2022 correction matters: with cognitive
ability no longer the stand-out predictor, composites that give it little or zero
weight lose almost no validity (a drop of .05 on revised values, versus .20 on the
old values) while reducing group differences [3].

# Question Bank

For interviewers and HR, these test whether a selection process is genuinely
evidence-based. For candidates, they surface what the research implies about
preparation. Nearly all are composed for this knowledge base in the style of the
cited literature; they are diagnostic prompts, not published interview questions,
and none is attributed to any employer.

## For interviewers: is your process evidence-based?

1. Which of the 15 documented structure components does your interview actually use, and how many? *(composed)*
2. Are your questions derived from a current job analysis, or from habit and the last person's CV? *(composed)*
3. Is every candidate asked the same core questions in the same order? *(composed)*
4. Do you rate each answer against anchored scales, or form one global impression at the end? *(composed)*
5. Given that anchored scales raised validity from .26 to .35 in the meta-analytic evidence, can you show your scales are anchored to observable behaviour? *(sourced: Levashina, Hartwell, Morgeson & Campion, reporting Taylor & Small)* [2]
6. Do at least two trained raters score independently before any discussion? *(composed)*
7. Has every panel member had rater-bias training, and can you name the biases it targets? *(composed)*
8. Do you take contemporaneous notes of evidence rather than impressions? *(composed)*
9. Do you use past-behaviour questions, situational questions, or both — and can you justify the mix from the job's complexity? *(composed)*
10. Would you claim a validity of ".51" for your interview, or the current best estimate of ".42, plus or minus .24"? *(sourced: Sackett, Zhang, Berry & Lievens)* [3]
11. Do you quote a single validity number, or the credibility interval around it? *(composed)*
12. Have you checked your structured interview for group differences, given that structure narrows but does not automatically erase them? *(composed)*
13. If you use a video or telephone stage, have you compensated for its lower media richness with more structure, not less? *(sourced: Levashina, Hartwell, Morgeson & Campion)* [2]
14. Do you combine ratings statistically, or let a dominant panellist's clinical judgement override the scores? *(composed)*
15. Can you distinguish, in your scoring, an honest self-promoting answer from a fabricated one, rather than rewarding fluency? *(composed)*
16. Does your process rely on cognitive-ability screening whose adverse impact you have not weighed against its now-smaller validity advantage? *(sourced: Sackett, Zhang, Berry & Lievens)* [3]
17. If someone asked for the evidence that your interview predicts performance, could you produce a local validity study or only a citation? *(composed)*
18. Have you confirmed your assessors are not marking candidates down for a non-standard accent, given the measured hireability gap of d = 0.47? *(sourced: Spence, Hornsey, Stephenson & Imuta)* [4]

## For candidates: what the research implies about preparing

1. Are you preparing genuine past examples, since past-behaviour questions predict at least as well as hypotheticals and resist faking better? *(sourced: Levashina, Hartwell, Morgeson & Campion)* [2]
2. Can you give evidence of the specific behaviour the anchored scale is looking for, rather than a general narrative? *(composed)*
3. Have you prepared for both "tell me about a time when…" and "what would you do if…" question types? *(composed)*
4. Are you ready to answer the same core questions every other candidate gets — meaning your differentiation is in the evidence, not in charm? *(composed)*
5. Do you understand that no research shows any particular answer *mnemonic* earns marks, so STAR is an organising aid, not a scoring key? *(composed)*
6. Are you relying on fluency and rapport, which structured scoring is explicitly designed to discount? *(composed)*
7. If you have a non-standard accent, do you know the bias is documented — and that clearer content is your lever, though the evidence suggests prejudice, not comprehension, drives much of the gap? *(sourced: Spence, Hornsey, Stephenson & Imuta)* [4]
8. Are you assuming a "good interview feel" predicts an offer, when even structured interviews explain only a modest share of performance variance? *(composed)*
9. Have you prepared concrete, quantified outcomes, given that anchored scales reward specific evidence over storytelling? *(composed)*
10. Do you know whether your target employer uses a structured process, so you can prepare evidence rather than conversation? *(composed)*
11. Are you treating the interview as a work sample — showing job-relevant behaviour — rather than a personality display? *(sourced: Sackett, Zhang, Berry & Lievens)* [3]
12. Have you prepared for a video or phone stage as a *more* structured, not more casual, encounter? *(sourced: Levashina, Hartwell, Morgeson & Campion)* [2]

## Testing the claims themselves

1. When a vendor claims their tool is "more valid than a human interview," have you asked which validity figure — pre- or post-2022 correction — they are comparing against? *(sourced: Sackett, Zhang, Berry & Lievens)* [3]
2. When someone asserts "Action-heavy answers score higher," can they cite a study, or is it coaching folklore? *(composed)*
3. When a guide says "STAR scores best," does it cite research or merely assert it? *(composed)*
4. Does a claimed validity generalise from US private-sector samples to a UK or EU public-sector competition, or is that an untested leap? *(composed)*
5. Has an "unbiased AI" screening tool been checked for accent and language bias, not just for the biases it advertises removing? *(sourced: Spence, Hornsey, Stephenson & Imuta)* [4]

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact.** Read against the
evidence above, several practical implications follow — but note where each is
inference rather than a finding.

- **Prepare evidence, not scripts.** The research rewards specific, job-relevant
  behavioural evidence matched to anchored scales [2]. STAR and its cousins are
  useful *organisers* for producing that evidence, but — see the verdicts below —
  no study shows the mnemonic itself earns marks. Treat structure as a way to
  avoid omitting the situation, your action or the result, not as a scoring key.
- **Have real past examples ready.** Because past-behaviour questions predict at
  least as well as situational ones and are harder to fake, a bank of genuine
  examples is a defensible bet regardless of which format you meet [2].
- **Expect both question types.** Prepare for "what did you do" *and* "what would
  you do"; the literature recommends employers use both, so candidates should
  drill both [2].
- **Do not over-invest in rapport.** Structured scoring is built to discount the
  fluency and likeability that unstructured interviews reward [2]; the marginal
  return on charm is low where the process is genuinely structured.
- **For interviewers designing a process:** the highest-leverage, evidenced moves
  are the cheap ones — anchor your scales, ask the same questions, rate each
  answer, use more than one trained rater, and take evidence-notes [2]. These are
  the components with the clearest empirical payoff.

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — cited.** What the research tells assessors, and how it connects
to the knowledge base's documented UK/EU practice.

The College of Policing's CVF guidance names **19 assessor biases** — expectancy
effect, confirmatory information, personal liking, primacy, stereotyping,
prototyping, halo/horns, quota, contrast, negative-information bias, similar-to-me,
non-verbal communication, fundamental attribution error, information overload /
selective attention, fatigue, rushing, central tendency, leniency and stringency —
and prescribes counter-measures in four groups rather than one per bias [7]. The
research does not test these biases inside the CVF, but it does test the counter-
measures the CVF relies on, and the support is real: anchored rating scales, rating
each answer, multiple raters, note-taking and interviewer training are exactly the
structure components meta-analytically associated with higher reliability and
validity and with smaller group differences [2] [3]. So the knowledge base can
say, on evidence, that the CVF's documented counter-measures are the *right* ones —
while noting that their effect *within CVF-based policing selection specifically*
has not been separately measured.

Two further assessor-relevant findings. First, **structure suppresses several of
those named biases at once**: because it "links the decision process to job-related
factors and limits the influence of extraneous information," it curbs the similar-
to-me, non-verbal and halo effects the CVF warns about [2]. Second, **rating each
answer beats rating at the end** — Levashina et al.'s analysis found that requiring
per-answer ratings, as opposed to a single end-of-interview rating, was associated
with smaller group differences, consistent with the Civil Service and CVF practice
of scoring each question independently [2] [5] [7]. The Civil Service 7-point scale
and the requirement to score evidence against criteria are, in these terms,
evidence-aligned instruments [5].

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact.** Each is tied to the
evidence it misreads.

- **Quoting the famous .51.** Repeating Schmidt & Hunter's pre-correction figure
  overstates any single interview's validity by up to a fifth; the current best
  estimate is .42 with a wide interval [1] [3].
- **Quoting any single number bare.** Ignoring the .18–.66 credibility interval
  implies a precision the evidence denies; well-designed interviews land high in
  the band, poorly designed ones low [3].
- **Believing the mnemonic is the method.** Treating STAR adherence as the thing
  being scored confuses an answer *aid* with a validated structure component — the
  evidenced components are on the assessor's side of the table (anchored scales,
  multiple raters), not in the candidate's acronym [2] (see verdicts).
- **Assuming "structured" is binary.** A process with two of fifteen components is
  far weaker than one with ten; "we use STAR" is not "we run a structured
  interview" [2].
- **Assuming structure erases bias.** It *narrows* group differences and can reduce
  them to near zero in well-designed field settings, but the evidence base is
  modest in size and does not license "bias-free" claims [2].
- **Generalising US findings uncritically to UK/EU public bodies.** The meta-
  analyses draw largely on US samples; treating .42 as a guaranteed local value is
  exactly the point-estimate error the credibility interval exists to prevent [3]
  (see Risks & Caveats).
- **Trusting "unbiased" screening tools on their own terms.** Accent and language
  bias is measurable and may be encoded in automated scoring of speech; a tool
  audited only for the biases it markets against may still carry it [4].

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.** Drills for both audiences.

1. **Score-the-process audit (interviewers, 30 minutes).** Take your current
   interview and tick which of the 15 structure components it uses [2]. Count them.
   Anything below six is below the research average; identify the two cheapest
   additions (usually anchored scales and per-answer rating).
2. **Interval, not point (everyone, 10 minutes).** Write down the validity you
   would claim for your interview. Now rewrite it as a mean-plus-interval using
   .42 ± .24 as the benchmark [3]. Notice how much less certain the honest
   statement is.
3. **Both-formats drill (candidates).** Take one competency and prepare a genuine
   past-behaviour answer *and* a situational answer for it, since employers are
   advised to use both [2]. Time each to two minutes.
4. **Bias-name test (interviewers).** From memory, list as many of the 19 CVF
   biases as you can [7]; then check which of your process features counter each.
   Gaps are where structure is thin.
5. **Evidence-versus-folklore sort (everyone).** Take three interview-prep claims
   you believe (e.g. "spend most of the answer on Action") and, for each, decide
   whether you can cite a study or only an assertion. Move the un-citable ones into
   the "reasonable coaching, unproven" column — where this document puts them.

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — cited. Signpost only; see `fairness-bias-legal` for full
treatment.** The research evidence intersects UK/EU obligations at two points.

- **Adverse impact and the Equality Act 2010.** Where a selection method produces
  group differences, it engages indirect-discrimination and public-sector
  equality-duty considerations; the Act makes discrimination bite on the
  *arrangements* an employer makes for deciding whom to offer employment (s.39)
  [9]. The evidence that structured interviews carry small group differences
  (Black–White d ≈ 0.23) and that structure narrows gaps is therefore legally as
  well as ethically material [2] [3]. Positive-action provisions (s.159) permit
  tightly bounded tie-break preference where candidates are as qualified as one
  another, but never a standing thumb on the scale [10].
- **Accent, language and proxy discrimination.** Accent bias can operate as a proxy
  for race or national origin; the meta-analytic finding that the hireability gap
  is larger for foreign than regional accents and tracks perceived social status,
  rather than genuine comprehensibility, indicates a fairness risk that criteria-
  based structured scoring is meant to contain [4]. ACAS guidance to employers to
  ask the same questions and score against the person specification operationalises
  this [8].
- **EU equal treatment.** Council Directive 2000/78/EC establishes the general
  framework for equal treatment in employment, covering selection criteria and
  recruitment conditions; the same evidence-based case for structure applies in
  EU-institution selection (see Further Reading for the Directive).

# Risks & Caveats

- **The evidence base is a small number of meta-analyses.** The headline claims
  rest on two Sackett et al. papers (2022 JAP; 2023 IOP) and one Levashina et al.
  review (2014). These are high-quality and open-access or DOI-resolvable, but they
  are not a large independent set, and the 2022 correction is itself recent and
  still generating scholarly reaction [3].
- **One supporting source was read at abstract level only.** The accent-bias meta-
  analysis (Spence, Hornsey, Stephenson & Imuta, 2024) was read via its published
  abstract and the PubMed record, not full text; the d = 0.47 figure and the
  "prejudice over processing-fluency" conclusion are reported as the authors state
  them, not independently checked against their tables [4].
- **US-sample generalisability.** The meta-analytic samples are largely US and
  largely private-sector. Applying .42 (or any figure) to UK/EU public-sector
  competency competitions is reasoned extrapolation; the *mechanisms* (structure,
  anchoring, multiple raters) plausibly transfer, but the *magnitudes* are not
  locally validated [3].
- **Some numbers are reported at one remove.** The Taylor & Small (2002) anchored-
  scale figures and the Huffcutt & Roth (1998) and Conway et al. (1995) findings
  are cited as reported within Levashina et al. (2014), whose full text was read;
  the primary papers themselves were not obtained (ResearchGate and paywalls
  blocked retrieval). They are attributed accordingly rather than cited standalone.
- **The criterion gap.** Validity research measures correlation with *job-
  performance ratings*. That is not what candidates care about (getting hired) nor
  a perfect measure of real performance — performance ratings are themselves
  unreliable, which is why corrections are needed at all [3]. A valid interview
  improves average outcomes; it does not determine any individual result.
- **Publication and range-restriction bias.** The whole 2022 episode shows the
  field's headline numbers were systematically inflated for decades; it is prudent
  to assume residual optimism remains and to prefer the lower end of credibility
  intervals for high-stakes decisions, as Sackett et al. themselves advise [3].

# Open Questions

The knowledge base's registered open questions, each with an explicit verdict.

- **Does answer structure (STAR vs another framework) affect interview scores?**
  **Verdict: no research located.** No peer-reviewed study testing whether using
  STAR — or any specific answer mnemonic — raises interview scores was found.
  Official UK guidance *offers* STAR and CAR but mandates neither, and no framework
  scores mnemonic adherence [11]. Careers-site claims that "STAR scores higher" are
  practitioner assertions, not findings. The *components* that raise validity sit
  on the assessor's side (anchored scales, multiple raters), not in the candidate's
  acronym [2]. This is a genuine gap; other documents may cite it to stop implying
  the mnemonic is evidence-based.
- **Is there empirical evidence that Action-heavy answers score higher?**
  **Verdict: no research located.** No study relating the *proportion* of an answer
  spent on the Action element to interview scores was found. The adjacent,
  evidenced facts are that anchored scales reward specific job-relevant behavioural
  evidence and that Civil Service guidance advises concentrating on the action and
  result [2] [11] — but "concentrate on the action" is official *coaching*, not a
  measured score effect. Treat "Action-heavy scores higher" as reasonable
  inference, not finding.
- **Is there evidence on accent and fluency bias in structured communication
  scoring?** **Verdict: evidenced for hiring evaluations generally; abstract-level
  reading.** A meta-analysis of 139 effect sizes (N = 4,576) finds standard-
  accented candidates rated more hireable (d = 0.47), stronger for high-
  communication jobs and foreign accents, and better explained by prejudice than by
  comprehension difficulty [4]. The evidence concerns hiring evaluations broadly;
  whether *structured* scoring with anchors fully neutralises it is not separately
  established, so the fairness risk should be assumed live.
- **Does structured interviewing predict job performance better than unstructured?**
  **Verdict: strongly evidenced.** The most consistent finding in a century of
  interview research, upheld across twelve-plus meta-analyses and in the current
  corrected estimates (.42 vs .19) [1] [2] [3].
- **Do the CVF's documented bias counter-measures have empirical support?**
  **Verdict: evidenced by proxy, not tested in situ.** The counter-measures are the
  structure components the literature associates with higher reliability and smaller
  group differences [2] [3]; their effect within CVF policing selection
  specifically has not been separately measured [7].
- **Is past-behaviour better evidenced than situational questioning?**
  **Verdict: mixed / slight edge to past-behaviour.** Both predict well; past-
  behaviour has a small advantage for complex jobs and lower group differences, and
  the literature recommends using both [2].
- **What would resolve the gaps?** A field study scoring the same answers with and
  without a fixed mnemonic; a study coding answer-element proportions against
  scores; and a UK/EU public-sector local validation of structured-interview
  validity would each convert an inference into evidence.
