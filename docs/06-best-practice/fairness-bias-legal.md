# Executive Summary

> **This document is signposting, not legal advice.** It records what named
> statutes, EU instruments, regulators and professional bodies have published
> about fairness in competency-based selection, with a citation on every
> factual claim, so that a reader knows *which primary obligation* to look at.
> It does not advise on individual cases, does not tell any reader whether a
> particular process or decision is lawful, and is no substitute for
> qualified legal advice or, in the UK, the ACAS helpline [22]. Where a real
> decision turns on the answer, go to the primary source cited and take
> proper advice.

This is the cross-cutting fairness and legal reference for the knowledge base.
It covers four things. First, the **UK statutory frame**: the nine protected
characteristics at Equality Act 2010 s.4 [1]; the prohibition on discriminating
in "the arrangements A makes for deciding to whom to offer employment" at s.39
[3]; the three-part reasonable-adjustments duty at s.20 [2]; the widely
misunderstood **s.60 restriction on health and disability enquiries before an
offer** [4]; the public sector equality duty at s.149 [5]; and the narrow
positive-action permission at s.159 [6]. Second, the **EU frame**: Council
Directive 2000/78/EC, which applies expressly to "selection criteria and
recruitment conditions" [16]; the GDPR and, for the EU institutions,
Regulation (EU) 2018/1725 [17] [18]; and the Staff Regulations' rules for
selection boards, including the gender-composition requirement [19].

Third, **candidate data and automation**: the ICO's draft recruitment guidance
[8] and its March 2026 *Recruitment rewired* report, which found that employers
who believed they were merely using decision *support* were in practice making
solely automated decisions [9] [10], and which sets out transparency
obligations, the article 22C safeguards and fairness expectations [11] [12].
Fourth, **bias and adjustments in practice**: the College of Policing's
published table of 19 named barriers to accurate assessment with prescribed
counter-measures [14]; adverse impact and the four-fifths rule, together with
the College's own caveat that the rule "does not have a legal footing" [14];
structured interviewing as a documented bias-reduction method [23]; and the
Cabinet Office's recommendation of diverse, trained interview panels alongside
its conditional finding of no adverse impact [15].

# Key Takeaways

- The Equality Act's protected characteristics are a **closed list of nine**
  [1]. EU staff law, by contrast, prohibits discrimination "based on any ground
  such as" a longer, **open-ended** list including language, political opinion,
  membership of a national minority, property and birth [19]. *(documented)*
- Discrimination law bites on **the arrangements for deciding whom to offer
  employment**, not merely on the final decision — so question design, sift
  criteria and scoring are all within scope [3]. *(documented)*
- **Equality Act s.60 prohibits asking about health or disability before an
  offer** or before inclusion in a pool from which an offer will be made, with
  narrow listed exceptions — including establishing whether adjustments are
  needed for an assessment, and whether the applicant can carry out a function
  "intrinsic to the work" [4]. A contravention is enforceable **only by the
  Commission**, but a candidate can bring a tribunal claim about the employer's
  *conduct in reliance on* the answer, and the burden of proof shifts [4].
  *(documented)*
- The reasonable-adjustments duty has **three distinct requirements** — on
  provisions/criteria/practices, on physical features and on auxiliary aids —
  and the disabled person may not be made to pay the cost [2]. ACAS states you
  must ask interviewees to tell you if adjustments are needed [22]; CIPD advises
  going further and asking **all** applicants proactively [23]. *(documented)*
- The ICO found employers **believed** they had meaningful human involvement
  while in practice "rubber-stamping" low-scoring candidates — which
  constitutes solely automated decision-making within article 22 [10].
  *(documented)*
- The ICO also observed genuine good practice: employers "typically provided
  reasonable adjustments for people with disabilities who weren't able to
  access automated recruitment methods. This often involved a phone interview"
  [12]. *(documented)*
- The **four-fifths rule** is the common test for adverse impact — but the
  College of Policing states plainly that although it is a useful and widely
  used guide, "it does not have a legal footing" [14]. Treat it as a
  monitoring trigger, never as a legal standard. *(documented)*
- Bias is not a vague hazard: the College of Policing names **19 specific
  barriers** to accurate assessment and prescribes concrete counter-measures —
  ORCE, assessor training, restricting pre-assessment information, scheduling
  breaks, and training on rating-scale use [14]. *(documented)*
- **Positive action under s.159 is narrow**: it applies only where A is *as
  qualified as* B, forbids a standing policy of preference, and must be a
  proportionate means to the aim [6]. It is not a tie-break of convenience.
  *(documented)*
- For candidates, the practical asks are usually cheap and specific — extra
  time, an alternative format, a ground-floor or accessible room, an
  interpreter, or a phone interview instead of an automated video stage
  [7] [12] [20] [22]. Ask early and ask concretely. *(guidance)*

# Purpose

This document answers, for **interviewers and HR**: *"Which published
obligation governs this part of my process, and where do I read it?"* — and
for **candidates**: *"What does a fair process look like, what may I be asked,
and how do I request an adjustment?"*

It exists because fairness questions in competency interviewing are
cross-cutting. They arise in question design, in sifting, in scoring, in video
and asynchronous formats, in panel composition and in record-keeping — and the
parent document, `best-practice-foundation`, deliberately treats them only at
signpost level, deferring the full treatment here. This document goes deeper on
each obligation it names, and does not restate the parent's general preparation
or scoring guidance.

Its job is **routing and accuracy**: to name the right instrument, quote it
faithfully, and stop. Where a claim would require legal judgement about a
specific set of facts, this document says so rather than guessing.

# Definitions

Terms used with a narrowed meaning in this document. General interview
vocabulary lives in the shared `GLOSSARY.md`.

- **Protected characteristic** — one of the nine characteristics listed at
  Equality Act 2010 s.4 [1]. Note that s.149's public sector equality duty
  applies to a list of **eight**, omitting marriage and civil partnership [5].
- **Reasonable adjustment** — the statutory duty at s.20, comprising three
  requirements: steps to avoid substantial disadvantage caused by a provision,
  criterion or practice; steps to avoid disadvantage caused by a physical
  feature; and provision of an auxiliary aid [2].
- **Reasonable accommodation** — the equivalent EU-law concept, defined in
  Directive 2000/78/EC art. 5 as appropriate measures to enable a person with a
  disability to access, participate in or advance in employment, "unless such
  measures would impose a disproportionate burden on the employer" [16].
- **Adverse impact** — where the success rate of a minority group on a
  selection method "is significantly lower than the majority group pass rate"
  [14].
- **Four-fifths rule** — the convention that adverse impact is present if the
  minority-group pass rate falls below 80% of the majority-group pass rate;
  explicitly recorded by the College of Policing as having **no legal footing**
  [14].
- **Solely automated decision** — under UK GDPR article 22A as introduced by
  the Data (Use and Access) Act, "a decision is based solely on automated
  processing if there is no meaningful human involvement in the taking of the
  decision" [10].
- **Meaningful human involvement** — the ICO's test: whether a human "can
  exercise real influence over decision before it is applied and has the
  authority, discretion and competence to alter it", as opposed to directly
  applying an automated outcome without meaningful consideration [10].
- **Barrier to effective assessment** — the College of Policing's term for a
  named assessor bias, each paired in its published table with a prescribed
  counter-measure [14].

# Framework Context

**Evidence layer — cited.** Five distinct bodies of obligation bear on a
competency interview. They are not alternatives; a single UK public-sector
interview can engage all but the EU-institution column at once.

| Obligation family | Governs | Primary instruments |
|---|---|---|
| UK equality law | Who may be disadvantaged, what may be asked, what must be adjusted | Equality Act 2010 ss.4, 20, 39, 60, 149, 159 [1] [2] [3] [4] [5] [6] |
| EU equal-treatment law | Access to employment including selection criteria, in member states | Council Directive 2000/78/EC [16] |
| Data protection | Candidate notes, scores, recordings and automated decisions | UK GDPR / DPA 2018 [8]; GDPR [17]; Regulation (EU) 2018/1725 for EU institutions [18] |
| EU staff law | Selection boards and non-discrimination in EU institution competitions | Staff Regulations, art. 1d and Annex III [19] |
| Professional standards | What a defensible process looks like in practice | ACAS [21] [22]; CIPD [23]; College of Policing [14]; Cabinet Office [15] |

The **UK list is closed**. Section 4 provides that "the following
characteristics are protected characteristics: age; disability; gender
reassignment; marriage and civil partnership; pregnancy and maternity; race;
religion or belief; sex; sexual orientation" [1]. ACAS reproduces the same nine
for recruitment and adds that discrimination "includes both direct and indirect
discrimination", with indirect discrimination arising where "a working
practice, policy or rule is the same for everyone but has a worse effect on
someone because of a protected characteristic" [21].

The **EU frame is differently shaped, in two ways.** Directive 2000/78/EC has a
narrower ground list than the Equality Act — its stated purpose is "combating
discrimination on the grounds of religion or belief, disability, age or sexual
orientation as regards employment and occupation" (art. 1), because sex and
race are covered by separate directives [16]. But its *material* scope is
explicit and wide: it applies to both public and private sectors in relation to
"conditions for access to employment, to self-employment or to occupation,
including selection criteria and recruitment conditions, whatever the branch of
activity and at all levels of the professional hierarchy, including promotion"
(art. 3(1)(a)) [16]. Meanwhile the **EU Staff Regulations**, which govern the
institutions' own competitions, use an open-ended formula: "any discrimination
based on any ground such as sex, race, colour, ethnic or social origin, genetic
features, language, religion or belief, political or any other opinion,
membership of a national minority, property, birth, disability, age, or sexual
orientation shall be prohibited" (art. 1d(1)) [19].

Both regimes carry a disability-adjustment duty, worded almost identically.
Directive art. 5 requires "appropriate measures, where needed in a particular
case, to enable a person with a disability to have access to, participate in,
or advance in employment, or to undergo training, unless such measures would
impose a disproportionate burden on the employer" [16]; Staff Regulations art.
1d(4) defines "reasonable accommodation" in materially the same words and adds
that a person with a disability meets the physical-fitness condition for
appointment "if he can perform the essential functions of the job when
reasonable accommodation is made" [19].

# Core Reference

**Evidence layer — cited.** Signposting only: each subsection names the
obligation and quotes or closely paraphrases it. It does not tell you whether

## Where discrimination law bites in a selection process

Equality Act s.39(1) provides that an employer "must not discriminate against a
person (B) — (a) in the arrangements A makes for deciding to whom to offer
employment; (b) as to the terms on which A offers B employment; (c) by not
offering B employment", with parallel prohibitions on victimisation at s.39(3);
s.39(5) states flatly that "a duty to make reasonable adjustments applies to an
employer" [3]. The operative words for interviewing are **"the arrangements …
for deciding"** — the reach is to the design of the process, not only to the
outcome. ACAS renders the same point for recruiters: discrimination "could
happen through: the arrangements you make for deciding who to offer the job;
the terms under which you offer the job; not offering someone the job" [21].

## Section 60: enquiries about disability and health before an offer

This is the provision most often misunderstood, so it is set out at length.
Section 60(1) provides that a person to whom an application for work is made
"must not ask about the health of the applicant (B) — (a) before offering work
to B, or (b) where A is not in a position to offer work to B, before including
B in a pool of applicants from whom A intends … to select a person to whom to
offer work" [4].

Four features deserve attention, all on the face of the section [4]:

- **The exceptions are listed and narrow.** Section 60(6) permits a question so
  far as necessary for: establishing whether B can comply with a requirement to
  undergo an assessment, or whether an adjustments duty arises in connection
  with an assessment; establishing whether B can carry out "a function that is
  intrinsic to the work concerned"; monitoring diversity; taking positive action
  in relation to disabled persons; or establishing whether B has a particular
  disability where that is an occupational requirement.
- **The "intrinsic function" exception is read *after* adjustments.** Where the
  employer reasonably believes an adjustments duty would arise, s.60(7)
  requires the intrinsic-function reference to be read as a function that would
  be intrinsic "once A complied with the duty".
- **Asking is not itself disability discrimination — relying on the answer may
  be.** Section 60(3): A "does not contravene a relevant disability provision
  merely by asking about B's health; but A's conduct in reliance on information
  given in response may be a contravention".
- **The enforcement split is unusual.** A contravention of s.60(1) is
  enforceable as an unlawful act "only by the Commission" (s.60(2)); but where
  B complains to an employment tribunal about A's conduct in reliance on the
  answer, s.60(5) treats the particulars of the complaint as **facts from which
  the tribunal could decide that A contravened the provision** — that is, the
  burden shifts.

ACAS operationalises this: "You must not ask a job applicant about their health
or any disability at any stage in the application or interview process, unless
an exception applies", listing as exceptions finding out whether adjustments to
the assessment process are needed, whether after adjustments the person could
do the essential parts of the role, the disability exception, the equality
monitoring form, and positive action [21]. ACAS adds that the employer *can*
ask once the applicant has been selected for a pool of potential candidates or
offered a job outright or conditionally [21].

## The reasonable-adjustments duty

Section 20 defines a duty in **three requirements** [2]: where a provision,
criterion or practice puts a disabled person at a substantial disadvantage, to
take reasonable steps to avoid it (s.20(3)); the same where a **physical
feature** causes the disadvantage (s.20(4)); and to provide an **auxiliary
aid** where its absence would cause substantial disadvantage (s.20(5)). Where
the first or third requirement concerns information, reasonable steps "include
steps for ensuring that in the circumstances concerned the information is
provided in an accessible format" (s.20(6)), and s.20(7) provides that the
employer is not entitled to require the disabled person to pay any part of the
cost of compliance [2]. For Part 5 (work), the applicable schedule is Schedule
8 [2].

GOV.UK's employer guidance puts the practical version: "You can ask if a
candidate needs an adjustment to the recruitment process to allow them to be
considered for the job, or you can wait to be told. You must make adjustments
if they're reasonable, for example allowing: wheelchair users to have their
interview on the ground floor; candidates to complete a written test using a
computer." It adds that after a job offer, the employer can ask what
adjustments are needed to *do* the job, and that an Access to Work grant cannot
be used to pay for reasonable adjustments [7].

ACAS goes further on the trigger: "By law you must ask anyone coming to an
interview to tell you if they need reasonable adjustments so they can attend",
giving as examples an accessible office, "giving someone more time to do a
written test that is part of an interview", and "providing a sign language
interpreter for someone who is deaf" [22]. Elsewhere ACAS notes the adjustment
duty "can be relevant at any stage of the recruitment process", instancing an
application form in a different format, and that if someone does not ask, "you
can ask them if reasonable adjustments might be needed" [21].

## Public sector equality duty and positive action

Section 149(1) requires a public authority, in exercising its functions, to
"have due regard to the need to" eliminate prohibited conduct, advance equality
of opportunity and foster good relations; s.149(2) extends this to any person
exercising public functions [5]. Advancing equality of opportunity involves due
regard in particular to removing or minimising connected disadvantages, meeting
different needs, and encouraging participation where it is disproportionately
low (s.149(3)); for disabled persons this includes "steps to take account of
disabled persons' disabilities" (s.149(4)) [5]. Section 149(6) provides that
compliance "may involve treating some persons more favourably than others; but
that is not to be taken as permitting conduct that would otherwise be
prohibited". Note the **list of relevant protected characteristics at s.149(7)
omits marriage and civil partnership**, so it runs to eight, not nine [5].

Section 159 permits positive action in recruitment and promotion where the
employer reasonably thinks persons sharing a protected characteristic suffer a
connected disadvantage or that their participation is disproportionately low
[6]. The conditions in s.159(4) are cumulative and strict: A must be "as
qualified as B to be recruited or promoted"; the employer must not have "a
policy of treating persons who share the protected characteristic more
favourably"; and the action must be "a proportionate means of achieving the
aim" [6]. The College of Policing's summary matches: s.159 "does not allow
employers to automatically treat people who share a protected characteristic
more favourably than those who do not have it" [14].

## Candidate data, automation and the ICO's 2026 findings

Interview notes, scores and recordings are personal data. The ICO's draft
*Employment practices and data protection: recruitment and selection* guidance
covers the pipeline — including automated decision-making and profiling,
shortlisting, testing and interviewing candidates, and keeping recruitment
records — and states it is aimed at employers and organisations recruiting on
their behalf, including agencies and consultancies [8]. **It is a draft**: the
page records that the consultation is closed, that "the final version will be
published in due course", and that the guidance "is under review and may be
subject to change" following the Data (Use and Access) Act [8].

The ICO's *Recruitment rewired* report (published alongside the March 2026 news
item) draws on evidence from **over 30 employers who engaged voluntarily
between March 2025 and January 2026** [9]. Its findings are directly relevant
to competency assessment, because the automation sits on exactly the artefacts
a competency process produces — application text, assessment responses and
interview answers.

**On meaningful human involvement** [10]: most employers considered their tools
to be decision *support* rather than decision-*making*, but "the evidence we saw
indicated that, in practice, employers were using the tools to make solely
automated decisions." Employers "could not consistently demonstrate how they
had mitigated the risk of their hiring managers relying disproportionately on
the scores when faced with a high volume of applications", and the ICO
"frequently saw evidence suggesting hiring managers were unlikely to review the
scores or responses of lower-scoring candidates" — rubber-stamping the score.
In the ICO's worked case study, a manager who glances at 'red' candidates
before rejecting them has "rubber-stamped" those rejections, and this
"constitutes solely ADM within the scope of article 22" [10]. The ICO's
conclusion is unambiguous: meaningful human involvement "must be applied to
every candidate, not just those who score highly" [10].

**On transparency and safeguards** [11]: organisations must provide information
about ADM at **three points in time** — when they first collect the
information, when a person makes a subject access request, and when they engage
in ADM. In recruitment the information "does not need to be complex or include
material that could allow candidates to 'game the system'"; what employers
should provide is how they use the tool to make decisions, how accurate it is,
and what safeguards are in place. The article 22C safeguards mean a person "has
a right to be informed about the decisions made about them; is given the
opportunity to make representations about such decisions; is given the
opportunity to obtain human intervention …; and is able to contest a decision"
[11]. The ICO recorded poor practice too — notably employers referring
candidates to the *tool provider's* privacy information rather than their own,
"because it suggests that accountability for decisions lies with the provider.
In reality, accountability lies with the employer using the tool" [11].

**On fairness and bias** [12]: organisations "are responsible for the
algorithms and tools they use to process personal information, even if they
didn't build the software or create the algorithm." The ICO sets out what
employers could consider at procurement: conducting trials to ensure results
limit bias, and requesting from developers information on the demographic
groups a model was trained on, whether underlying bias has been detected or may
emerge, and any algorithmic fairness testing conducted [12]. It records good
practice actually observed — procurement-stage bias questions, trials, and
ongoing monitoring including "dashboards that provided ongoing bias monitoring
and monthly bias reviews" — and notes employers typically collected optional
demographic data only to monitor for bias, not to make decisions [12]. Its
worked example of unfairness is a tool trained on previous successful hires who
"were men in their mid- to late 40s", exposing all other gender and age groups
to unfair disadvantage [12]. The ICO also records, **without endorsing it**,
that "many automated recruitment tool providers market their tools as fairer
and less biased than human recruiters" [12].

For candidates, the ICO's public-facing summary states the rights plainly: a
fair outcome, with the ICO expecting organisations "to test regularly for bias";
to know if ADM is being used, since "employers need to tell you if automated
decisions are being made about your application"; and to "challenge a decision
and ask for a 'human review'" [13]. The ICO wrote to **16 organisations** likely
to be using ADM about jobseekers, which "have now committed to acting on our
recommendations" [13].

At **EU institutions** the equivalent instrument is Regulation (EU) 2018/1725,
which "applies to the processing of personal data by all Union institutions and
bodies" and whose article 24 mirrors GDPR article 22: the data subject has "the
right not to be subject to a decision based solely on automated processing,
including profiling, which produces legal effects concerning him or her or
similarly significantly affects him or her" [18] [17]. The Staff Regulations
separately provide that an official's personal file "shall contain no reference
to his political, trade union, philosophical or religious activities and views,
or to his racial or ethnic origin or sexual orientation" [19].

## Selection boards and adjustments in EU competitions

Staff Regulations Annex III art. 3 governs competition selection boards: the
board "shall consist of a chairman designated by the Appointing Authority and
of members designated by the Appointing Authority and the Staff Committee, each
designating the same number"; members must be of a function group and grade "at
least equal to that of the post to be filled"; and — the gender rule — **"if a
selection board consists of more than four members, it shall comprise at least
two members of each gender"** [19]. Article 1d(2) further provides that the
principle of equal treatment does not prevent the institutions "from
maintaining or adopting measures providing for specific advantages in order to
make it easier for the under-represented sex to pursue a vocational activity"
[19].

EPSO's published process for adjustments (the page is now titled *Reasonable
Accommodations*) requires candidates to indicate their specific needs **in the
application form** and to provide supporting documents "that explicitly
describe the nature of their disability and justify the requested
accommodations" — medical certificates, disability certificates or specialist
reports [20]. Each request is assessed individually by the **EPSO Accessibility
Team** against the candidate's functional limitations, the type of test and
testing method, the actions required during the test, and the supporting
documentation; external experts may be consulted "with full respect for data
confidentiality" [20]. EPSO states four qualifications explicitly: accommodations
"are granted on a case-by-case basis"; "final arrangements may differ from the
initial request"; "multiple medical conditions do not automatically lead to
cumulative accommodations"; and a certificate is sufficient only if it
explicitly describes the disability and justifies the accommodation requested
[20]. Published categories of accommodation include scheduling time and
locations, special equipment, test formats and layouts, personal devices,
assistance for visually and hearing-impaired and neurodiverse candidates, and
service dogs; the list is stated to be "non-exhaustive and non-contractual"
[20].

# Question Bank

Questions for **auditing a process**, not for assessing a candidate's
competence. For interviewers and HR, these test whether a selection process is
lawful and fair by design; for candidates, they cover how to request an
adjustment and how to recognise a fair process.

A note on provenance: the primary sources in this area publish *obligations*
and *findings*, not question sets, so most entries below are **composed** for
this knowledge base in the style of the cited instrument. Only where a source
literally poses a question is it labelled *(sourced …)*. Composed questions are
never attributable to any employer or regulator.

## For interviewers — lawful process design

1. Which of the nine protected characteristics could the wording of each of our questions disadvantage, directly or indirectly? *(composed)*
2. Can we point to the part of the job description that every scored criterion derives from? *(composed)*
3. Do our arrangements for deciding whom to shortlist — not just the final decision — stand up to scrutiny under s.39? *(composed)*
4. Is there any question in the pack that asks about health or disability, in any form, before offer? *(composed)*
5. If we do ask a health question, which specific s.60(6) exception are we relying on, and can we state it in one sentence? *(composed)*
6. Where we rely on the "intrinsic function" exception, have we assessed that function as it would be after reasonable adjustments were made? *(composed)*
7. Are we treating a candidate's answer to a health or adjustment question as information used only for adjustments, and not as scoring evidence? *(composed)*
8. If we are a public authority, what record shows we had due regard to the s.149 aims when we designed this campaign? *(composed)*
9. Where we are contemplating positive action, are the two candidates genuinely as qualified as each other on our own published criteria? *(composed)*
10. Do we have a standing policy of preferring one group — which s.159 does not permit — or a case-by-case, proportionate judgement? *(composed)*
11. Are our interview questions, order and scoring criteria fixed in advance and identical across candidates? *(composed)*
12. Does anything in our process differ between candidates for a reason we could not defend in writing? *(composed)*

## For interviewers — bias in the room

 1. What pre-assessment information have panellists seen, and did they truly need it before the interview? *(composed)*
 2. Are we recording contemporaneous, near-verbatim evidence before classifying and evaluating it, or scoring on impression? *(composed)*
 3. Which candidate did we assess immediately before this one, and is our score a comparison rather than a measurement against criteria? *(composed)*
 4. Has any panellist scored the whole interview off the strength of a single strong or weak answer? *(composed)*
 5. Are we warmer towards this candidate because something about them resembles us? *(composed)*
 6. Are we reading confidence, eye contact or gesture as evidence of competence when the criteria say nothing about them? *(composed)*
 7. Are we attributing a poor outcome in the candidate's example to their character rather than to their circumstances? *(composed)*
 8. How many candidates has each assessor seen today without a break, and at what point does fatigue start affecting scores? *(composed)*
 9. Are our scores clustering in the middle of the scale, or at one end, across all candidates? *(composed)*
10. Did every panellist score independently before any discussion took place? *(composed)*
11. Is this panel diverse, and has every member had interview-skills training before sitting? *(composed)*

## For interviewers — automation, data and adverse impact

 1. The critical question for any automated stage: can a human exercise real influence over the decision before it is applied, with the authority, discretion and competence to alter it — or are they simply applying the system's output? *(sourced: Information Commissioner's Office)* [10]
 2. Does our human reviewer look at low-scoring candidates with the same care as high-scoring ones, or only sign off the rejections? *(composed)*
 3. What did the vendor tell us about the demographic groups their model was trained on, and about bias they have detected or tested for? *(composed)*
 4. Do our candidates learn that automated decision-making is in use — at collection, on request, and at the point the decision is made? *(composed)*
 5. Can a candidate make representations, obtain human intervention and contest an automated decision, in a timely way? *(composed)*
 6. Are we pointing candidates at our own privacy information, or at the tool provider's? *(composed)*
 7. What alternative route exists for a candidate who cannot access an automated assessment method at all? *(composed)*
 8. If we compare pass rates by group, is any group falling below four-fifths of the highest group's rate — and if so, what does that trigger in our process? *(composed)*
 9. Do we know whether our scores, notes and recordings are stored securely, for how long, and under whose policy? *(composed)*

## For candidates — adjustments and a fair process

 1. What is automated decision making? *(sourced: Information Commissioner's Office)* [13]
 2. What are my rights when a decision about my application is made without a real person involved? *(sourced: Information Commissioner's Office)* [13]
 3. What will the assessment involve, how long will it take, and what technology will be used? *(composed)*
 4. What adjustment do I actually need — extra time, an alternative format, an accessible room, an interpreter, a phone interview instead of a video one — and can I state it in one specific sentence? *(composed)*
 5. When and to whom do I make the request, and does the application form itself have a place for it? *(composed)*
 6. What supporting documentation is required, and does it explicitly describe the nature of my disability and justify what I am asking for? *(composed)*
 7. If an automated stage is used, can I ask for it to be reviewed by a person, and how do I do that? *(composed)*
 8. Was I asked the same questions as other candidates, and were the criteria published in advance? *(composed)*

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — this is guidance for using the document, not legal advice
and not documented fact.** Nothing here creates a new claim; each point routes
back to a citation above.

- **Route the question before answering it.** Most fairness questions in
  interviewing resolve to one of four instruments: *may we ask this?* → s.60
  [4]; *must we change something?* → s.20 [2]; *is the process itself
  disadvantaging someone?* → s.39 and indirect discrimination [3] [21]; *is a
  machine deciding?* → the ADM provisions [10] [11]. Naming the instrument
  first prevents the common failure of arguing about fairness in the abstract.
- **For interviewers, the cheapest defensible posture is structure.** The
  documented counter-measures — same questions in the same order, criteria
  agreed before the first interview, independent scoring before discussion, a
  panel rather than a sole interviewer, contemporaneous evidence rather than
  impressions [14] [23] — are all things you do *before* any candidate arrives.
  They cost nothing at interview time and they are the practices the cited
  sources exist to recommend.
- **Treat s.60 as a design constraint, not a live judgement.** Deciding
  mid-interview whether a health question falls in an exception is where
  processes go wrong. The workable approach is to separate the channels
  entirely: adjustment requests go to a named person outside the scoring panel,
  and the panel receives only what it needs to run the assessment — which is
  also the College of Policing's prescribed counter-measure for the expectancy
  effect [14].
- **For candidates, be specific and be early.** The published processes reward
  a concrete request over a general disclosure: EPSO requires the need to be
  indicated in the application form with documentation that "explicitly
  describe[s] the nature of your disability and justif[ies] the requested
  accommodations" [20], and ACAS frames the UK duty around the employer asking
  and the candidate telling [22]. "I need 25% extra time on the written
  exercise" travels further than "I have a condition that affects me".
- **Ask for the thing, not the diagnosis.** The adjustment that matters is the
  step that removes the disadvantage — extra time, alternative format,
  accessible room, interpreter, phone instead of video [7] [12] [20] [22]. The
  documentation supports the request; it is not the request.
- **Expect the answer to be individual.** EPSO states that accommodations are
  granted case by case, that final arrangements may differ from what was asked,
  and that multiple conditions do not produce cumulative accommodations [20].
  A negotiated outcome is the normal outcome, not a refusal.
- **If an automated stage is in the way, the ICO's own findings are the
  strongest thing to point at.** Employers "typically provided reasonable
  adjustments for people with disabilities who weren't able to access
  automated recruitment methods. This often involved a phone interview" [12] —
  a documented, observed practice rather than a speculative ask.

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — cited.** What assessors are actually told about fairness.

## The named barriers and their prescribed counter-measures

The College of Policing publishes a table of **19 barriers to effective
assessment**, each with a description and a prescribed remedy [14]. The named
barriers are: **expectancy effect**, **confirmatory information**, **personal
liking**, **primacy effect**, **stereotyping**, **prototyping**, **halo/horns
effect**, **quota effect**, **contrast effect**, **negative information bias**,
**similar to me effect**, **non-verbal communication**, **fundamental
attribution error**, **information overload and selective attention**,
**fatigue**, **rushing**, **central tendency bias**, **leniency** and
**stringency** [14].

The counter-measures are prescribed in groups rather than one per barrier [14]:

| Barrier group | Prescribed counter-measure |
|---|---|
| Expectancy effect | ORCE; "only give assessors information they truly need in advance of the assessment" |
| Confirmatory information, personal liking, primacy, stereotyping, prototyping, halo/horns, quota, contrast, negative information bias, similar to me | Comprehensive assessor training covering the role, the criteria and identifying biases, e.g. unconscious bias training; ORCE |
| Non-verbal communication, fundamental attribution error, information overload, fatigue, rushing | Training in identifying biases; ORCE; process designers to "factor in breaks and not schedule the assessment of too many candidates by one assessor" |
| Central tendency, leniency, stringency | Training in how rating scales are used and encouragement "to use the full length of the scale"; ORCE |

**ORCE** — observe, record, classify, evaluate — is the structured sequence the
College prescribes because it "breaks down the assessing process into component
parts and helps to avoid bias": observe everything the candidate does and says;
"take contemporaneous notes, as verbatim as possible, whilst observing";
classify the observed behaviours into competencies and values **after** the
candidate has finished; then evaluate and award a rating [14]. Its ordering is
the point — classification and judgement come after recording, not during it.

The College also sets a **minimum content for assessor training**: that
assessors understand their role and the role being assessed for; that all
assessors share an understanding of the criteria; familiarisation with the
questions or exercise materials and with the competencies and values;
understanding the rating scale and calibration with other assessors; barriers to
accurate assessment; unconscious bias; and assessing and interviewing skills
[14]. For volume processes it adds that "assessor training and calibration
becomes more critical", and that a quality-assurance process should monitor
assessors "to ensure they are performing consistently to an agreed standard"
[14].

## Method choice, adverse impact and the four-fifths rule

The College names two factors that "must be considered" for any selection
method: **validity** — better performance predicts or correlates with better
performance in the role — and **fairness** — "it does not create advantage or
disadvantage for particular groups that are unrelated to their ability to do
the job"; and it adds that for a process to be fair and valid it must be
**reliable**, meaning consistent and standardised "so that every candidate has
the same opportunity" [14].

On adverse impact, the guidance states that it is "the responsibility of
recruiting organisations to ensure that selection processes comply with the
Equality Act 2010"; that a method has adverse impact "when the success rate of
the minority group is significantly lower than the majority group pass rate";
and that "commonly the 80 per cent or four-fifths rule is used to determine
whether adverse impact is present" [14]. The caveat is the College's own, and
should travel with every use of the rule: "Although the four fifths rule is a
useful guide for evaluation and is commonly used in practice, **it does not
have a legal footing**" [14].

Two method-specific findings are recorded [14]. Ability tests "have a tendency
to cause adverse impact whereby white candidates perform significantly better
than candidates from some black and minority ethnic backgrounds", so thresholds
need care and such tests "should only be used where ability is clearly relevant
to the role"; SJTs "tend to have lower adverse impact than ability tests" but
"typically have lower score ranges and validity". On application forms, the
College notes that name and education details "may reveal gender, ethnicity and
age, which can introduce potential bias" and that it is "good practice to
remove any identifying information from the assessment of competency or
value-based questions" [14]. On interviews specifically: unstructured
interviews "have very poor validity", highly structured interviews "have been
shown to be the most valid", and "interviews are susceptible to interviewer
bias, so interviewers should be well trained and keep assessing skills up to
date" [14].

On decisions, the College records that **arithmetic** decision-making — ranking
by score — "has been shown to be the most valid decision-making method", while
rational methods in which assessors discuss and decide "have been shown to be
less effective in selecting the best candidates and are difficult to justify
from a fairness point of view" [14].

## Panels: composition, training and the Cabinet Office's diversity finding

Cabinet Office guidance for delegated-grade (EO–Grade 6) recruitment states:
"It is recommended that all interview panels should be diverse, and all
panellists should have undertaken training on interview skills prior to sitting
on an interview panel" [15]. For video interviewing it requires panellists to
hold a pre-discussion on running the interview, that checks are made that the
technology "is available, accessible and compatible for all panel members and
candidates taking part", and that "the panel should consider if any reasonable
adjustments are required, arising either from access to technology or a range
of other conditions" [15].

The same guidance reports a diversity analysis whose conclusion is explicitly
**conditional**, and the condition is the whole point: "Analysis indicated
that, on the whole, there are no adverse impacts indicated with outcomes based
on protected characteristics — **as long as** selection methods are delivered
in a structured and consistent way, and training is provided to and/or official
guidance is followed by those who are involved in delivering and
assessing/making selection decisions" [15]. The finding is evidence for
structure and training, not a general assurance that Success Profiles
interviewing is bias-free.

The CIPD reaches the same place from the professional-body side. Structured
interviews "involve asking a predefined set of questions, in the same order, to
all candidates", with responses scored "using consistent criteria against a set
of pre-agreed criteria for each question", and using them "is an effective way
to minimise bias and ensure there is a level playing field for candidates from
marginalised groups" [23]. Its named elements of a well-structured interview
[23]: fixed questions and scoring criteria, which makes responses comparable
"and makes it less likely that decisions will be based on personal biases";
**a panel rather than a sole interviewer**, since "having multiple interviewers
in the same interview has been shown to lead to fairer and more accurate
results than having multiple interviews with only one interviewer"; preparing
interviewers in advance and having the panel meet to assign questions and take
independent notes; and assigning scores **independently before group
discussion**, because discussing candidates earlier means interviewers "are
more likely to be influenced by other interviewers' opinions, and potentially
by the opinion of the most senior person there" [23].

CIPD is also the source for the most candidate-friendly adjustment practice
located: rather than waiting for requests, employers should "explicitly and
proactively ask all applicants if they would like to request any once
applications have been submitted", noting that adjustments "are often
considered once a candidate has been hired" when offering them at the selection
stage "can support a more diverse range of candidates to participate" [23]. It
adds that recruiters should tell candidates in advance what to expect,
"including how long it will take, what technology, if any, will be used, [and]
the type of assessment they will undergo" [23], and that organisations "must …
assess any technology before adopting it, making sure it's been robustly
tested, provides a good candidate experience, and is fair and inclusive" [23].

ACAS supplies the record-keeping and equality-monitoring baseline: interviews
should be carried out "by more than 1 person to reduce the risk of
discriminating"; interviewers should be prepared and "understand the law on
discrimination"; recordings require the person's permission first; notes and
recordings "must … store them securely and confidentially [and] follow data
protection rules"; and decisions should be checked as "based on how each
applicant met each point in the job description and person specification" [22].
On monitoring forms, ACAS is specific: keep the information separate from the
application form and CV, "anyone involved in interviewing or deciding who to
hire must not have access to the information", and applicants should not be
asked to enter their name or other identifying information [21].

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact.** Each pitfall names
the documented obligation or finding it runs against.

## Interviewer-side

- **Treating s.60 as a rule about tact.** It is a prohibition with listed
  exceptions [4]. "We only asked sympathetically" is not one of them.
- **Asking a health question and then using the answer.** Section 60(3) makes
  the asking itself not a disability contravention — but conduct in reliance on
  the answer may be, and s.60(5) shifts the evidential burden [4]. The risk is
  in what you do with it.
- **Letting adjustment information reach the scoring panel.** Nothing in s.20
  requires the panel to know why an adjustment was made [2], and the College's
  prescribed counter-measure to the expectancy effect is to give assessors only
  information they truly need in advance [14].
- **Reading the four-fifths rule as a legal test.** The College states it "does
  not have a legal footing" [14]. Using it as a compliance threshold both
  overclaims and under-protects.
- **Citing the Cabinet Office "no adverse impact" finding without its
  condition.** The finding holds "as long as" methods are structured and
  consistent and assessors are trained or follow official guidance [15].
  Quoting the first half is a misrepresentation.
- **Believing you have meaningful human involvement because a human clicked.**
  This is precisely the gap the ICO found: employers thought they had decision
  support; the evidence indicated solely automated decisions [10].
- **Reviewing only the top of the ranked list.** The ICO's position is that
  meaningful human involvement "must be applied to every candidate, not just
  those who score highly" [10].
- **Outsourcing accountability to the vendor.** Pointing candidates at the tool
  provider's privacy notice is recorded by the ICO as poor practice, because
  "accountability lies with the employer using the tool" [11].
- **Assuming a diverse panel is sufficient on its own.** The Cabinet Office
  recommendation pairs diversity with interview-skills training for every
  panellist [15]; CIPD pairs panel format with independent pre-discussion
  scoring [23]. Composition without process does not deliver the effect.
- **Group discussion before independent scores.** CIPD's stated reason is
  seniority and social influence contaminating judgement [23]; the College
  separately warns that discussion-based decisions are "difficult to justify
  from a fairness point of view" [14].
- **Assessing too many candidates in a row.** Fatigue, rushing and information
  overload are named barriers with a scheduling remedy, not personal failings
  [14].
- **Scoring the same narrow band for everyone.** Central tendency, leniency and
  stringency are named barriers whose remedy is training on scale use and
  encouragement to use the full range [14].
- **Rejecting positive action as unlawful, or reaching for it too readily.**
  Section 159 permits it only where candidates are as qualified as each other,
  with no standing policy of preference, proportionately [6].

## Candidate-side

- **Disclosing a condition instead of requesting an adjustment.** The published
  processes act on specific requests: EPSO requires the need indicated in the
  application form with documentation justifying the accommodation asked for
  [20].
- **Requesting late.** Adjustment routes in the cited guidance sit at
  application and invitation stage [7] [20] [22]; a request made in the room
  leaves nothing to arrange.
- **Assuming an automated stage is non-negotiable.** The ICO recorded employers
  typically providing an alternative, often a phone interview, where candidates
  could not access automated methods [12].
- **Not asking whether ADM is in use.** Employers must tell candidates when
  automated decisions are being made about their application [13]; the right to
  contest and to human review depends on knowing it happened [11] [13].
- **Answering a pre-offer health question at length because it was asked.**
  That such a question was put does not make the answer scoreable, and the
  candidate-protective machinery sits in what the employer then does with it
  [4].

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.** Concrete drills, for both audiences.

1. **Question-pack audit (interviewers, 45 minutes).** Take a live interview
   pack. For each question, write the criterion in the job description it
   derives from [21] [22]. Flag any question with no line back. Separately,
   check every question against s.60 [4]: does it touch health or disability at
   all, and if so, which listed exception applies?
2. **Barrier self-scan (interviewers, 20 minutes after each panel).** Take the
   19 named barriers [14] and mark the three most likely to have operated in
   the session just completed. For each, write the counter-measure the College
   prescribes for that group, and one change to the next session's set-up.
3. **Independent-scoring discipline drill (panel, one session).** Score every
   candidate independently and record the scores before any discussion, per
   CIPD [23] and the Cabinet Office observe/record/classify/evaluate sequence
   [15]. Afterwards, measure how far panellists moved in discussion. Large
   movements are a signal about process, not about the candidate.
4. **Adverse-impact dry run (HR, 60 minutes).** Take last cycle's pass rates by
   group at each stage. Compute the four-fifths ratio [14]. Then write, in one
   sentence, what your process *does* when a ratio falls below 0.8 — and note
   that the rule has no legal footing, so the trigger is an internal one you
   must define [14].
5. **ADM inventory (HR, 90 minutes).** List every automated or scored stage.
   For each, answer questions 24–29 above in writing against the ICO's tests
   [10] [11] [12]. Any stage where a human's review of low scorers cannot be
   evidenced is the stage to look at first.
6. **Adjustment request rehearsal (candidates, 15 minutes).** Write your
   request in one sentence naming the adjustment, the stage it applies to, and
   the disadvantage it removes. Check it against the categories EPSO publishes
   — scheduling, equipment, format and layout, personal devices, assistance,
   aids [20] — or against the GOV.UK and ACAS examples [7] [22]. Then identify
   the person and the moment you will send it to.
7. **Fair-process checklist (candidates, before and after interview).** Ask in
   advance what the assessment involves, how long it takes and what technology
   is used [23]. Afterwards note: same questions as others? criteria published?
   more than one interviewer [22]? any automated stage disclosed [13]? A "no"
   is not proof of anything — it is a question worth asking the employer.

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — cited. Signposting only; this is not legal advice.**

- **This document does not advise on individual cases.** It records published
  obligations and points to the primary text. ACAS itself notes that "the law
  around discrimination and protected characteristics can be complex" and
  directs employers with questions about their circumstances to its helpline
  [21] [22]. For anything with consequences, read the cited instrument and take
  qualified advice.
- **Territorial limits.** The Equality Act 2010 sections relied on here are
  marked "E+W+S" — England, Wales and Scotland [1] [3] [4] [5]. Northern
  Ireland's separate regime is out of scope.
- **UK: the core recruitment provisions.** Protected characteristics, s.4 [1];
  discrimination and victimisation in the arrangements for deciding whom to
  offer employment, s.39 [3]; the three-requirement adjustments duty, s.20 [2];
  the pre-offer restriction on health and disability enquiries with its listed
  exceptions and its unusual enforcement split, s.60 [4]; the public sector
  equality duty, s.149, whose relevant-characteristics list runs to eight [5];
  and positive action in recruitment on strict cumulative conditions, s.159
  [6].
- **EU: equal treatment in access to employment.** Directive 2000/78/EC
  establishes a general framework combating discrimination on grounds of
  religion or belief, disability, age or sexual orientation in employment and
  occupation (art. 1), applying to "conditions for access to employment …
  including selection criteria and recruitment conditions" in both public and
  private sectors (art. 3(1)(a)), and requiring reasonable accommodation for
  disabled persons short of a disproportionate burden (art. 5) [16]. Direct
  discrimination is less favourable treatment in a comparable situation on a
  listed ground; indirect discrimination arises from an apparently neutral
  provision, criterion or practice (art. 2) [16].
- **EU institutions: staff law.** Non-discrimination on an open-ended list of
  grounds and the reasonable-accommodation definition sit at Staff Regulations
  art. 1d [19]; selection-board composition, including "if a selection board
  consists of more than four members, it shall comprise at least two members of
  each gender", at Annex III art. 3 [19]; and the prohibition on recording
  political, trade union, philosophical, religious, racial, ethnic or
  sexual-orientation matters in an official's personal file at art. 26 [19].
  EPSO's reasonable-accommodations process is the operational route [20].
- **Candidate data.** In the UK, the ICO's draft recruitment and selection
  guidance covers automated decision-making, shortlisting, testing and
  interviewing, and recruitment records under the UK GDPR and DPA 2018 [8]; the
  ADM provisions require meaningful human involvement or the article 22C
  safeguards, plus transparency at three points in time [10] [11]. In the EU,
  GDPR art. 22 [17] and, for the institutions, Regulation (EU) 2018/1725 art.
  24 [18] give the equivalent right not to be subject to solely automated
  decisions with legal or similarly significant effects. ACAS requires
  interview notes and recordings to be stored securely and confidentially,
  with recording only by prior permission [22].
- **Fairness is also a data-protection concept, not only an equality one.** The
  ICO frames fairness as handling personal information "in ways people
  reasonably expect" and not "in ways that have unjustified adverse effects on
  people", and treats failures of human involvement, transparency and
  safeguards as themselves pointing to unfairness [12].
- **Where the evidence is silent.** No source located for this document states
  a lawful maximum for interview panel size, a required ratio of panel
  diversity, a mandated adverse-impact threshold in UK law, or a legal
  entitlement to any *specific* adjustment. Those are matters for the primary
  instruments and, in a real case, for advice.

# Risks & Caveats

- **This is signposting, and signposting decays.** Every quotation was verified
  on 2026-07-20, but statutes are amended and guidance is reissued. Check the
  primary source before relying on any wording here.
- **The ICO recruitment and selection guidance is a draft.** The page states the
  consultation is closed, the final version "will be published in due course",
  and the guidance "is under review and may be subject to change" because of the
  Data (Use and Access) Act [8]. Anything sourced to [8] may move.
- **The ADM law changed mid-evidence.** The ICO notes its engagement with
  employers "largely took place whilst the pre-DUAA ADM provisions were in
  force", and that it therefore focused on areas consistent between old article
  22 and new articles 22A–22D [11]. The findings are robust; the article
  numbering in older material is not.
- **The College of Policing guidance predates the 2024 CVF.** `cop-vbr-selection-2018`
  is a 2018 document written against the 2016 framework, and is used here only
  for its framework-agnostic process content — barriers, ORCE, adverse impact,
  method validity [14]. Do not read its competency or value names as current.
- **The Cabinet Office diversity finding is conditional and undated as to its
  underlying analysis.** The guidance reports the conclusion but not the
  dataset, sample or method [15]; it should not be cited as a general finding
  about Success Profiles interviewing.
- **EU ground lists differ from UK ones and from each other.** Directive
  2000/78/EC covers four grounds [16]; the Equality Act nine [1]; the Staff
  Regulations an open-ended list [19]. Sex and race are covered in EU law by
  separate directives not examined here — a deliberate scope exclusion, not an
  omission by oversight.
- **EUR-Lex is hostile to automated retrieval.** All four EU instruments cited
  here were opened and read in a real browser; link checkers report EUR-Lex
  URLs as live even when no automated client can read them, so a green link
  gate is not evidence that these citations were verified. They were, on
  2026-07-20, by direct reading.
- **The EPSO adjustments URL now redirects.** The registered URL resolves to
  `eu-careers.europa.eu/en/reasonable-accommodations`, and the page is titled
  *Reasonable Accommodations* [20]. Content was read at the destination.
- **The question bank is mostly composed, by necessity.** Statutes and
  regulators publish duties, not question sets. Only four entries could be
  labelled *(sourced …)*; the rest are written for this knowledge base and are
  labelled accordingly. They are audit prompts, not a compliance instrument.
- **Nothing here is legal advice.** Stated once in the Executive Summary, once
  in Legal & Fairness Considerations, and once more here, because it is the
  claim most likely to be forgotten between reading and acting.
