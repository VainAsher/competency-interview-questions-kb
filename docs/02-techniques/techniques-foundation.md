# Executive Summary

This is the foundation document for the Techniques category. It maps the answer
structures that candidates are advised to use in competency-based (behavioural)
interviews across the UK and EU markets, and — the mirror image — what those
structures give an assessor to score. The anchor technique is STAR (Situation,
Task, Action, Result), which is explicitly recommended by the UK Civil
Service's own candidate guidance and by the National Careers Service, and is
the default recommendation of most university careers services. Around STAR
sits a family of documented variants, one of which also carries official
backing: CAR (Context, Action, Results) is named in GOV.UK's *A brief guide to
competencies* (Civil Service Resourcing) as the alternative to STAR — "Use
either STAR or CAR approach for writing your competency" — and its elements are
corroborated by the University of Cambridge Careers Service. The remaining
variants rest on careers-service and practitioner documentation:
STARR/STAR(R) with an added
Reflection step (University of Reading, Arden University, and graduate
employers via targetjobs), CARL (Context, Action, Results, Learning —
documented in the University of Edinburgh Reflection Toolkit as an adaptation
of an interview technique), and SOAR (Situation, Obstacle, Action, Result),
which so far is documented mainly in practitioner careers writing rather than
official guidance.

The document serves both audiences. For candidates it records who recommends
which structure, what each element is for, and how to select evidence from
work, study, volunteering and life outside work. For interviewers it records
the documented link between structure and scoring: structured interviews with
consistent questions and criteria are the professional-body recommendation
(CIPD, ACAS), and the official guidance is explicit that behaviours are scored
on what the candidate did — which is precisely what the Action element of any
of these frameworks surfaces. It also records the documented boundary of the
technique: UK Civil Service strengths questions are expressly *not* to be
answered with rehearsed, pre-structured examples.

Confidence is **Medium**: the STAR and CAR recommendations and the
assessment-process facts rest on official UK government, EPSO,
professional-body and statutory sources, but the documentation of the
remaining alternative structures (STARR/STAR(R), CARL, SOAR) rests on
university careers services and named practitioner sources, and some inference
is involved in comparing them. Deep-dives on STAR itself, on the alternative
frameworks, and on building an evidence bank are planned as child documents.

# Key Takeaways

- STAR (Situation, Task, Action, Result) is the answer structure named in UK
  Civil Service candidate guidance and by the National Careers Service for
  competency/behaviour questions, on application forms as well as at interview.
  *(documented)*
- CAR (Context, Action, Results) is **also officially documented**: GOV.UK's
  *A brief guide to competencies* names it as the alternative for candidates
  who "cannot relate to the STAR method" and tells applicants to "Use either
  STAR or CAR approach". STAR remains the most common approach within the
  Civil Service; CAR is equally official, not equally prevalent. (That guidance
  is dated 2016 and predates Success Profiles — cited for answer structure
  only; see Risks & Caveats.) *(documented)*
- The further alternatives are close cousins of STAR but rest on non-official
  documentation: STARR/STAR(R) adding Reflection (Reading, Arden, graduate
  employers via targetjobs), CARL adding Learning (Edinburgh), and SOAR
  replacing Task with Obstacle (practitioner sources only). *(documented)*
- EPSO's competency-based interview is structured — questions are designed so
  assessors can evaluate selected general competencies, about 10 minutes per
  competency — but the EPSO pages reviewed do not prescribe a named answer
  mnemonic. *(documented)*
- Structure earns nothing by itself: assessors score the behaviour or
  competency evidenced, and official guidance highlights that the framework's
  job is to set context, demonstrate *your* actions and state outcomes.
  *(documented)*
- Technique has a documented boundary: for UK Civil Service strengths
  questions, candidates are told **not** to rehearse answers, so a pre-built
  STAR story is the wrong tool. *(documented)*
- Evidence does not have to come from paid work — official guidance explicitly
  allows examples from volunteering and life outside work, which matters for
  graduate-scheme and early-careers candidates. *(documented)*
- Choose the structure per question: result-led questions suit CAR's
  compression, failure/learning questions reward STARR/CARL's reflection step,
  initiative questions map naturally onto SOAR's obstacle framing. *(guidance)*
- Interviewers should score content against the published criteria, not
  fluency with the mnemonic; consistent questions and criteria are the
  CIPD/ACAS-recommended basis for fair comparison. *(documented)*

# Purpose

This document answers two questions. For a candidate: *which answer structure
should I use, on whose authority, and how do I fit my experience into it?* For
an interviewer or HR designer: *what does a well-structured answer look like
when I am scoring it, and what does published guidance say about assessing
consistently?* It is the Tier-1 foundation for the Techniques category:
breadth over depth, with deep-dives delegated to planned child documents
(star-technique, alternative-answer-frameworks, building-evidence-bank).

# Definitions

- **Competency-based (behavioural) question** — a question asking for a real
  past example that demonstrates a defined behaviour or competency, as used in
  UK Civil Service behaviours assessment and EPSO's competency-based interview.
- **STAR** — Situation, Task, Action, Result: a structure for evidencing a
  skill through a past example.
- **CAR** — Context, Action, Results: a three-part compression of STAR.
- **STARR / STAR(R)** — STAR plus a final Reflection element on what was
  learned or would be done differently.
- **CARL** — Context, Action, Results, Learning: a reflective four-part
  structure adapted from interview technique.
- **SOAR** — Situation, Obstacle, Action, Result: a practitioner variant that
  replaces Task with Obstacle.
- **Strengths question** — a question about what a candidate enjoys and does
  well, used in UK Civil Service Success Profiles; expressly not to be
  answered with rehearsed examples.
- **Structured interview** — an interview using a predefined set of questions
  asked consistently to all candidates and scored against consistent criteria.

# Framework Context

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

STAR's authority in the UK market is official, not folklore. GOV.UK's Success
Profiles guidance on Civil Service Behaviours states that candidates may be
asked to give examples of when they demonstrated a particular behaviour, and
recommends the STAR method for structuring those examples — allowing the
candidate to set context, demonstrate the actions they took and describe the
outcome [1]. The Civil Service's own recruitment blog repeats the advice at
both stages of the process: "Use the STAR method (Situation, Task, Action,
Result) to structure your responses" for applications, and for interviews,
"Research common Civil Service interview questions for the grade and similar
roles and prepare examples of your work using the STAR method" [3]. The
National Careers Service — the UK government careers advice service — publishes
a dedicated STAR page defining the four elements and recommending the method
for CVs, application forms and interviews [4], and points readers to STAR when
answering common work-history interview questions [5].

Official UK guidance does not, however, document STAR alone. GOV.UK's *A brief
guide to competencies*, published by Civil Service Resourcing on 12 April 2016,
states that "There are many different approaches you could take to writing
competencies, such as the STAR, or the CAR method. As to which one you chose to
use, it's all down to your personal style of writing" [8]. (That page was
written for the competency framework that Success Profiles replaced in 2018 and
is cited here solely for its answer-structure guidance, which is
framework-agnostic and uncontradicted by the current Success Profiles guidance
[1]; none of its framework content is relied on — see Risks & Caveats.) It
records that, as at 2016, "Within the Civil Service, the most common approach
is: The STAR method", then introduces the
alternative in terms: "Not everyone can relate to the STAR method, so an
alternative approach is: The CAR approach — Context - Explain the situation;
what, where and when… Actions - Make sure that you explain how you did
something not just what you did… Results - What was the outcome?" [8]. Its
summary closes: "Use either STAR or CAR approach for writing your competency"
[8]. CAR therefore has official-guidance backing in the UK market, on a par
with STAR as to *standing*, while STAR is the more prevalent Civil Service
approach on the same source's own account [8] and the one named in current
Success Profiles guidance [1].

The same guidance also gives official backing to the reflective move that the
named reflective variants formalise: on the Result element it instructs "Was it
a successful outcome? If not what did you learn from the experience?" and "If
the result was not entirely successful describe what you learned from this and
what you would do differently next time", and for CAR's Results, "Remember if
your result is not positive, describe what you have learnt" [8]. This is
official backing for the reflective *move*, not for STARR or CARL as named
structures — GOV.UK does not name either, nor SOAR [8]. On the Action element
it adds the assessor-facing emphasis "Keep the focus on you… Try to use 'I'
rather than 'we'", and on proportion, "Keep the situation and task parts brief.
Concentrate on the action and the result" [8].

Within Success Profiles, the technique's remit is bounded: behaviours are
example-based, but strengths are assessed differently. GOV.UK's strengths
guidance defines strengths as "the things that we do regularly, do well and
that motivate us" and instructs candidates: "Do not rehearse your answers -
because we are looking for your initial response" [2].

On the EU side, EPSO (the European Personnel Selection Office) runs a
competency-based interview "designed to evaluate a number of selected general
competencies, depending on the competition and grade", with candidates
"interviewed for about 10 minutes per competency" and questions "structured to
allow the assessors to evaluate the candidate's selected general competencies"
[6]. The competencies themselves are defined in EPSO's Competency Framework,
which "defines the competencies that are considered essential to perform
effectively in any given job"; the Notice of Competition is "the only official
document that contains detailed information about the actual tests used by
EPSO for a given competition" [7]. The EPSO pages reviewed describe structure
on the assessor's side but do not prescribe a named answer mnemonic to
candidates [6][7].

University careers services — the main advice channel for graduate-scheme
candidates — document the family of structures by name. Oxford's Careers
Service tells candidates to "Use the S.T.A.R technique and make sure to
emphasise your actions if describing a situation" [13]. The University of
Reading documents STAR as "a useful structure for answering application form
and interview questions" and adds a second R for reflection [12]. The
University of Cambridge Careers Service corroborates CAR — "context, action,
results" [14] — with element definitions consistent with the official GOV.UK
treatment [8]. Arden University documents STAR(R) with an optional Reflection
element [16]. The University of Edinburgh's Reflection Toolkit documents CARL
(Context, Action, Results, Learning), noting "The CARL model is adapted from a
job interview technique" [15]. In the private-sector graduate market, the
employer advice channel targetjobs carries Fidelity International's guidance on
STARR, where reflection is presented as "the difference between a good answer
and a GREAT answer" [17].

# Core Reference

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## STAR: the documented elements

The National Careers Service defines the four elements as: "Situation - the
situation you had to deal with; Task - the task you were given to do; Action -
the action you took; Result - what happened as a result of your action and
what you learned" [4]. It recommends the method for showing how you meet the
skills and qualities in a job description, advises keeping examples concise
and conversational rather than sounding over-rehearsed, and states that
examples may be drawn from work, volunteering or personal experience [4]. The
University of Reading's version assigns each element a job: Situation "set[s]
the scene", Task describes what was to be completed and why, Action is "what
**you** did to complete the task" — the element that demonstrates the
competency — and Result "outline[s] the outcome" and its impact [12]. Oxford's
guidance adds the assessor-facing emphasis: emphasise your actions [13].

## CAR: the three-part compression

GOV.UK's *A brief guide to competencies* sets out the official version:
"Context - Explain the situation; what, where and when. Simply describe the
challenge that you faced… Actions - Make sure that you explain how you did
something not just what you did. What action or steps did you take?… Results -
What was the outcome? What results did you achieve?… Use numbers and
percentages whenever possible" [8]. Cambridge's Careers Service corroborates
CAR as: "**C**ontext: describe the
situation you were in, the task at hand, the problems you faced… **A**ction:
what was your role?… be specific about how you worked and what you did.
**R**esults: what happened? Were you successful?" [14]. Structurally, CAR
folds STAR's Situation and Task into a single Context element; the same
evidence fits both shapes — which is why the official guidance can leave the
choice to "your personal style of writing" [8].

## STARR / STAR(R) and CARL: adding reflection

Reading and Arden both document a reflection extension. Arden describes the
extra (R) as optional: the candidate can "reflect on the Situation, Task,
Action and Result" to show "what you have learnt from certain circumstances,
and evidence how you might develop in the future" [16]. Fidelity
International's guidance on targetjobs makes the employer's motive explicit:
"We think that reflecting on your actions and how you might do something
differently next time, can be the difference between a good answer and a GREAT
answer" [17]. Edinburgh's CARL is the same idea from the reflective-practice
side: Context ("Briefly describe the context of you[r] experience"), Action
("Explain what actions you took"), Results ("Explain what happened as a result
of your actions"), Learning ("Identify what you have learned") — and the
toolkit records that the model is adapted from a job interview technique [15].
Note that the National Careers Service's own definition of the STAR Result
element already includes "what you learned" [4], and GOV.UK's competencies
guidance directs candidates with an unsuccessful outcome to "describe what you
learned from this and what you would do differently next time" [8] — so the
reflective variants formalise a move the official guidance already asks for.
That backing is for the move, not for the labels: GOV.UK names neither STARR
nor CARL (nor SOAR), and their standing as structures remains careers-service
and practitioner documentation [8][12][15][16][17][18].

## SOAR: the practitioner variant

SOAR (Situation, Obstacle, Action, Result) is documented in named, dated
practitioner careers writing as "a structured way to answer behavioral
interview questions", replacing STAR's Task with Obstacle to emphasise
problem-solving [18]. No official UK or EU guidance reviewed for this document
names SOAR; it is recorded here as practitioner practice, not as an
officially recommended technique [18].

## Selecting evidence

The documented guidance on evidence selection is consistent across sources: the
job description or advert lists the skills, qualities or behaviours sought, and
the example is chosen to show how the candidate meets them [4][1]. Candidates
without much work history are explicitly permitted to use examples from outside
paid work — volunteering and personal experience in the National Careers
Service guidance [4] — and the Civil Service blog advises identifying the key
behaviours required for the target role and preparing specific examples that
demonstrate them [3]. For strengths questions the documented preparation is
different in kind: reflect on personal strengths and preferred ways of
working, but do not rehearse, because assessors want the initial response [2].

# Question Bank

Each entry carries a provenance label per the house rules. The one-line note

## Published questions from public careers guidance

1. When have you faced a challenging situation? *(sourced: National Careers Service)* [5] — Note: classic full-STAR territory; the challenge is the Situation, and the marks live in the Action [4][13].
2. Can you tell us about a personal achievement at work? *(sourced: National Careers Service)* [5] — Note: suits CAR's compression [14]: brief context, then lead with actions and a concrete, measurable Result.
3. Have you ever taken the initiative? *(sourced: National Careers Service)* [5] — Note: maps naturally onto SOAR's obstacle/objective framing [18] — what you chose to tackle unprompted, then Action and Result.
4. Have you ever failed at a task? *(sourced: National Careers Service)* [5] — Note: a reflection question in disguise; STARR/CARL fit best because the Learning step is the actual answer [15][16][17].
5. What are your main strengths? *(sourced: National Careers Service)* [5] — Note: not a story question — do not force STAR; UK Civil Service strengths guidance says unrehearsed, authentic responses are wanted [2].

## Composed — in the style of UK Civil Service behaviours [1]

1. Tell us about a time you had to deliver a piece of work to a tight deadline while quality concerns were being raised. What did you do? *(composed)* — Note: STAR with a deliberately fat Action section; the tension between pace and quality is resolved in what *you* did [1][13].
2. Describe a situation where you used incomplete or conflicting evidence to reach a decision that others questioned. *(composed)* — Note: STAR with a strong Task element — name the decision that was yours to own before narrating actions [1].
3. Give an example of working with people from very different backgrounds or professional areas to deliver a shared outcome. *(composed)* — Note: CAR works well [14]; the interpersonal actions are the evidence, so compress context hard.

## Composed — in the style of the EPSO competency-based interview [6]

1. Describe an occasion when you had to reprioritise your work at short notice. How did you decide what mattered most? *(composed)* — Note: use a compressed STAR; EPSO allows roughly 10 minutes per competency including follow-ups, so the example must land quickly [6].
2. Tell us about a time you explained a complex or technical issue to a non-specialist audience. *(composed)* — Note: STAR with the Action expanded into steps (how you adapted the message), since the questions are structured for assessors to evaluate the competency [6].

## Composed — private-sector and graduate-scheme style

1. Tell me about a time something you were responsible for went wrong. Looking back, what would you do differently? *(composed)* — Note: the question explicitly invites the second R — answer in STARR/CARL, ending on learning applied since [15][17].
2. Describe a project or activity you started on your own initiative. What obstacles did you hit, and what came of it? *(composed)* — Note: SOAR-shaped by construction [18]; naming the obstacle distinguishes initiative from assigned work.
3. Give an example from university, work or life outside work where you kept a team on track when motivation dropped. *(composed)* — Note: STAR with non-work evidence — official guidance expressly allows volunteering and personal examples [4].

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact.** It points back to
the evidence layer throughout.

**Pick the structure per question, not per interview.** All five documented
structures carry the same payload — context, your actions, the outcome — so
the choice is about emphasis. As a working rule: use STAR as the default (it
is the one named in official UK guidance [1][3][4]); collapse to CAR when the
question is achievement- or result-led and context is obvious [14]; extend to
STARR/CARL whenever the question contains "went wrong", "failed", "learned"
or "differently" [15][16][17]; borrow SOAR's obstacle framing for initiative
questions [18]. Never bring a story structure to a strengths question — the
documented instruction is not to rehearse [2].

**Weight the answer towards Action.** Oxford's documented advice — emphasise
your actions [13] — generalises: Situation and Task/Context exist only to make
the Action legible, and the Result exists to prove the Action worked. A
serviceable skeleton for the first sourced question above ("When have you
faced a challenging situation?" [5]): one or two sentences of situation
("In my placement year, our two-person support rota lost its senior member the
week a major client onboarded"); one sentence of task ("I was left owning the
onboarding calls"); four to six sentences of first-person action, each
starting with a verb; one or two sentences of result with a number or a named
consequence, plus — if the question invites it — one sentence of reflection.

**Speak as "I", evidence as "we".** Assessors are evaluating the behaviour
*you* demonstrated [1]; acknowledge the team in the Situation, then narrate
your own decisions and actions.

**Build the story once, flex it many ways.** Because CAR, STAR, STARR and CARL
are nested, one well-chosen example can be compressed or extended live to fit
the question and the time available — useful in EPSO-style interviews where
time per competency is limited [6]. Preparing six to eight such examples,
mapped against the behaviours or competencies in the advert [3][1], is the
core of interview preparation; the planned building-evidence-bank document
will treat this in depth.

**For interviewers**: a well-structured answer is not a better answer, it is a
more *scoreable* one — the structure surfaces the actions and outcomes your
criteria ask about [1]. If a candidate's answer lacks structure, a neutral
probe ("what did you do next?", "what was the outcome?") retrieves the same
information; consistency of questioning across candidates is the documented
fairness mechanism [10][11].

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

Behaviours in the UK Civil Service are assessed through multiple methods —
application-form examples, tests, interviews, assessment-centre exercises and
simulations — against a published set of nine behaviours (Seeing the Big
Picture, Changing and Improving, Making Effective Decisions, Leadership,
Communicating and Influencing, Working Together, Developing Self and Others,
Managing a Quality Service, Delivering at Pace), with recruiting managers
choosing the behaviours relevant to the role and level [1]. The same guidance
frames STAR from the assessor's side: it lets the candidate set context, show
the actions taken and describe outcomes — i.e. it produces the material the
behaviour is scored on [1]. Strengths, by contrast, are assessed through
interviews, recorded video interviews, situational strengths tests, simulation
assessments or personality tests, with no right or wrong answers and an
explicit instruction against rehearsal [2].

In EPSO competitions, the competency-based interview evaluates selected
general competencies specified per competition and grade, at about 10 minutes
per competency, with questions structured so that assessors can evaluate those
competencies [6]; the competencies are defined in EPSO's Competency Framework
and the Notice of Competition is the authoritative statement of what a given
competition tests [7].

Professional-body guidance converges on structure as the fairness mechanism.
CIPD's selection guidance recommends structured interviews — "asking a
predefined set of questions, in the same order, to all candidates", scored
against consistent criteria — because this makes it "easier for the interview
panels to make direct and fair comparisons between candidates, using objective
criteria"; it further recommends panel interviews over single interviewers and
that interviewers "assign scores to each candidate response independently
before discussing them as a group" [10]. ACAS advises employers to ask each
applicant "the same questions where possible" to ensure equal treatment, and
to check that the final decision is "based on how each applicant met each
point in the job description and person specification" [11]. Read together
with the candidate-side guidance, the documented picture is symmetrical: the
candidate's structure exists to feed the assessor's criteria, and the
assessor's consistency exists to make comparisons of that evidence fair
[1][10][11].

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — labelled guidance,** each item tied back to the documented
criteria it violates.

- **Front-loading context.** Spending half the answer on Situation starves the
  element that is actually scored — the documented advice is to emphasise your
  actions [13][1].
- **"We" throughout.** An answer narrated entirely as team achievement leaves
  no individual behaviour to score; the guidance asks what *you* did [1][4].
- **No result.** Arden's guidance notes the Result is often overlooked —
  "Without this part, the interviewer will not know what the outcome was"
  [16]. An answer without an outcome is an anecdote, not evidence.
- **Forcing STAR onto strengths questions.** The UK guidance explicitly wants
  unrehearsed initial responses to strengths questions [2]; a polished story
  answers the wrong question.
- **Sounding scripted.** The National Careers Service advises keeping examples
  conversational to avoid sounding over-rehearsed [4]; recite the structure,
  not a script.
- **One mega-example.** Reusing a single example for every question narrows
  the behaviours evidenced; official advice is to prepare examples against the
  key behaviours required for the role [3].
- **(Interviewers) crediting polish over content.** The documented basis for
  decisions is how the applicant met the job description and person
  specification [11], scored consistently [10] — not fluency with a mnemonic.
- **(Interviewers) inconsistent probing.** Rescuing one candidate's
  unstructured answer with follow-ups but not another's undermines the
  same-questions consistency ACAS and CIPD recommend [11][10].

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.** Drills a reader can run this week.

1. **Build a starter evidence bank.** Take a live job advert; list its named
   behaviours or competencies [1][3]. Against each, note one real example —
   work, study, volunteering or outside life all count [4]. Aim for six to
   eight distinct examples covering the list.
2. **Write one STAR answer, then compress and extend it.** Draft an answer to
   the challenging-situation question (Question Bank, first sourced entry) in
   full STAR [4]; rewrite it as CAR in half the length [14]; then
   add a Reflection sentence to make it STARR [16][17]. Notice what each shape
   foregrounds.
3. **Timed delivery.** Answer two Question Bank items aloud in about two
   minutes each, keeping most of the time in Action. For EPSO-style practice,
   remember the whole competency slot is around 10 minutes including
   follow-ups [6].
4. **Self-score against published criteria.** Record yourself, then score the
   answer only on evidence of the target behaviour as defined in the published
   framework [1] — not on delivery. If you cannot find the behaviour in your
   own transcript, the assessor cannot either.
5. **Mock panel (pairs).** One person asks three Question Bank items
   consistently, probes neutrally ("what did you do next?"), takes notes, and
   scores independently before discussing — mirroring the CIPD-recommended
   discipline [10].
6. **Strengths contrast drill.** Have a partner ask the strengths question
   from the Question Bank ("What are your main strengths?") cold and answer
   without preparation, then reflect on your preferred ways of working as the
   guidance suggests [2] — feel the difference from example-based answering.

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.** Signposts only; siblings treat these
in depth.

- **Reasonable adjustments.** Under the Equality Act 2010, section 20, there
  is a duty to make adjustments where a disabled person would otherwise be at
  a substantial disadvantage — including adjusting provisions, criteria or
  practices and providing auxiliary aids [9]. ACAS guidance applies this to
  recruitment: employers must make reasonable adjustments for disabled
  applicants and interviewees, for example ensuring the venue is accessible or
  providing a sign language interpreter [11]. A rigidly timed, orally
  delivered STAR interview is a "practice" that may need adjusting.
- **Consistency as fairness.** ACAS advises asking each applicant the same
  questions where possible to ensure equal treatment, and basing decisions on
  how each applicant met the job description and person specification [11];
  CIPD frames the same discipline as the basis for fair comparison [10].
- **Candidate data.** ACAS notes that interviews may only be recorded with the
  person's permission and that interview notes must be stored securely and
  confidentially in line with data protection law [11].

# Risks & Caveats

- Careers-service pages are living web content: definitions and page structure
  change without version history. All URLs were opened and verified on
  2026-07-19; the mnemonics' exact wording varies between sources.
- Variant names are not standardised. STARR, STAR(R), STAR-L and similar
  labels circulate in careers materials with overlapping meanings; this
  document records only the variants it could verify to a named publisher.
  Treat any other label as unverified until documented.
- SOAR rests on practitioner sources only; it should not be presented to
  candidates as an officially recommended technique.
- **Currency of the GOV.UK competencies guidance [8].** *A brief guide to
  competencies* is dated 12 April 2016 and was written for the pre-2018 Civil
  Service competency framework (10 competencies in 3 clusters), which Success
  Profiles replaced in 2018 [1]. The page is still live with no withdrawal or
  superseded banner, which makes the staleness easy to miss. It is cited in
  this document **only** for answer-structure guidance (STAR and CAR), which is
  framework-agnostic and not contradicted by Success Profiles; none of its
  framework content is relied on here, and its continued publication should not
  be read as evidence that the pre-2018 framework is current. For framework
  content, Success Profiles [1][2] governs.
- Employer practice varies. This document records published guidance;
  individual employers (including departments and EU institutions) may issue
  their own candidate instructions, which take precedence for that process.
- EPSO's selection model has been undergoing reform; the Notice of Competition
  for a specific competition is the only authoritative statement of its tests
  [7], and details here may lag current competitions.

# Open Questions

- **Does any EU-institution guidance name an answer structure?** The EPSO
  pages reviewed describe structured assessment but no candidate-side
  mnemonic. A sweep of institution-specific careers pages (Commission,
  Parliament, Council — noting consilium.europa.eu bot-blocks checkers) would
  settle whether STAR appears in official EU candidate guidance.
- **Graduate-market prevalence data.** Institute of Student Employers survey
  data on selection methods is member-gated and could not be cited; a
  publicly citable ISE statistic on the prevalence of competency/strengths
  interviewing in graduate recruitment would strengthen the graduate-scheme
  sections.
- **Academic evidence base.** The occupational-psychology literature on
  structured-interview validity is largely paywalled; an open-access
  authoritative source would let the Assessor Perspective cite effect-size
  evidence rather than professional-body guidance alone.
- **Origin of STAR.** None of the official sources reviewed attributes the
  mnemonic's origin; a documented history would be useful context for the
  planned star-technique deep-dive.
