# Executive Summary

This document is the deep treatment of STAR — Situation, Task, Action, Result —
for the UK and EU competency-interview markets. Its parent,
`techniques-foundation`, establishes *that* STAR is the officially recommended
structure and how it sits alongside CAR, STARR, CARL and SOAR. This document
does not repeat that. It goes after the four things that actually decide
whether a STAR answer scores: **what belongs in each element and what does
not**; **proportioning** — the ratio of Action to scene-setting, which is where
most answers fail; **timing** — how one example must survive both a roughly
ten-minute EPSO competency slot [10] and a one-to-three-minute asynchronous
video box [25]; and the **documented limit** of the method, namely that UK
Civil Service strengths questions expressly must not be answered with a
rehearsed structure [3][9].

The proportioning evidence is better than commonly supposed, and it starts with
central government. GOV.UK's *A brief guide to competencies*, published by Civil
Service Resourcing, instructs verbatim: "Keep the situation and task parts
brief. Concentrate on the action and the result" [27]. That is an official
instruction on proportioning *direction*. Four independent careers sources point
the same way — the University of Reading states that Action "is the longest part
of your answer" [18]; targetjobs that "proportionally, you should dedicate most
of your response to the Action part" [24]; My World of Work calls Action "the
most important part" [26]; Oxford tells candidates to "make sure to emphasise
your actions" [19]. A published NHS trust diagram goes further and puts a
number on it, bracketing Situation, Task and Action together as 70% of the
answer and Result as the remaining 30% [12] — a split verified visually here
because its column layout misreads under automated text extraction.

What no central publisher supplies is a **numeric ratio**. GOV.UK gives the
direction and no percentages [27][1][2]; EPSO gives a time budget and no
composition guidance [10]. Every specific ratio in circulation, including the
one in this document's coaching layer, is careers-service material or
synthesis. That narrower claim is the honest version of the finding.

The assessor-side explanation for all of this is the ORCE sequence — observe,
record, classify, evaluate. College of Policing guidance instructs assessors to
"only classify behavioural evidence" and to "classify each behaviour once — do
not double count", then evaluate "based on quality and quantity of the
classified evidence" [11]. Cabinet Office guidance imposes the same sequence on
Civil Service panels, who "observe, record, classify, evaluate and score each
question independently" before reconciling arithmetically [7]. Scene-setting
generates nothing classifiable. That is the mechanism, not a coaching opinion.

Confidence is **High**. Every evidence-layer claim — the elements, the
proportioning direction, first-person attribution, poor-result handling, the
scoring scales, the ORCE sequence, the EPSO timing and the strengths limitation
— is independently cited to central government, Cabinet Office, College of
Policing, NHS-trust, police-force or EPSO sources. The one substantial caveat is
that the clearest central proportioning instruction sits on a 2016 page written
for the Civil Service competency framework that Success Profiles superseded in
2018 [27]; the advice is framework-agnostic and is nowhere contradicted, but its
provenance is recorded in Risks & Caveats. The specific numeric split offered in
Model Answer Guidance is this knowledge base's own synthesis and is labelled as
such; per the confidence convention, labelled coaching-layer synthesis does not
cap the rating.

# Key Takeaways

- **Central government publishes the proportioning direction.** GOV.UK: "Keep
  the situation and task parts brief. Concentrate on the action and the result"
  [27]. Reading calls Action "the longest part of your answer" [18]; targetjobs
  says dedicate "most of your response" to it [24]; My World of Work calls it
  "the most important part" [26]. *(documented)*
- What is **not** published centrally is a numeric ratio. GOV.UK gives direction
  without percentages [27]; EPSO gives timing without composition [10]. Any
  specific split is careers-service guidance or synthesis. *(documented)*
- One published NHS trust diagram quantifies the split — Situation, Task and
  Action bracketed together at **70%**, Result at **30%** [12]. This is a
  values-based-interview weighting, where reflection is deliberately heavy; it
  is not a Civil Service rule. *(documented)*
- The mechanism is ORCE. Assessors "only classify behavioural evidence" and
  "classify each behaviour once — do not double count" [11]; Civil Service
  panels run the same observe-record-classify-evaluate sequence [7]. Unclassifiable
  narrative earns nothing. *(documented)*
- The scoring scale rewards evidence volume, not narrative quality: a 5 is
  "substantial positive evidence of the behaviour", a 3 is "moderate positive
  evidence but some negative evidence demonstrated" [8]. Behaviours score on the
  7-point scale, strengths on the 4-point scale [7]. *(documented)*
- "I", not "we", is a documented requirement, not a stylistic preference — and
  it is documented centrally. GOV.UK: "Use 'I' not 'we'. This is about your role
  in the task and how you affected the outcome", and "Be careful not to take
  credit of something that you did not do" [27]. Hampshire Constabulary
  instructs: "Instead of saying, 'We did XYZ,' say, 'I did XYZ'" [15];
  Dyfed-Powys marks good evidence as "using I instead of we/us/them" [13].
  *(documented)*
- Assessors cannot fill gaps for you. GOV.UK states that "the sift panel cannot
  infer what is not included in the example and can only assess what you have
  actually written" [27] — the candidate-facing statement of the same rule ORCE
  imposes on assessors [11]. *(documented)*
- The same example must compress and expand. EPSO's legacy CBI allowed about
  ten minutes per competency [10]; asynchronous video gives "between one and
  three minutes to answer each question, with a timer on screen" [25], and
  offers "no opportunity for the interviewer to ask for clarification" [21].
  *(documented)*
- A weak or unmeasurable Result is survivable, and central guidance says how to
  handle it: "If the result was not entirely successful describe what you
  learned from this and what you would do differently next time" [27]. GOV.UK
  also states there is "no expectation that you need to demonstrate every part
  of each example to be successful" [1], and the NCS definition of Result
  already includes "what you learned from the experience" [5]. *(documented)*
- STAR has a hard boundary: for strengths questions, "do not rehearse your
  answers because we are looking for your initial response" [3][9]. Bringing a
  prepared STAR story to a strengths question answers the wrong question.
  *(documented)*
- Evidence need not come from paid work — GOV.UK allows volunteering and
  hobbies [1][2], NCS allows "work, home or volunteering" [5], and Manchester
  lists societies, placements and course-rep roles [22]. *(documented)*
- Buzzwords are not evidence. Devon & Cornwall Police warn against "CVF
  'Bingo'": "buzz words don't score" [14]. *(documented)*

# Purpose

This document answers the questions a candidate or assessor still has *after*
accepting that STAR is the right structure. Specifically: how long should each
element be, and on whose authority? What do I do when the Result was bad, or
cannot be measured? How do I deliver the same example in sixty seconds and in
three minutes? When should I *not* use STAR at all? And, for the interviewer:
why does published marking guidance reward Action detail and ignore
scene-setting?

It is a Tier-4 deep-dive. It assumes the reader knows what STAR stands for and
that official UK guidance recommends it — both established in
`techniques-foundation`.

# Scope

**Covered:** the composition of each STAR element, including explicit
exclusions; the proportioning evidence and what it does and does not say;
handling poor, partial and unmeasurable Results; timing and compression across
EPSO-style competency slots, panel interviews and asynchronous video;
first-person attribution; tense and specificity; and the documented interaction
between STAR and strengths questions.

**Not covered:** the comparative case for CAR, STARR, CARL and SOAR
(`alternative-answer-frameworks`); systematic construction and maintenance of a
personal example library (`building-evidence-bank`); the Success Profiles
framework itself (`uk-civil-service-success-profiles`); the mechanics of
recorded-video platforms (`video-asynchronous-interviews`); marking scales in
full (`scoring-and-marking`); and recovery tactics mid-interview
(`common-mistakes-recovery`).

**Market note:** the EPSO material here describes the **legacy** competency
interview. EPSO's current generalist competition model contains no competency
interview at all — see Risks & Caveats.

# Definitions

- **Proportioning** — the share of an answer's length or speaking time
  allocated to each STAR element. Used in this document specifically to mean the
  Action-to-scene-setting ratio.
- **Scene-setting** — the Situation and Task elements taken together; the
  narrative context that makes the Action intelligible.
- **ORCE** — observe, record, classify, evaluate: the structured assessor
  sequence used in College of Policing and Cabinet Office guidance [11][7].
- **Classifiable evidence** — a stated behaviour an assessor can assign to a
  named competency or value during the Classify step [11]. Contrast with
  narrative that is true but attaches to no competency.
- **Compression** — delivering the same underlying example at a shorter length
  by cutting scene-setting and action *granularity*, not by cutting the Action
  element itself.
- **Unmeasurable Result** — an outcome that is real but has no number attached,
  or whose effect could not be isolated from other causes.

# Framework Context

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## Who recommends STAR, in whose words

Four distinct classes of UK publisher recommend STAR, and their wordings differ
in ways that matter for this document's argument.

**Central government — Success Profiles.** GOV.UK's Success Profiles guidance on
Civil Service Behaviours tells candidates: "In your own examples, consider using
the STAR method which allows you to set the scene, show what and how you did and
the overall outcome" [1]. The identical formulation appears in the Success
Profiles guidance on Experience [2]. Two features of that sentence are
load-bearing: STAR is *optional* ("consider using"), and its stated job is
threefold — set the scene, show what **and how** you did, state the outcome. The
"how" is explicit central-government wording, and it is the part candidates most
often omit.

**Central government — the fuller treatment.** GOV.UK also publishes *A brief
guide to competencies* (Civil Service Resourcing, 12 April 2016), which is by
some margin the most detailed central-government treatment of STAR located for
this document, and the only central source that addresses proportioning,
first-person attribution and unsuccessful outcomes in one place [27]. It records
that "within the Civil Service, the most common approach is: The STAR method",
walks through all four elements, then instructs: "**Keep the situation and task
parts brief. Concentrate on the action and the result**" [27]. It also names CAR
as an alternative — "Not everyone can relate to the STAR method, so an
alternative approach is: The CAR approach" — and closes "Use either STAR or CAR
approach for writing your competency" [27].

**Currency caveat, stated up front.** That page is dated 2016 and was written for
the *Civil Service competency framework* — "10 common Civil Service
competencies, separated into 3 clusters: set direction, engage people, deliver
results" [27] — which Success Profiles superseded in 2018. The page remains live
on GOV.UK and carries no withdrawal notice, and its answer-technique content is
framework-agnostic and nowhere contradicted by current Success Profiles guidance
[1][2]. This document therefore cites it for **answer technique only**, never for
framework content. See Risks & Caveats.

**The Civil Service recruitment blog** is more directive than the guidance,
and applies STAR at both stages: "Use the STAR method (Situation, Task, Action,
Result) to structure your responses" for applications, and "Research common
Civil Service interview questions for the grade and similar roles and prepare
examples of your work using the STAR method" for interviews [4].

**The National Careers Service** — the UK government careers service —
publishes a dedicated STAR page defining the elements as "Situation - the
situation you had to deal with; Task - the task you were given to do; Action -
the action you took; Result - what happened as a result of your action and what
you learned from the experience" [5]. Note that the official NCS definition of
*Result* already contains a learning clause. It advises drawing on examples
"from work, home or volunteering", keeping "examples short and to the point",
speaking "in a conversational way so as not to appear too rehearsed", and being
"prepared to answer follow-up questions about the examples you give" [5].

**University careers services** supply the operational detail the government
sources omit. Reading assigns each element a job and, uniquely, a relative
size: Situation "set[s] the scene … when it was, where you were, whether you
were working individually or as a team (include the size of the team/ who you
reported to) and your responsibilities"; Task is "the task you completed,
explaining the purpose of it and the timescales"; Action is "what **you** did
to complete the task (rather than saying 'we' did)" — and Reading states
directly that "this is the longest part of your answer"; Result "outline[s] the
outcome. Was it successful; what impact did it have; what feedback did you get?"
[18]. Oxford's Careers Service is terser and points the same way: "Use the
S.T.A.R technique and make sure to emphasise your actions if describing a
situation" [19]. Manchester records that STAR is "recommended by employers" and
that evidence "can be evidenced through your studies and extra-curricular
activities such as paid work, volunteering, involvement with a student society,
work experience, a placement year or experience gained as a course rep" [22].

## Where STAR appears in non-Civil-Service UK frameworks

STAR is not confined to Success Profiles. Hampshire Constabulary's CVF guidance
presents it as "one successful format" for evidencing against the College of
Policing Competency and Values Framework, usable "on your application and in
interview" [15]. Barnsley Hospital NHS Foundation Trust's candidate guidance
calls it "a good technique for answering questions in VBI [values-based
interviewing]" and publishes a diagram of "the amount of time you should spend
on each in your answers" [12].

## The EU picture

EPSO's competency-based interview page describes an interview "designed to
evaluate a number of selected general competencies, depending on the competition
and grade the candidate has applied for, as specified in the Notice of
Competition", with questions "structured to allow the assessors to evaluate the
candidate's selected general competencies", and candidates "interviewed for
about 10 minutes per competency" [10]. EPSO does **not** name STAR or any other
mnemonic. The ten-minute figure is the only published per-competency time
budget located for this document, and it is what makes the compression drills
below concrete rather than arbitrary.

# Core Reference

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## The four elements: what belongs, and what does not

**Situation.** GOV.UK's specification is the central anchor: "Describe the
situation you found yourself in. You must describe a **specific** event or
situation. Be sure to give enough detail for the job holder to understand. Where
are you? Who was there with you? What had happened?" [27] — with the countervailing
instruction two paragraphs later to keep this part brief [27]. Reading's
specification is the fullest published: when it was,
where you were, whether you worked individually or in a team, the size of that
team, who you reported to, and your responsibilities [18]. Hampshire adds that
the situation "can be drawn from work experience, a volunteer position, or any
other relevant event" and instructs candidates to "be as specific as possible"
[15]. What does *not* belong is fixed by the assessor's Classify step: College
of Policing guidance directs assessors to "only classify behavioural evidence"
[11], so organisational background that contains no behaviour of yours is, by
the published sequence, unclassifiable. Barnsley's diagram phrases the Situation
prompts as "Give an example of…" and "**Briefly** outline a situation when.." —
the brevity instruction is in the published wording [12].

**Task.** GOV.UK: "The job holder will want to understand what you tried to
achieve from the situation you found yourself in. What was the task that you had
to complete and why? What did you have to achieve?" [27]. Reading: the task
completed, "explaining the purpose of it and the timescales" [18]. Barnsley's published Task prompts are "What was your
objective?" and "What was your role?" [12]. Hampshire: "describe your
responsibility in that situation" [15]. The consistent content is *your*
obligation and its constraints. Task is where the answer establishes that the
subsequent actions were yours to take — which is what makes them classifiable
to you rather than to your team.

**Action.** GOV.UK's wording is "show what **and how** you did" [1][2] —
method, not just deed. Reading: "what **you** did to complete the task (rather
than saying 'we' did)", and "this is the longest part of your answer" [18].
Hampshire: "describe how you completed the task or endeavoured to meet the
challenge. Focus on what you did rather than what your team, boss, or
co-worker did. Instead of saying, 'We did XYZ,' say, 'I did XYZ'" [15]. My
World of Work: "This is the most important part. Describe how you handled the
situation and conquered the problem. You're trying to show them how you
assessed the situation and stayed proactive" [26]. What does not belong is
anybody else's action, and — per the double-counting rule below — the same
behaviour restated in different words [11].

**Result.** NCS: "what happened as a result of your action and what you learned
from the experience" [5]. Reading: "Was it successful; what impact did it have;
what feedback did you get?", extending to "what you learned and what you would
do differently next time" [18]. My World of Work adds: "This is a good time to
quantify the impact you had with statistics or other evidence" [26]. Barnsley's
Result prompts are the most reflective located: "How did you feel about it?
What impact did it have on you/on others? What have you done differently? What
did you learn from it?" [12].

## Proportioning: what the sources actually establish

**The direction is centrally documented.** GOV.UK's *A brief guide to
competencies* states it in two sentences: "Keep the situation and task parts
brief. Concentrate on the action and the result" [27]. Its summary section adds
a second, subtler proportioning instruction — about composition *within* the
Action rather than between elements: "When writing your competency example make
sure you cover both 'what' you did and 'how' you did it. **In most examples you
should focus more words on the 'how' than the 'what'.** Follow this by a brief
summary of the 'outcome'" [27]. The same page warns against narrative bulk in
its own right: "Don't get caught up telling a story in your example. Just give
enough to show - how you went about the task, why you did it the way you did and
describe any obstacles you encountered" [27].

Four independent careers sources point the same way [18][19][24][26]. Only one
located source attaches a number, and its provenance deserves care.

Barnsley Hospital NHS Foundation Trust publishes a bracketed diagram
accompanying the sentence: "The format to follow and the amount of time you
should spend on each in your answers is illustrated in this diagram" [12]. The
diagram brackets **Situation, Task and Action together at 70%**, and **Result
alone at 30%** [12]. Two cautions apply. First, this document verified the
bracket **visually** by rendering the page, because the diagram's two-column
layout is not reliably recoverable by automated text extraction — the figures
extract adjacent to the Task and Action lines and would support the opposite
reading. Second, it is a values-based-interview weighting: the same page tells
candidates "the outcome of the situation is the focus here; you should spend
most of your time discussing the learning or changes you took rather than just
description of what happened" [12], and 30% on Result alone is far more than a
Civil Service behaviour answer typically warrants. It is evidence that a public
UK employer publishes a numeric split; it is **not** a general rule.

The narrower negative finding still holds and still matters: **no central UK or
EU source located for this document publishes a proportioning *ratio*.** GOV.UK
gives the direction — brief situation and task, concentrate on action and result
[27] — but attaches no percentages, and the Success Profiles pages, the Civil
Service blog and EPSO say nothing about element length at all [1][2][4][10]. NCS
says only "keep examples short and to the point" [5]. So the direction is
official; any specific *ratio* a candidate is given is careers-service guidance
or synthesis, and should be labelled as such.

## Handling a poor or unmeasurable Result

Four documented points bear on this.

First, central guidance gives an explicit instruction for the case. GOV.UK: "If
the result was not entirely successful describe what you learned from this and
what you would do differently next time" [27]. Its CAR section repeats it —
"Remember if your result is not positive, describe what you have learnt" [27] —
and its Result element asks "Was it a successful outcome? If not what did you
learn from the experience?" [27]. An unsuccessful outcome is an anticipated
case in official guidance, not an edge case.

Second, partial demonstration is explicitly tolerated. GOV.UK states: "There is
no expectation that you need to demonstrate every part of each example to be
successful" [1].

Third, the official definition of Result is not restricted to success. NCS
defines it as "what happened as a result of your action **and what you learned
from the experience**" [5]; Reading extends it to "what you would do
differently next time" [18]; Barnsley's published Result prompts include "What
have you done differently? What did you learn from it?" [12]. Learning is
inside the element, not a bolt-on.

Fourth, published question banks *actively solicit* failure. Bath's bank
includes "Tell me about a time when you felt you were unable to meet your
responsibilities" and "Tell me about a time when a problem was not solved the
way you would have liked" [23]; targetjobs lists "What has been your biggest
failure?" [24]; NCS lists "have you ever failed at a task?" [6]. A method that
could only narrate successes could not answer the published questions.

Dyfed-Powys Police's marking guide makes the assessor-side consequence
explicit: good evidence includes that "the candidate talks openly about
reflecting and learning the lessons" [13].

## Timing: the same example at two lengths

The published time budgets differ by nearly an order of magnitude.

| Format | Published time budget | Source |
|--------|----------------------|--------|
| EPSO competency-based interview (legacy) | "about 10 minutes per competency" | [10] |
| Asynchronous / pre-recorded video | "between one and three minutes to answer each question, with a timer on screen indicating how long you have left" | [25] |
| Pre-recorded video, generally | "a specific amount of time to respond to each question"; "no opportunity for the interviewer to ask for clarification" | [21] |
| College of Policing / force interview | "you only have a limited amount of time to respond" — express yourself "clearly and concisely" | [14] |

Three consequences follow from the published wording. The EPSO ten minutes is a
*competency* budget, not an answer budget — it covers follow-up probing, and
NCS advises candidates to be "prepared to answer follow-up questions about the
examples you give" [5], so the opening answer must leave room. The video
one-to-three minutes is an *answer* budget, hard-stopped by a timer [25]. And
because recorded formats permit no clarification [21], the Task element cannot
be left implicit — there is no probe coming to recover it.

targetjobs' published preparation advice for the recorded format is to time
yourself in practice to avoid being cut off mid-answer, to draft in written form
first to test relevance, and to keep notes in bullet form rather than reading
from a script [25]. Retake policy varies: "some employers allow you multiple
retakes, others only a single attempt", and completion deadlines are "usually
within a week" [25].

## "I" versus "we"

This is one of the best-evidenced points in the document, it is documented on
both the candidate and the assessor side, and the strongest statement is
central.

GOV.UK is unambiguous and says it twice. In its writing-tips section: "**Use 'I'
not 'we'.** This is about your role in the task and how you affected the
outcome" [27]. And within its Action element: "Keep the focus on you. What
specific steps did you take and what was your contribution? Remember to include
how you did it, and the behaviours you used. Try to use 'I' rather than 'we' to
explain your actions that lead to the result. **Be careful not to take credit of
something that you did not do**" [27]. That last clause is the integrity
boundary on the whole technique: first-person framing is required, but not at
the cost of claiming others' work.

Other candidate-side sources agree: Reading — say what "**you** did to complete
the task (rather than saying 'we' did)" [18]. Hampshire — "Instead of saying,
'We did XYZ,' say, 'I did XYZ'" [15]. My World of Work — on Task, "make sure to
focus on what you were specifically assigned to, not your team" [26].
targetjobs — "tell the interviewer what *your* specific actions were" [24].

Assessor side: Dyfed-Powys Police's published interview marking guide lists,
under "What does good evidence look like?", that "the candidate is using I
instead of we/us/them" [13]. This is a marking criterion in a published guide,
not advice.

Note that Reading's own Situation specification asks candidates to state
"whether you were working individually or as a team (include the size of the
team/ who you reported to)" [18] — so the team is named in Situation, and the
first person governs Action.

## Specificity and evidence, not assertion

The central statement of why specificity matters is GOV.UK's: "Don't assume the
sift panel has any knowledge of the situation or context. It's all down to you.
**The sift panel cannot infer what is not included in the example and can only
assess what you have actually written**" [27]. That sentence is the
candidate-facing mirror of the ORCE Classify rules below — an assessor scores
what is present, not what is implied. GOV.UK adds two concrete devices:
"Consider using active verbs to create greater impact" [27], and, on Results,
"Don't be shy about taking credit for your behaviour. Quote specific facts and
figures easily understandable" and "Use numbers and percentages whenever
possible" [27].

Dyfed-Powys' marking guide defines good evidence as: "The candidate used clear
and detailed sentences, **providing evidence rather than statements**. The
conversation is directed towards their attributes, skills and experience …
They have listened and understood the question and subsequently provided a
suitable detailed example — clearly demonstrating the 'beginning, middle and
end' or the 'how, why, when, what'" [13]. Hampshire instructs candidates to "be
as specific as possible" [15]. Devon & Cornwall Police tell candidates to
"express yourself clearly and concisely" and "be specific" [14].

The same force publishes the sharpest documented warning against substituting
vocabulary for evidence — "CVF 'Bingo' — buzz words don't score. Please don't
try and get as many buzz words in as possible, it is about you answering the
question and being authentic in your response, not repeating buzz words from
the CVF Framework" [14]. Its companion warning is against "answering the
competency and not the question" [14].

## The documented limit: STAR and strengths questions

Success Profiles assesses behaviours and strengths differently, and the
difference disqualifies STAR from one of them.

GOV.UK defines strengths as "the things that we do regularly, do well and that
motivate us" and instructs: "Do not rehearse your answers - because we are
looking for your initial response" [3]. The Civil Service Strengths Dictionary
carries the fuller statement: "If attending an interview, you may be asked
questions to understand whether you have strengths relevant to the job role. It
is important to remember that there are no right or wrong answers to these
questions. Do not rehearse your answers because we are looking for your initial
response. The qualities required for the role will be described in the job
description. The best way to prepare is to reflect on what you feel your
personal strengths are and your preferred ways of working" [9].

The scoring architecture reinforces the split: Cabinet Office guidance states
that "experience, technical skills and behaviours should be scored using the 7
point scale. Strengths should be scored using the 4 point scale" [7] — different
instruments, different evidence. And strengths may be assessed by "interviews,
recorded video interviews, bespoke situational strengths test, simulation
assessment, personality test" [3], several of which have no narrative slot at
all.

This is a genuine limit of the method and worth stating plainly: STAR is a tool
for evidencing *past behaviour on demand*. A strengths question asks what you
are like, and the published instruction is that a rehearsed answer defeats its
purpose [3][9].

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact.** It points back to
the evidence layer throughout.

## The proportioning rule of thumb

The direction is not in doubt and is not this document's invention: GOV.UK
instructs candidates to "keep the situation and task parts brief" and
"concentrate on the action and the result" [27], and four careers sources agree
that Action is the longest element [18][19][24][26]. What no central publisher
supplies is a number — the only located numeric split is an NHS
values-based weighting rather than a general rule [12]. So the direction below is
**documented**; the specific percentages are **this knowledge base's synthesis,
calibrated against that direction**, and must not be quoted as official.

For a UK behaviour or competency answer, a workable target is roughly:

- **Situation: 10–15%.** Just enough to satisfy Reading's checklist — when,
  where, team size, reporting line, your responsibilities [18] — while honouring
  the central instruction to keep it brief [27]. Barnsley's own published prompt
  says "*briefly* outline a situation" [12].
- **Task: 10–15%.** Your objective and your role [12][27]; the element that
  makes the actions attributable to you. Also to be kept brief [27].
- **Action: 55–65%.** The largest element by a clear margin [18][24][26], the
  element central guidance says to concentrate on [27], and the only one that
  generates classifiable behavioural evidence [11]. Within it, weight the *how*
  over the *what* [27].
- **Result: 15–20%.** Outcome, impact, feedback, learning [18][5] — the other
  element central guidance says to concentrate on [27], with figures where they
  exist [27].

Where the employer publishes its own weighting, that wins. In an NHS
values-based interview, follow Barnsley's split and give the Result
substantially more room, because the trust states outright that "the outcome of
the situation is the focus here" [12].

## Why the Action element scores: use the ORCE lens

Rehearse against the assessor's actual sequence rather than against your own
sense of a good story. An assessor observes, records, then **classifies** — and
is instructed to "only classify behavioural evidence" and to "classify each
behaviour once — do not double count" [11]. Then they evaluate "based on
quality and quantity of the classified evidence" [11].

Three coaching consequences:

1. **Sentences that contain no behaviour of yours produce no classifiable
   evidence.** Company background, market conditions and org-chart detail are
   invisible to the Classify step.
2. **Restating the same behaviour in different words adds nothing**, because
   assessors classify each behaviour once [11]. Five *distinct* actions beat one
   action described five ways.
3. **Quantity of distinct evidence is explicitly part of the evaluation** [11],
   which is the practical reason the Action element wants breadth as well as
   depth. The COPFS descriptors show the same logic on the 7-point scale: a 5 is
   "substantial positive evidence of the behaviour", a 3 only "moderate positive
   evidence but some negative evidence demonstrated" [8].

## A worked example at two lengths

The single most useful drill is delivering one example at both a video length
and a panel length. The example below is composed for this knowledge base.

**The 60-second version** — for a timed video box of one to three minutes [25],
where no clarification is possible [21]:

> *(Situation, ~8s)* Last winter I was the only permanent analyst on a
> three-person reporting team when our lead left mid-quarter.
> *(Task, ~7s)* I had to keep the weekly board pack running while we recruited.
> *(Action, ~35s)* I mapped every input the pack depended on and found four
> that only the lead had ever touched. I rewrote those four queries with
> comments so anyone could run them. I moved the pack's deadline forward by a
> day so a failure had recovery time. I trained the two contractors on the two
> highest-risk inputs and paired with each of them for one cycle. When the
> finance feed broke in week three, I published with a documented caveat rather
> than delaying, and flagged it to the board secretary the same morning.
> *(Result, ~10s)* We did not miss a single pack across the eleven weeks. The
> documentation became the team's handover standard, and I now write the runbook
> before I automate anything, not after.

That is roughly 60 seconds spoken. Note what it does: Situation and Task cost
15 seconds combined; the Action carries **five distinct decisions**, each a
first-person verb; the Result contains a number, a durable consequence and a
learning clause [5][18].

**The 3-minute version** — same example, panel setting. The correct expansion
is *not* more scene-setting. Keep Situation and Task at roughly the same 15–20
seconds and spend the additional two minutes on:

- **Action granularity** — for each of the five decisions, add the *how* and the
  *why*, which is the part central guidance names explicitly: "show what **and
  how** you did" [1][2]. How did you identify the four fragile inputs? What did
  you weigh in moving the deadline rather than reducing scope?
- **A decision you rejected** — panels can probe; volunteering the alternative
  you considered and why you discarded it pre-empts the probe and adds a
  distinct classifiable behaviour [11].
- **Result texture** — the feedback you received, which Reading names as part of
  Result [18], and the impact on others, which Barnsley names [12].

Both versions must remain the *same* example. Compression is the discipline of
cutting scene-setting and action granularity; it is never the discipline of
cutting the Action element.

## Tense and attribution

Use the simple past for Situation, Task and Action — these are things that
happened. Use the present or present perfect only in the learning clause of the
Result, where the claim is about a change that persists ("I now write the
runbook first"), which is what Barnsley's prompt "What have you done
differently?" invites [12].

On attribution: name the team once, in the Situation, exactly as Reading's
specification asks [18], then hold the first person through the Action.
"Instead of saying, 'We did XYZ,' say, 'I did XYZ'" [15] — and remember that
Dyfed-Powys marks "using I instead of we/us/them" as an explicit indicator of
good evidence [13].

## When the Result was poor

Answer the question as asked; published banks solicit failure directly
[6][23][24]. A workable shape: state the outcome plainly and early — do not
bury it; keep the Action element at full length, because the behaviours you
demonstrated are still classifiable regardless of outcome [11]; and convert the
learning into a *change already made* rather than an intention. The evidence
supports this: partial demonstration is tolerated [1], learning sits inside the
Result element by definition [5][18], and assessors mark candidates who "talk
openly about reflecting and learning the lessons" as showing good evidence [13].

When the result is genuinely unmeasurable, substitute a *named* consequence for
a number — a decision that was taken, a practice that was adopted, feedback that
was given [18]. Do not invent a metric. And do not claim credit you cannot
isolate: state your contribution precisely and let the outcome be the team's.

## For interviewers

If a candidate front-loads context, the ORCE-consistent response is to probe
neutrally for behaviour ("what did you do next?", "how did you do that?"), not
to score them down for narrative style — the evaluation is on classified
behavioural evidence [11]. Probe consistently across candidates: ACAS advises
asking each applicant "the same questions where possible" and basing the
decision on how each applicant met the job description and person specification
[17]. And score independently before discussing — Cabinet Office guidance
requires panel members to "observe, record, classify, evaluate and score each
question independently, then calculate an overall score", reconciling by
arithmetic average [7].

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## ORCE: assessors classify behaviours

The reason Action detail scores and scene-setting does not is procedural. The
College of Policing's values-based recruitment guidance instructs that "trained
assessors should follow a structured process such as ORCE (observe, record,
classify, evaluate), which breaks down the assessing process into component
parts and helps to avoid bias" [11]. The published rules for the Classify step
are:

- "After candidate has left the room"
- "Independent of any other assessor(s)"
- "**Only classify behavioural evidence**"
- "Only classify behaviour from that assessment"
- "**Classify each behaviour once — do not double count**"
- "Classify behaviours according to competencies or values and assessor
  guidance" [11]

And for Evaluate: "After classifying"; "Independent of any other assessor(s)";
"**Based on quality and quantity of the classified evidence**"; "Consider the
level of role and aim of the assessment process (benchmarking)"; "Consider what
the candidate missed or if anything detracted from their performance" [11].

This is not policing-only. Cabinet Office guidance for delegated-grade (EO–Grade
6) Civil Service recruitment states that "panel members should observe, record,
classify, evaluate and score each question independently, then calculate an
overall score before jointly agreeing a merit list using the arithmetic method"
[7]. The same four verbs, in the same order, in central Civil Service guidance.

The candidate-facing implication is direct and is the central claim of this
document: an assessor's score is a function of behaviours successfully
classified. Situation and Task sentences contain no behaviour to classify.
Repeating one behaviour cannot raise the count, because it is classified once
[11]. Therefore the Action element — distinct, first-person, method-bearing —
is where the score comes from.

## What the scales reward

The Civil Service 7-point scale, published with descriptors by COPFS, is
explicitly a scale of *evidence volume and valence*:

| Score | Label | Descriptor |
|-------|-------|------------|
| 1 | Not Demonstrated | "No positive evidence and/ or substantial negative evidence demonstrated" |
| 2 | Minimal Demonstration | "Limited positive evidence and/ or mainly negative evidence demonstrated" |
| 3 | Moderate Demonstration | "Moderate positive evidence but some negative evidence demonstrated" |
| 4 | Acceptable Demonstration | "Adequate positive evidence and any negative evidence would not cause concern" |
| 5 | Good Demonstration | "Substantial positive evidence of the behaviour" |
| 6 | Strong Demonstration | "Substantial positive evidence; includes some evidence of exceeding expectations" |
| 7 | Outstanding Demonstration | "The evidence provided wholly exceeds expectation at this level" |

Source: COPFS [8]. Not one descriptor mentions structure, narrative or
articulacy. Every one is a statement about the quantity and valence of evidence.
The authority for the scale itself is central: Cabinet Office guidance states
that "experience, technical skills and behaviours should be scored using the 7
point scale. Strengths should be scored using the 4 point scale" [7].

The College of Policing's example A–D scale reads the same way — grade A is
"substantial positive evidence of the competency observed **with regard to the
quality and quantity of the evidence** and few or no areas for development
observed"; grade B is "positive evidence of the competency observed and some
areas for development but, **on balance, more positive evidence** with regard to
the quality and quantity of the evidence" [11]. Quality *and quantity* appears
in both grade descriptors.

## What a published marking guide calls good evidence

Dyfed-Powys Police publish, in a CVF Level 2 interview marking guide, an
explicit "What does good evidence look like?" statement: "The candidate used
clear and detailed sentences, providing evidence rather than statements. The
conversation is directed towards their attributes, skills and experience. The
candidate is using I instead of we/us/them. They have listened and understood
the question and subsequently provided a suitable detailed example — clearly
demonstrating the 'beginning, middle and end' or the 'how, why, when, what'.
The candidate talks openly about reflecting and learning the lessons" [13].

Read against the STAR elements, that criterion set is: evidence over assertion
(Action detail), first person (attribution), completeness (all four elements),
and reflection (the learning clause of Result). The same guide cautions that
the CVF behaviours "should not be used in isolation or as a checklist … because
the behaviours are just examples", and that assessment must take account of
"performance against the wider competency description (not just the behavioural
examples)" [13] — the assessor-side counterpart to the buzzword warning below.

## What assessors are told to discount

Devon & Cornwall Police publish a "Common mistakes" list for their CVF force
interview: "Didn't answer the question - Answered the competency and not the
question"; "Not the right CVF level - Be mindful of the level you are answering
at"; "Pre-empting the interview board — Don't pre empt what you think the panel
want to hear"; and "CVF 'Bingo' — buzz words don't score. Please don't try and
get as many buzz words in as possible, it is about you answering the question
and being authentic in your response, not repeating buzz words from the CVF
Framework" [14]. The same force records that its interview "will follow a
structured format with a set list of questions … designed to make the process as
fair and objective as possible", with six questions in total [14].

The College of Policing also publishes a list of assessment barriers that ORCE
is intended to counter, including the expectancy effect — "tendency for
assessors to generate either a positive or negative expectation from
pre-assessment information … and for their evaluation and decisions to follow
these expectations" — countered by "using ORCE model of assessment" and by
giving assessors only information they truly need in advance [11].

## The EPSO time budget

EPSO's legacy competency-based interview allocated "about 10 minutes per
competency", with questions "structured to allow the assessors to evaluate the
candidate's selected general competencies", the competencies themselves being
"specified in the Notice of Competition" [10]. That budget covers the assessor's
questioning as well as the candidate's answer — which is the documented basis
for the coaching advice above that an opening answer should leave room for
probing.

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — labelled guidance,** each tied to the documented criterion it
violates.

- **Front-loading the Situation.** The commonest failure, and the one central
  guidance names directly: "Keep the situation and task parts brief" [27], and
  "Don't get caught up telling a story in your example" [27]. Four careers
  sources agree Action should dominate [18][19][24][26], and the Classify step
  ignores non-behavioural narrative [11]. A minute of context in a 90-second
  video answer scores approximately nothing.
- **Treating "we" as modesty.** It reads as unattributable evidence. GOV.UK is
  explicit — "Use 'I' not 'we'" [27] — as are Reading, Hampshire, targetjobs and
  My World of Work [18][15][24][26], and Dyfed-Powys marks it [13]. The opposite
  error is also named centrally: "be careful not to take credit of something
  that you did not do" [27].
- **Stating *what* without *how*.** Central guidance says "what **and how** you
  did" [1][2], and instructs that "in most examples you should focus more words
  on the 'how' than the 'what'" [27]; My World of Work asks candidates to show
  "how you assessed the situation" [26]. A list of deeds without method is thin
  evidence.
- **Assuming the panel will fill in the gaps.** "The sift panel cannot infer
  what is not included in the example and can only assess what you have actually
  written" [27]. Context you did not state does not exist.
- **Padding the Action by restating one behaviour.** Assessors "classify each
  behaviour once — do not double count" [11]. Rephrasing adds length, not score.
- **Omitting the Task in a recorded interview.** There is "no opportunity for
  the interviewer to ask for clarification" [21], so nothing recovers an
  unstated remit.
- **Compressing by cutting the Action.** The instinct under a timer is to rush
  the middle. Cut Situation and Task instead — they are the elements the
  published prompts already describe as "brief" [12].
- **Abandoning an example because the outcome was poor.** Partial demonstration
  is expressly tolerated [1], learning is inside the Result definition [5][18],
  and published banks ask for failures directly [6][23][24].
- **Inventing a metric for an unmeasurable result.** Quantifying is advised
  where possible [26], not required; a fabricated number is a fairness and
  integrity problem, and probing will find it.
- **Buzzword stuffing.** "Buzz words don't score" [14].
- **Answering the competency rather than the question.** Named as a common
  mistake in published force guidance [14].
- **Bringing a rehearsed STAR story to a strengths question.** The published
  instruction is "do not rehearse your answers - because we are looking for your
  initial response" [3][9]; strengths are also scored on a different scale [7].
- **Sounding scripted.** NCS advises a "conversational way so as not to appear
  too rehearsed" [5]; Oxford warns against "over-communicat[ing] or waffl[ing]"
  and advises taking "your cue from the interviewer" [19].
- **(Interviewers) crediting fluency.** No descriptor on the 7-point scale [8]
  or the A–D scale [11] refers to articulacy — every one refers to evidence.
- **(Interviewers) probing unevenly.** Rescuing one candidate's unstructured
  answer and not another's cuts against ACAS's same-questions guidance [17] and
  against the independent-scoring discipline in Cabinet Office guidance [7].

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.** Drills a reader can run this week.

1. **The stopwatch audit.** Record a full answer to Question Bank item 1.
   Timestamp where Situation ends, Task ends and Action ends. Compute the
   percentages. If Action is under half, you have found your problem — the cited
   direction is that it should be the longest element [18][24][26].
2. **The classify drill.** Transcribe one of your answers. Highlight every
   sentence that states a behaviour *you* performed. Delete everything else and
   count what remains — that is approximately what an assessor classifies [11].
   Now check for duplicates, since each behaviour classifies once [11].
3. **The 60/180 drill.** Deliver one example in 60 seconds and again in 3
   minutes (Question Bank, compression drills 1–3). Diff the two transcripts.
   If the extra two minutes went anywhere other than Action granularity and
   Result texture, redo it.
4. **The pronoun pass.** Read an answer transcript and mark every "we", "us"
   and "the team". Rewrite so they appear only in the Situation
   [18][15][13].
5. **The "how" pass.** For each action sentence, ask whether it says *how*. Add
   the method where it is missing — this is the wording central guidance uses
   [1][2].
6. **Self-score on the published scale.** Score your own answer against the
   COPFS descriptors [8]. Be honest about the gap between "moderate positive
   evidence" (3) and "substantial positive evidence" (5) — the difference is
   usually two or three more distinct actions, not better delivery.
7. **The bad-result rehearsal.** Answer three of the poor-Result questions
   above. Keep the Action at full length in each. End on a change already made
   [12][13].
8. **The no-clarification rehearsal.** Record an answer with a hard timer and no
   retake, mirroring the published format constraints [25][21]. Play it back and
   ask whether a stranger could state your Task.
9. **The EPSO slot simulation.** Have a partner ask one competency question and
   then probe for the remainder of a ten-minute block [10]. Your opening answer
   should occupy well under half the slot.
10. **The strengths contrast.** Have a partner ask two strengths questions from
    the bank cold, unprepared, then reflect afterwards on your preferred ways of
    working as the guidance advises [3][9]. Feel how different the task is from
    example-based answering — that difference is the boundary of STAR.
11. **The evidence-breadth check.** List your prepared examples and their
    sources. If they are all from one paid role, widen the pool — studies,
    societies, placements, course-rep roles, volunteering and hobbies are all
    documented as legitimate [22][1][2][5].
12. **(Interviewers) the calibration mock.** Run a mock assessment or a
    discussion of expected performance at each grade before the first live
    candidate — the College of Policing names this benchmarking or
    frame-of-reference training [11]. Then score independently before
    discussing [7].

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.** Signposts only.

- **Reasonable adjustments and timed formats.** The Equality Act 2010, section
  20, imposes a duty to make adjustments where a provision, criterion or
  practice puts a disabled person at a substantial disadvantage, including
  providing auxiliary aids [16]. A hard on-screen answer timer [25] and a
  fixed per-competency slot [10] are both practices capable of engaging that
  duty. The Civil Service Strengths Dictionary states the point in candidate
  terms: adjustments may include "providing documents in large print or braille,
  allowing more time for a test or interview or providing assistance at an
  assessment centre" [9]. EPSO operates its own specific-adjustments route,
  assessed case by case.
- **Structure as a fairness mechanism.** ACAS advises asking each applicant "the
  same questions where possible" and basing the decision on how each applicant
  met the job description and person specification [17]. Devon & Cornwall
  describe their structured format with a set question list as "designed to make
  the process as fair and objective as possible" [14].
- **Bias control is the stated purpose of ORCE.** College of Policing guidance
  presents ORCE as a means that "helps to avoid bias", and catalogues barriers
  it counters — expectancy effect, confirmatory information, personal liking,
  primacy effect, stereotyping [11]. Independent classification before
  discussion is part of the control [11][7].
- **Feedback must rest on observed behaviour.** The same guidance states that
  feedback should explain "that decisions are based on what was observed during
  an assessment, which is only a snapshot of their performance … it is not to
  say they don't hold the values, but that they have not evidenced them" [11].
  This is the fairness counterpart of the whole document: candidates are scored
  on evidence given, not on qualities held.
- **Appeals and adverse circumstances.** Candidates "should be provided with the
  opportunity to raise any concerns or circumstances which they feel may have
  affected their performance", through a clear, communicated process [11]. Devon
  & Cornwall commit to verbal feedback within three weeks for candidates
  unsuccessful at force interview [14].

# Risks & Caveats

- **The 70/30 figure is a single-employer, single-context datum.** It comes from
  one NHS trust's candidate guidance, in a values-based-interview setting where
  the trust states the outcome "is the focus" [12]. It should never be
  generalised into "the STAR ratio". This document verified the bracket visually
  by rendering the PDF page, because the diagram's layout extracts ambiguously —
  a text-only extraction would support the opposite reading. Any downstream
  reuse of this figure should re-verify visually.
- **No central proportioning *ratio* exists — but the direction is central.**
  An earlier version of this document recorded that no central publisher
  addressed proportioning at all. That was wrong: GOV.UK instructs "Keep the
  situation and task parts brief. Concentrate on the action and the result"
  [27]. What remains unsupported centrally is any *percentage*. The
  10–15 / 10–15 / 55–65 / 15–20 split in Model Answer Guidance is this
  document's synthesis, calibrated against that official direction and four
  careers sources [18][19][24][26]. It is labelled coaching and should stay
  labelled.
- **The anchor proportioning source is a 2016 page for a superseded framework.**
  *A brief guide to competencies* is published by Civil Service Resourcing,
  dated 12 April 2016, and describes the Civil Service competency framework —
  "10 common Civil Service competencies, separated into 3 clusters" [27] — which
  Success Profiles superseded in 2018. The page is still live on GOV.UK and
  carries no withdrawal notice, and this document uses it **only** for
  answer-technique content (proportioning, first-person attribution, handling
  unsuccessful outcomes, specificity), which is framework-agnostic and is nowhere
  contradicted by current Success Profiles guidance [1][2]. It must not be cited
  for framework content: its competency list and cluster structure are out of
  date. A reader should treat the technique advice as durable and the framework
  furniture around it as historical.
- **WebFetch does not surface this page's full content.** The summarising layer
  returns the STAR block and silently omits the proportioning instruction, the
  CAR section and the summary. It was verified here by raw HTML extraction. Any
  future re-verification of this source must use raw HTML, not WebFetch.
- **EPSO's current model has no competency interview.** The ten-minutes-per-
  competency figure [10] comes from a legacy EPSO test page that remains live
  but is not linked from current staff-category test pages. EPSO's current
  generalist competition model (Notice EPSO/AD/427/26) comprises reasoning
  tests, EU knowledge, digital skills and a free-text essay, with no assessment
  centre and no interview. The timing figure is cited here as a **published
  benchmark for competency-interview pacing**, which remains useful for EU
  agency and institution interviews that still run them — not as a description
  of current EPSO practice. See `epso-competency-framework`.
- **Careers-service pages are living content.** Reading, Oxford, Manchester,
  Bath, targetjobs and My World of Work pages carry no version history and can
  change without notice. All were opened and verified on 2026-07-20.
- **Police sources predate the current CVF.** The College of Policing
  values-based recruitment guidance is dated 2018 and predates the 2024 CVF
  [11]; only its framework-agnostic process content (ORCE, rating-scale design,
  assessment barriers) is used here. The Dyfed-Powys marking guide uses the 2024
  competency names [13].
- **COPFS is a departmental publication of a central scale.** The seven
  descriptor labels are published by COPFS [8], but the authority for the scale
  is Cabinet Office guidance [7]. Do not describe the scale as departmental.
- **Barnsley's guidance is dated 2015.** It remains published on the trust's
  site at a 2023 asset path, but the 70/30 diagram should be treated as
  long-standing trust practice rather than current national NHS guidance.
- **Compression advice is untested synthesis.** The claim that answers should be
  compressed by cutting Situation and Task rather than Action follows logically
  from the ORCE classification rules [11] and the proportioning direction
  [18][24][26], but no source states it. It is coaching.

# Open Questions

- **Does any central UK or EU source publish an element *ratio*?** The
  *direction* is now documented — GOV.UK says keep situation and task brief and
  concentrate on action and result [27] — but no central publisher attaches
  percentages. A Cabinet Office or EPSO statement quantifying proportioning
  would convert this document's numeric coaching split into a documented one.
- **Has the proportioning instruction been restated under Success Profiles?**
  The clearest central wording sits on a 2016 page for the superseded competency
  framework [27]. A post-2018 restatement in Success Profiles guidance would
  remove the currency caveat entirely; none was located.
- **Is there empirical evidence that Action-heavy answers score higher?** The
  argument here is procedural (ORCE classification rules [11]) rather than
  empirical. An open-access occupational-psychology study relating answer
  composition to structured-interview ratings would settle it.
- **How do other NHS trusts weight the elements?** Barnsley publishes 70/30
  [12]. A survey of published trust VBR packs would establish whether this is a
  sector convention or a local one.
- **What is the evidential basis for the one-to-three-minute video window?** The
  figure is reported by a careers publisher [25] and corroborated only
  qualitatively by Oxford [21]. Vendor documentation would be stronger.
- **Do EU agencies running competency interviews publish per-competency
  timings?** EPSO's ten minutes [10] is legacy. Agency-level published timings
  would give the compression drills a current EU anchor.
- **Does the Civil Service publish assessor-facing STAR guidance?** The
  candidate-facing recommendation is published [1][2][4]; a corresponding
  assessor instruction on how to weight structural completeness against evidence
  volume has not been located.
