# Executive Summary

The parent document, `techniques-foundation`, records *that* answer structures
other than STAR exist and who names them. This document does the harder job:
it establishes **how much authority each structure actually carries**, and
**which question types each one handles better than STAR**.

The two findings that matter most are asymmetries the reader will not guess
from a comparison table. First, the structures do **not** stand on equal
evidential footing — but the dividing line does not fall where the parent
document places it. **Two** structures are official-guidance-backed, not one:
the Civil Service Resourcing guide to competencies sets out all four STAR
elements [1] and then names CAR as a peer, telling candidates "Not everyone can
relate to the STAR method, so an alternative approach is: The CAR approach" and
concluding "Use either STAR or CAR approach for writing your competency" [1].
STARR and CARL are documented by named university careers services
[15][16][17][19], and CARL additionally appears in a **UK police force's own
candidate guidance**, which offers "S.T.A.R , C.A.R.L" as options [9] — a
materially stronger position than the parent document implies. SOAR, by
contrast, rests in this market on a **single named practitioner source** [20];
the only institutional documentation located is an undated careers-service
handout from a **Canadian** university that is itself adapted from a magazine
article [21]. No UK or EU official guidance reviewed for this document names
SOAR.

Second, and more useful than any ranking: **no published framework reviewed
requires any structure at all.** The Cabinet Office guidance that sets the
central scoring rules for delegated-grade recruitment specifies the 7-point
scale for behaviours and directs that interview questions give candidates
"ample opportunity to provide the required level of evidence" — and names no
answer mnemonic anywhere in the document [3]. Two police forces tell candidates
in terms to "use a structure which you feel works for you" [9][10], and warn
that "CVF `Bingo'" — reciting framework buzzwords — "don't score" [9][10]. The
structure is a delivery mechanism for evidence; the evidence is what is marked.

Confidence is **Medium**, and cannot honestly be higher. The document's spine —
STAR's official standing, the scoring rules, the fact that no structure is
mandated — rests on primary Cabinet Office, GOV.UK and police-force sources.
But the reflective structures are documented mainly by careers services, the
SOAR comparison rests partly on one commercial blog, and the
"which structure beats STAR when" judgements are reasoned synthesis, clearly
confined to the coaching layer.

# Key Takeaways

- **The structures are not equally authoritative.** STAR **and CAR** are both
  official-guidance-backed — GOV.UK names them together and says "Use either
  STAR or CAR approach" [1][2][5]; CARL and STARR are
  university-careers-documented [15][16][17][19]; SOAR is, in the UK/EU market,
  single-practitioner [20]. Any comparison table that presents all five as
  peers is misleading. *(documented)*
- **CARL outranks its reputation.** Beyond the University of Edinburgh
  Reflection Toolkit [15], CARL is named alongside STAR in Devon & Cornwall
  Police candidate guidance as a structure candidates may use [9]. It is the
  only non-STAR structure located in UK employer-issued guidance. *(documented)*
- **No reviewed framework mandates a structure.** The central Cabinet Office
  scoring guidance names no mnemonic at all [3]; police guidance says "use a
  structure which you feel works for you" [9][10]; the behaviours guidance says
  "consider using" STAR, not "use" [2]. *(documented)*
- **EPSO names no answer mnemonic — and its current model has no competency
  interview.** The current AD5 procedure consists of reasoning, EU knowledge
  and digital-skills tests plus a free-text essay, with no interview or
  assessment centre [7]. The competency-based interview page describing about
  ten minutes per competency is a surviving legacy page, not current practice
  [8]. *(documented)*
- **Reflection variants have a documented trigger.** The University of Reading
  states the second R "is useful when you are asked to give an example of a
  difficult situation" [19]; Arden calls the Reflection element optional and
  question-dependent [17]. *(documented)*
- **CAR is the compression tool, and time pressure is real.** Cambridge's
  three-part CAR folds Situation and Task into Context [16]; pre-recorded video
  interviews commonly allow "between one and three minutes to answer each
  question, with a timer on screen" [25]. *(documented)*
- **SOAR's contribution is a framing, not an authority.** Replacing Task with
  Obstacle reframes assigned work as identified challenge [20][21]. Borrow the
  framing for initiative and problem-solving questions; do not tell a UK or EU
  employer you are "using SOAR". *(guidance)*
- **Decide by the verb in the question, not by preference.** Words like
  "learned", "differently", "looking back" call for STARR or CARL; "problem",
  "obstacle", "initiative" call for SOAR framing; a hard time box calls for CAR;
  everything else defaults to STAR. *(guidance)*

# Purpose

This document answers a question the foundation document deliberately left
open: *given that several answer structures exist, which one should I use for
this question — and how much does it matter?*

For a **candidate**, it supplies a decision heuristic that works under
interview pressure, worked contrasts showing the same example told two ways,
and an honest account of which structures are safe to name aloud in a UK or EU
process. For an **interviewer or assessment designer**, it supplies the
documented position that structure is not a scoring criterion, the evidence
that candidates are told to choose their own, and the implications for probing
and for reasonable adjustments.

# Scope

**Covered:** the documented answer structures other than plain STAR — CAR,
STARR/STAR(R), CARL and SOAR — including each one's elements, who publishes it,
what evidential standing that publisher gives it, and the question types it
handles better than STAR; a decision heuristic for choosing under pressure;
worked contrasts of one example told in two structures; the assessor-side
position that structure is not scored.

**Not covered:** STAR itself in depth, including per-competency worked answers
(`star-technique`); systematic evidence selection and preparation
(`building-evidence-bank`); the competency families the structures evidence
(`competencies-foundation`); scoring scales and marking mechanics in depth
(`scoring-and-marking`); resilience and adaptability content used here only as
example material (`resilience-adaptability`). Structures circulating under
other labels — STAR-L, PAR, CCAR and similar — are **out of scope because they
could not be verified to a named publisher**; see Risks & Caveats.

**Market:** UK and EU. One cited source (Brock University) is Canadian and is
included only because it is the sole institutional documentation of SOAR
located; it is explicitly not evidence of UK or EU practice.

# Definitions

Terms used in this document with a narrowed meaning. General terms live in the
shared `GLOSSARY.md`.

- **Answer structure (mnemonic)** — a memorised element order a candidate uses
  to organise a spoken or written example. Distinct from the *assessment*
  structure an employer imposes (same questions, same criteria).
- **Evidential standing** — the class of publisher documenting a structure,
  used in this document at three levels: **official-guidance-backed** (named in
  government, regulator or public-body candidate guidance);
  **institution-documented** (named by a university careers service or a
  published employer candidate pack); **single-practitioner** (named only in
  commercial careers writing).
- **Reflection element** — a closing step, added after Result, stating what was
  learned or what would be done differently. The second R in STARR and the L in
  CARL.
- **Obstacle framing** — SOAR's substitution of Obstacle for Task, shifting the
  narrative from work assigned to a challenge identified.
- **Time-boxed format** — an interview format with an enforced per-answer limit,
  such as a pre-recorded video interview with an on-screen timer [25].

# Framework Context

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## The standing of each structure

STAR's position in the UK is genuinely official. Civil Service Resourcing
publishes a GOV.UK guide to competencies that sets out all four elements —
Situation ("Describe the situation you found yourself in. You must describe a
specific event or situation"), Task ("The job holder will want to understand
what you tried to achieve"), Actions ("What did you do? The job holder will be
looking for information of what you did, how you did it and why") and Results
("Don't be shy about taking credit for your behaviour. Quote specific facts and
figures") — and adds that in most examples a candidate "should focus more words
on the 'how' than the 'what'" [1]. The current Success Profiles behaviours
guidance links to that guide, telling candidates: "In your own examples,
consider using the STAR method which allows you to set the scene, show what and
how you did and the overall outcome" [2]. The National Careers Service, the UK
government careers service, publishes a dedicated STAR page [5].

Note the verb in [2]: **"consider using"**. Even STAR — the most widely
recommended structure in this market — is offered, not imposed. The same guide
is explicit that the choice between approaches "is all down to your personal
style of writing" [1].

**CAR shares STAR's official standing.** The same Civil Service Resourcing
guide introduces both together — "There are many different approaches you could
take to writing competencies, such as the STAR, or the CAR method" — records
that "Within the Civil Service, the most common approach is: The STAR method",
then presents CAR as a first-class alternative: "Not everyone can relate to the
STAR method, so an alternative approach is: The CAR approach" [1]. Its summary
instructs: "Use either STAR or CAR approach for writing your competency" [1].
CAR is therefore not a university-taught compression of an official structure —
it is itself named in UK government candidate guidance.

The remaining alternatives sit a rung down, and not all on the same rung.

| Structure | Elements | Documented by | Evidential standing |
|-----------|----------|---------------|---------------------|
| STAR | Situation, Task, Action, Result | Civil Service Resourcing [1]; Success Profiles behaviours guidance [2]; National Careers Service [5]; universities [19][26]; police forces [9][10] | **Official-guidance-backed** — the Civil Service's "most common approach" [1] |
| CAR | Context, Action, Results | **Civil Service Resourcing, as an express alternative to STAR** [1]; University of Cambridge Careers Service [16]; ancestor of CARL per Edinburgh [15] | **Official-guidance-backed** |
| CARL | Context, Action, Results, Learning | University of Edinburgh Reflection Toolkit [15]; **named as a candidate option in Devon & Cornwall Police force-interview guidance** [9] | **Institution-documented, incl. one UK employer** |
| STARR / STAR(R) | STAR + Reflection | University of Reading [19]; Arden University [17]; Fidelity International via targetjobs [18] | **Institution-documented, incl. one named employer** |
| SOAR | Situation, Obstacle, Action, Result | The Interview Guys, named and dated [20]; Brock University CareerZone, undated, Canada [21] | **Single-practitioner in the UK/EU market** |

**This asymmetry is the point of the table.** Reading the rows as five
interchangeable options would misrepresent the evidence: two rows are UK
government guidance, two rows are careers-service teaching material with some
employer uptake, and one row is a commercial blog with a non-UK/EU handout
beside it.

**A partial qualification on the reflection element.** GOV.UK does not name
STARR or CARL, but it does endorse the *move* those mnemonics formalise. The
same guide instructs that where "the result was not entirely successful",
candidates should "describe what you learned from this and what you would do
differently next time", and repeats the point under CAR's Results step:
"Remember if your result is not positive, describe what you have learnt" [1].
So the reflective step has partial official backing **as a technique for
unsuccessful outcomes** — but STARR and CARL as named structures remain
university-documented [15][17][19], and nothing in official guidance endorses
those labels.

## What the UK public sector actually tells candidates

Two police forces publishing candidate guidance against the College of Policing
Competency and Values Framework make the permissive position explicit.
Hampshire Constabulary tells transferees and re-joiners: "Practice your answer
structure to underpin your response. Use a structure which you feel works for
you there are many out there but one successful format is S.T.A.R" [10]. Devon
& Cornwall Police uses near-identical wording but offers **two** named options:
"Use a structure which you feel works for you there is many out there but here
are some options S.T.A.R , C.A.R.L" [9].

This is the strongest located evidence that a UK employer treats a non-STAR
structure as acceptable, and it is why CARL is ranked above CAR and level with
STARR in the table above.

## The EU position

EPSO names no answer mnemonic, and the question is now partly moot. The current
Graduates (Administrators AD5) procedure comprises reasoning tests, an EU
knowledge test, a digital skills test and a free-text essay on EU matters —
**with no interview, assessment centre or oral test listed** [7]. EPSO's
competency-based interview page, which states that "Candidates are interviewed
for about 10 minutes per competency" and that "The questions are structured to
allow the assessors to evaluate the candidate's selected general competencies",
remains live but describes the **pre-2023 model**; it is not linked from the
current staff-category test pages and should be read as archive [8]. Neither
page recommends any answer structure to candidates [7][8].

# Core Reference

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## CARL: Context, Action, Results, Learning

The University of Edinburgh's Reflection Toolkit defines the four steps as:
Context — "Briefly describe the context of you experience"; Action — "Explain
what actions you took"; Results — "Explain what happened as a result of your
actions"; Learning — "Identify what you have learned" [15]. The toolkit is
dated 15 October 2024 [15].

Two details from Edinburgh matter for this document. First, **provenance**: the
toolkit records that CARL derives from "The CAR model of interview techniques,
for example BlueSteps (2011) CAR interview Technique", adapted for reflective
practice [15]. CARL is therefore not a rival lineage to STAR but a reflective
extension of CAR — which is itself a compression of the same material STAR
carries. Second, a **warning that transfers directly to interviews**: the
toolkit cautions that "describing is not the same as being critically
reflective" and advises asking "why" throughout, rather than defaulting to
description [15]. A CARL answer that narrates events and appends "I learned a
lot" has failed the model on its own terms.

Edinburgh also notes that CARL "does not explicitly require further planned
actions" [15] — the Learning step closes the loop on what was understood, not
necessarily on what was subsequently done.

## STARR / STAR(R): STAR plus Reflection

Arden University describes the elements with unusually explicit weighting:
Situation "is where you paint the picture and provide context"; Task "is where
you explain exactly what your responsibility was"; Action "is the section you
will spend most of your time on"; Result "is the section most people miss or
forget about" — and, without it, "the interviewer will not know what the
outcome was" [17]. Of the second R, Arden is precise: "this is an optional
element and doesn't need to be used for every question", used to "explain what
you might change, or do differently if you were to encounter the same scenario
again" [17].

The University of Reading documents the same extension and supplies the
sharpest published statement of **when** it applies: there is "a second R -
reflect, where you talk about what you learned and what you would do
differently next time. This R is useful when you are asked to give an example
of a difficult situation" [19]. Reading also states that the Action component
"is the longest part of your answer" [19].

From the employer side, Serena Vaughan of Fidelity International, writing on
targetjobs, sets out Situation, Task, Action, Result, Reflection and states
that "reflecting on your actions and how you might do something differently
next time, can be the difference between a good answer and a GREAT answer"
[18].

**STARR versus CARL.** Both end on learning; they differ in the front half.
STARR keeps STAR's four-part opening including a distinct Task element
[17][19], while CARL compresses Situation and Task into a single Context step
[15]. STARR therefore preserves the "what was mine to do" signal; CARL buys
roughly a quarter of the answer's length back and spends it on Learning.

## CAR: the officially named compression

CAR is the only non-STAR structure in this document defined in UK government
candidate guidance. Civil Service Resourcing sets out its three elements:
Context — "Explain the situation; what, where and when. Simply describe the
challenge that you faced. Give the reader some background, just enough to set
the scene"; Actions — "Make sure that you explain how you did something not
just what you did. What action or steps did you take? How did you do it?…
Summarise as best as you can"; Results — "What was the outcome? What results
did you achieve?… Use numbers and percentages whenever possible" [1].

The University of Cambridge Careers Service teaches the same structure and
corroborates the element definitions: "**C**ontext: describe the situation you
were in, the task at hand, the problems you faced. **A**ction: what was your
role? What did you do? Be specific about how you worked and what you did.
**R**esults: what happened? Were you successful? What did you learn?" [16].

Note that the official wording makes the compression's purpose explicit: the
Context step exists to give "just enough to set the scene" [1], and the same
guide's STAR treatment issues the parallel instruction "Keep the situation and
task parts brief. Concentrate on the action and the result" [1]. Compression is
not a concession to time pressure — it is the officially stated preference.

Two structural observations follow from the published wording. Cambridge's
Context element explicitly absorbs "the task at hand" [16], confirming that CAR
is a compression rather than an omission — the Task information is still
required, just not given its own beat. And Cambridge's Results element already
carries "What did you learn?" [16], exactly as the National Careers Service
STAR page folds learning into Result [5]. The reflective variants formalise a
step the compressed variants already gesture at.

Cambridge also records the interview lengths this compression serves: first
interviews "normally last 20-50 minutes", while further interviews "can last
anything from an hour up to a full day" [16]. For candidates facing an
enforced per-answer limit, targetjobs records that pre-recorded video
interviews usually allow "between one and three minutes to answer each
question, with a timer on screen indicating how long you have left" [25].

Where a candidate is verbose rather than time-pressed, Cambridge offers a
non-mnemonic alternative: "discipline your reply with a 'firstly-secondly-
thirdly' structure" [16].

## SOAR: Situation, Obstacle, Action, Result

SOAR is documented in named, dated practitioner careers writing by Mike Simpson
of The Interview Guys, published 20 April 2025 and modified to 19 June 2026, as
a structured way to answer behavioural questions: Situation is "Brief context
about the environment or circumstances"; Obstacle is "The specific problem or
challenge you faced"; Action is "The steps you took to solve the problem";
Result is "The measurable outcomes and what you learned" [20]. The article
argues that the obstacle framing "naturally showcase[s] critical thinking,
initiative, and problem-solving abilities" because it "Highlights challenges
rather than assignments" [20].

**The honest statement of SOAR's standing.** That source cites no official body
or employer as recommending SOAR [20]. The only institutional documentation
located is a careers-service handout from **Brock University in Canada**, which
describes SOAR as "a story-telling or narrative approach to answering questions
in a job interview", advises setting the scene briefly because the next three
parts "are more critical from the interviewer's perspective", and asks of the
Obstacle step: "How have you helped your employer overcome a big obstacle, like
an economic downturn, a small budget or an aggressive deadline?" [21]. That
handout is undated and states that it is itself "Adapted from" a 2017 Forbes
article [21].

So: SOAR has a coherent published definition and a plausible rationale, but in
the UK and EU market covered by this knowledge base it rests on **one named
practitioner source** [20]. No UK or EU official guidance reviewed for this
document — the Civil Service competency guide [1], Success Profiles behaviours
guidance [2], the delegated-grade scoring guidance [3], the National Careers
Service [5], EPSO's current or legacy pages [7][8], or the two police force
candidate packs [9][10] — names SOAR.

## What no source requires

The Cabinet Office guidance governing delegated-grade (EO to Grade 6)
recruitment sets the central scoring rules: "Experience, technical skills and
behaviours should be scored using the 7 point scale. Strengths should be scored
using the 4 point scale" [3]. It directs that "Interview questions should be
designed to allow the candidate ample opportunity to provide the required level
of evidence to demonstrate their capability for the role" [3]. **The document
does not name STAR, CAR, CARL, STARR, SOAR or any other answer structure
anywhere** — verified by full-text extraction on 2026-07-20 [3]. The scoring
instrument is the evidence and the scale, not the mnemonic.

# Question Bank

Thirty-seven practice questions, grouped so that each `##` sub-heading
exercises a different structure. The lead-in note under each heading explains
why that structure suits that question type — those notes are **coaching**,
pointing back to the evidence layer; the questions themselves carry their own
provenance labels.

## Questions that suit full STAR

*Why STAR: the work was assigned, the remit matters to the assessment, and
there is time for four beats. This is the default and the officially named
option [1][2].*

1. Can you give me an example of when you worked in a team? *(sourced: targetjobs)* [23]
2. Give an example of a situation when you worked on a tight deadline. *(sourced: targetjobs)* [23]
3. Describe a project you worked on that required close attention to detail. *(sourced: University of Bath Human Resources)* [22]
4. When have you faced a challenging situation? *(sourced: National Careers Service)* [6]
5. Describe a time when you had to build a team. How did you go about building the team, and was it successful in achieving its goals? *(sourced: University of Bath Human Resources)* [22]
6. Tell us about a time you were given clear objectives by someone else and those objectives changed midway through delivery. *(composed)*
7. Describe a situation where your formal remit and what the situation actually needed were not the same thing. *(composed)*

## Questions that suit CAR — compression under a hard time limit

*Why CAR: the achievement is the point and the context is self-evident, so
folding Situation and Task into a single Context step [1][16] buys time for
Action and Result. CAR is named in official Civil Service guidance as an
express alternative to STAR [1], so it can be used without hedging. Reach for
it in a timed video interview, where one to three minutes per answer is common
[25].*

1. Can you tell us about a personal achievement at work? *(sourced: National Careers Service)* [6]
2. Give me an example of a time when you set a goal and were able to meet or exceed it. *(sourced: University of Bath Human Resources)* [22]
3. Describe a negotiating experience that you feel was particularly successful. *(sourced: University of Bath Human Resources)* [22]
4. Tell us about a time when your contribution made a big difference to a team's success. *(sourced: targetjobs)* [24]
5. When have you previously delivered excellent customer service? *(sourced: targetjobs)* [23]
6. In ninety seconds, tell us about the piece of work you are most proud of and why. *(composed)*

## Questions that suit STARR — the question itself invites reflection

*Why STARR: each of these contains an explicit backward-looking clause, or a
negative outcome that cannot be redeemed by a Result alone. Reading's guidance
is that the second R "is useful when you are asked to give an example of a
difficult situation" [19]; Arden treats it as optional and question-dependent
[17].*

1. What was the most difficult customer service situation you ever had to deal with? How did you handle the situation? Would you do anything differently if you handled a similar problem in the future? *(sourced: University of Bath Human Resources)* [22]
2. Tell me about a time when a problem was not solved the way you would have liked. How did you handle the situation? *(sourced: University of Bath Human Resources)* [22]
3. What has been your biggest failure? *(sourced: targetjobs)* [23]
4. Have you ever failed at a task? *(sourced: National Careers Service)* [6]
5. Tell me about a time when you felt you had not performed to the best of your ability. What did you do about it? *(sourced: University of Bath Human Resources)* [22]
6. Tell me about a time when you felt you were unable to meet your responsibilities. *(sourced: University of Bath Human Resources)* [22]
7. Tell me about a time when a project you were in charge of fell behind schedule. What did you do about it? *(sourced: University of Bath Human Resources)* [22]

## Questions that suit CARL — where the learning, not the result, is the evidence

*Why CARL: these questions concern judgement and interpersonal repair, where
the outcome is often ambiguous and the assessable material is what the
candidate now understands. CARL's compressed Context leaves room for a
substantial Learning step [15] — and Edinburgh's warning applies: describing is
not reflecting [15]. CARL is also the one non-STAR structure named in UK
employer candidate guidance [9].*

1. Tell me about a time when you had a disagreement with another team member. How was it resolved? *(sourced: targetjobs)* [24]
2. Tell me about a time when you worked with a colleague who was not completing their share of work. What did you do? *(sourced: University of Bath Human Resources)* [22]
3. Give an example of a time when you faced an ethical dilemma. *(sourced: targetjobs)* [23]
4. Describe a situation where you had difficulty getting others to agree with your decisions. How did you handle it? *(sourced: University of Bath Human Resources)* [22]
5. Tell us about something you changed in the way you work because of feedback you received. *(composed)*
6. Describe an experience that changed your mind about how you approach a particular kind of problem. *(composed)*

## Questions that suit SOAR's obstacle framing — problem-solving and initiative

*Why the obstacle framing: in each of these the work was not handed down, so a
Task element would understate the candidate. Naming the obstacle distinguishes
a challenge identified from an instruction received [20][21]. Borrow the
framing; do not announce the mnemonic — it is not named in any UK or EU
official guidance reviewed here [20].*

1. Give an example of a time when you handled a major crisis. *(sourced: targetjobs)* [23]
2. Describe a time when you had to resolve a difficult problem on your own. How did you go about it? *(sourced: University of Bath Human Resources)* [22]
3. Tell me about a time when a problem arose and your manager was unavailable. How did you handle the situation, and how did things turn out? *(sourced: University of Bath Human Resources)* [22]
4. Give me an example of a time that you showed initiative and took the lead. *(sourced: University of Bath Human Resources)* [22]
5. Have you ever taken the initiative? *(sourced: National Careers Service)* [6]
6. Please describe a situation where you used your creativity or initiative to solve a problem. *(sourced: University of Bath Human Resources)* [22]
7. Give me an example of a high-pressure situation you have faced this past year and how you resolved it. *(sourced: University of Bath Human Resources)* [22]

## Questions where no story structure is the right tool

*Why no structure: these ask for a preference, an opinion or an approach, not a
past incident. Success Profiles strengths guidance instructs candidates not to
rehearse answers "because we are looking for your initial response" [4]; a
pre-built four-part story answers a question that was not asked.*

1. What are your main strengths? *(sourced: National Careers Service)* [6]
2. How do you work best: in a team or by yourself? *(sourced: targetjobs)* [24]
3. In your opinion, what makes a team work well together? *(sourced: targetjobs)* [24]
4. Would you prefer to lead a team or provide back-up support? *(sourced: targetjobs)* [24]
5. What is your approach to giving feedback to teammates? *(sourced: targetjobs)* [24]

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact.** It points back to
the evidence layer throughout.

## A decision heuristic you can run under pressure

You will not have time to weigh five options mid-interview. Run these four
checks in order and stop at the first that fires.

1. **Is it asking for a preference, an opinion or an approach rather than an
   incident?** If yes, use no story structure at all — answer directly. This is
   the strengths case, where the documented instruction is not to rehearse [4].
2. **Does the question contain a backward-looking word** — *learned*,
   *differently*, *looking back*, *failure*, *not performed*, *would you
   change*? If yes, use **STARR** if the remit matters, **CARL** if the
   interpersonal judgement matters. Reading's documented trigger is a
   "difficult situation" [19].
3. **Was the work self-started, or was the difficulty the thing you identified
   rather than the thing you were given?** If yes, use **SOAR's obstacle
   framing** in place of Task [20][21].
4. **Otherwise, use STAR** [1][2] — and **compress it to CAR**, equally
   official and equally safe to use [1][16], if you are
   in a timed format, such as a video interview with an on-screen timer [25],
   or if you have already used a long example and want to vary pace.

A single sentence version to memorise: *opinion → no structure; "learned" →
STARR or CARL; "I spotted it" → obstacle framing; clock → CAR; else STAR.*

## Worked contrast: the same example in two structures

Take one incident — a candidate covering a colleague's caseload during an
unplanned absence, missing an internal deadline but protecting the client
deliverable.

**Told as STAR** [1][17]. *Situation:* two-person team, colleague signed off at
short notice, four weeks before a client milestone. *Task:* "I was asked to
carry both caseloads until cover arrived." *Action:* four to six first-person
sentences — triaged the combined list against the milestone, moved two internal
reports to the following cycle after agreeing with the sponsor, drafted a
handover note so cover could start cold, escalated one item I could not
absorb. *Result:* client milestone met; the two internal reports landed a cycle
late.

**Told as CARL** [15]. *Context:* one sentence fusing the situation and the
remit — "when my colleague was signed off at short notice I picked up both
caseloads four weeks out from a client milestone." *Action:* the same
first-person sentences, marginally compressed. *Results:* same outcome, stated
plainly including the slippage. *Learning:* "I escalated on day nine when the
arithmetic had been obvious on day two. I now put a decision date in the diary
at the point I take on unplanned work, and I have used it twice since."

Notice what changed. STAR spends a beat establishing that carrying both
caseloads was legitimately *yours* — useful where the assessor is testing
ownership or level. CARL spends that beat on the Learning instead, and can
afford to state the slippage openly because the learning redeems it. Told as
STAR, the late reports are an unexplained blemish on the Result; told as CARL,
they are the setup.

**The same incident as SOAR** [20][21] would replace the Task beat with the
obstacle — "the real problem was not the workload, it was that no one had a
current view of which client commitments were load-bearing" — which is a
stronger answer *if* the question asked about problem-solving and a weaker one
if it asked what you were responsible for.

## Handling the structures in the room

**Do not announce the mnemonic.** Naming your structure aloud spends words on
process rather than evidence, and the police guidance is explicit that reciting
framework language does not earn marks [9][10]. The structures are scaffolding
for your preparation, not content.

**Prepare the example once, in the longest form.** Because CAR, STAR, STARR and
CARL are nested — Cambridge's Context absorbs Situation and Task [16], STARR is
STAR plus a step [17], CARL is CAR plus a step [15] — one well-built example
can be delivered in any of them. Draft in STARR, then drop the reflection for
STAR, then fuse the opening for CAR. Do not prepare four separate stories.

**Weight Action regardless of structure.** Every source that discusses
weighting says the same thing: Arden calls Action "the section you will spend
most of your time on" [17], Reading calls it "the longest part of your answer"
[19], Brock advises brevity in the opening because the later parts "are more
critical from the interviewer's perspective" [21], and the official Civil
Service guide says focus more words on the "how" than the "what" [1].

**If you use CARL or STARR, make the last step do work.** Edinburgh's caution
is that describing is not being critically reflective [15]. "I learned the
importance of communication" is description. A usable Learning step names what
you now do differently and gives evidence that you have done it.

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

**Assessors score evidence against criteria, not mnemonics. No published
framework reviewed for this document requires any particular answer
structure.** The claim is supported four ways.

First, the **central UK scoring guidance is silent on structure**. The Cabinet
Office guidance governing delegated-grade (EO to Grade 6) recruitment sets out
the assessment architecture and the scales — "Experience, technical skills and
behaviours should be scored using the 7 point scale. Strengths should be scored
using the 4 point scale" — and instructs that interview questions be designed
so the candidate has "ample opportunity to provide the required level of
evidence to demonstrate their capability for the role" [3]. Full-text
extraction of that document on 2026-07-20 found **no occurrence of STAR or any
other answer mnemonic** [3]. What is scored is the level of evidence against
the criterion.

Second, the **candidate-facing official guidance is permissive, not
mandatory** — and offers a choice. Success Profiles behaviours guidance says
"consider using the STAR method" [2]; the Civil Service Resourcing competency
guide presents two approaches, states that which one a candidate chooses "is
all down to your personal style of writing", and closes "Use either STAR or CAR
approach for writing your competency" [1]. An assessor who expected a single
prescribed shape would be expecting something the guidance declines to require.

Third, **employers say so in terms**. Hampshire Constabulary tells candidates
to "Use a structure which you feel works for you there are many out there"
[10]; Devon & Cornwall Police says the same and lists two [9]. Devon & Cornwall
adds the marking position directly: "we are only making an assessment based on
what you tell us about how you have or would dealt with situations, nothing
else" [9]. Both forces list "CVF `Bingo' — buzz words don't score" among common
mistakes, explaining that it "is about you answering the question and being
authentic in your response, not repeating buzz words from the CVF Framework"
[9][10]. Both also list "Answering the competency and not the question" as a
common mistake [9][10] — the failure mode of a candidate driving a prepared
structure through a question it does not fit.

Fourth, **professional-body guidance locates fairness in the assessor's
consistency, not the candidate's structure**. CIPD recommends structured
interviews — "asking a predefined set of questions, in the same order, to all
candidates" — with responses "scored using consistent criteria against a set of
pre-agreed criteria", and that interviewers "assign scores to each candidate
response independently before discussing them as a group" [13]. ACAS advises
asking each applicant "the same questions where possible... to ensure you're
treating applicants in the same way", and to check decisions "are based on how
each applicant met each point in the job description and person specification"
[14].

**Process facts that shape which structure is viable.** Devon & Cornwall force
interviews present six questions in total, with panels of two, and questions
shown to the candidate before being asked [9]; Hampshire notes a maximum
interview length of 60 minutes, two interviewers, and that applications are
assessed by two assessors with each answer scoring up to 8 [10]. Both forces
tell candidates they may draw on "work, home, education or volunteering" roles
[9][10]. On the EU side, the legacy EPSO competency-based interview allowed
about ten minutes per competency [8], though the current AD5 model contains no
interview at all [7].

**Implication for interviewers**, drawn from the same sources: if a candidate's
answer lacks structure, the documented remedy is a neutral probe applied
consistently, since equal treatment rests on comparable questioning [13][14] —
not a lower score for disorganisation, which is not among the published
criteria [3].

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — labelled guidance,** each item tied to the documented
criterion it violates.

- **Treating the five structures as equally endorsed.** Telling a UK public-
  sector panel you are "using the SOAR method" invokes something no official
  guidance reviewed here names [20]; STAR and CAR are the two with government
  documentation behind them [1][2].
- **Driving a prepared structure through the wrong question.** Both police
  forces list "Answering the competency and not the question" as a common
  mistake [9][10]. Structure should follow the question's verb, not precede it.
- **Reciting the mnemonic aloud.** Related to the documented "CVF `Bingo'"
  warning that buzzwords do not score [9][10]; narrating your process consumes
  the time your evidence needs.
- **Reflection that only describes.** Edinburgh's own caution against
  description masquerading as reflection [15] is the exact failure of "I
  learned a lot from that experience" as a Learning step.
- **Dropping the Result to make room for Reflection.** Arden identifies Result
  as "the section most people miss" and warns that without it "the interviewer
  will not know what the outcome was" [17]. STARR and CARL add a step; they do
  not licence removing one.
- **Compressing to CAR by cutting Action.** CAR compresses the *opening* —
  Cambridge's Context explicitly absorbs "the task at hand" [16]. Cutting the
  element every source says should be longest [17][19][21][1] is the wrong
  economy.
- **Assuming a reflective structure rescues a weak example.** A Learning step
  explains a poor outcome; it does not supply evidence of the behaviour being
  assessed, which is what is scored [3].
- **Preparing four versions of one story.** The structures are nested
  [15][16][17], so this multiplies rehearsal without adding evidence — and
  over-rehearsal cuts against the documented preference for authentic responses
  [9][10].
- **(Interviewers) crediting structural fluency.** The scoring instruction is
  the evidence level against the criterion [3] and the match to the person
  specification [14] — not the tidiness of the delivery.
- **(Interviewers) probing inconsistently.** Rescuing one unstructured answer
  with follow-ups but not another undermines the same-questions consistency
  CIPD and ACAS recommend [13][14].

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.** Drills to run this week.

1. **Structure-selection sort, ten minutes.** Take the thirty-seven Question
   Bank items with the headings covered. Assign each to a structure using only
   the four-step heuristic above, then reveal the headings. Every mismatch is a
   cue word you are not yet reading — most commonly a buried "differently"
   [19].
2. **The two-structure retell.** Take one prepared example. Deliver it as STAR
   [1], then immediately as CARL [15] without re-drafting. Record both. The
   test is whether the CARL version's Learning step names a changed behaviour
   with evidence, or merely a sentiment [15].
3. **Ninety-second CAR drill.** Set a timer to ninety seconds — inside the one-
   to-three-minute band reported for pre-recorded video interviews [25] — and
   answer three items from the CAR group. If you run out of time, check that
   you cut the opening rather than the Action [16][17][19].
4. **Reflection-quality audit.** Write out the Learning or Reflection sentence
   from four of your prepared examples. Score each: does it name a specific
   changed practice, and can you cite an occasion since where you applied it?
   Anything failing both is description, not reflection [15].
5. **Obstacle rewrite.** Take two examples currently framed as assigned work.
   Rewrite the second beat as an obstacle you identified rather than a task you
   received [20][21]. Note which of the two genuinely improves — if the work
   really was assigned, the rewrite will feel like a claim you cannot support.
6. **Strengths contrast, in pairs.** Have a partner ask two items from the
   "no story structure" group cold, and answer without preparation, as the
   Success Profiles strengths guidance directs [4]. Then have them ask a STAR
   item. The felt difference is the boundary of the whole technique.
7. **Mock panel with blind scoring, in threes.** One candidate, two assessors.
   Ask the same three questions, probe neutrally, and score independently
   before discussing — the CIPD-recommended discipline [13]. Assessors score
   evidence against a written criterion only, and must not award or deduct for
   structure [3].

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.** Signposts only; siblings treat these in
depth.

- **Structure preference and reasonable adjustments.** Under the Equality Act
  2010, section 20, there is a duty to make adjustments where a provision,
  criterion or practice puts a disabled person at a substantial disadvantage,
  including providing auxiliary aids [11]. An enforced per-answer time limit —
  such as the one-to-three-minute video-interview window with an on-screen
  timer [25] — is such a practice, and it interacts directly with structure
  choice: a candidate who needs processing time is pushed toward compression
  regardless of what the question suits. Both police forces reviewed invite
  candidates to raise adjustments with the recruitment team before interview
  [9][10].
- **Adjustments in EU selection.** EPSO states that candidates may request
  accommodations by indicating their needs in the application form with
  supporting documents, that each request "is assessed individually by the EPSO
  Accessibility Team" against the candidate's functional limitations and the
  test method, and that accommodations are "granted on a case-by-case basis"
  [12].
- **Do not make structure a criterion.** Decisions must be based on how each
  applicant met each point in the job description and person specification
  [14], scored on the published scales [3]. Marking a candidate down for
  narrating out of order risks penalising communication style rather than the
  assessed competency, and neither the scoring guidance [3] nor the force
  guidance [9][10] supports it.
- **Consistency of probing.** Equal treatment rests on asking each applicant
  the same questions where possible [14] and scoring against pre-agreed
  criteria [13]; unequal rescue-probing of unstructured answers is a fairness
  risk as well as a scoring one.
- **Candidate data.** Where interviews are recorded or notes taken, they must
  be stored securely and confidentially in line with data protection rules
  [14].

# Risks & Caveats

- **SOAR is the weakest link in this document and the comparison table should
  be read with that in mind.** Its UK/EU documentation is a single named
  practitioner article [20]; the supporting institutional handout is Canadian,
  undated, and self-described as adapted from a magazine article [21]. This is
  the principal reason the document is rated Medium rather than High.
- **Unverified labels are excluded, not disproven.** STAR-L, PAR, CCAR and
  similar mnemonics circulate in careers materials. None could be traced to a
  named publisher during research on 2026-07-20 and none is documented here.
  Absence from this document means unverified, not non-existent.
- **Careers-service pages are living content.** Edinburgh [15], Cambridge [16],
  Arden [17], Reading [19] and Bath [22] publish without version histories;
  wording may change without notice. All URLs were opened and verified on
  2026-07-20.
- **Force guidance is force-specific.** The Devon & Cornwall [9] and Hampshire
  [10] documents are candidate packs issued by individual forces, not College
  of Policing national policy. The CARL endorsement in [9] is real but local;
  do not generalise it to all UK policing or the wider public sector.
- **Police guidance predates or straddles the CVF 2024 revision.** Hampshire's
  pack lists PC Level 1 competencies using 2024 CVF names such as "We
  collaborate" and "We support and inspire" [10]; the process content cited
  here is framework-agnostic, but competency names in force documents should be
  checked against the current College of Policing framework.
- **EPSO reference [8] is an archive page that returns HTTP 200.** The
  competency-based interview it describes is not part of the current
  competition model [7]. It is cited here only as evidence that EPSO named no
  answer mnemonic even when it ran interviews. Automated link checking cannot
  detect this staleness.
- **The "when each beats STAR" mapping is synthesis.** Only Reading publishes
  an explicit trigger for a variant [19] and Arden an explicit optionality
  statement [17]. The rest of the mapping is reasoned from each structure's
  published element definitions and is confined to the coaching layer.
- **One-employer evidence.** Fidelity International's STARR guidance [18] is a
  single named employer via a careers channel; it does not establish
  private-sector prevalence.

# Open Questions

- **Does any other UK employer name a non-STAR structure in candidate
  guidance?** Devon & Cornwall Police naming CARL [9] was the only instance
  located. A systematic sweep of published force, NHS trust and departmental
  candidate packs would establish whether this is isolated or a pattern, and
  would materially change CARL's standing.
- **What is the original CAR source?** Edinburgh attributes CARL to "BlueSteps
  (2011) CAR interview Technique" [15]. That primary source was not located;
  retrieving it would settle CAR's provenance and, by extension, CARL's.
- **Is there any academic evidence that answer structure affects scores?** The
  documented position is that evidence is scored, not structure [3][13][14].
  Whether structured answers nonetheless attract higher ratings — a rater-
  fluency effect — is an empirical question no source reviewed addresses.
- **Does SOAR have any UK or EU institutional documentation?** Only a Canadian
  university handout was located [21]. A UK or EU careers service or employer
  naming SOAR would change its standing in the table.
- **Do EU institutions recruiting outside the EPSO competition route name an
  answer structure?** EPSO does not [7][8]. Agency-level candidate guidance was
  not swept for this document.
