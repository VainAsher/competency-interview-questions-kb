# Executive Summary

An evidence bank is a maintained personal store of real examples, indexed
against a published competency framework, that a candidate reuses and re-pitches
across applications instead of rebuilding preparation from scratch each time.
The parent document, `techniques-foundation`, established the answer structures
— STAR and its relatives — that turn one example into one scoreable answer. This
document goes a layer beneath that: it is about the *stock* of examples those
structures draw on, how that stock is sourced, indexed, calibrated to a grade
band, maintained, and audited for gaps.

The central argument of this document is an assessor-side one, and it is
documented rather than inferred. The College of Policing's guidance on
values-based recruitment sets out the ORCE model — observe, record, classify,
evaluate — and instructs assessors, at the classify step, to "Classify each
behaviour once – do not double count" [4]. Evaluation then rests on "the quality
and quantity of the classified evidence" [4]. The practical consequence for a
candidate is severe: a single strong example carried into every question does
not multiply. Once its behavioural evidence has been classified against one
competency, it cannot legitimately be counted again for the next. Breadth of
*distinct* examples is therefore not a stylistic preference — it is what the
scoring model rewards. That is the organising principle for everything else
here.

Around that spine sit four practical systems. **Sourcing**: official guidance is
explicit that evidence need not come from paid work — the Civil Service
Behaviours document names "work experience, volunteering or in connection with a
hobby" [1], and the University of Manchester careers service names studies, paid
work, volunteering, student societies, work experience, a placement year and
course-rep experience as places professionalism is evidenced [17]. **Mapping**:
one episode usually yields evidence against several competencies when framed
differently, and the target framework determines the index — nine Civil Service
Behaviours at six grade-banded levels [1], 36 strengths that are expressly "not
defined by grade" [2], EPSO's eight general competencies with five observable
anchors each [6], the CVF's six competencies at three cumulative levels [5], and
the six NHS Constitution values [8]. **Calibration**: the same episode is pitched
differently at different bands, a practice the CVF guidance illustrates directly
with one indicator producing different expected behaviour for a sergeant and an
inspector [4]. **Maintenance and gap analysis**: a bank decays, and the
coverage question is answerable only against a specific advert.

Confidence is **Medium**. The framework structures, the ORCE classification rule,
the level ladders and the four-behaviour recommendation are all documented in
official sources. The bank *format* — the fields to record, the weekly habit,
the audit cadence — is coaching synthesis built on top of those documents and is
labelled as such throughout.

# Key Takeaways

- Assessors are instructed to classify each behaviour once and not double count,
  and to evaluate on the quality *and quantity* of classified evidence [4] —
  so reusing one example across every question caps the score achievable.
  *(documented)*
- Evidence legitimately comes from outside paid work: Civil Service guidance
  names work experience, volunteering and hobbies [1]; the National Careers
  Service names volunteering and personal experience [10]; a university careers
  service names studies, part-time work, volunteering, societies, placements
  and course-rep roles [17]. *(documented)*
- Employers are advised to prioritise "no more than four behaviours" as critical
  to a delegated-grade Civil Service role [3], and recruiting managers pick a
  selection of the nine [1] — so a bank is audited against the advert, never
  against the whole framework. *(documented)*
- The index depends on the target framework: nine Behaviours at six grade-banded
  levels [1]; 36 strengths, expressly not defined by grade [2]; EPSO's eight
  competencies with five anchors apiece, forty in total [6]; the CVF's six
  competencies at three cumulative levels [5]; six NHS Constitution values [8].
  *(documented)*
- Level calibration is a documented assessor concern, not candidate folklore:
  CVF guidance shows the *same* competency indicator generating different
  expected behaviour for a sergeant and for an inspector, and tells recruiters
  to contextualise assessment to the role [4]. *(documented)*
- One episode can be indexed against several competencies because competency
  definitions describe different facets of observable behaviour; the same
  incident framed around what you decided, what you said, and what you shipped
  produces three different answers. *(guidance)*
- A workable bank record has a fixed field set — episode, date, one-line handle,
  the actions in first person, the measurable result, the competencies it can
  serve, the level it is pitched at, and the confidentiality note. *(guidance)*
- Gaps are normal and are closed by acquiring evidence, not by inventing it:
  a genuinely uncovered competency is a development action for the next quarter,
  and drawing on more than one example — ideally from both on and off a course
  — is the published advice [17]. *(guidance)*

# Purpose

This document answers a systems question rather than a technique question. For a
candidate: *what should I actually keep, in what form, indexed how, so that
preparing for the next application is an hour of retrieval rather than a weekend
of recall?* For an interviewer or assessment designer: *what does the scoring
model I am running imply about the preparation candidates must do, and where does
that create fair or unfair advantage?*

It exists because the parent document, `techniques-foundation`, closes on the
observation that preparing six to eight examples mapped against the behaviours
in an advert is the core of interview preparation, and defers the systematic
treatment to this child. That treatment is here.

# Scope

**Covered:** the legitimate sources of evidence, including for candidates with
little or no career history; the record format for a single bank entry; mapping
one example to several competencies; indexing a bank against a named target
framework (Civil Service Behaviours and Strengths, EPSO competencies and
anchors, the police CVF, NHS values); pitching the same example at different
grade bands; maintenance cadence and decay; coverage-gap analysis and what to do
about a genuine gap; the assessor-side scoring facts that make breadth
necessary.

**Not covered here:** the internal anatomy of a single answer — STAR's elements
and worked answers belong to `star-technique`, and the CAR/STARR/CARL/SOAR
comparison to `alternative-answer-frameworks`. The competency families
themselves belong to `competencies-foundation`. Framework-specific question
practice belongs to `uk-civil-service-behaviour-questions` and
`epso-competency-framework`. Interviewer-side process design, including panel
scoring mechanics, belongs to `best-practice-foundation`. Strengths-based
questioning is touched only where it bounds what a bank is useful for.

# Definitions

- **Evidence bank** — a maintained personal store of real past examples, indexed
  against a competency framework, from which interview and application answers
  are drawn. Document-specific usage; not a term used in the official
  frameworks cited here.
- **Bank entry / episode** — one real event, recorded once, that may be indexed
  against more than one competency.
- **Index** — the mapping from an episode to the competencies, behaviours,
  strengths, anchors or values it can evidence.
- **Coverage** — the proportion of the competencies named in a specific advert
  for which the bank holds at least one distinct, level-appropriate episode.
- **Coverage gap** — a named competency in the target framework for which the
  bank holds no distinct episode, or only an episode already committed
  elsewhere.
- **Level calibration** — adjusting which facts of an episode are foregrounded so
  the evidence matches the level descriptor for the target grade or rank.
- **Anchor** — in the EPSO framework, one of the "observable elements" that
  compose a general competency [6].
- **ORCE** — observe, record, classify, evaluate: the structured assessment
  sequence set out in College of Policing guidance [4].

# Framework Context

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

A bank has no shape until it has a target. Each of the major UK and EU
frameworks presents a differently shaped index, and the differences change what a
well-built bank looks like.

**UK Civil Service — Behaviours.** The Civil Service Behaviours document defines
nine behaviours — Seeing the Big Picture, Changing and Improving, Making
Effective Decisions, Leadership, Communicating and Influencing, Working
Together, Developing Self and Others, Managing a Quality Service, Delivering at
Pace — and states that "Civil Service Behaviours are specific to the grade level
of the job role" [1]. The document sets out six levels mapped to grade bands:
Level 1 (AA and AO or equivalent), Level 2 (EO), Level 3 (HEO and SEO), Level 4
(Grade 7 and 6), Level 5 (Deputy Director) and Level 6 (Director and Director
General) [1]. It is explicit that "recruiting managers will choose a selection of
behaviours which are best suited to the specific job role. You will not be asked
to demonstrate all Civil Service Behaviours for one role" [1].

**UK Civil Service — Strengths.** The Strengths Dictionary is a differently
shaped index: 36 named strengths with definitions, and the statement that "Civil
Service Strengths are not defined by grade" [2]. It defines three elements that
determine whether something is a strength — Performance ("you can perform an
activity/behaviour to a high level of capability or proficiency"), Engagement
("you feel motivated, enthused and empowered when doing the activity") and Use
("you do the activity regularly and as often as possible") [2]. The Dictionary
records that "The strengths are mapped to the most relevant Civil Service
Behaviour(s) but any strength can be assessed if suitable for the role" [2].
Critically for anyone building a bank, the same document instructs candidates:
"Do not rehearse your answers because we are looking for your initial response"
[2] — the boundary of what a bank is for.

**EPSO.** EPSO's Competency Framework "defines the competencies that are
considered essential to perform effectively in any given job, by the EU
institutions and bodies that EPSO serves" [7]. The published anchors leaflet
names the eight general competencies — Critical thinking, analysing & creative
problem-solving; Decision-making & getting results; Information management
(digital and data literacy); Self-management; Working together; Learning as a
skill; Communication; Intrapreneurship — and states that "Each general
competency is composed of various observable elements, called anchors" [6]. Each
of the eight carries five anchors, forty in total [6]. The anchors are unusually
useful for banking because they are written as observable acts: "Responds calmly
to obstacles and is able to distance themselves from frustration and negativity"
(Self-management), "Proactively shares knowledge for the benefit and learning of
others on an ongoing basis" (Working together), "Seizes every opportunity to
learn, even from failure" (Learning as a skill) [6].

**Policing — the CVF.** The 2024 Competency and Values Framework contains six
competencies — We are emotionally aware, We take ownership, We collaborate, We
support and inspire, We analyse critically, We are innovative and open-minded —
and three values: Courage, Respect and empathy, Public service [5]. "Each
competency has three levels, which can be used flexibly to allow for a better fit
with frontline and non-frontline policing roles, and at different levels of
seniority": Level 1 Practitioner, Level 2 Supervisor/middle manager, Level 3
Senior manager/executive [5]. "The levels are cumulative. This means that those
working at higher levels should have built on their behaviours from the preceding
levels" [5].

**NHS.** The NHS Constitution for England names six NHS values: working together
for patients; respect and dignity; commitment to quality of care; compassion;
improving lives; everyone counts [8].

| Framework | Index units | Levels | Bank implication |
|-----------|-------------|--------|------------------|
| Civil Service Behaviours [1] | 9 behaviours | 6, mapped to grade bands AA/AO → Director General | Index by behaviour *and* record the level the episode supports |
| Civil Service Strengths [2] | 36 strengths | None — "not defined by grade" [2] | Not bankable as stories; prepare by reflection, not rehearsal [2] |
| EPSO [6][7] | 8 general competencies, 40 anchors | Not levelled in the anchors leaflet [6] | Index at anchor granularity — the anchors read as observable acts |
| Police CVF [5] | 6 competencies, 3 values | 3, cumulative (Practitioner → Senior manager/executive) | Higher levels assume the lower; a Level 2 answer must still contain Level 1 behaviour |
| NHS Constitution [8] | 6 values | None stated in the Constitution [8] | Index by value; evidence is conduct-led rather than achievement-led |

# Core Reference

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## Where evidence legitimately comes from

The official position is not that unpaid experience is a fallback; it is named
alongside employment. The Civil Service Behaviours document, describing how
candidates may be asked for examples of a behaviour, states that "This might be
at work or somewhere else such as work experience, volunteering or in connection
with a hobby" [1]. The National Careers Service, in its STAR guidance, likewise
records that examples may be drawn from work, volunteering or personal
experience [10]. The Civil Service recruitment blog advises identifying the key
behaviours required for the target role and preparing specific examples that
demonstrate them, at both application and interview stages [9].

For candidates with little or no career history the most explicit published
inventory of sources comes from the University of Manchester's careers service,
which records that professionalism "can be evidenced through your studies and
extra-curricular activities such as paid work, volunteering, involvement with a
student society, work experience, a placement year or experience gained as a
course rep" [17]. The same page names specific roles that generate evidence —
PASS Leader and peer-mentoring positions, volunteering including departmental
roles, work experience alongside study, and student society committee positions
[17]. It also carries the single most bank-relevant instruction located for this
document: "When applying to an employer, try to draw on more than one example
when evidencing professionalism and ideally from both on and off your course"
[17].

## How much coverage a role actually demands

A bank is audited against an advert, not against a framework, because the
framework is never assessed whole. Cabinet Office guidance for delegated-grade
(EO to Grade 6) recruitment states: "As a guide, it is recommended that no more
than four behaviours are prioritised as critical to the role. This will allow the
panel to sufficiently test the criteria at interview stage, along with a
candidate's strengths and experience" [3]. The Behaviours document itself says
recruiting managers choose a selection and that candidates "will not be asked to
demonstrate all Civil Service Behaviours for one role" [1]. The same guidance
adds a scoping rule with direct bank consequences: where experience can be
gained within six months of commencing a role, it should not be treated as a
requirement [3].

The policing equivalent is level-scoped rather than count-scoped: "Most roles
will have the level specified for each competency as part of the professional
profile and this will normally be the level that should be assessed against" [4].

## One episode, several competencies

Competency frameworks describe overlapping facets of the same observable
conduct, which is why one episode can be indexed several ways. The overlap is
visible in the published definitions. The Civil Service defines Working Together
as forming "effective partnerships and relationships with people both internally
and externally… sharing information, resources and support", Communicating and
Influencing as communicating "purpose and direction with clarity, integrity and
enthusiasm" while respecting "the needs, responses and opinions of others", and
Delivering at Pace as taking "responsibility for delivering timely and quality
results with focus and drive" [1]. A single episode in which a candidate
convened a stalled cross-team piece of work, explained the position to a
sceptical stakeholder, and shipped it to a deadline contains observable material
answering to all three definitions — but each answer must foreground a different
part of the episode.

EPSO's anchors make the same point at finer grain, because the anchors are
written as discrete observable elements within a competency [6]: the act of
sharing a lesson learned from a piece of work maps to "Proactively shares
knowledge for the benefit and learning of others on an ongoing basis" under
Working together, while the reflection that produced the lesson maps to "Seizes
every opportunity to learn, even from failure" and "Identifies opportunities to
apply lessons learned and improve their performance" under Learning as a skill
[6].

The Strengths Dictionary documents an analogous cross-index in the other
direction, recording that "The strengths are mapped to the most relevant Civil
Service Behaviour(s) but any strength can be assessed if suitable for the role"
[2]. *(The Dictionary's strengths-to-behaviours mapping table itself is
deliberately not reproduced in this document — see Risks & Caveats.)*

There is, however, a documented limit on how far one episode may be stretched
within a single assessment, and it is the assessor's rule rather than the
candidate's: see Assessor Perspective.

## Level and grade calibration

The frameworks are explicit that the same behaviour reads differently at
different bands. The Civil Service Behaviours document sets out a ladder for each
behaviour across all six levels. Working Together, for instance, runs from Level
1 — "Proactively contribute to the work of the whole team and remain open to
taking on new and different roles. Get to know your colleagues and build
supportive relationships… Ask for help when needed and support others when the
opportunity arises" — through Level 3, "Encourage joined up team work within own
team and across other groups. Establish professional relationships with a range
of stakeholders… Invest time to develop a common focus and genuine positive team
spirit" — to Level 5, "Proactively create, maintain and promote a strong network
of contacts across the organisation and externally. Embed an inclusive culture of
creating positive and supportive teams… Encourage and establish mechanisms to
share knowledge and resources across boundaries to support the business" [1].
The subject matter is constant; the scope, the durability and the systemic reach
escalate.

The CVF makes the same move over three levels. "We collaborate" at Level 1 reads
"I work cooperatively with others to get things done, willingly giving help and
support to colleagues"; at Level 2, "I build strong relationships across a broad
range of stakeholders, including colleagues, partners and communities, and use
these connections to accomplish goals"; at Level 3, "I create a culture and
environment in which partnership working flourishes and creates tangible
benefits for all" [5]. Because "the levels are cumulative" [5], a Level 2 answer
that contains no Level 1 behaviour is incomplete rather than advanced.

The clearest published statement that the *same* evidence is pitched differently
by band comes from College of Policing recruitment guidance, which works the
point through explicitly. Taking the competency indicator "I actively encourage
and support learning within my teams and colleagues", the guidance sets the
expected behaviour for a sergeant assessment as encouraging a team of PCs,
special constables, PCSOs and staff to complete set learning "including
allocating time for this", and for an inspector assessment as encouraging "own
teams and those of other inspectors to complete set CPD" while managing
"resources (time, workload, staffing) so that individuals have sufficient time
for this and other learning" [4]. The surrounding guidance states the principle
directly: "the assessment should be contextualised to the role… the assessment
content (questions/exercises) and the expectations of performance should be
relevant to the specific job being recruited for" [4].

Strengths are the exception that proves the shape: they carry no level ladder at
all, being "not defined by grade" [2].

## Currency and review

No official source located for this document prescribes a maintenance cadence for
a candidate's personal preparation material. What is documented is the reflective
step that generates fresh bank content. The University of Edinburgh's Reflection
Toolkit sets out CARL — Context, Action, Results, Learning — where the final step
is to "Identify what you have learned", and notes that "identifying new learning
is an extremely valuable process and will in many cases affect practice on its
own" [20]. The EPSO framework encodes the same habit as a competency in its own
right, Learning as a skill, whose anchors include "Identifies opportunities to
apply lessons learned and improve their performance" [6]. The maintenance habit
recommended in this document's coaching layer is built on those two documented
points and is labelled as guidance.

1. Take your strongest episode and state it three ways in one minute each: once foregrounding what you decided, once what you communicated, once what you delivered. Which version is weakest, and why? *(composed)*
2. Pitch the same episode at two adjacent levels of your target framework. Name the single fact you added at the higher level that made it land. *(composed)*

4. Which of your bank entries is now more than two years old, and does anything more recent say the same thing better? *(composed)*

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — this is guidance, not documented fact.** It points back to the
evidence layer throughout and introduces no new facts.

## A concrete bank format

One episode, one record. The field set below is the smallest one that survives
contact with an actual application; each field exists because something in the
evidence layer asks for it.

| Field | Content | Why it is there |
|-------|---------|-----------------|
| Handle | Six words you would recognise in a panic — "the onboarding week we lost the lead" | Retrieval speed under pressure |
| Date and setting | Month, year, organisation or course, your role | Currency judgements and the "at work or somewhere else" framing [1][17] |
| Situation and task | Three sentences maximum | Context exists to make the action legible |
| Actions, first person | Five to eight bullets, each starting with a verb, each something *you* did | Behaviours are "the actions and activities that people do" [1] |
| Result | One measurable or named consequence | Evaluation rests on quality and quantity of evidence [4] |
| Learning | One sentence — what you changed afterwards | The CARL learning step [20]; EPSO's Learning as a skill anchors [6] |
| Competencies served | The behaviours, anchors, CVF competencies or values this could evidence — ranked, strongest first | The one-episode-several-competencies mapping |
| Level pitched | Which level descriptor the actions actually reach, and what would be needed for the next one up | Behaviours are grade-specific [1]; CVF levels are cumulative [5] |
| Confidentiality note | Anything that must be anonymised or omitted | See Legal & Fairness Considerations |
| Committed to | Left blank until you use it in a live application, then record where | Prevents accidental double-commitment in one interview |

Keep it in whatever you will actually open — a spreadsheet with one row per
episode and one column per framework competency is the format that makes
coverage gaps visible at a glance, because an empty column is a gap.

## Mapping one example to several competencies

The technique is to hold the episode constant and move the camera. For each
episode, write three one-line versions:

- **The decision version.** What was the judgement only you could make, on what
  information, with what risk? This is the version that serves Making Effective
  Decisions [1] or EPSO's Decision-making and getting results [6].
- **The people version.** Who did you have to bring with you, what did you
  actually say, and what changed in their position? This serves Communicating
  and Influencing or Working Together [1], EPSO's Communication or Working
  together [6], or CVF collaboration [5].
- **The delivery version.** What shipped, by when, at what quality, against what
  constraint? This serves Delivering at Pace or Managing a Quality Service [1].

If all three versions read the same, the episode is thin and belongs to one
competency only. That is useful information, not a failure — it tells you the
bank needs another episode.

Rank the mapping. An episode's strongest competency is the one where the *action
bullets* — not the situation — sit squarely inside the published descriptor.
Weak mappings are the ones you reach for when panicking, and they score badly.

## Calibrating to a grade or level

Work from the published descriptor for the *target* band, not the band you
currently occupy. Read the descriptor for one level below and one level above as
well, because the difference between them tells you which facts to foreground.
As a working rule drawn from the ladders quoted above [1][5]:

- **Moving up a level usually means widening the blast radius.** The same
  episode moves from "my team" to "across other groups" to "across the
  organisation and externally" [1]. If you cannot honestly widen it, do not
  claim the level — add the *systemic* fact instead: what you put in place that
  outlived the episode.
- **Cumulative frameworks reward the base, not just the peak.** Because CVF
  levels are cumulative [5], a Level 2 answer should still contain the Level 1
  cooperative behaviour; stripping it out to sound senior removes evidence.
- **Do not over-pitch.** Where the professional profile specifies the level, that
  is normally the level assessed against [4]. An answer aimed two bands high
  reads as poor judgement about the role.
- **Contextualise to the role, as assessors are told to.** The sergeant and
  inspector expectations for one indicator differ by scope and by resource
  control [4]; ask which of those two shapes your target role actually is.

## The weekly maintenance habit

Fifteen minutes, same slot each week. Three moves:

1. **Capture.** Add any episode from the past week that produced a decision, a
   difficult conversation, a delivery under constraint, or a lesson. Handle,
   date, action bullets — nothing more. Polishing is a separate job.
2. **Promote.** Pick one raw capture from earlier and complete its fields,
   including the level pitch and the competency ranking.
3. **Retire.** Mark one entry that has been superseded by better, more recent
   evidence. Do not delete it; a retired entry is still the answer to
   "tell me about early in your career".

Quarterly, do one longer pass: re-read the target framework's descriptors, re-rank
mappings, and check that nothing in the bank has aged into a liability.

## Coverage-gap analysis

Run this against a live advert, because the framework is never assessed whole and
Civil Service guidance recommends no more than four behaviours are prioritised as
critical [3][1].

1. List the competencies the advert names, and the level each is pitched at.
2. Against each, name the episodes you would use — and mark any episode that
   appears twice.
3. Any competency whose only episode is already committed elsewhere is a gap,
   because of the classify-once rule [4]. Treat it as unfilled.
4. Any competency with no episode at all is a hard gap.
5. Any competency where your episode sits a level below the advert is a
   calibration gap — usually fixable by finding the systemic fact, sometimes
   not.

**What to do about a real gap.** In order of honesty:

- **Search wider before concluding.** Studies, part-time work, volunteering,
  societies, placements and course-rep roles are all published sources of
  evidence [17][1][10]; most "gaps" at early-career stage are retrieval
  failures, not absences.
- **Use a smaller, genuine example rather than a large, borrowed one.** A modest
  episode that is unmistakably yours produces classifiable behavioural evidence
  [4]; a team achievement narrated as "we" does not.
- **Go and acquire the evidence.** A gap identified in July is a development
  objective for the autumn: volunteer for the thing, ask for the shadowing, take
  the committee role. This is the only remedy that closes the gap permanently.
- **Never invent.** Fabricated evidence fails on probing, and the assessment
  model is built to probe — assessors record notes "as verbatim as possible"
  and classify only behaviour observed in that assessment [4].
- **Say so, briefly, if asked.** A candid "I have not led a team, but here is the
  closest thing I have done and what I learned from it" is a scoreable answer;
  silence is not.

## What a bank is *not* for

Strengths questions. The Strengths Dictionary is explicit: "Do not rehearse your
answers because we are looking for your initial response", and the recommended
preparation is to reflect on your strengths and preferred ways of working [2].
Bringing a banked story to a strengths question answers a question that was not
asked.

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

The reason breadth matters is mechanical, and it is written down. College of
Policing guidance instructs trained assessors to follow ORCE — observe, record,
classify, evaluate — "which breaks down the assessing process into component
parts and helps to avoid bias" [4]. The four steps are specified: observe and
listen to everything the candidate does and says; take contemporaneous notes "as
verbatim as possible" while observing; classify the observed behaviours into
competencies and values after the candidate has finished; and decide how well the
candidate demonstrated the competencies and values, awarding a rating [4].

The classify step carries the rule that governs bank design. Assessors are told
to classify "after the candidate has left the room", "independent of any other
assessor(s)", to "Only classify behavioural evidence", to "Only classify
behaviour from that assessment", and — decisively — to "**Classify each behaviour
once – do not double count**" [4]. Evaluation then happens "after classifying",
"based on quality and quantity of the classified evidence", with consideration of
"the level of role and aim of the assessment process (benchmarking)" and of "what
the candidate missed or if anything detracted from their performance" [4].

Read that sequence from the candidate's side. A single example redeployed across
four questions is observed four times but yields one classification per distinct
behaviour it evidences; it cannot be counted again for the next competency, and
the *quantity* half of the evaluation criterion goes unfed. The candidate who
brings four distinct episodes has four independent bodies of classified evidence.
The candidate who brings one has one, plus three answers that consumed panel time
without adding evidence.

The published rating scale makes the same point about volume. The example scale
in the guidance runs A to D, where A is "Substantial positive evidence of the
competency observed with regard to the quality and quantity of the evidence and
few or no areas for development observed", and D is "Substantial areas for
development identified and little or no positive evidence of the competency
observed with regard to the quality and quantity of the evidence" [4]. Quantity
appears in every band descriptor.

The same guidance names the biases a bank interacts with. The **contrast effect**
— "Candidate's performance is compared to and evaluated against previous
candidates' performance" — and the **halo/horns effect**, where a strong showing
in one exercise is assumed to carry across, are listed as barriers to effective
assessment, to be overcome by using the ORCE model and by assessor training [4].
An assessor working the model properly will not let one brilliant example
illuminate the rest of the interview.

On the Civil Service side, the scoping guidance limits how much a bank must
cover: no more than four behaviours are recommended as critical to a
delegated-grade role, precisely so "the panel [can] sufficiently test the
criteria at interview stage, along with a candidate's strengths and experience"
[3]. The Behaviours document confirms that a selection, not the full set, is
assessed for any one role [1].

Professional-body guidance converges on the consistency mechanisms that make
banked evidence comparable across candidates. CIPD's selection guidance
recommends asking "all candidates the same questions in the same order and score
responses according to the objective criteria", and that "Interviewers should
assign scores to each candidate response independently before discussing them as
a group" [15]. ACAS advises that "When interviewing it's a good idea to ask each
applicant the same questions where possible… to ensure you're treating applicants
in the same way", and to "Check your decisions are based on how each applicant
met each point in the job description and person specification" [16].

For interviewers designing a process, the implication runs the other way: if your
question set allows a candidate to answer every question from one episode, your
questions are not discriminating between competencies. Varying the domain of the
prompt — delivery, conflict, learning, external partnership — forces distinct
evidence into the room, which is what the classify-once rule needs in order to
produce a spread of ratings.

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — labelled guidance,** each item tied to the documented
criterion it violates.

- **The one-example candidate.** Carrying a single flagship story into every
  question. Assessors classify each behaviour once and do not double count [4],
  so the second and third outings add nothing to the score while consuming the
  time that fresh evidence needed.
- **Banking outcomes instead of actions.** Recording "the project delivered
  early" without the bullets of what you personally did. Behaviours are defined
  as "the actions and activities that people do" [1], and assessors classify
  behavioural evidence only [4].
- **Indexing by story, not by competency.** A bank organised as a list of
  anecdotes cannot answer "which competency is uncovered?"; a bank indexed
  against the advert's named behaviours can, and the advert names only a
  handful [3][1].
- **Ignoring the level.** Preparing content-perfect examples pitched at the
  wrong band. Behaviours are grade-specific [1] and CVF levels are cumulative
  [5]; where the profile specifies the level, that is normally what is assessed
  [4].
- **Over-pitching to sound senior.** Stripping the cooperative detail out of an
  answer to make it sound strategic removes the base evidence a cumulative
  framework expects to see [5].
- **Treating unpaid experience as second-class.** Official guidance names work
  experience, volunteering and hobbies [1], and named careers guidance adds
  studies, societies, placements and course-rep roles [17]. Excluding them
  manufactures gaps that do not exist.
- **Letting the bank go stale.** Examples that were current three roles ago
  invite "have you done anything since?" — and the reflective learning step that
  keeps a bank alive is itself a documented practice [20][6].
- **Rehearsing strengths answers out of the bank.** Explicitly contrary to the
  published instruction not to rehearse [2].
- **Recording other people in identifiable detail.** See Legal & Fairness
  Considerations; anonymise at capture, not at interview.
- **(Interviewers) writing a question set one story can answer.** If four
  prompts all invite the same episode, the classify-once rule [4] leaves you
  with one competency's worth of evidence and four questions' worth of time
  spent.

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.** Concrete drills; each can be started this week.

1. **Ninety-minute build.** Take a live advert. List its named competencies and
   levels. Open a spreadsheet: one row per episode, one column per competency.
   Fill twelve rows from memory using the sourcing prompts in the Question Bank,
   recording only handle, date, three action bullets and a result. Stop at
   ninety minutes.
2. **Empty-column audit.** Sort the sheet. Every competency column with no strong
   entry is a hard gap; every column whose only entry is also the strongest entry
   in another column is a soft gap under the classify-once rule [4]. Write the
   list. It is your preparation plan.
3. **Three-camera drill.** Take one episode and deliver it aloud three times in
   sixty seconds each — decision version, people version, delivery version.
   Record all three. The version you found hardest names the competency your
   bank is thinnest on.
4. **Ladder climb.** Find your target behaviour's descriptor at your level, one
   below and one above [1] or [5]. Rewrite one episode at each of the three
   levels. Identify the single added fact that carries each step. If you cannot
   find it, the episode is capped at the lower level — record that in the bank.
5. **Non-work sweep.** Spend twenty minutes only on the composed sourcing prompts
   for candidates with little career history. Even mid-career candidates
   typically surface two usable episodes from volunteering, governance or
   study [17][1].
6. **Double-commitment test.** Simulate an interview from a real advert: assign
   one episode per question in advance and forbid reuse. If you run out of
   episodes before you run out of questions, you have found the exact size of
   your gap.
7. **Weekly capture, four weeks.** Run the fifteen-minute habit for a month and
   then compare the four captured episodes against the four you would have
   recalled from memory. The gap between them is the argument for the habit.
8. **Anonymisation pass.** Re-read every entry and strip names, patient or
   service-user identifiers, and anything commercially confidential, replacing
   them with roles. Do this once at capture and it never needs doing under time
   pressure.

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.** Signposts only; siblings treat these in
depth.

- **Reasonable adjustments.** The Equality Act 2010, section 20, imposes a duty
  to make adjustments where a disabled person would otherwise be at a substantial
  disadvantage, including adjusting provisions, criteria or practices and
  providing auxiliary aids [12]. The Civil Service Behaviours document invites
  candidates to tell the recruiting manager if they require adjustments, giving
  documents in large print or braille, more time for a test or interview, and
  assistance at an assessment centre as examples [1]. The CVF states that it
  "should be used in ways that allow for differing abilities", including those
  stemming from disabilities or neurodiversity, and that "Reasonable adjustments
  should always be made for people who have disabilities that impact on their
  ability to demonstrate the competencies. This is in line with equality
  legislation" [5]. A written or structured personal evidence record can itself
  function as preparation support for candidates with memory, processing or
  anxiety-related conditions; where retrieval under time pressure is the barrier,
  an adjustment such as advance sight of question themes is the formal route.
- **Assessment standards and individual circumstances.** The CVF directs that
  assessment of competency behaviours takes account of "individual
  circumstances", "the specific requirements of a role" and the "competency
  description (not just the behavioural examples)" [5] — relevant to candidates
  whose evidence comes from atypical routes.
- **Consistency as the fairness mechanism.** ACAS advises asking each applicant
  the same questions where possible to ensure equal treatment, and basing
  decisions on how each applicant met each point in the job description and
  person specification [16]; CIPD frames the same discipline as same questions,
  same order, objective criteria, with independent scoring before group
  discussion [15].
- **Bias barriers the bank interacts with.** Expectancy effect, confirmatory
  information, halo/horns, contrast effect and similar-to-me effect are all named
  barriers to effective assessment, to be countered by ORCE and by assessor
  training [4].
- **Candidate data and third parties.** Employers and recruiters must comply with
  the UK GDPR and the Data Protection Act 2018 when handling candidates'
  personal information, across the whole process "from advertising vacancies
  through to deleting information about candidates" [13]. ACAS adds that
  recordings or notes made in interviews must be stored securely and
  confidentially, in line with data protection rules [16]. A candidate's own
  bank sits outside that employer-facing regime, but it will often contain
  personal information about colleagues, patients, service users or clients, and
  may contain employer-confidential material; anonymising at the point of
  capture is the low-cost protection.

# Risks & Caveats

- **The Strengths Dictionary mapping table is deliberately not reproduced.** The
  official strengths-to-behaviours mapping is laid out in multiple columns and
  scrambles under automated PDF text extraction, producing plausible but wrong
  associations. This document cites only the Dictionary's linear text — the 36
  definitions, the three strength elements, the not-defined-by-grade statement
  and the mapping caption [2] — and does not restate the table. Anyone needing
  the mapping should read page 7 of the Dictionary visually.
- **EPSO's competition model has changed and no longer includes a competency
  interview in the current generalist route.** The current published test list
  for Graduates (Administrators AD5) comprises reasoning tests, an EU knowledge
  test, a digital skills test and a free-text essay, with no oral test listed
  [14]. The competency framework and its anchors remain the definitional target
  [6][7], but a bank built for an EPSO *interview* may be preparing for a stage
  that does not exist in a given competition. The Notice of Competition is the
  authoritative statement for any specific competition; see the sibling document
  `epso-competency-framework`.
- **The ORCE guidance predates the 2024 CVF.** The values-based recruitment and
  selection guidance cited for ORCE, the rating scale and the sergeant/inspector
  calibration example was published in 2018, against the 2016 competency set [4].
  Its *process* content — the ORCE sequence, classify-once, the barrier list, the
  contextualisation principle — is framework-agnostic and is what is cited here;
  its competency *names* are not, and the 2024 framework [5] is used for those.
- **The Civil Service Behaviours document is the 2018 Success Profiles
  publication.** Level descriptors and grade bands are cited from it directly
  [1]; departments may add local guidance that takes precedence for their own
  competitions.
- **The NHS values are cited from the Constitution [8].** Individual trusts
  publish their own values sets and question banks; a bank targeting a specific
  trust should be indexed against that trust's published values as well.
- **The bank format is synthesis.** No source located for this document
  prescribes a field set, a weekly cadence or a coverage-audit method for
  candidate preparation material. Those are this document's coaching layer, built
  on documented scoring mechanics, and should be adapted freely.
- **The classify-once rule is documented for policing assessment.** It is cited
  from College of Policing guidance [4] and is a mainstream assessment-centre
  practice, but this document has not located an equivalent verbatim instruction
  in Civil Service, EPSO or NHS assessor guidance. Treat the principle as
  strongly evidenced for CVF-based assessment and as a reasonable working
  assumption elsewhere.

# Open Questions

- **Is there an equivalent published classify-once instruction in Civil Service
  or EPSO assessor guidance?** Locating one would raise this document's central
  claim from "documented for policing, assumed elsewhere" to "documented across
  markets". Civil Service assessor-facing material is largely internal; an
  FOI-released panel handbook would settle it.
- **Does any official source recommend a preparation cadence or record format?**
  None was located. If a government careers service or professional body
  publishes an evidence-log template, the coaching layer here could be replaced
  with cited guidance.
- **How many distinct examples do panels actually see per candidate?** No
  published data was located on example reuse rates or their effect on scores.
  Occupational-psychology work on evidence volume in structured interviews would
  be valuable and is largely paywalled.
- **What is the shelf life of an example?** Practitioner advice about "recent"
  examples circulates widely but no cited source located for this document
  specifies a recency threshold. Employer-published guidance stating one would
  be worth registering.
- **Do the EPSO written-test anchors reward evidence breadth in the same way?**
  Anchor-based marking survives in the written test, but whether the classify-once
  logic applies to a single essay is unverified.
