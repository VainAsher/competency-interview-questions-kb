# Executive Summary

Competency questions do not arrive in one standard package. In current UK and
EU practice the same underlying question — "show us evidence of this
competency" — is delivered through several documented formats: one-to-one and
panel interviews, telephone screening calls, live and pre-recorded (asynchronous)
video interviews, and assessment centres in which a structured interview sits
alongside group exercises, presentations, in-tray/e-tray exercises and
situational judgement tests. The UK Civil Service documents both
competency-style *behaviour* questions (prepared examples of past actions) and
*strengths* questions (unrehearsed responses about what you do well and enjoy)
within the same Success Profiles framework; EPSO documents structured
competency-based and situational competency-based interviews within EU
selection procedures. Private-sector graduate recruitment leans heavily on
online assessments, on-demand video interviews and (often virtual) assessment
centres, as documented in named employers' public guidance and sector survey
reporting.

This is the Tier-1 foundation document for the Formats category: breadth over
depth. For each format it records what it is, who documents using it, and how
competency questions appear in it — for candidates preparing and for
interviewers weighing the documented pros, cons and fairness considerations of
each format. Per-format deep-dives (panel interviews, video/asynchronous
interviews, assessment-centre exercises) are planned child documents.

Confidence is **Medium**: definitions and process facts rest on official
framework, regulator and professional-body documentation, but claims about how
*prevalent* each format is rest partly on careers-service and sector-survey
reporting, and one EPSO assessment-centre source is an archived snapshot.

# Key Takeaways

- The same competency can be probed in any format; what changes is the
  delivery, the time pressure, and who is watching. The UK Civil Service lists
  interviews, video interviews, assessment centres and job simulations as
  channels for assessing the same Success Profiles elements. *(documented)*
- The Civil Service documents a formal distinction between behaviour questions
  (structured, considered examples) and strengths questions (genuine first
  response, explicitly not to be rehearsed). *(documented)*
- EPSO documents structured competency-based interviews (about 10 minutes per
  competency) and situational competency-based interviews, and an Assessment
  Centre model in which at least two trained assessors observe every exercise.
  *(documented)*
- Graduate-scheme pipelines commonly run online assessment → on-demand video
  interview → assessment centre → final interview; PwC UK documents exactly
  this sequence publicly. *(documented)*
- ACAS advises more than one interviewer where possible to reduce
  discrimination risk, asking each applicant the same questions, and holding
  online interviews to the same standard as in-person ones. *(documented)*
- CIPD guidance is that structured interviews with predefined questions and
  consistent scoring minimise bias, and that panels scoring independently
  before discussion are fairer than lone interviewers. *(documented)*
- Sector reporting on the ISE Student Recruitment Survey 2025 highlights
  undisclosed candidate AI use during interviews as the most commonly reported
  form of assessment misconduct — a live integrity issue for remote formats.
  *(documented)*
- For candidates: prepare STAR-style evidence once, then adapt its delivery to
  the format — compressed for telephone and pre-recorded video, interactive for
  panels, distributed across exercises at an assessment centre. *(guidance)*

# Purpose

This document answers two questions. For candidates: "the invitation says
telephone interview / video interview / assessment centre — what will the
competency questions look like there, and how should my preparation change?"
For interviewers and HR: "which formats are documented in UK and EU practice,
what does authoritative guidance say each is good and bad at, and what fairness
obligations attach to each?" It is the orientation layer for the Formats
category; child documents will go deeper on individual formats.

# Scope

Covered: the documented formats competency interviews take in the UK and EU —
panel interviews; one-to-one structured interviews; telephone screening;
live and pre-recorded (asynchronous) video interviews; assessment centres and
the exercises that surround the interview within them (group exercises,
presentations, in-tray/e-tray, situational judgement tests); and the
strengths-based versus competency/behaviour-based distinction as documented in
Civil Service Success Profiles. UK Civil Service, EPSO and private-sector
graduate-scheme practice all receive visible weight.

Not covered here: question-answering technique (STAR etc. — see
techniques-foundation), competency-by-competency question banks (see
competencies-foundation), full framework descriptions (see
uk-frameworks-foundation and eu-frameworks-foundation), and per-format
deep-dives (planned child documents: panel-interviews,
video-asynchronous-interviews, assessment-centre-exercises). Technical,
case-study and academic interviews are out of scope except where they carry
competency questions.

# Definitions

Narrow, document-specific usages; see the shared glossary for general terms.

- **Panel interview** — an interview conducted by more than one interviewer at
  once, scoring the same candidate against the same criteria.
- **Structured interview** — an interview using a predefined set of questions,
  asked in the same order to all candidates, with responses scored against
  pre-agreed criteria.
- **Asynchronous (pre-recorded) video interview** — an interview with no live
  interviewer: questions are presented on screen and the candidate records
  timed answers on a platform for later review. Also called "on-demand" or
  "recorded" video interview.
- **Assessment centre** — a selection event (in person or virtual) combining
  several tasks — typically an interview plus exercises such as presentations,
  group exercises, written analyses or in-tray/e-tray simulations — observed
  by multiple assessors.
- **Strengths-based interview** — an interview probing what the candidate does
  regularly, does well and finds energising, expecting unrehearsed responses,
  as distinct from a competency/behaviour interview seeking prepared examples
  of past performance.
- **Telephone screening** — an early-stage interview by phone used to filter a
  large applicant pool before later-stage interviews or assessment centres.

# Framework Context

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

The UK Civil Service recruits against Success Profiles, which assess up to five
elements per role: behaviours, strengths, ability, experience and technical
skills [1]. The framework is format-plural by design: behaviours can be
assessed through application forms, judgement and personality tests, an
interview, an assessment centre, or job-related simulations including a
presentation, in-tray exercise, written analysis, group exercise, role-play and
oral briefing [2]. Strengths can be assessed through "interviews, recorded
video interviews, bespoke situational strengths test, simulation assessment,
personality test" [3]. The Civil Service Careers site tells applicants that
after applying they may face online tests, a video interview, a final
interview and an assessment centre, and that video interviews come in two
types: "a regular face-to-face interview in real-time via a video feed" and "a
pre-recorded interview where you will be asked to pre-record answers in
response to interview questions presented to you via an online platform" [4].

EPSO (the European Personnel Selection Office) documents two structured
interview instruments for EU-institution selection: the competency-based
interview (CBI), which evaluates selected general competencies with candidates
"interviewed for about 10 minutes per competency" using questions "structured
to allow the assessors to evaluate the candidate's selected general
competencies" [5], and the situational competency-based interview (SCBI), "an
interview that aims to assess general competencies by asking the candidate how
he/she would react in a specific situation" [6]. EPSO's Assessment Centre
model assesses general and/or specific competencies "by means of different
exercises, the content of which is validated by the selection board: a case
study, an oral presentation, a structured interview, an interview in the
field, an e-tray, etc.", with each exercise observed by at least two trained
assessors; per an archived EPSO FAQ, the non-case-study tests were "normally
held remotely" [7].

In UK private-sector and graduate-scheme practice, formats are documented in
employers' own public guidance: PwC UK's early-careers process runs online
assessment (behavioural test plus cognitive assessment), then an on-demand
video interview, then a virtual assessment centre, then a final "business
assessment" interview with a senior leader [8]. EY UK tells candidates their
"interview experience at EY may be a mix of in-person, video and phone
interviews" [9]. The Institute of Student Employers' Student Recruitment
Survey 2025 gathers selection-practice data from the UK's largest group of
early-careers employers [10]; reporting on it states it drew on 155 employers
who together handled over 1.8 million applications and hired more than 31,000
students [11].

# Core Reference

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## One-to-one and panel interviews

Face-to-face interviews — questioning by "an individual or panel of
interviewers" — remain the most common interview form according to Oxford
University Careers Service, typically lasting at least 30 minutes [14]. In
Civil Service recruitment the final interview "will usually be face-to-face in
person or via video", usually lasts 45–60 minutes, and "you may be asked to
describe specific occasions when you have demonstrated a certain skill or
behaviour" [4]. The same interview may also carry strengths questions: "You
may also be asked questions about what interests you and what you enjoy doing.
These questions will help the interview panel assess your strengths and
preferred style of working" [4]. A Civil Service Careers walkthrough of a
typical campaign describes being "interviewed by 3 people" who ask questions
on the strengths and the behaviours listed in the job advert [4]. ACAS advises
that "if possible, each interview should be carried out by more than 1 person
to reduce the risk of discriminating", and that interviewers should ask each
applicant the same questions where possible [12]. CIPD defines the structured
interview as asking "a predefined set of questions, in the same order, to all
candidates", with responses "scored using consistent criteria against a set of
pre-agreed criteria for each question" [15].

## Telephone screening

Careers-service guidance documents telephone interviews as an early-round
filter: they are "often used by employers in the first round of their
application process to filter large numbers of applicants", with successful
candidates progressing to a face-to-face interview or assessment centre
[14]. Prospects states that a member of the HR team usually conducts the
call at a pre-arranged time, that a telephone interview typically lasts
between 20 and 45 minutes, and that candidates "should expect the same
questions as in a face-to-face interview", listing behavioural examples such
as "Tell me about a time you worked in a team" [13]. Competency questions
therefore appear in this format in compressed, audio-only form.

## Video interviews — live and pre-recorded (asynchronous)

The Civil Service documents the two video-interview types quoted in Framework
Context above [4]. Prospects describes the live type as "similar to a regular
face-to-face interview" conducted over Microsoft Teams or Zoom with an
interviewer or panel in real time, and the pre-recorded type as answering
"pre-recorded or even written questions on screen" by recording an answer
"often to a time limit", which "helps employers who have lots of candidates,
as they can simply watch your answers at a time that suits them"; video
interviews are "often used as an early screening method" [16]. Oxford
University Careers Service notes pre-recorded video interviews "are used by
some large employers, particularly for applications to graduate schemes"
[14]. PwC UK documents its on-demand video interview in detail: it is
"conducted virtually via the SHL platform", with an expected duration of one
hour and a one-week completion window; "you will be presented with a series of
questions on the screen and asked to describe your experience. Instead of
having an interviewer, your answers will be recorded and reviewed afterwards"
[8]. The Success Profiles Strengths guidance explicitly lists "recorded video
interviews" among the ways strengths are assessed [3].

## Assessment centres

CIPD describes assessment centres as requiring candidates "to complete several
different tasks", often combining "behavioural ratings, cognitive and
personality assessments obtained from multiple sources", overseen by trained
selectors [15]. In Civil Service recruitment, "an assessment centre will
usually consist of a combination of different selection methods, such as an
interview, a presentation, a written exercise and/or a role-play exercise"
[4]. Oxford University Careers Service describes centres lasting a half or
full day, combining presentations, team exercises, written tests, interviews
and psychometric tests, and allowing employers "to compare the performance of
lots of candidates at the same time" [14]. PwC UK runs "a virtual immersive
assessment centre" at which the candidate works "with up to five other
candidates", supported by a facilitator [8]. EPSO's Assessment Centre
combines a case study, an oral presentation, a structured interview, an
interview in the field and an e-tray, as set out in the relevant notice of
competition, with candidates "observed/evaluated in a systematic way by at
least 2 trained assessors" during each exercise [7]. The competency interview
is thus one scored exercise among several: within Success Profiles, the same
behaviours may also be evidenced through job simulations such as group
exercises, in-tray exercises and role-plays [2], and CIPD reports that
skill-based assessments (work samples, situational judgement tests, simulation
exercises, assessment centres) "have been found to be better predictors of
performance on the job" than approaches such as unstructured interviews [15].

## Strengths-based versus competency/behaviour-based interviewing

Success Profiles documents both modes side by side. Behaviours are "the
actions and activities that people do which result in effective performance in
a job", and candidates "may be asked to give examples of when you have
demonstrated a particular behaviour" [2]. Strengths are "the things we do
regularly, do well and that motivate us", and the guidance instructs: "Do not
rehearse your answers - because we are looking for your initial response"
[3]. Civil Service Careers renders the contrast operationally: candidates
"answer the strengths questions with your genuine first response" and "answer
the behaviour questions with well structured and considered examples" [4].
Oxford University Careers Service adds that in strengths interviews assessors
attend to "not only what you say but also how you say it", looking for tone,
body language and expression demonstrating genuine motivation, so "there is no
real right or wrong answer" [14].

## Where the ISE survey sits

The ISE Student Recruitment Survey is the sector benchmark for UK
early-careers selection practice; the 2025 edition is available to ISE members
[10]. Public reporting by ISE's joint CEO records its scale (155 employers,
1.8 million applications, 31,000+ hires) and its findings on assessment
integrity in remote formats, discussed under Assessor Perspective below [11].

# Question Bank

How the *same* competency — working with others / teamwork — is probed across
formats, plus the format-defining variants an interviewer would pair with it.
Sourced questions are published by the named source; composed questions were
written for this knowledge base in the style of the cited frameworks and are
not attributed to any employer.

## Telephone screening

1. Tell me about a time you worked in a team. *(sourced: Prospects, "Telephone interviews", June 2025)* [13]
2. Describe a time you had to solve a difficult problem. *(sourced: Prospects, "Telephone interviews", June 2025)* [13]
3. In no more than two minutes, describe the team you currently work in and the part you personally play in how it performs. *(composed)*

## One-to-one and panel competency interviews

1. Tell me about a time when you worked as part of a team to deliver a goal. *(sourced: Oxford University Careers Service, "Types of Interview")* [14]
2. Describe a situation in which you had to persuade someone round to your way of thinking; how did you use your communication skills to convince them? *(sourced: Oxford University Careers Service, "Types of Interview")* [14]
3. Tell me about a time when you demonstrated strong leadership skills. *(sourced: Oxford University Careers Service, "Types of Interview")* [14]
4. You said the team missed its first deadline — what did you, personally, do differently in the week that followed? *(composed — panel-style follow-up probe)*

## Strengths-based interviews

1. When are you at your best? *(sourced: Oxford University Careers Service, "Types of Interview")* [14]
2. How would a close friend describe you? *(sourced: Oxford University Careers Service, "Types of Interview")* [14]
3. How do you feel when working on a disorganised project? What do you do in situations like this? *(sourced: Oxford University Careers Service, "Types of Interview")* [14]
4. When a group task stalls, do you find yourself drawn to restarting the discussion or to quietly fixing the work itself? Which energises you more? *(composed)*

## Pre-recorded (asynchronous) video interviews

1. Describe a time you supported a struggling colleague while still meeting your own deadlines. You have 60 seconds to prepare and two minutes to record your answer. *(composed — timed on-demand format)*

## Assessment centres — structured and situational interview exercises

1. In the scenario pack you have just read, a colleague whose input you depend on has missed two handovers without explanation. How would you approach the situation, and what would you do if it continued? *(composed — in the style of EPSO's situational competency-based interview)*
2. Thinking back to this morning's group exercise: how did you make sure the quieter members of your group contributed to the final recommendation? *(composed — assessment-centre interview drawing on an observed exercise)*

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — guidance, not fact.**

- **Build the evidence once, deliver it per format.** The competency evidence
  underneath a good answer does not change between formats — what changes is
  packaging. Prepare a bank of STAR-structured examples mapped to the
  advertised criteria (see techniques-foundation), then rehearse three
  deliveries: a 90-second compressed version (telephone, pre-recorded video),
  a 3–4 minute interactive version that survives follow-up probing (panel),
  and a headline version you can attach to an exercise debrief (assessment
  centre).
- **Respect the behaviours/strengths boundary.** For behaviour questions the
  documented expectation is considered, structured examples; for strengths
  questions it is your genuine first response, explicitly not rehearsed
  [3][4]. Preparing for a strengths interview therefore means self-knowledge
  work — knowing honestly what energises you — not scripting answers. Forcing
  a STAR story into "When are you at your best?" answers the wrong question.
- **Pre-recorded video: answer the timer as well as the question.** With a
  visible time limit and no interviewer to redirect you, front-load the
  situation and your action; a recruiter reviewing recordings later [8] sees
  your first sentence for every question. Practise recording yourself —
  practitioner guidance stresses rehearsal precisely because the format feels
  unnatural [16].
- **Panels: answer the asker, include the room.** Direct your answer to the
  panel member who asked, glancing to the others; expect a different panellist
  to probe your example. If three interviewers each own different Success
  Profiles elements [4], expect the same story to be mined from different
  angles — do not repeat one example for every element.
- **Assessment centres: the interview is one data point you can steer.** Where
  a centre combines an interview with group exercises and simulations [4][7],
  assessors triangulate. Consistency matters more than any single star
  performance: claiming collaboration in the interview and dominating the
  group exercise is the visible failure mode.
- **Telephone: compensate for the missing channel.** With no visual feedback,
  signpost structure verbally ("the situation was… what I did was… the result
  was…") and keep answers inside a few minutes — screening calls are short and
  question-dense [13].

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

- ACAS advises interviewing with more than one person where possible "to
  reduce the risk of discriminating", asking each applicant the same questions
  where possible, holding online interviews "in the same way as any interview
  you hold in person", obtaining consent before recording, and storing notes
  and recordings securely and confidentially [12].
- CIPD's selection guidance holds that structured interviews — predefined
  questions in a fixed order, scored against pre-agreed criteria — are "an
  effective way to minimise bias and ensure there is a level playing field for
  candidates from marginalised groups"; it further reports research findings
  that a panel interviewing together produces fairer and more accurate results
  than multiple one-to-one interviews (citing Huffcutt, Culbertson and
  Weyhrauch, 2013), and that interviewers should score independently before
  discussing candidates to avoid being influenced by senior colleagues (citing
  the Behavioural Insights Team, 2017) [15].
- CIPD also recommends combining methods — supporting interview insights with
  other data such as psychometric tests or task simulations — and reports that
  skill-based assessments out-predict unstructured interviews for job
  performance [15].
- EPSO's Assessment Centre model builds multi-assessor scoring in by design:
  every exercise is observed "in a systematic way by at least 2 trained
  assessors", exercise content is validated by the selection board, and final
  results rest on the evaluation of each competency across exercises [7]. Its
  interview instruments are explicitly structured: about 10 minutes of
  questioning per competency in the CBI [5], and situation-anchored
  questioning in the SCBI [6].
- Remote formats have a documented integrity problem. Reporting on the ISE
  Student Recruitment Survey 2025 records that undisclosed or unpermitted
  candidate AI use during interviews was the most commonly reported form of
  assessment misconduct (cited by 61% of respondents), that only 15% of
  employers reported never suspecting or identifying cheating, and that the
  share encountering cheating frequently doubled year on year from 7% to 15%
  [11]. PwC UK publicly documents counter-measures: periodic reviews
  "comparing candidate video interview responses, assessing if transcripts are
  identical and proactively looking for duplicate accounts", with
  disqualification where the process is undermined [8].
- A documented advantage of recorded formats for assessors: the recording can
  be re-watched rather than relying only on notes [16] — with the
  corresponding data-protection duties noted under Legal & Fairness below
  [12][17].

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — guidance, not fact.**

- **Candidates: treating every format as the same interview.** Reciting a
  rehearsed STAR script at a strengths interview collides with the documented
  instruction not to rehearse [3]; improvising at a behaviour interview wastes
  the documented invitation to bring considered examples [2][4].
- **Candidates: ignoring the clock in asynchronous video.** Recorded formats
  impose hard time limits [8][16]; answers that spend ninety seconds on
  scene-setting get cut off before the action and result.
- **Candidates: performing to the wrong audience at assessment centres.**
  Competing with other candidates in a group exercise reads badly when the
  competency being scored across exercises is collaborative [7][15].
- **Candidates: treating a telephone screen as informal.** It is a scored
  interview carrying the same competency questions as later rounds [13].
- **Interviewers: unstructured "chat" interviews.** Dropping the predefined
  question set and scoring criteria removes exactly the features CIPD
  identifies as bias-reducing [15], and diverging from consistent questions
  cuts against ACAS guidance on treating applicants the same way [12].
- **Interviewers: lone interviewing and groupthink scoring.** Interviewing
  alone where a second interviewer is feasible, or discussing candidates
  before independent scoring, both run against the cited guidance [12][15].
- **Interviewers: bolting a format on without adjusting for access.** Each
  format shifts the adjustment burden differently (technology, time limits,
  travel, group settings); the duty to make reasonable adjustments applies to
  the recruitment process, whatever the format [18][19].

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.**

1. **Format-transfer drill.** Take one teamwork example. Deliver it three
   ways this week: (a) recorded on your phone with a 2-minute cap and one
   retake maximum, (b) spoken aloud on a phone call to a friend who interrupts
   with two follow-up probes, (c) written as a 90-second exercise-debrief
   answer. Compare against the behaviours descriptors you are targeting [2].
2. **Strengths honesty pass.** Without preparation, answer aloud: "When are
   you at your best?" and "How do you feel when working on a disorganised
   project?" [14]. Then check: did your answer describe something you do
   regularly, do well and find motivating — the documented definition of a
   strength [3]?
3. **Mock panel.** Recruit two friends; give each a different competency and
   the same scoring sheet; have them score you independently before comparing
   notes, mirroring the independent-scoring practice CIPD describes [15].
4. **Tech rehearsal for video.** Practitioner guidance is unambiguous that
   platform, camera, lighting and timing should be tested days before, not on
   the day [16]. Do a full dress-rehearsal recording and review it.
5. **Interviewer drill: structure audit.** Before your next interview round,
   write the question set and scoring criteria down and fix the order; after
   the round, check what percentage of what you actually asked was on the
   sheet. Compare your practice against ACAS and CIPD guidance [12][15].

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

- **Reasonable adjustments.** Under the Equality Act 2010, section 20, the
  duty to make reasonable adjustments comprises three requirements: where a
  provision, criterion or practice, a physical feature, or the lack of an
  auxiliary aid puts a disabled person at a substantial disadvantage,
  reasonable steps must be taken to avoid the disadvantage or provide the aid
  [18]. GOV.UK guidance for employers gives interview-stage examples:
  allowing "wheelchair users to have their interview on the ground floor" and
  "candidates to complete a written test using a computer" [19]. ACAS
  guidance likewise expects employers to ask interviewees about adjustment
  needs, giving examples such as accessible venues, extra time for written
  tests, and a sign-language interpreter [12]. Every format in this document
  — telephone, video platform, timed recording, group exercise — is a
  "provision, criterion or practice" to which the duty can attach [18].
- **Consistency as fairness.** ACAS links asking each applicant the same
  questions to treating applicants in the same way [12]; CIPD links
  structure, panels and independent scoring to reduced bias and a "level
  playing field" [15].
- **Candidate data.** Interview notes and recordings are personal data:
  ACAS requires securely and confidentially stored notes and recordings, and
  explicit consent before recording an online interview [12]. The ICO's
  employment-practices guidance on recruitment and selection sets out how UK
  GDPR and the Data Protection Act 2018 apply across the whole recruitment
  process — from advertising through to deleting candidate information —
  including the handling of sensitive information such as health data, and
  distinguishes what employers *must* (law), *should* (expected practice) and
  *could* (options) do [17]. Recorded and platform-mediated formats
  (asynchronous video, virtual assessment centres) therefore carry documented
  data-protection obligations alongside their logistical advantages [17].
- **Automated decision-making in recruitment.** In March 2026 the ICO
  published *Recruitment rewired*, a report and draft guidance on the fair and
  responsible use of automation in recruitment, based on evidence gathered
  from over 30 employers that spoke to the ICO voluntarily between March 2025
  and January 2026 [20]. Among the uses it records are the automated scoring
  of candidates' competencies and skills from transcriptions of in-person or
  video interviews, and the evaluation of the language, tone and content of
  candidates' speech in video interviews to predict their personality types;
  the ICO states that it did not see examples of employers using AI to process
  biometric data, such as emotion detection in video interviews [21]. For
  candidates, the ICO states that where decisions are made without a real
  person involved, jobseekers have the right to be told that automated
  decision-making is being used and how the tools work, to a fair outcome for
  which the ICO expects employers to test regularly for bias, and to contest
  the decision and request review by a real person [22].
- **EU selection context.** EPSO's model embeds procedural fairness through
  structure: selection-board-validated exercise content and a minimum of two
  trained assessors per exercise [7].

# Risks & Caveats

- **Prevalence claims are the weak layer.** Which format is "most common" and
  how many employers use video interviews rests on careers-service and
  sector-survey reporting [11][13][14][16], not on official statistics; the
  underlying ISE 2025 survey report is member-only [10], so its figures are
  cited here through named secondary reporting.
- **EPSO sources are in flux.** EPSO's web estate has migrated (epso.europa.eu
  redirects to eu-careers.europa.eu), several legacy pages now 403/404 for
  automated tools, and the Assessment Centre FAQ cited here is a 2022
  Internet Archive snapshot [7]; EPSO's selection model has been undergoing
  reform, so the exercise mix for any given competition is governed by its
  notice of competition [7]. Treat competition-specific detail as
  time-sensitive.
- **Employer processes change yearly.** PwC's and EY's process pages [8][9]
  describe current cycles as of the access date; graduate-scheme stages,
  platforms and timings are routinely redesigned — reporting on the ISE 2025
  survey notes a third of employers had already redesigned selection processes
  in response to generative AI [11].
- **Named-employer detail is illustrative, not exhaustive.** PwC and EY are
  cited because their guidance is public and verifiable, not because they are
  the only or the typical users of these formats.
- **Bot-blocked source.** The CIPD factsheet [15] serves browsers but blocks
  automated link checkers; a human should verify it periodically.

# Open Questions

- Per-format depth: panel dynamics and scoring mechanics, asynchronous-video
  platform practice (retakes, AI scoring, adjustments), and
  assessment-centre exercise design are deferred to the planned child
  documents (panel-interviews, video-asynchronous-interviews,
  assessment-centre-exercises).
- Reliable, citable UK prevalence statistics per format (e.g., what share of
  employers use pre-recorded video at first stage) — would be resolved by
  access to the full ISE Student Recruitment Survey 2025 dataset or an
  equivalent open dataset.
- The current, post-reform shape of EPSO competitions (which retain an
  Assessment Centre stage, and in what form) — would be resolved by citing a
  current notice of competition in the eu-frameworks deep-dive.
- **Resolved 2026-07-20** — whether any UK regulator guidance specifically
  addresses algorithmic/AI scoring of recorded video interviews beyond the
  ICO's general recruitment guidance [17]. It now does: the ICO's March 2026
  *Recruitment rewired* report [20] covers automated scoring in recruitment,
  including scoring from video-interview transcriptions and the evaluation of
  candidates' speech to predict personality types [21]. Summarised under Legal
  & Fairness above; the per-format treatment belongs in the planned
  video-asynchronous-interviews document. **Still open:** whether any regulator
  addresses the *scoring methodology* itself — model validation or accuracy
  expectations, as distinct from transparency, human-involvement and
  bias-testing duties — and whether an EU-level equivalent to the ICO report
  exists for EU employers.
