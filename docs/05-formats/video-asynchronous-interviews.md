# Executive Summary

"Video interview" names two quite different assessments. A **live** video
interview is a conventional interview carried over a video link: real people,
real time, follow-up questions, visible reactions. An **asynchronous**
(pre-recorded, on-demand) video interview has no interviewer present at all —
a question appears on screen, a preparation timer runs, and the candidate
records a time-boxed answer that is reviewed later [1]. The UK Civil Service
documents exactly this two-type split in its candidate guidance [1], and
Cabinet Office guidance for delegated-grade (EO–Grade 6) recruitment sets out
when live video interviewing is acceptable and what panels must check before
using it [2].

The asynchronous format is now routine in UK high-volume and graduate
recruitment, and named employers publish its mechanics. KPMG UK's published
application process places a video interview at stage 3, in which candidates
"will be presented with 6 pre-recorded questions asked by our AI avatar Leah",
are "given a set time to prepare and then record" their responses, and whose
"recorded answers will be reviewed and scored by a member of our Student
Recruitment team" [21]. Deloitte UK's published job-simulation stage asks
candidates to "provide written responses and record video responses", and its
preceding immersive online assessment contains questions that are "'time
factored', meaning the time you take to respond is considered as part of your
performance score for that area" [22]. That is the defining tension of the
format: the delivery is automated, but the human judgement may or may not be.

The regulatory picture changed materially in March 2026. The Information
Commissioner's Office published *Recruitment rewired*, a report on automated
decision-making (ADM) in recruitment based on evidence from over 30 employers
gathered between March 2025 and January 2026 [4]. Among the automated uses it
recorded were "scoring candidates' competencies and skills from written
responses to interview questions and transcriptions of in-person or video
interviews" and "evaluating the language, tone and content of candidates'
speech in video interviews to predict their personality types" — while noting
that the ICO "did not see examples of employers using AI to process biometric
data, such as emotion detection in video interviews" [5]. Its headline finding
is that many employers believe they have human oversight when in practice they
are making solely automated decisions [6].

Confidence is **Medium**. The legal, regulatory and Civil Service layers rest
on primary sources (ICO, Cabinet Office, legislation.gov.uk, EPSO) and are
strong. The *mechanics* of the format — how much think time, how many retakes,
how long each answer runs — rest largely on careers-service and named-employer
publications, which vary by platform and change yearly. Claims about the
accuracy of AI scoring are deliberately **not** made in the evidence layer:
vendor accuracy claims are marketing unless independently evidenced, and the
ICO itself records that "many automated recruitment tool providers market their
tools as fairer and less biased than human recruiters" without endorsing that
marketing [9].

# Key Takeaways

- Live and asynchronous video are different assessments, not two settings of
  one thing. The Civil Service documents both: "a regular face-to-face
  interview in real-time via a video feed" and "a pre-recorded interview where
  you will be asked to pre-record answers in response to interview questions
  presented to you via an online platform" [1]. *(documented)*
- Cabinet Office guidance says it is "acceptable to conduct live video
  interviews online" and that panels must check the technology "is available,
  accessible and compatible for all panel members and candidates", and
  "consider if any reasonable adjustments are required, arising either from
  access to technology or a range of other conditions" [2]. *(documented)*
- Asynchronous interviews are time-boxed by design: careers-service and
  practitioner guidance describes a set response time per question with an
  on-screen timer, commonly one to three minutes, and a completion window
  usually of about a week [19][21]. *(documented)*
- Retake policy is employer- and platform-specific and must be checked before
  starting: some employers allow multiple retakes, others a single attempt
  [19][21]. *(documented)*
- Automation of delivery is not automation of scoring. KPMG UK publishes an
  AI avatar delivering the questions with answers "reviewed and scored by a
  member of our Student Recruitment team" [21]; the ICO's test for whether a
  decision is "solely automated" turns on whether a human has real authority,
  discretion and competence to alter it [6]. *(documented)*
- Candidates subject to solely automated decisions have safeguard rights: to
  be informed, to make representations, to obtain human intervention, and to
  contest the decision [8][10]. *(documented)*
- The reasonable-adjustment duty applies to the recording platform, the timer
  and the format itself. The ICO found employers "typically provided reasonable
  adjustments for people with disabilities who weren't able to access
  automated recruitment methods", often a phone interview [9]; the Equality
  Act 2010 s.20 duty attaches to any provision, criterion or practice [12].
  *(documented)*
- For candidates: rehearse to the clock, not to the page. With no interviewer
  to redirect you and no follow-up question coming, every answer must be
  self-contained — result stated inside the time limit, not implied.
  *(guidance)*
- For interviewers: the asynchronous format removes probing, so the question
  set must do the probing. A question that depends on a follow-up to yield
  scoreable evidence will not yield it here. *(guidance)*

# Purpose

This document answers two questions that the Formats foundation deliberately
left open.

For candidates: "the invitation says on-demand / pre-recorded video interview —
what actually happens, how much time will I get, can I re-record, is a machine
scoring me, and how do I change a STAR answer that works in a room into one
that works into a camera?"

For interviewers and HR: "if we deliver competency questions asynchronously,
what does published UK guidance require of us — on accessibility, on scoring,
and on the data-protection consequences of automated scoring — and how should
the question set change when no one can probe?"

It goes deeper than `formats-foundation`, which established that video
interviews exist in two types and that PwC-style pipelines use them. It does
not restate that ground.

# Scope

Covered: the live/asynchronous distinction and what changes between them; the
documented mechanics of asynchronous delivery (on-screen question, preparation
time, response timer, retakes, completion window); named published UK employer
examples (KPMG UK, Deloitte UK) and Civil Service guidance; what UK regulators
and public guidance actually say about AI and automated scoring of recorded
answers; the accessibility and fairness duties that attach to the format in the
UK (Equality Act 2010 s.20; Cabinet Office guidance) and the EU (EPSO's
reasonable-accommodations procedure); a question bank suited to self-contained
asynchronous delivery; and coaching on camera technique, pacing and structure.

Not covered: general STAR technique (see `techniques-foundation`); panel
dynamics and live-interview scoring mechanics (see `panel-interviews`);
assessment-centre exercise design including virtual centres (see
`assessment-centre-exercises`); the graduate pipeline as a whole (see
`uk-graduate-schemes`); and telephone screening, covered in
`formats-foundation`.

Deliberately excluded: vendor claims about the predictive validity or accuracy
of specific AI interview-scoring products. No such claim is made in the
evidence layer of this document, because none could be verified against an
independent, citable source. Named platforms are recorded only where a careers
service names them as being in use [21].

# Definitions

Document-specific narrowings; see the shared glossary for general terms.

- **Asynchronous (pre-recorded, on-demand) video interview** — an interview
  with no interviewer present, in which questions are presented on screen and
  the candidate records timed answers on a platform for later review [1].
- **Live video interview** — a real-time interview conducted over a video link
  with one or more interviewers present [1].
- **Preparation (think) time** — the interval between a question appearing and
  recording beginning, during which the candidate may plan but not record.
- **Response window / timer** — the maximum recording length for a single
  answer, typically displayed as an on-screen countdown [21].
- **Retake** — a permitted re-recording of an answer, replacing the previous
  attempt. Availability and count are set by the employer or platform [19][21].
- **Time-factored scoring** — scoring in which the time taken to respond forms
  part of the performance score, as published by Deloitte UK for its immersive
  online assessment [22].
- **Automated decision-making (ADM)** — as used by the ICO in this context,
  "decisions based solely on automated processing of personal information that
  produce legal or similarly significant effects on individuals" [4].
- **Meaningful human involvement** — under UK GDPR article 22A, involvement
  such that "a human can exercise real influence over [a] decision before it is
  applied and has the authority, discretion and competence to alter it"; its
  absence makes a decision solely automated [6].

# Framework Context

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## The UK Civil Service position

Civil Service candidate guidance states that "Some recruitment stages include a
video interview, rather than over the phone or in person", and names the two
types: "a regular face-to-face interview in real-time via a video feed" and "a
pre-recorded interview where you will be asked to pre-record answers in
response to interview questions presented to you via an online platform" [1].
The same guidance states that "Reasonable adjustments can be made for any
element of the assessment process" [1]. Within Success Profiles, "recorded
video interviews" are listed among the methods by which the Strengths element
may be assessed [3].

Cabinet Office guidance on applying the Success Profile Guides during delegated
grade (EO–Grade 6) recruitment carries a dedicated **Video Interviewing**
section. It states that "It is acceptable to conduct live video interviews
online, where candidates and multiple panel members join the interview at a
predetermined time, and this format is compatible with Strengths-based
assessment", and that this "may be preferable in some cases, for example if
panel members and the candidates are in multiple locations or you require the
ability to record or view interviews live" [2]. It then imposes two duties on
the panel: "it is important that the panellists engage in a pre-discussion to
agree the running of the interview and who will be leading on different
questions", and "it is also essential that checks are made to ensure that the
chosen technology is available, accessible and compatible for all panel members
and candidates taking part, and the panel should consider if any reasonable
adjustments are required, arising either from access to technology or a range
of other conditions" [2].

Two things are notable about that Cabinet Office text. First, it addresses
**live** video interviewing only; central delegated-grade guidance does not
authorise or describe asynchronous pre-recorded interviewing as a Civil Service
panel method, even though the candidate-facing site tells applicants they may
encounter one [1][2]. Second, it places the accessibility check *before* the
interview and frames it as covering both technology access and "a range of
other conditions" [2].

## The EU position

EPSO runs its selection tests online and operates a published
reasonable-accommodations procedure: candidates indicate their needs "in the
application form and provide supporting documents that explicitly describe the
nature of their disability and justify the requested accommodations", with
acceptable documentation including "medical certificates", "disability
certificates" and "specialist reports" [14]. Each request is "assessed
individually by the EPSO Accessibility Team", based on "the candidate's
functional limitations", "the type of test and testing method", "the actions
required during the test" and supporting documentation; accommodations "are
granted on a case-by-case basis" and "final arrangements may differ from the
initial request" [14]. EPSO's structured interview instruments themselves — the
CBI and SCBI — remain live, assessor-observed exercises rather than
asynchronous recordings (see `formats-foundation` and
`eu-frameworks-foundation`).

The ICO's *Recruitment rewired* report (published March 2026, based on evidence
from over 30 employers gathered between March 2025 and January 2026) records
that employers "tended to employ automated recruitment early in the recruitment
process, before direct human interaction", for tasks including "scoring

# Core Reference

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## What actually happens in an asynchronous interview

The documented sequence is: a question is presented on screen; a preparation
interval runs; the candidate records an answer against a limit; the recording
is submitted; the next question appears. Civil Service guidance describes the
candidate being "asked to pre-record answers in response to interview questions
presented to you via an online platform" [1]. KPMG UK publishes the
preparation-then-record structure directly: candidates "will be given a set
time to prepare and then record your responses" to six questions, with 40
minutes to allow for the stage overall [21].

Oxford University Careers Service documents that candidates receive "a specific
amount of time to respond to each question", that employers set completion
deadlines, and that some platforms "allow you to do a practice question and/or
re-record your answers and choose the one you think is best" [19]. targetjobs
records that "You are usually given a short time of between one and three
minutes to answer each question, with a timer on screen indicating how long you
have left", and that "There will also be a deadline by which you must complete
a pre-recorded interview – this is usually within a week" [23]. The same
guidance names Sonru, SparkHire and HireVue as services used for pre-recorded
video interviews [23].

## Retakes are not guaranteed

Retake policy is set per employer and per platform, and the published guidance
is explicit that candidates must check before starting: "Before you start a
pre-recorded video interview, make sure to check whether you are able to
re-record responses and, if so, how many retakes you get. Some employers allow
you multiple retakes, others only a single attempt" [23]. Oxford's guidance
similarly treats practice questions and re-recording as things "some" platforms
allow, not defaults [19].

## What the format removes

Oxford University Careers Service states the defining constraint directly:
"There is no opportunity for the interviewer to ask for clarification" [19].
National Careers Service guidance for pre-recorded formats tells candidates to
"imagine you are talking to a real person" precisely because no one is there
[17]. Practitioner guidance records that in a pre-recorded interview the
candidate "will not be speaking to anyone" and "will not have any visual clues
from an interviewer to encourage you" [19][23].

The employer-side rationale is equally documented: recruiters use pre-recorded
formats "to save time and resources in the early stages of the assessment
process, especially if they are recruiting large numbers of graduates" [19],
and pre-recorded interviews are "used by some large employers, particularly for
applications to graduate schemes" [20].

## Time as a scored variable

Deloitte UK publishes that within its immersive online assessment "Some
questions are 'time factored', meaning the time you take to respond is
considered as part of your performance score for that area" [22]. This is a
documented instance of the clock forming part of the score rather than merely
bounding the answer. Deloitte's job-simulation stage separately requires
candidates to "record video responses" alongside written responses and ranking
tasks [22]. Note the limit of this evidence: Deloitte publishes time-factoring
for the *immersive online assessment*, and publishes video recording for the
*job simulation*; it does not publish that its recorded video responses are
themselves time-factored.

## Camera-adapted delivery: what published guidance instructs

National Careers Service guidance instructs candidates to "find a quiet space
with no interruptions", to ensure "good natural light or use a lamp so that the
interviewer can see you well", to use a computer or laptop with the camera at
eye level, to "keep up good eye contact – look at your camera rather than at
the screen", to "sit up straight and have good body language", and to "smile
and look interested" [17]. For pre-recorded interviews specifically it advises
practising "in front of a mirror or with a friend", and to "check out if you
can stop or re-record any of your answers if something goes wrong" [17]. Oxford
adds: look directly at the camera, speak clearly, and practise under identical
conditions before submitting [19].

## AI and automated scoring — what is actually published

This section records only what named, citable sources state.

**What employers publish.** KPMG UK publishes AI *delivery* with human
*scoring*: an AI avatar asks the six questions; "your recorded answers will be
reviewed and scored by a member of our Student Recruitment team" [21].

**What the regulator observed.** The ICO's report records that automated
scoring of interview content does occur in the UK market, specifically "scoring
candidates' competencies and skills from written responses to interview
questions and transcriptions of in-person or video interviews", and
"evaluating the language, tone and content of candidates' speech in video
interviews to predict their personality types" [5]. It also records that it did
**not** observe emotion detection or other biometric processing in video
interviews among the employers it engaged [5].

**Where the line falls legally.** The ICO's key finding is "that many employers
engaging in automated recruitment are likely relying on solely automated
decisions as part of this process. This means they're using solely automated
systems without meaningful human involvement, and the decisions these systems
take have legal or similarly significant effects on people" [4]. The test is
whether "a human can exercise real influence over [the] decision before it is
applied and has the authority, discretion and competence to alter it", as
against "simply directly applying the outcome of an automated system without
meaningful consideration" [6]. The ICO found that employers commonly claimed
human oversight via a dashboard review, but "could not consistently demonstrate
how they had mitigated the risk of their hiring managers relying
disproportionately on the scores when faced with a high volume of applications"
— with lower-scoring candidates in effect "rubber-stamping" [6]. In its
worked use cases, a tool that "analyses candidates' responses to a series of
interview questions" and ranks them *is* out of ADM scope where a recruiter
"goes through all the scores and profiles, including the responses, to weigh up
the tool's recommendations" and makes the final call [7].

**On vendor accuracy claims.** The ICO records, without endorsing, that "many
automated recruitment tool providers market their tools as fairer and less
biased than human recruiters" [9]. Its own public-perceptions research found
people "were particularly wary of more profiling-based automation, such as
online behavioural assessments" [4]. No independently evidenced accuracy figure
for AI scoring of recorded competency answers was located for this document;
see Open Questions.

## Sector context on AI in recruitment

The Institute of Student Employers' public 2025 statistics record that 79% of
employers were redesigning or reviewing recruitment because of AI, with
one-third redesigning selection processes and 46% reviewing; that 62% expect to
use AI within five years and 70% anticipate increased automation; and that
"Only 15% use AI exclusively in gamified assessments" [16]. On the candidate
side, the most commonly reported form of assessment misconduct was "candidates
using AI during interviews without disclosure or permission (61%)", and "The
proportion of employers who frequently encountered cheating more than doubled
this year, rising from 7% last year to 15%" [16]. Reporting by ISE's joint CEO
sets the same findings alongside the survey's scale and its integrity theme
[25]. Undisclosed candidate AI use is a remote-format problem in particular:
an unattended recording session is the easiest place in a pipeline to use an
undisclosed tool.

# Question Bank

Questions suited to **asynchronous delivery** — that is, self-contained
questions that yield scoreable evidence without any follow-up, because no
follow-up is possible [19]. Sourced questions are published by the named
source; composed questions were written for this knowledge base in the style of
the cited frameworks and are not attributed to any employer. No question here
is a real or leaked employer question.

## Published behavioural questions that stand alone on camera

Each of these is self-contained: it names the situation *and* the required
reflection in one prompt, so the candidate has no reason to wait for a probe.

1. Describe a time when you had to resolve a difficult problem on your own. How did you go about it? *(sourced: University of Bath HR, "Behavioural questions for interviewing")* [24]
2. Tell me about a time when a problem arose and your manager was unavailable. How did you handle the situation, and how did things turn out? *(sourced: University of Bath HR, "Behavioural questions for interviewing")* [24]
3. Describe a time when you had to build a team. How did you go about building the team, and was it successful in achieving its goals? *(sourced: University of Bath HR, "Behavioural questions for interviewing")* [24]
4. Tell me about a time when a project you were in charge of fell behind schedule. What did you do about it? *(sourced: University of Bath HR, "Behavioural questions for interviewing")* [24]
5. Tell me about a time when you worked with a colleague who was not completing their share of work. What did you do? *(sourced: University of Bath HR, "Behavioural questions for interviewing")* [24]
6. Tell me about a situation where you had to adjust quickly to changes over which you had no control. What strategies did use to handle the situation? *(sourced: University of Bath HR, "Behavioural questions for interviewing")* [24]
7. Give me an example of a high-pressure situation you have faced this past year and how you resolved it. *(sourced: University of Bath HR, "Behavioural questions for interviewing")* [24]
8. Tell me about a time when you had to make an unpopular decision. How did you manage the situation? *(sourced: University of Bath HR, "Behavioural questions for interviewing")* [24]
9. Tell me about a time when you felt you had not performed to the best of your ability. What did you do about it? *(sourced: University of Bath HR, "Behavioural questions for interviewing")* [24]

11. Tell me about a time when you worked as part of a team to deliver a goal. *(sourced: Oxford University Careers Service, "Types of Interview")* [20]
12. Describe a project that you planned or organised. What did you do to make it a success? *(sourced: Oxford University Careers Service, "Types of Interview")* [20]
13. Tell me about a time when you demonstrated strong leadership skills. *(sourced: Oxford University Careers Service, "Types of Interview")* [20]

## Composed prompts written for timed asynchronous delivery

Written in the style of the cited frameworks. Each carries its own timing
instruction, mirroring the published prepare-then-record structure [21], and
each is closed — the result is requested inside the prompt so no probe is
needed.

1. You have 30 seconds to prepare and two minutes to record. Describe a time you had to deliver work to a deadline you did not set. Say what you changed about how you worked, and what the outcome was. *(composed)*
2. You have 30 seconds to prepare and 90 seconds to record. Tell us about a time you disagreed with a decision made by someone more senior than you. Cover what you said, how you said it, and what happened next. *(composed)*
3. You have one minute to prepare and two minutes to record. Describe an occasion when you noticed a problem that was not your responsibility. What did you do, and what was the measurable effect? *(composed)*
4. In no more than two minutes: describe a time you had to explain something technical or unfamiliar to someone who needed to act on it. Tell us how you checked they had understood. *(composed)*
5. In no more than 90 seconds: tell us about a time you changed your approach because of feedback. Name the feedback, the change, and the difference it made. *(composed)*
6. You have two minutes. Describe a situation where you had to work with limited information. Explain how you decided to proceed and how the decision turned out. *(composed)*
7. You have two minutes. Tell us about a time you supported a colleague who was struggling while still meeting your own commitments. Include what you gave up to do it. *(composed)*
8. In no more than two minutes: describe a time your first attempt at something did not work. Tell us what you did second, and what you would do first if it happened again. *(composed)*
9. You have 90 seconds. Tell us about a time you had to prioritise between two things that both mattered. Explain your reasoning and the result. *(composed)*

## Adapting a STAR answer to a two-minute recording — drill prompts

These are rehearsal prompts, not employer questions. Use them against a single
prepared example.

1. Record the same teamwork example three times — at 60, 90 and 120 seconds — and identify what you cut each time. Which cut lost the most evidence? *(composed)*
2. Record your chosen example with the Result stated in the **first** sentence, then again with the Result last. Play both back: which version survives being stopped at 90 seconds? *(composed)*
3. Record a version in which the Situation and Task together take no more than 20 seconds. Did the Action get more or less specific? *(composed)*
4. Record your answer to a question you have not read in advance, with 30 seconds of preparation. Compare its structure to your rehearsed version. *(composed)*
5. Record one answer with a single retake permitted and one with unlimited retakes. Compare how long each took in total and which recording you would actually submit. *(composed)*
6. Record an answer, then watch it with the sound off. Where is your eye-line, and for how long is it on the camera rather than the screen? *(composed)*

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — guidance, not fact. It points back to the evidence above and
introduces no new facts.**

- **Front-load the result, then earn it.** The documented constraint is a hard
  timer with no clarifying question available [19][23]. A room-paced STAR
  answer spends its first thirty seconds on context and relies on the
  interviewer to say "and what did *you* do?". Nobody will. State the outcome
  in the first sentence — "I turned a two-week slip into an on-time delivery" —
  then use the remaining time to show the situation, your action and the
  evidence. If the recording is cut at the limit, the scoreable claim is
  already on tape.
- **Budget the time out loud in rehearsal.** Against a one-to-three-minute
  window [23], a workable split for a two-minute answer is roughly 20 seconds
  Situation and Task, 60–70 seconds Action, 25–30 seconds Result and
  reflection. Practise to a visible timer, because the published format shows
  you one [23].
- **Use the preparation interval as a structure interval, not a script
  interval.** The published structure gives "a set time to prepare and then
  record" [21]. In 30 seconds you cannot write a script, but you can write four
  words: the example's name, your one action, the number that proves it, and
  the reflection. Reading a script to camera is visible; four words are not.
- **Answer the whole prompt, including the sub-question.** Several published
  asynchronous-suitable questions carry two or three parts — "How did you
  handle the situation? Would you do anything differently…?" [24]. In a live
  interview a forgotten sub-question gets re-asked. Here it is simply
  unanswered evidence. Name the parts as you go: "First, how I handled it…
  Second, what I'd change…".
- **Talk to the lens, not the preview window.** The instruction is explicit:
  "look at your camera rather than at the screen" [17], and Oxford's guidance
  is to look directly at the camera [19]. Watching your own preview reads on
  the recording as looking away. Cover or minimise the self-view.
- **Manufacture the missing audience.** With no visual cues from an interviewer
  [19], energy drops. The published counter-measure is to "imagine you are
  talking to a real person" and to practise in front of a mirror or with a
  friend [17]. Some candidates place a photograph or a sticky-note face beside
  the lens; the point is to keep the delivery conversational rather than
  recited.
- **Decide your retake rule before you start, not during.** Because retake
  allowances differ and may be a single attempt [19][23], set a personal rule
  in advance — for example, "I re-record only if I stopped mid-sentence or gave
  the wrong example" — and check the platform's allowance on the instructions
  screen first. Burning three retakes chasing a perfect first answer is the
  common way to arrive at the last question with none left.
- **Treat pace as content where time is scored.** Deloitte publishes that some
  questions are time-factored [22]. Where an employer says so, a long silence
  before answering is not neutral. Where an employer does not say so, do not
  assume it.
- **Interviewers: put the probe inside the question.** Since no follow-up is
  possible [19], write questions that request the result and the reflection
  explicitly, as the published question banks do [24]. A question ending "…tell
  me about a time you led a team" will return a story; the same question ending
  "…and what did the team deliver that it would not have delivered otherwise?"
  will return evidence.
- **Interviewers: publish the mechanics up front.** KPMG and Deloitte both
  publish stage structure, question count and expected duration [21][22].
  Candidates who know the shape of the assessment spend their preparation time
  on content rather than on guessing the format — and unpublished mechanics
  disadvantage exactly the candidates with the least insider knowledge.

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## How recorded answers are said to be scored

KPMG UK publishes that "your recorded answers will be reviewed and scored by a
member of our Student Recruitment team" — a named human function, reviewing
recordings asynchronously [21]. Deloitte UK publishes that its job-simulation
stage combines ranking tasks, written responses and recorded video responses,
and that candidates receive a feedback report which "isn't the outcome of your
assessment" [22]. Within the Civil Service, recorded video interviews are
listed as a method of assessing the Strengths element [3], and Cabinet Office
guidance notes that recording or viewing interviews live is one reason a panel
might prefer video [2].

## What automated scoring of recordings looks like in practice

The ICO's engagement with over 30 employers records these automated uses
relevant to recorded interviews: "scoring candidates' competencies and skills
from written responses to interview questions and transcriptions of in-person
or video interviews", and "evaluating the language, tone and content of
candidates' speech in video interviews to predict their personality types"
[5]. It records no observed use of emotion detection or other biometric
processing in video interviews [5].

## The oversight test assessors are held to

The ICO's report sets out what human review must amount to if a decision is not
to count as solely automated. A human must: assess and review the decision "at
an appropriate point to ensure they have an actual impact on whether a
candidate is selected or eliminated"; have "the ability to influence the
recruitment decision"; have "the discretion and … authority to alter" it; have
"the qualifications and training to disagree with the tool's recommendations or
predictions and can overturn them"; and not "attach disproportionate weight to
the tool's recommendations" [7]. Where multiple candidates are similar, "a
human makes the decision about who to interview (although they can consider the
software's recommendations)" [7].

Its findings on real practice are blunt. Employers "mainly based" their claim
of human involvement on hiring managers reviewing scores on a dashboard, but
"the evidence we saw indicated that, in practice, employers were using the
tools to make solely automated decisions" [6]. In the ICO's worked case study,
a manager who prioritises "green" candidates and "only take[s] a glance at
'red' candidates before rejecting them" has "'rubber-stamped' the 'red'
candidates' rejections", which "constitutes solely ADM within the scope of
article 22" [6]. The ICO further found the treatment inconsistent *within* a
single process: "A successful candidate will have their score, profile and
responses weighed up by a hiring manager. Another candidate may be rejected …
based on a low score generated by an automated process" [6]. Its stated
expectation is that where meaningful human involvement is used, "they must
ensure that it is applied consistently to all candidates within a hiring stage"
[4].

## Bias monitoring expected of employers using these tools

The ICO states that "Organisations are responsible for the algorithms and tools
they use to process personal information, even if they didn't build the
software or create the algorithm", and that when procuring a tool they could
conduct "trials to ensure that results limit bias" and request from developers
"the demographic groups a model was trained on", "whether any underlying bias
has been detected or may emerge" and "any algorithmic fairness testing that it
has conducted" [9]. It recorded good practice among employers who "asked
developers about their own bias testing as part of procurement", "conducted
trials to check that results limited bias" and ran "ongoing monitoring of their
tools and outputs, such as dashboards that provided ongoing bias monitoring and
monthly bias reviews" [9]. Its third headline recommendation is that
"employer's should expand and adopt good practice in monitoring for fairness
and bias" [4].

## Consistency and structure duties that carry over from live interviews

ACAS guidance applies to online interviews the same standard as in-person ones:
employers should "hold the interview in the same way as any interview you hold
in person", check "the candidate has a reliable method of communication", have
"a backup plan in case of connectivity issues", and — where recording — "get
the person's permission first" [15]. Recordings and notes must be stored
"securely and confidentially" in line with data-protection rules [15].

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — guidance, not fact.**

- **Candidates: rehearsing a room answer and delivering it to a camera.** The
  format removes the interviewer's clarifying question [19]; an answer built to
  be probed arrives incomplete and stays incomplete.
- **Candidates: spending the timer on setup.** Against a one-to-three-minute
  window [23], thirty seconds of "so, a bit of background about the team…" is a
  quarter to a half of the answer, before any evidence.
- **Candidates: chasing perfection with retakes.** Where retakes are limited or
  single [19][23], repeated re-recording of early questions leaves nothing for
  a genuinely spoiled later one — and later answers are recorded under more
  fatigue, not less.
- **Candidates: reading.** The preparation interval is short by design [21];
  eyes tracking a script are visible to a reviewer watching the recording [21].
- **Candidates: looking at themselves.** The published instruction is to look
  at the camera rather than the screen [17][19]; the self-view is the most
  common reason candidates fail it.
- **Candidates: assuming silence is free.** Where an employer publishes
  time-factored scoring [22], deliberation is scored; assuming that everywhere,
  or nowhere, are both errors.
- **Candidates: undisclosed AI assistance.** Sector data records undisclosed
  candidate AI use during interviews as the most commonly reported form of
  assessment misconduct, at 61%, with employers frequently encountering
  cheating rising from 7% to 15% year on year [16]. Unattended recording is
  precisely where this is looked for.
- **Interviewers: writing live questions and delivering them asynchronously.**
  A question whose evidence depends on a probe returns no probe-able evidence
  here [19]; the published banks that work asynchronously carry the follow-up
  inside the question [24].
- **Interviewers: calling dashboard review "human oversight".** The documented
  regulatory position is that a glance-and-reject is a rubber stamp and
  constitutes solely automated decision-making [6]; the safeguards then apply
  whether or not the employer intended them to.
- **Interviewers: applying human review only to strong candidates.** The ICO's
  expectation is consistency "to all candidates within a hiring stage" [4];
  reviewing recordings only above a score threshold creates precisely the
  inconsistency it identifies [6].
- **Interviewers: pointing candidates at the vendor's privacy notice.** The ICO
  records this as poor practice "because it suggests that accountability for
  decisions lies with the provider. In reality, accountability lies with the
  employer using the tool" [8].
- **Interviewers: leaving adjustments to the platform.** Cabinet Office
  guidance places the accessibility and adjustment check on the panel, before
  the interview, covering both technology access and other conditions [2].

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.**

1. **The 90-second cut.** Take one prepared STAR example. Record it at 120
   seconds, then re-record at 90, then at 60. Write down what you removed at
   each step. Anything you removed twice was never load-bearing; anything you
   fought to keep is your core evidence. Rebuild your standard answer around
   it.
2. **Result-first rewrite.** Record your strongest example twice: once ending
   with the result, once opening with it. Play both back and stop each at 75
   seconds. Which one has already made its case? Adopt that ordering as your
   default for timed formats [19][23].
3. **Cold-start drill.** Have a friend pick three questions at random from the
   published banks above [20][24] and read one to you. Take exactly 30 seconds,
   then record for two minutes with no retake. Repeat daily for a week. This
   rehearses the actual published structure — prepare, then record [21].
4. **Eye-line audit.** Record any two-minute answer, then watch it muted.
   Count the seconds your eyes are on the lens versus the screen. Cover your
   self-view and repeat; compare [17][19].
5. **Sub-question checklist.** Take three multi-part published questions [24].
   Before recording, write the parts as a numbered list on paper beside the
   camera. Afterwards, tick off which parts you actually answered. Most
   candidates miss the reflection clause the first time.
6. **Dress rehearsal against the real constraints.** Test the platform, camera,
   microphone, lighting and background days ahead, not on the day [17]. Do one
   full run — quiet room, camera at eye level, lamp on your face — and review
   it as an assessor would.
7. **Interviewer drill: probe-removal test.** Take your live competency
   question set. For each question, ask: "if the candidate gives a thin answer,
   what would I ask next?" Then rewrite the question so that the follow-up is
   already inside it. Anything you cannot rewrite that way is not an
   asynchronous question [19][24].
8. **Interviewer drill: oversight audit.** For your last recruitment round,
   sample ten rejected candidates from below your tool's threshold. Can you
   evidence that a trained human reviewed each recording or response and could
   have overturned the outcome? If not, you are inside the ADM provisions and
   the article 22C safeguards apply [6][7][8].

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## Reasonable adjustments

Under the Equality Act 2010, section 20, where a provision, criterion or
practice, a physical feature, or the absence of an auxiliary aid puts a
disabled person at a substantial disadvantage, reasonable steps must be taken
to avoid the disadvantage or to provide the aid [12]. An asynchronous video
interview is a provision, criterion or practice in that sense: the platform,
the preparation interval, the response timer, the retake allowance and the
requirement to appear on camera are all features to which the duty can attach
[12]. GOV.UK guidance for employers gives recruitment-stage examples of
adjustments, including allowing candidates "to complete a written test using a
computer" [13].

Central Civil Service guidance is unusually explicit for a video format.
Cabinet Office delegated-grade guidance requires that "checks are made to
ensure that the chosen technology is available, accessible and compatible for
all panel members and candidates taking part", and that "the panel should
consider if any reasonable adjustments are required, arising either from access
to technology or a range of other conditions" [2]. The candidate-facing Civil
Service guidance states that "Reasonable adjustments can be made for any
element of the assessment process" [1]. ACAS guidance expects employers to ask
applicants whether they need adjustments to take part [15].

The ICO's report supplies an observed practice worth naming: it "found that
employers typically provided reasonable adjustments for people with
disabilities who weren't able to access automated recruitment methods. This
often involved a phone interview" [9]. An alternative-format route is therefore
documented as normal practice, not an exceptional concession.

In the EU context, EPSO operates a published accommodations procedure for its
online tests, assessed case by case by its Accessibility Team against the
candidate's functional limitations, the test type and testing method, and the
actions required during the test [14].

## Automated decision-making and candidate rights

Where scoring of recorded answers is solely automated — no meaningful human
involvement — the UK GDPR ADM provisions apply [4][6]. The ICO states the
safeguards plainly: a person "has a right to be informed about the decisions
made about them", "is given the opportunity to make representations about such
decisions", "is given the opportunity to obtain human intervention (sometimes
referred to as 'human review')", and "is able to contest a decision" [8]. Its
candidate-facing guidance puts the same rights as: a fair outcome, the right to
know if ADM is being used, and the right to "challenge a decision and ask for a
'human review'" [10].

Transparency obligations bite at three points: "when they first collect
people's information", "when people ask for their information" (subject access)
and "when they engage in ADM" [8]. In a recruitment context the ICO says the
information "does not need to be complex or include material that could allow
candidates to 'game the system'", but should cover "how they use the tool to
make decisions", "how accurate the tool is" and "what safeguards are in place"
[8]. It records as poor practice employers who refer candidates to the tool
provider's privacy information rather than their own, since accountability
"lies with the employer using the tool" [8].

The ICO also confirms that ADM in hiring is lawful where justified: "Yes,
employers can use ADM in the hiring process if they have a valid reason. In
fact, the law has changed to make it easier for organisations to innovate and
use automation in this way", provided safeguards are in place [10]. Alongside
the report, the ICO "wrote to 16 organisations likely to be using ADM to make
decisions about jobseekers, and they have now committed to acting on our
recommendations to improve practices" [10].

## Recordings as personal data

ACAS requires permission before recording an interview, and that notes and
recordings are stored securely and confidentially in line with data-protection
rules [15]. The ICO's employment-practices guidance on recruitment and
selection sets out how UK GDPR and the Data Protection Act 2018 apply across
the whole recruitment process, including the handling of sensitive information
[11]. A recorded video interview is a richer personal-data artefact than an
interview note — it contains image and voice — so retention, access and
deletion decisions carry correspondingly more weight [11][15].

## Fairness monitoring

Fairness, in the ICO's framing, "means organisations should handle personal
information in ways people reasonably expect" and not "in ways that have
unjustified adverse effects on people" [9]. Where an employer cannot show that
it periodically reviews a tool's decisions "to check for accuracy, fairness or
bias", nor tracks how often human reviewers overturn low scores, the ICO's
assessment is that "people are likely to be having their information processed
unfairly" [9]. See `fairness-bias-legal` for the wider treatment.

# Risks & Caveats

- **Format mechanics are the weak evidential layer.** Preparation time, timer
  length, retake allowance and completion window vary by employer and platform.
  The one-to-three-minute figure and the roughly one-week window are
  careers-service reporting [19][23], not a standard. Always read the
  employer's own instruction screen.
- **Named-employer processes change annually.** KPMG's six-question AI-avatar
  stage and Deloitte's job simulation are as published at the access date
  [21][22]; graduate pipelines are routinely redesigned — ISE records 79% of
  employers redesigning or reviewing recruitment because of AI [16].
- **The ICO material is recent and partly in draft.** *Recruitment rewired* was
  published in March 2026, is based on "voluntary engagement, not an audit or
  investigation", and its accompanying ADM guidance was in consultation [4].
  Treat the report as authoritative for the ICO's stated expectations and its
  observations of the market, not as settled case law.
- **No AI-accuracy claim is made here, by design.** Vendor claims about the
  accuracy or fairness of AI interview scoring are marketing unless
  independently evidenced; the ICO records that such marketing exists without
  endorsing it [9]. Nothing in this document should be read as evidence that
  automated scoring of recorded competency answers is or is not accurate.
- **Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence.** The ICO's finding that it
  saw no emotion detection in video interviews [5] describes the 30+ employers
  who engaged voluntarily; it is not a statement about the whole UK market.
- **Civil Service asynchronous practice is under-documented.** Candidate-facing
  guidance says pre-recorded interviews may occur [1], but the Cabinet Office
  delegated-grade guidance addresses live video interviewing only [2]. This
  document does not claim a central Civil Service standard for asynchronous
  interviewing, because none was located.
- **Bot-blocked and fetcher-hostile sources.** `prospects.ac.uk` (a common
  source for video-interview advice) is a confirmed repeat block for automated
  checkers and is not cited here; EPSO's remotely-proctored-testing page
  returned 403 to automated fetchers and is likewise not cited. Civil Service
  Careers and ICO pages required a browser user-agent to read, though they
  link-check normally.

# Open Questions

- Is there any published, independently evidenced measure of the accuracy or
  adverse impact of AI scoring applied to recorded competency answers in the
  UK? None was located. Resolution: an ICO enforcement outcome, an EHRC
  investigation, or peer-reviewed validation work with named tools.
- Does any UK employer publish that a recorded competency answer is scored
  **solely** by an algorithm with no human review? None was found — KPMG
  publishes the opposite [21]. Resolution: employer transparency notices
  published under the ADM safeguards [8].
- Does the Civil Service operate asynchronous video interviewing centrally, and
  under what guidance? Candidate guidance implies it exists [1]; central
  delegated-grade guidance covers live video only [2]. Resolution: a
  departmental recruitment pack or a Cabinet Office update.
- What retake and think-time defaults do the major platforms named by careers
  services (Sonru, SparkHire, HireVue) actually apply, and are they
  configurable per employer? Resolution: platform documentation citable without
  a vendor marketing frame.
- Do EU institutions use asynchronous video interviewing anywhere in EPSO or
  agency selection? No evidence located; EPSO's documented interview
  instruments are live [14] (see `eu-frameworks-foundation`).
- How are reasonable adjustments to *timers* handled in practice — extra
  preparation time, longer response windows, additional retakes? The duty
  clearly attaches [2][12], but no published schedule of standard video-format
  adjustments was located.
