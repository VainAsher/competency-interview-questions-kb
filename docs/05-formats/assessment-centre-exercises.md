# Executive Summary

An assessment centre is not one long interview. It is a multi-exercise,
multi-assessor event in which the *same* competencies are sampled several
different ways, and the competency interview is only one of the scored
exercises. This document is the deep-dive on everything *around* that
interview: group and team exercises, in-tray and e-tray simulations, written
exercises and case studies, prepared and unseen presentations, role-plays with
trained actors, and situational judgement tests where they form part of the
selection day. For each it records what the exercise is, which competencies it
is documented as targeting, how it is observed and scored, and how long it
runs.

The strongest published evidence in this space is UK public-sector. The
College of Policing publishes, exercise by exercise, exactly which Competency
and Values Framework (CVF) competencies each of its national exercises
assesses — a written exercise scored on *respect and empathy*, *we analyse
critically*, *we support and inspire* and *we collaborate*, and a twelve-question
briefing exercise scored on *public service*, *we are emotionally aware*, *we
take ownership* and *we are innovative and open-minded* — together with the
timings, the preparation rules and the automatic-fail policy [2]. Its
values-based recruitment guidance publishes the assessor-side machinery in
equal detail: the **ORCE** model (observe, record, classify, evaluate), an A–D
competency rating scale with descriptors, a catalogue of assessor biases with
countermeasures, and the finding that arithmetic (score-based) decision making
outperforms group discussion [4]. Cabinet Office SCS guidance publishes an
assessment matrix mapping each Success Profiles element to each exercise, and
instructs panels to "observe, record, classify, evaluate and score each
question independently" before agreeing an overall score [5]. Private-sector
practice is documented far more thinly: employers name their stages — Deloitte's
job simulation and final-stage topic discussion [18], KPMG's half-day Launch
Pad [19], Unilever's Discovery Centre [20], Barclays' multi-stage centre [21] —
but rarely publish exercise briefs or scoring criteria.

Confidence is **Medium**. The exercise-by-exercise competency mappings,
timings, rating scales and assessor process rest on official College of
Policing, Cabinet Office, GOV.UK and EPSO documentation. But the generic
taxonomy of exercise types — assigned versus unassigned group roles,
in-tray prioritisation heuristics, planned versus on-the-spot presentations —
rests substantially on named university careers services rather than on
employer or regulator documents, and several named employers publish stage
names without exercise detail.

# Key Takeaways

- The Civil Service documents six named job-related simulations through which
  behaviours may be assessed: presentation, in-tray exercise, written
  analysis/exercise, group exercise/discussion, role-play and oral briefing
  [1]. Every exercise in this document maps to that list. *(documented)*
- The College of Policing publishes a **per-exercise competency map**: its
  written exercise assesses four named CVF competencies and values, its
  briefing exercise assesses a different four, and its competency-based
  interview a third set of five [2]. Exercises are deliberately not
  interchangeable — each samples a different slice of the framework.
  *(documented)*
- Assessment centres are defined by professional-body guidance as a
  **multi-rater, multi-method** approach — multiple exercises, multiple
  assessors, several dimensions — whose validity depends on candidates
  receiving "the same types and numbers of tasks to complete within the same
  timeframe" [4][12]. *(documented)*
- The published assessor discipline is **ORCE**: observe, record (verbatim,
  no interpretation), classify into competencies only after the candidate has
  left, then evaluate — each stage carried out independently of other assessors
  before any discussion [4]. Cabinet Office SCS guidance uses the same
  observe/record/classify/evaluate sequence for interview panels [5].
  *(documented)*
- **Arithmetic decision making beats discussion.** College of Policing guidance
  states that ranking candidates by score "has been shown to be the most valid"
  decision method, while assessors discussing performances and deciding
  together "have been shown to be less effective... and are difficult to
  justify from a fairness point of view" [4]. *(documented)*
- In-tray and e-tray exercises are the *same* exercise in different media, and
  are scored on prioritisation and justification rather than on reaching a
  single correct answer: "there is no one right answer, the important thing is
  to provide logical and clearly explain the reasons for your decisions" [14].
  *(documented)*
- Group exercises come in two documented shapes: **unassigned/leaderless**,
  where candidates get "individual briefing documents, with different
  information to each other" and must reach a joint decision, and **assigned
  role**, where a candidate is asked to chair or lead [15]. The competency being
  sampled is different in each. *(documented)*
- For candidates: contribution quality, not airtime, is what is recorded. The
  observation unit is a behaviour classified against a named competency [4], so
  three well-evidenced interventions score better than continuous talking that
  classifies into nothing. *(guidance)*
- For designers: the CVF indicators alone are "not sufficient" for a complex
  exercise — you must write exercise-specific assessor guidance derived from a
  pilot with good performers, and it must be guidance, "not a 'model answer'"
  [4]. *(documented)*

# Purpose

This document answers two questions.

For **candidates**: "the invitation says assessment centre — what are the
exercises that are not the interview, what competency is each one actually
measuring, and how do I prepare for each without over-rehearsing?" It gives
published exercise briefs, timings, the observable behaviours assessors are
told to record, and drills.

For **interviewers, HR and assessment designers**: "if I am building or running
a selection day, what does documented UK/EU practice say about exercise choice,
competency-to-exercise mapping, assessor training, rating scales, scoring
mechanics and the biases the design has to defend against?" The Assessor
Perspective section is written to be usable as a design checklist, because the
College of Policing and Cabinet Office publish enough of their own machinery to
support one.

# Scope

**Covered:** the non-interview exercises that surround a competency interview at
an assessment centre, in UK and EU public and private practice — group and team
exercises (assigned and unassigned roles); in-tray and e-tray exercises;
written exercises and case studies; presentations, both prepared in advance and
set on the day; role-plays and simulations with role actors; and situational
judgement tests where they form part of the selection day. For each: what it
is, which competencies it targets, how it is observed and scored, and how long
it runs. Also covered: the assessor-side mechanics that make an exercise
scoreable — ORCE, rating scales, exercise-specific assessor guidance, assessor
bias, and score-based versus discussion-based decisions.

**Not covered:** the competency interview itself as a format (see
`formats-foundation` and `panel-interviews`); asynchronous video mechanics (see
`video-asynchronous-interviews`); STAR and other answer techniques (see
`techniques-foundation`); and the **EPSO assessment-centre day end-to-end**,
which is the subject of `eu-institutions-assessment-centres`. EPSO material
appears here only as a comparator for exercise *design*, cross-referenced
rather than repeated. Ability and psychometric testing is covered only where it
runs inside the assessment day. Technical and academic-panel exercises are out
of scope except where they carry competency scoring.

# Definitions

Narrow, document-specific usages; see the shared glossary for general terms.

- **Exercise** — one scored, time-bounded activity within an assessment centre,
  producing behavioural evidence against one or more named competencies.
- **Work sample test** — an exercise that "allows the assessors to see the
  candidate in action in an activity that represents a sample of the role",
  as distinct from an interview where the candidate talks about past or
  hypothetical action [4].
- **Unassigned-role (leaderless) group exercise** — a group task in which no
  candidate is designated leader and candidates typically hold different
  briefing information from one another [15].
- **Assigned-role group exercise** — a group task in which a candidate is
  explicitly asked to chair a meeting or lead the group [15].
- **In-tray / e-tray exercise** — a simulation of the administrative flow of a
  role: a set of emails, memos, reports, messages and documents to read,
  prioritise and act on within a time limit. The two terms describe the same
  exercise, paper-based versus computer-based [14][16].
- **Briefing exercise** — an exercise in which the candidate takes a role, is
  given preparation material, and responds verbally to a structured set of
  questions about the situation; the College of Policing runs a twelve-question,
  three-part version nationally [2].
- **Role actor** — a trained person who plays a character (customer, colleague,
  member of the public) in a role-play, observed by a separate assessor [4][15].
- **ORCE** — observe, record, classify, evaluate: the linear assessor process
  used to convert observed behaviour into a competency rating [4].
- **Exercise-specific assessor guidance** — a document translating generic
  competency indicators into observable, exercise-specific evidence statements,
  produced by piloting the exercise with existing good performers [4].
- **Adverse impact** — a selection method producing a minority-group pass rate
  materially below the majority rate; commonly evaluated using the 80 per cent
  (four-fifths) rule, which "does not have a legal footing" in the UK [4].

# Framework Context

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## Where exercises sit in UK Civil Service Success Profiles

Success Profiles is explicitly exercise-plural. GOV.UK guidance on Civil
Service Behaviours lists the ways behaviours may be assessed as including an
application form requiring written examples, a Civil Service judgement test, a
personality test, an interview and an assessment centre — and names six
job-related simulations: a **presentation**, an **in-tray exercise**, a
**written analysis/exercise**, a **group exercise/discussion**, a **role-play**
and an **oral briefing** [1]. Behaviours may also be assessed through
situational judgement tests or situational interview questions asking how a
candidate would behave in hypothetical scenarios [1]. Civil Service Careers
tells candidates that an assessment centre "will usually consist of a
combination of different selection methods, such as an interview, a
presentation, a written exercise and/or a role-play exercise" [8].

At Senior Civil Service level the exercise mix is published as an explicit
matrix. Cabinet Office guidance for HR Director recruitment sets out a
sifting stage, a pre-interview leadership assessment comprising an **Individual
Leadership Assessment (ILA)** and a **Staff Engagement Exercise (SEE)** — both
of which "are not scored", with findings and suggested probing questions shared
with the interview panel — and an interview assessment which "will also
includes a 5-minute presentation covering a set topic related to the
candidate's leadership experience" [5]. The matrix maps which Success Profiles
element each exercise carries: technical and behaviours at sift; behaviours at
the staff engagement exercise; technical, behaviours, strengths and experience
at interview and presentation [5].

## Where exercises sit in the police Competency and Values Framework

The College of Policing runs a national, multi-exercise assessment process for
police constable recruitment and publishes the competency mapping for each
exercise. For initial police recruitment "the competencies review criteria is
set at level 1" [2][3]. The online assessment process consists of three
exercises — a competency-based interview, a **written exercise** and a
**briefing exercise** [2] — preceded by a sift which is either in-force or the
**national sift**, itself made up of a situational judgement test and a
behavioural styles questionnaire [3].

| Exercise | CVF competencies and values assessed | Published timing |
|----------|--------------------------------------|------------------|
| Competency-based interview | courage; public service; respect and empathy; we take ownership; we are innovative and open-minded [2] | ~40 minutes; five questions; 60 s thinking + 300 s answer each [2] |
| Written exercise | respect and empathy; we analyse critically; we support and inspire; we collaborate [2] | ~10 min reading + ~40 min work, hard cap 120 minutes [2] |
| Briefing exercise | public service; we are emotionally aware; we take ownership; we are innovative and open-minded [2] | ~60 minutes total: ~10 min untimed preparation, then ~50 min of 12 questions in three parts, 60 s thinking + 180 s answer each [2] |
| Situational judgement test (national sift) | we analyse critically; we are innovative and open-minded; we take ownership; we are emotionally aware; we collaborate; we support and inspire [3] | untimed, expected ~45 minutes [3] |
| Behavioural styles questionnaire (national sift) | the six SJT competencies plus respect and empathy; courage; public service [3] | untimed, expected ~20 minutes [3] |

Note that these names are the **2024 CVF** names ("we collaborate", "we support
and inspire"), not the superseded 2016 wording — see Risks & Caveats.

## Where exercises sit in EU-institution selection

EPSO's Assessment Centre assesses general and/or specific competencies "by
means of different exercises, the content of which is validated by the
selection board: a case study, an oral presentation, a structured interview, an
interview in the field, an e-tray, etc.", with candidates "observed/evaluated
in a systematic way by at least 2 trained assessors" in each exercise [6].
EPSO publishes complete candidate assignment booklets for some exercises; the
booklet for the situational competency-based interview in open competitions
EPSO/AD/365-370/19 states that the exercise "is designed to assess the
following competencies: Analysis & Problem Solving, Learning & Development,
Prioritising & Organising, Resilience, Working with Others, and Leadership",
that "no previous knowledge is required", and that "the content of this
document is fictitious" though "derived from actual events" [7]. The
competency labels in that 2020 booklet predate EPSO's 2023 competency
framework — the EU-side treatment belongs to `eu-institutions-assessment-centres`
and is not restated here.

## Where exercises sit in UK graduate schemes

Named employers publish stage structures rather than exercise briefs.
**Deloitte** publishes a four-stage assessed process whose third stage is a
**Job Simulation** using work-based scenarios in which candidates "rank options
in order of preference" and give "written responses and record video
responses", assessing behavioural skills, and whose final stage combines a
~30-minute **topic discussion** ("a two-way discussion") with a ~50-minute
skills and motivation interview; the topic discussion requires preparation
"researching and conducting analysis on a topic" [18]. **KPMG** publishes a
half-day, in-person **Launch Pad** that "combines assessed and non-assessed
activities" at which candidates demonstrate the "skills needed to succeed in
your role", with outcomes issued within two working days [19]. **Unilever**
publishes a **Discovery Centre** described as "a 'day in the life' of a Future
Leader at Unilever... filled with several exciting activities including project
meetings, business challenges, a team meeting with Unilever colleagues, and an
interview" [20]. **Barclays** documents an assessment centre of "two or three
stages" including "a motivational interview with a member of the leadership
team", at which candidates demonstrate "the relevant strengths, behaviours or
technical skills required for the role" [21]. The **NHS Graduate Management
Training Scheme** runs online tests followed by interviews and an assessment
centre assessing "communication, numeracy, planning and analytical thinking
skills" [22].

Careers-service reporting places the typical centre at "a half or full day",
combining presentations, team exercises, written tests, interviews and
psychometric tests, and lets employers "compare the performance of lots of
candidates at the same time" [17].

# Core Reference

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## The assessment centre as a method

Assessment centres are "a multi-rater, multi-method approach... candidates are
given multiple exercises or opportunities to demonstrate their suitability for
the role and are assessed by multiple people in the course of that assessment
across several dimensions — usually competencies" [4]. In practice they "often
look like a combination of several different work-sample type exercises, eg, a
presentation, a written report and a roleplay exercise" [4]. The documented
trade-off is explicit: "well-designed assessment centres can have very good
validity but they are time consuming and costly and need to be carefully
designed to ensure they are accurate and valid predictors of performance"; the
major benefit is "a very thorough and detailed assessment of each candidate in
multiple scenarios and by different people, thereby being more likely to see
the candidate's real or typical performance than a singular one-off interview
or test" [4]. CIPD describes them as requiring candidates "to complete several
different tasks", often combining "behavioural ratings, cognitive and
personality assessments obtained from multiple sources", and requires that
tasks be "administered in a systematic way, with candidates being given the
same types and numbers of tasks to complete within the same timeframe, so that
they have equal opportunity to demonstrate their abilities" [12].

The underlying logic of the non-interview exercises is the **work sample**:
"while interviews allow a candidate to talk about things they have done or
things they may do in the future, a work sample test allows the assessors to
see the candidate in action in an activity that represents a sample of the
role" — an analysis task for an analytical role, a community engagement
exercise for a police officer [4]. Work sample exercises "often assess task
performance or skill as well as the demonstration of competencies or values (eg,
a teaching task may assess empathy and communication skills, as well as
accurately describing the subject matter)" [4]. CIPD reports that skill-based
assessments "have been found to be better predictors of performance on the job
when compared with traditional approaches such as tests, reviewing job
experience, education, or unstructured interviews" [12].

## Group and team exercises

Two documented structures. In an **unassigned-role (leaderless)** group
exercise, candidates receive "individual briefing documents, with different
information to each other" and must "make a group decision, whilst using
negotiation and compromising skills" [15]. In an **assigned-role** exercise,
organisations test leadership by asking a candidate to "chair a meeting or lead
a group", with assessors observing delegation, leveraging team strengths,
monitoring progress, maintaining focus and ensuring decisions are reached [15].
Other documented group formats include ice-breakers intended to help candidates
relax, practical problem-solving tasks with physical items, and discussions on
industry-relevant topics [15]. University of York guidance describes the same
family — discussions on employer-relevant topics, current affairs or news
items, which "may involve case studies or assigned leadership roles", and group
tasks such as "a practical activity (eg building a tower out of straws) or
problem solving task based on a workplace scenario; it could involve role play"
[16].

The observation model is that "assessors observe a small group of candidates
and may 'score' candidates against a list of skills or attributes they are
looking for", attending to teamwork, collaboration, communication,
problem-solving, originality of ideas and the ability to work cooperatively
"without dominating" [16].

Group exercises are the natural home of the Civil Service "group
exercise/discussion" simulation [1] and of collaboration-family competencies —
the College of Policing scores *we collaborate* in its written exercise and
across its national sift [2][3].

## In-tray and e-tray exercises

E-tray and in-tray exercises "are essentially the same thing, with the only
difference being that one is computer-based while the other is paper-based",
and are "usually given to an individual at an assessment centre, however they
can sometimes be given as a group exercise" [14]. Candidates receive
"background information on the company, your role within it and a scenario"
along with materials such as "memos, emails, telephone messages, reports and
letters" [14]. University of York guidance describes the same design: the
exercise simulates "the administrative aspects of the job. You are given a
range of material which could include emails, letters, notes and phone
messages", and "you must decide on your priorities for responding to these and
deal appropriately with each item. You may need to draft replies and will need
to justify your reasoning" [16]. Kent's guidance adds that candidates are given
their role, an organisational chart and objectives, and must "prioritise the
items in order" — analysing reports, drafting replies, delegating tasks —
within a time limit [15].

**What is assessed.** Kent lists the competencies as "prioritisation, analysis,
working under pressure, problem solving, adaptability, organising, decision
making and communication" [15]. LSE lists the assessed abilities as absorbing
information "quickly and accurately", prioritising "this information and any
arising actions", and planning and making decisions "within a time constraint",
alongside time management, analytical thinking, written communication and
commercial awareness [14]. York adds reading comprehension and performance
under pressure [16].

**How it is scored.** LSE's guidance is explicit that "there is no one right
answer, the important thing is to provide logical and clearly explain the
reasons for your decisions" [14]. The published prioritisation cues are the
sender of the item, when it was written, whether deadlines are mentioned,
confidentiality markings, dependencies on other tasks, and business impact;
the recommended sequence is most urgent tasks, then urgent non-trivial tasks,
then trivial tasks [14].

**How long.** LSE's worked example is a 90-minute task in which candidates
should "spend no more than 15 minutes" on the initial read of the materials
[14].

## Written exercises and case studies

The College of Policing publishes a fully specified national written exercise:
the candidate takes "the role of a police constable and complete[s] an urgent
written task for your line manager", is "provided with four items of
information", and is instructed to "only use the information provided and do
not add or invent any information"; all answers go "in the same response box"
[2]. The instructions take approximately 10 minutes to read, the exercise
"should take approximately 40 minutes to complete", and a hard limit of 120
minutes applies, with whatever is in the text box at time-out being submitted
[2]. Two design decisions are published and unusually candid: **spelling and
grammar are not assessed** ("we appreciate candidates will be completing the
written exercise on different devices and some may have access to spell-check
while others may not... to ensure a fair approach, we will not be assessing
candidates' spelling or grammar"), and **pasting text in from another
application "will not be accepted and may result in an automatic failure"** [2].

Case studies are documented as individual or group exercises requiring analysis
of real-world problems with "qualitative and quantitative data", in which
candidates construct arguments supporting their conclusions, "sometimes
presented or submitted as written recommendations", assessing analysis,
problem-solving, communication, commercial awareness, working under pressure
and decision making [15]. York describes the case study as "based on a
fictional situation, simulating the kind of business problem you are likely to
come across in the role you have applied for. You are given a set of
documents... and a problem to solve", with output as "a written report and/or a
presentation, and can expect questions on your recommendations"; assessors
attend to analytical thinking, logical reasoning, commercial awareness and the
ability to justify recommendations persuasively [16]. EPSO lists a case study
as one of its Assessment Centre exercises [6].

## Presentations — prepared and unseen

Two documented modes. **Planned** presentations give advance instructions and
research time and typically run 5–10 minutes on industry-related topics;
**on-the-spot** presentations assign the topic at the assessment centre and
evaluate "performance under pressure and verbal communication skills" [15].
York records that candidates "may prepare talks in advance or be assigned
topics at the assessment centre", with assessors attending to communication,
time management, delivery under pressure, body language and the ability to
respond to questions [16].

The Civil Service publishes a concrete prepared-presentation specification at
Director level: a **5-minute presentation covering a set topic related to the
candidate's leadership experience**, delivered within a blended interview that
assesses experience, technical skills, behaviours and strengths [5]. Deloitte's
final stage includes a ~30-minute **topic discussion** — "a two-way discussion"
with an assessor asking questions — for which candidates must prepare by
"researching and conducting analysis on a topic" [18]. EPSO runs an oral
presentation as a named Assessment Centre exercise [6].

## Briefing exercises and oral briefings

The Civil Service names "oral briefing" as a simulation type [1]. The College
of Policing publishes a national example in full. The candidate takes the role
of a police constable "and will have responsibility for dealing with some
issues presented to you", provides verbal responses to questions about those
issues, and is given preparation materials, using only the information provided
[2]. The structure: approximately 10 minutes of untimed, unassessed preparation
on part one, then approximately 50 minutes across three parts of four questions
each — 12 questions in total — with new information introduced at parts two and
three, and for every question 60 seconds of thinking time and 180 seconds to
answer [2]. Each question is presented both on screen and read aloud by a
pre-recorded assessor video [2]. Candidates are told to "consider the issues
raised and how the police can build positive relationships" [2].

## Role-plays and simulations

Role-plays give the candidate "a brief" outlining the situation and their
assigned role and a "limited amount of time to prepare"; an actor plays a
character such as a customer or colleague while a separate assessor observes,
with communication and negotiation skills the primary focus, particularly for
customer-facing roles [15]. The College of Policing's worked design example is
a role-play on a diversity topic, and it shows the exercise-specific assessor
guidance side by side with the generic CVF indicators — for *we are emotionally
aware* (level 2), the observable evidence statements include "candidate
specifically asks the role actor to challenge their thinking", "candidate
addresses the role actor's inappropriate language about Mr Walker" and
"candidate communicates sensitively and warmly but changes style to challenge
role actor's inappropriate behaviour immediately and firmly when required" [4].

Simulations may also be delivered remotely. Deloitte's Job Simulation presents
work-based scenarios in which candidates rank options by preference and submit
written and recorded video responses [18].

## Situational judgement tests within the selection day

The College of Policing publishes its national SJT design in full. The test
"assesses your judgement and decision-making skills in job-relevant
situations"; each scenario presents four possible actions, and candidates rate
each action on a five-point effectiveness scale whose anchors are published
verbatim: **counterproductive** ("an inappropriate action that will have a
negative impact or make the situation worse"), **ineffective** ("a poor action
which will not help to resolve the situation"), **slightly effective**,
**effective** ("a reasonable action that will help to resolve the situation")
and **very effective** ("one of the best actions that could be taken to resolve
the situation") [3]. Candidates must rate each action independently, may give
the same rating to more than one action, and "do not have to use the full range
of the rating scale" [3]. The exercise is untimed but expected to take about 45
minutes [3].

Alongside it runs a **behavioural styles questionnaire** measuring "typical
behaviour and preferences at work" on a published seven-point agree/disagree
scale, with forced-choice ranking of statements triggered "depending on your
responses" [3]. Both are automatically scored by the platform; SJT and BSQ
scores "are combined to give an overall score", and overall performance is
"scored against a comparison group of applicants to policing roles displayed as
a percentile" [3].

More generally, SJTs "give candidates a number of scenarios and usually a series
of answers which the candidate has to rank or rate according to their
effectiveness"; they "tend to be best when they are designed specifically for
the role (as opposed to an off-the-shelf test)", are objectively marked, and
"tend to have lower adverse impact than ability tests" — but "typically have
lower score ranges and validity, however, and therefore may only accurately sift
out the lowest performers" [4]. Ability tests, by contrast, "have a tendency to
cause adverse impact whereby white candidates perform significantly better than
candidates from some black and minority ethnic backgrounds" and "should only be
selected, used and interpreted by those who are appropriately trained to do so
through the British Psychological Society qualifications in test use" [4].

## Assessed-throughout and non-assessed elements

Not everything on the day is scored, and centres publish this inconsistently.
KPMG's Launch Pad "combines assessed and non-assessed activities" alongside
networking [19]. The Civil Service's Individual Leadership Assessment and Staff
Engagement Exercise "are not scored", but their findings are shared with the
interview panel together with suggested probing questions [5]. Conversely,
careers-service guidance warns that "you may be assessed throughout the time
you are with the employer — on a company tour or even at lunch or in the bar in
the evening", with assessors evaluating candidates' behaviour toward all staff
[16]. The College of Policing publishes an **automatic fail policy**: conduct
contravening the Code of Ethics, behaving inappropriately toward others, or
acting "to obtain an unfair advantage over other candidates" is referred to an
independent Quality Assurance team, and an automatic fail "means that you fail
the assessment process regardless of your performance in the individual
exercises" and cannot be appealed [2].

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — guidance, not fact. It points back to the evidence above and
introduces no new facts.**

## Contributing to a group exercise without dominating

The assessment unit is not airtime; it is a **behaviour that an assessor can
record verbatim and classify against a named competency** [4]. Assessors are
instructed to classify each behaviour once and not to double-count [4], so
repeating the same point louder generates no additional score. Three
high-classification interventions are worth more than continuous talking:

1. **Structure the task in the first two minutes.** "We've got 30 minutes and
   three decisions to make — shall we take five for individual briefs, fifteen
   to compare, ten to agree?" This is directly classifiable under organising
   and collaboration competencies.
2. **Bring in a specific person by name and reason.** "Priya, your brief has the
   cost data — what does it say?" This is the observable form of the
   "cooperatively without dominating" behaviour York names [16] and of the
   quieter-member behaviour Kent attributes to good chairing [15].
3. **Force a decision before the clock runs out.** Kent lists ensuring
   decisions are reached among what assessors observe in the assigned-role
   variant [15]; groups that run out of time have collectively failed the
   "delivering" dimension.

If your brief conflicts with others' — the documented design of the
unassigned-role exercise [15] — say what your brief contains and why, then
negotiate. Concealing your brief to "win" misreads the exercise: the assessed
skills are negotiation and compromise [15].

## Prioritisation heuristics for in-trays

The published cues are the sender, the date, stated deadlines, confidentiality
markings, dependencies on other tasks and business impact, worked most-urgent
first, then urgent non-trivial, then trivial [14]. Two practical corollaries:

- **Justify, don't guess.** The scored artefact is the reasoning, not a hidden
  correct order — "there is no one right answer... provide logical and clearly
  explain the reasons for your decisions" [14]. Write one sentence of
  justification per item even when not asked; if you run out of time, an
  unactioned item with a stated rationale still evidences decision making.
- **Read for dependencies before you read for urgency.** LSE's advice to cap
  the initial read at 15 minutes of a 90-minute task [14] exists so you spot
  that item 12 is answered by item 3. Skimming for dependencies on the first
  pass converts a long tray into a short one.
- **Do not invent facts.** Where the exercise says use only the material
  provided — as the College of Policing's written exercise does explicitly [2] —
  imported outside knowledge is at best unscoreable and at worst a rule breach.

## Structuring a presentation to competencies

Work backwards from the criteria, not forwards from the topic. If the
specification is "a set topic related to the candidate's leadership experience"
in five minutes [5], the assessable content is *leadership behaviour*, not
sector commentary. A serviceable skeleton for a five-minute slot:

- 45 seconds — the situation and the decision you owned.
- 2 minutes — what you did, in first-person singular; this is where behaviour
  is classified.
- 1 minute — result, with a number if you have one.
- 45 seconds — what you would do differently, which is where reflective
  competencies get evidenced.
- Leave 30 seconds of headroom; time management is an observed dimension [16].

For unseen presentations, the assessed quality is performance under pressure
and verbal communication [15], so a visibly simple structure announced up front
("three points, then my recommendation") buys more credit than dense content.

## Worked skeleton — a 90-minute e-tray

**Minutes 0–12** — read everything once. Mark each item A (action today), B
(this week), C (no action). Note any item that references another.
**Minutes 12–20** — write the priority order and one justification line per
item. Do this before drafting anything, so that if you run out of time the
prioritisation evidence — the primary assessed competency [14][15] — is already
on the page.
**Minutes 20–70** — draft the A items in order. Delegate explicitly and by
name where the org chart Kent describes supports it [15]; delegation is an
observed behaviour, but only if you write down who and why.
**Minutes 70–85** — handle any injected new information, and state what it
changed. Revising a priority order with a stated reason evidences adaptability
[15].
**Minutes 85–90** — final pass on the justifications, not the prose. Where an
exercise publishes that spelling and grammar are not assessed [2], polish is
the lowest-value use of the last five minutes.

## Preparing without over-rehearsing

Several published processes explicitly bar prepared material: the College of
Policing states that prepared notes or scripts "may result in an automatic
failure", while permitting notes made *during* the exercise [2]. Preparation
that is safe under those rules is process preparation — practising
prioritisation, timing and structure — not scripted content.

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.** This section is written to be usable as
a design checklist, because the College of Policing and Cabinet Office publish
enough of their own machinery to support one.

## Design: choose exercises for validity and fairness

Two factors govern method choice: **validity** — "better performance on the
selection method predicts or is correlated with better performance in the
role" — and **fairness** — "it does not create advantage or disadvantage for
particular groups that are unrelated to their ability to do the job" [4]. For a
process to be fair and valid it must first be reliable: "the assessment methods
and environment must be consistent and standardised so that every candidate has
the same opportunity" [4]. CIPD's parallel requirement is that candidates be
given "the same types and numbers of tasks to complete within the same
timeframe" [12].

Method-specific documented trade-offs [4]:

| Method | Documented strength | Documented weakness |
|--------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Application form sift | quick check of basic criteria | resource intensive; identifying information "may reveal gender, ethnicity and age, which can introduce potential bias" — good practice is to remove it |
| Ability tests | quick and objective | "tendency to cause adverse impact"; require BPS test-use qualification |
| SJTs | objectively marked; lower adverse impact than ability tests; best when bespoke | lower score ranges and validity; "may only accurately sift out the lowest performers" |
| Work sample tests | can be highly valid | must accurately reflect the role; harder for complex roles; expectations must be defined in advance, usually by trialling with good performers |
| Assessment centres | very good validity; multi-scenario, multi-assessor view of typical performance; generates detailed feedback | time consuming and costly; must be carefully designed |
| Structured interviews | "shown to be the most valid" interview form | can be seen as rigid; many organisations use semi-structured variants |
| Unstructured interviews | — | "very poor validity" |

## Design: write exercise-specific assessor guidance

Generic competency indicators are not enough. "For a more complex exercise
involving a realistic scenario, for example, a roleplay exercise or written
exercise, the indicators in the CVF will not be sufficient on their own. The
exercise designers will need to identify what good demonstration of the
competency looks like in the context of the exercise" — usually by having good
performers already in the role complete the exercise as a trial and using their
responses to generate assessor guidance [4]. Crucially: "there will often be
several different ways a candidate could demonstrate the competency in any
given scenario — this is about creating supportive guidance for assessors, not
a 'model answer'" [4]. The published worked example pairs each CVF indicator
with concrete, exercise-specific observable statements [4].

Assessment content should be contextualised to the role and level: the same
competency indicator produces different expected behaviours for a sergeant and
an inspector, and the assessment content and performance expectations "should
be relevant to the specific job being recruited for" [4].

## Design: publish the competency-to-exercise map

The College of Policing publishes which competencies each exercise assesses,
and they deliberately differ between exercises [2][3] (see the table under
Framework Context). Cabinet Office SCS guidance publishes the equivalent as an
assessment matrix showing which Success Profiles element is carried by each
assessment stage [5]. Publishing the map is what allows a candidate to prepare
proportionately and an auditor to check that every advertised criterion is
assessed somewhere.

## Observation: ORCE

The published assessor process is **ORCE — observe, record, classify,
evaluate**, "an established, linear model of assessment" that "breaks down the
assessing process into component parts and helps to avoid bias" [4]. Its
published stage rules [4]:

- **Observe** — watching, listening to or reading the candidate's assessment;
  carried out simultaneously with recording; **independent of any other
  assessor**.
- **Record** — "detailed notes of behavioural evidence (as close to verbatim as
  possible)", with "no assuming or interpreting"; record behavioural evidence
  only; independent of other assessors.
- **Classify** — **after the candidate has left the room**; independent of
  other assessors; classify behavioural evidence only, only from that
  assessment, "classify each behaviour once — do not double count", against
  competencies, values and the assessor guidance.
- **Evaluate** — after classifying; independent; based on the quality *and*
  quantity of classified evidence; considering the level of the role
  (benchmarking) and "what the candidate missed or if anything detracted from
  their performance".

ORCE "should be used with paired assessment or in groups with assessors first
carrying out each stage independently and then meeting to discuss their grades
and evidence and then agreeing on a final grade" [4]. Cabinet Office guidance
imports the same sequence into interview panels: "panel members should observe,
record, classify, evaluate and score each question independently before jointly
calculating and agreeing an overall score" [5]. EPSO's model builds the same
independence in structurally: every exercise is observed "in a systematic way
by at least 2 trained assessors" and exercise content is validated by the
selection board [6].

## Scoring: rating scales and calibration

"Almost all assessment processes will employ scoring of some sort. Typically,
assessors will award a score for each competency area and then for the
interview or exercise overall, using a rating scale" [4]. The published police
example is a four-point A–D scale defined by quantity and quality of evidence
against areas for development: **A** substantial positive evidence and few or
no development areas; **B** positive evidence with some development areas but
on balance more positive; **C** some positive evidence but on balance more
development areas; **D** substantial development areas and little or no
positive evidence [4]. The scale "must have descriptors alongside each score
and these should be clear and unambiguous", and multiple assessors must be
calibrated — "by conducting a mock assessment or having a discussion about
expected levels of performance at each grade, in advance of the first live
candidate assessment... often referred to as benchmarking or frame of reference
training" [4].

The Civil Service uses different scales for different elements: "experience,
technical skills and behaviours should be scored using the 7 point scale.
Strengths should be scored using the 4 point scale", with a 3-point A/B/C scale
acceptable at sift [5].

Where an exercise is machine-scored, the mechanics may be published: the police
national sift is "automatically scored by the assessment platform", SJT and BSQ
scores "are combined to give an overall score", and results are expressed as a
percentile "against a comparison group of applicants to policing roles" [3].

## Decision making: arithmetic beats discussion

This is the single most actionable published finding for centre designers.
"Arithmetic decision making, where the candidates' scores are used to rank
candidates according to their performance, has been shown to be the most valid
decision-making method", and can incorporate thresholds on the competencies,
values or exercises identified as most important **in advance** [4]. By
contrast, "rational decision-making methods, where groups of assessors discuss
the candidates' performances and decide who should be successful, have been
shown to be less effective in selecting the best candidates and are difficult
to justify from a fairness point of view" [4]. Discussion retains a legitimate
narrow role: "where candidates are borderline or where there are a number of
candidates tied with the same score or score band" [4]. Full and accurate score
records should be kept throughout "so that there is a final verified record to
be used in decision making and also forming an audit trail" [4].

## Assessor bias: the published catalogue

College of Policing guidance publishes a barriers table with countermeasures
[4]. Summarised, the named barriers are: **expectancy effect** (pre-assessment
information generating positive or negative expectations); **confirmatory
information** (seeking evidence that confirms first impressions); **personal
liking**; **primacy effect**; **stereotyping**; **prototyping** (bias toward an
imagined ideal type for the role); **halo/horns effect** (strong or weak
performance in one exercise colouring all others — the specific risk a
multi-exercise centre creates); **quota effect**; **contrast effect** (judging a
candidate against the previous candidate); **negative information bias**;
**similar-to-me effect**; **misreading non-verbal communication**;
**fundamental attribution error**; **information overload and selective
attention**; **fatigue**; **rushing**; and **central tendency, leniency and
stringency** in scale use [4]. The published countermeasures are: using the
ORCE model; giving assessors "only... information they truly need in advance of
the assessment"; comprehensive assessor training including unconscious-bias
training; scheduling that factors in breaks and does not "schedule the
assessment of too many candidates by one assessor"; and training assessors to
use the full length of the rating scale [4].

Assessor training or briefing "prior to any assessment process helps to ensure
that assessors are competent and fair", and volume processes "should also have
a quality assurance process to ensure that candidates are being assessed fairly
and consistently... monitoring assessors, role actors and other staff" [4]. The
College of Policing publishes its own implementation of that: assessors are "a
mixture of police officers, police staff and people who have been specifically
chosen from the community", all trained per exercise and "continually
monitored", and they know only the candidate's name and email — "they will not
have seen any other information about you and they will not know whether you
have applied before" [2]. That last point is the expectancy-effect
countermeasure implemented operationally.

ACAS's general interviewing guidance points the same way: interview with more
than one person where possible "to reduce the risk of discriminating", ask each
applicant the same questions where possible, and store notes and recordings
securely and confidentially [13].

## Feedback and appeals

Candidates should be told when to expect results, and feedback is good
practice for successful and unsuccessful candidates alike; it should be
constructive, "based on their actual performances at the assessment centre or
interviews", and timely [4]. Feedback on values in particular must be based on
behavioural evidence and framed as a snapshot: "it is not to say they don't
hold the values, but that they have not evidenced them" [4]. Candidates should
have "the opportunity to raise any concerns or circumstances which they feel
may have affected their performance", via a clear, communicated process [4].
The College of Policing publishes a worked version: personalised feedback
reports giving the overall score per exercise plus "the kinds of performance
that distinguishes better-performing candidates from those who did not perform
as effectively" [2][3]; appeals submitted to the recruiting force within 24
hours of taking the assessment, considered only where the candidate did not
meet the standard, though "procedural issues... will still be investigated as
they may have important implications for the assessment process as a whole"
[2]. Resits are permitted from three months after the assessment, capped at two
attempts in a 12-month period [2][3].

## Evaluation

Selection methods should be evaluated regularly, monitored for adverse impact
on under-represented groups — which "will require collecting monitoring data,
such as age, gender or ethnicity" held in line with data-protection law — and
any changes should themselves be monitored for benefit, ideally by before-and-
after comparison [4]. Predictive-validity studies comparing assessment scores
with subsequent job performance "are challenging to conduct in organisations
and require some specialist analysis skills, however, these are of great
benefit and can show whether the selection process is working" [4].

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — guidance, not fact.**

## Candidates

- **Treating the group exercise as a competition.** The documented assessed
  behaviours are negotiation, compromise and cooperation "without dominating"
  [15][16]; every assessor scores every candidate separately, so suppressing a
  peer does not raise your own classified evidence [4].
- **Silence as a strategy.** The mirror-image failure. Evaluation rests on "the
  quality and quantity of the classified evidence" [4]; a candidate who
  contributes nothing has generated no evidence to classify, which is scored as
  absence, not neutrality.
- **Optimising the in-tray for completion rather than justification.** Clearing
  every item with no stated reasoning discards the criterion the exercise
  actually publishes — logical, explained decisions [14].
- **Importing outside information.** Where the brief says use only what is
  provided [2][7], invented facts are unscoreable; in the police written
  exercise, pasting text from another application "may result in an automatic
  failure" [2].
- **Bringing prepared scripts.** Published guidance for the police online
  process bars notes prepared before the assessment, on pain of automatic
  failure, while permitting notes taken during it [2].
- **Over-polishing prose in a written exercise.** Where spelling and grammar
  are published as unassessed [2], the last five minutes are better spent on
  reasoning.
- **Assuming the informal parts are off the record.** Careers-service guidance
  warns that candidates may be assessed throughout their time with the employer
  [16], and KPMG's Launch Pad openly mixes assessed and non-assessed activities
  [19]. Conduct toward other candidates and staff sits within the police
  automatic-fail policy [2].
- **Answering at the wrong level.** Where a framework defines competency levels
  and the process publishes the level assessed — level 1 for initial police
  recruitment [2][3] — answering a level-1 exercise with strategic
  organisation-wide content evidences the wrong band.
- **Presenting for the topic instead of the criteria.** A presentation
  specified as covering leadership experience [5] is scored on leadership
  behaviour, not on the quality of the subject-matter commentary.

## Interviewers and designers

- **Sharing impressions before independent classification.** ORCE requires
  observing, recording, classifying and evaluating independently *before*
  assessors meet [4]; Cabinet Office guidance says the same for panels [5].
  A corridor conversation between exercises undoes the design.
- **Deciding by discussion.** Group discussion as the primary decision method
  is documented as less effective and hard to justify on fairness grounds [4];
  its documented place is borderline and tied cases only.
- **Letting one exercise colour the rest.** The halo/horns effect is a named,
  documented barrier [4] and multi-exercise centres are structurally exposed to
  it — which is why classification happens per exercise, after that exercise.
- **Giving assessors the application form.** The published countermeasure to
  the expectancy effect is to give assessors "only... information they truly
  need in advance" [4].
- **Running exercises without exercise-specific guidance.** Generic indicators
  "will not be sufficient on their own" for a realistic scenario exercise [4].
- **Overloading assessors.** Information overload, fatigue and rushing are
  named barriers with a scheduling countermeasure: build in breaks and cap the
  number of candidates per assessor [4].
- **Unequal task sets.** CIPD requires the same types and numbers of tasks in
  the same timeframe for every candidate [12]; a centre that runs one group
  short by a candidate, or overruns one group's exercise, has broken that.
- **Using an off-the-shelf SJT as a headline filter.** SJTs are documented as
  best when role-specific and as possibly "only accurately sift[ing] out the
  lowest performers" [4].
- **Adding an ability test by default.** Ability tests carry a documented
  tendency to adverse impact and a BPS test-use qualification requirement, and
  "should only be used where ability is clearly relevant to the role" [4].

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.** Concrete drills a reader can run this week.

1. **Timed in-tray build.** Write yourself 15 fictional inbox items across four
   senders, three deadlines and one confidentiality marking. Give yourself 45
   minutes: 7 to read, 8 to write a justified priority order, 30 to action.
   Then self-score against the published criteria — prioritisation, analysis,
   working under pressure, problem solving, adaptability, organising, decision
   making, communication [15] — and specifically check whether every item
   carries a stated reason [14].
2. **Group-exercise behaviour log.** In your next real meeting, log your own
   contributions as an assessor would: verbatim note, then classify each into a
   named competency, once only, after the meeting ends [4]. Count how many of
   your contributions classify into nothing. That number is your dominance
   problem or your silence problem, whichever it turns out to be.
3. **Five-minute leadership presentation.** Using the published Civil Service
   Director specification — five minutes, a set topic related to your
   leadership experience [5] — record yourself against the skeleton in Model
   Answer Guidance. Cut until you land at 4:30.
4. **Unseen-presentation drill.** Have someone hand you a one-page article and
   a 20-minute clock, then present for 10 minutes and take questions — the
   documented on-the-spot design [15]. Assess yourself on structure and time
   management rather than content [16].
5. **SJT calibration.** Work the two published College of Policing example
   scenarios [3]. Rate each action, then write one sentence on *why* before
   comparing with a study partner. Practise the published rules: rate actions
   independently of one another, and do not force yourself to use the whole
   scale [3].
6. **Debrief rehearsal.** After any of the drills above, answer aloud: "Talk me
   through the order in which you tackled that, and why." Assessment-centre
   interviews commonly mine the exercises you have just completed [2][5].
7. **Designer drill: write assessor guidance.** Take one competency you recruit
   against and one exercise you run. Produce five exercise-specific observable
   evidence statements, in the style of the College of Policing's published
   worked example [4]. Then check them against the test: are these guidance, or
   have you written a model answer [4]?
8. **Designer drill: audit your decision rule.** Write down how your last
   selection decision was actually made. If it was assessors talking, compare
   against the documented finding that score-based ranking is the more valid
   method and discussion belongs to borderline and tied cases [4].

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

- **Reasonable adjustments apply to every exercise.** Under the Equality Act
  2010, section 20, where a provision, criterion or practice, a physical
  feature, or the absence of an auxiliary aid puts a disabled person at a
  substantial disadvantage, reasonable steps must be taken to avoid the
  disadvantage or provide the aid [9]. Every exercise in this document — a
  timed in-tray, a group setting, a standing presentation, a platform-delivered
  briefing exercise — is a provision, criterion or practice to which the duty
  can attach. GOV.UK's employer guidance gives directly relevant examples,
  including allowing wheelchair users to be interviewed on the ground floor and
  allowing candidates to complete a written test using a computer [10]. College
  of Policing guidance states that organisations must take steps to ensure no
  unlawful discrimination "in a range of circumstances which includes the
  provision of testing and assessment", and that recruiters "should proactively
  work with candidates to identify what would be an appropriate accommodation
  or adjustment" [4].
- **Adjustment practice in published processes.** The College of Policing
  publishes that assessment timings "may vary from those described in this
  document" where a timings-related adjustment has been granted [2], and that
  an accessibility toolbar is available to all candidates regardless of
  adjustment status [2]. For the national sift it publishes an important
  design consequence: "as both exercises in the national sift are untimed, we
  do not offer additional time as a reasonable adjustment", while listing
  self-managed accommodations — text-to-speech, coloured overlays, 300 per cent
  zoom, keyboard navigation and self-facilitated breaks — that need no
  permission [3]. Designing an exercise as untimed is itself an accessibility
  decision.
- **Adverse impact monitoring.** Recruiting organisations are responsible for
  ensuring selection processes comply with the Equality Act 2010 and do not
  unfairly discriminate against protected-characteristic groups [4]. Adverse
  impact is commonly evaluated with the 80 per cent (four-fifths) rule — a
  minority-group pass rate below four-fifths of the majority rate — but the
  guidance is explicit that "although the four fifths rule is a useful guide
  for evaluation and is commonly used in practice, it does not have a legal
  footing" [4]. Where adverse impact is detected, "further analysis should be
  conducted to verify if the difference is statistically significant and the
  possible reasons for this" [4]. Ability tests carry a specifically documented
  adverse-impact risk [4].
- **Positive action.** Section 159 of the Equality Act 2010 (positive action in
  recruitment and promotion) "may be applicable where candidates are equally
  qualified" [4].
- **Candidate data.** Monitoring data collected for adverse-impact analysis
  "should be collected, used and stored in line with data protection laws" [4],
  and ACAS requires interview notes and recordings to be stored securely and
  confidentially [13]. Assessment centres generate an unusually large personal-
  data footprint — verbatim behavioural notes, recorded video responses,
  platform telemetry, feedback reports. The ICO's employment-practices guidance
  on recruitment and selection sets out how UK GDPR and the Data Protection Act
  2018 apply across the whole recruitment process, including sensitive
  information such as health data, and distinguishes what employers *must*,
  *should* and *could* do [11]. There is a specific published warning against
  leakage in the other direction: assessors should "not reveal sensitive
  personal information in scoring sheets or feedback reports" [4].
- **Assessment integrity and its limits.** The College of Policing's automatic
  fail is decided by an independent Quality Assurance team reviewing evidence,
  and is not appealable — but a candidate who has *not* met the standard may
  appeal on extenuating or procedural grounds within 24 hours, and procedural
  concerns are investigated regardless of outcome [2]. Publishing the standard,
  the reviewer and the window is what makes such a policy defensible.
- **Emotive content.** Where exercises probe values, assessors "should be alert
  to signs that candidates are becoming upset or emotional when describing
  events or when playing a role in an exercise", and such information must be
  treated sensitively [4]. This is a live design consideration for role-plays
  built around conflict, bereavement or discrimination scenarios.

# Risks & Caveats

- **The public-sector evidence is deep; the private-sector evidence is thin.**
  Almost every specific claim in this document about exercise timing, competency
  mapping, rating scales and assessor process comes from the College of
  Policing, the Cabinet Office, GOV.UK or EPSO. Named private employers publish
  stage names — Launch Pad [19], Discovery Centre [20], Job Simulation [18],
  a two-or-three-stage centre [21] — but not exercise briefs or scoring
  criteria, and their pages describe the current recruitment cycle only. Do not
  read the public-sector detail as describing what a given private employer
  does.
- **The generic exercise taxonomy rests on careers services, not employers.**
  The assigned/unassigned group distinction, the in-tray prioritisation
  heuristics and the planned/on-the-spot presentation split are documented by
  named university careers services [14][15][16] rather than by the employers
  running the exercises. They are credible, current and consistent across three
  independent institutions, but they are class-4 practitioner sources, and this
  is a principal reason the document is rated Medium rather than High.
- **CVF version currency.** The competency names used here — "we collaborate",
  "we support and inspire", "we analyse critically", "we are emotionally
  aware" — are the **2024** CVF names, as published in the 2024 online
  assessment process guidance [2] and the 2026 national sift guidance [3]. The
  2016 framework used different wording and is superseded; separately, an older
  College PDF titled "Online assessments candidate guide" remains live but
  carries 2016 competency names and is deliberately not cited here. See
  `police-competency-values-framework` for the transition detail.
- **The values-based recruitment guidance predates the current CVF.** Reference
  [4] is dated 2018. Its *process* content — ORCE, rating scales, assessor
  barriers, decision-making methods, adverse impact, work-sample design — is
  framework-agnostic and is the only basis on which it is cited here. Its
  competency and value *names* are not relied on.
- **The EPSO material cited is illustrative and partly archived.** The
  Assessment Centre exercise list is cited via a 2022 Internet Archive snapshot
  because EPSO's live FAQ 403s automated fetchers [6], and the published SCBI
  assignment booklet is dated 2020 and names a "Leadership" competency that
  EPSO's 2023 framework no longer carries [7]. Current EU practice is the
  subject of `eu-institutions-assessment-centres`, not this document.
- **Cabinet Office SCS guidance is dated 2020.** The HR Director assessment
  matrix, the five-minute presentation and the scoring scales [5] describe
  guidance issued in April 2020; departments vary in implementation and the
  guidance may have been superseded departmentally.
- **Timings are exercise-specific, not general.** The 90-minute in-tray [14],
  120-minute written-exercise cap [2] and 45-minute SJT [3] are the published
  parameters of *those* exercises. There is no documented standard duration for
  an assessment-centre exercise in general.
- **Bot-blocked source.** The CIPD factsheet [12] serves browsers but blocks
  automated link checkers; a human should verify it periodically.

# Open Questions

- **What do private-sector centres actually contain?** No named UK employer
  located for this document publishes its assessment-centre exercise briefs,
  competency-to-exercise map or scoring criteria. Resolvable only by employer
  disclosure or by an ISE-style survey with exercise-level breakdown.
- **How prevalent is the group exercise now?** Careers services still describe
  it as standard [15][16], but no located source gives a current UK prevalence
  figure, and virtual delivery may have shifted the mix. Resolvable via the
  full ISE Student Recruitment Survey dataset.
- **What is the published evidence base for group-exercise validity
  specifically?** The cited guidance evaluates assessment centres as a whole
  [4][12] rather than exercise by exercise. Resolvable from the occupational-
  psychology literature; a candidate for an academic-sourced follow-up.
- **How are remote group exercises assessed?** ORCE assumes classification
  "after the candidate has left the room" [4]. No located source addresses how
  the observe/record discipline transfers to a breakout room with
  screen-sharing and chat.
- **Do UK regulators address AI-scored assessment-centre exercises?** The ICO's
  guidance is general to recruitment [11]; nothing located speaks to automated
  scoring of written exercises or recorded briefing responses. Candidate for a
  best-practice child document.
- **Is the four-fifths rule the right monitoring test for exercise-level
  scores?** The cited guidance applies it to methods and notes it has no legal
  footing [4]; whether centres monitor per-exercise or only end-to-end is
  undocumented in the located sources.
