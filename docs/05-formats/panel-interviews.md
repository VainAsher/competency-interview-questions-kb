# Executive Summary

The panel interview is the default shape of the competency interview in UK and
EU public-sector hiring, and it is a *procedural* device before it is a
conversational one. ACAS advises that "if possible, each interview should be
carried out by more than 1 person to reduce the risk of discriminating" [17];
the Civil Service Commission's Recruitment Principles require that "a selection
panel of two or more people must be set up to oversee the appointments process"
and that it "must be chaired either by a civil servant or for the most senior
competitions, by a Civil Service Commissioner" [4]; and for EU institutions the
Staff Regulations make the panel a statutory body — a Selection Board with a
chair designated by the Appointing Authority and equal numbers of members
designated by the Appointing Authority and the Staff Committee, whose
proceedings are secret [10]. A panel is therefore not "more interviewers"; it
is a governance mechanism with a chair, a record, and an audit trail.

The most consequential thing this document adds to
[formats-foundation](formats-foundation.md) is the documented **scoring
mechanics**. Cabinet Office guidance for delegated-grade (EO–Grade 6)
recruitment states that "Panel members should observe, record, classify,
evaluate and score each question independently, then calculate an overall score
before jointly agreeing a merit list using the arithmetic method", that
variances "can be discussed", that panellists "may decide to change their
score(s) or stand by them", but that "ultimately individual panel scores should
be reconciled by taking an arithmetic average which will serve as the final
scores for the candidate" [1]. The College of Policing gives the same
observe–record–classify–evaluate sequence a name — **ORCE** — and specifies
that each of the four stages is carried out "independent of any other
assessor(s)" before assessors meet to agree a grade [7]. The two frameworks
converge on one rule: *the discussion happens after the scoring, not instead of
it.*

Confidence is **High**. The core claims — panel composition, chairing,
independent scoring, arithmetic reconciliation, ORCE, note-taking and fairness
duties — rest on Cabinet Office guidance, the Civil Service Commission's
Recruitment Principles, College of Policing guidance, NHS VBR guidance, the EU
Staff Regulations, ACAS and CIPD. The softer material — how it *feels* to be
questioned by three people, who to address, how to handle interruption — is
labelled coaching, and the one prevalence-flavoured claim in the document
("panel interviews are most common") is attributed to the careers service that
makes it [21] rather than asserted.

# Key Takeaways

- A panel is a governance device with a documented minimum size and a named
  chair: two or more members for Civil Service competitions, chaired by a civil
  servant or, for the most senior competitions, by a Civil Service Commissioner
  [4][5]. *(documented)*
- The Civil Service scoring rule is explicit and mechanical: score
  **independently first**, then reconcile **by arithmetic average**; discussion
  of marked variances is allowed, changing your score is allowed, but the
  average is what stands [1]. *(documented)*
- The College of Policing's **ORCE** model (observe, record, classify,
  evaluate) breaks assessment into four stages each performed independently of
  other assessors, and is repeatedly named as the counter-measure to expectancy
  effects, primacy, halo/horns, contrast effects and central tendency [7].
  *(documented)*
- Records are the deliverable. Policing guidance requires "full and accurate
  records of scores for each candidate … so that there is a final verified
  record to be used in decision making and also forming an audit trail" [7];
  NHS guidance tells interviewers to finalise notes immediately, "as leaving
  this until later can result in distorted information" [9]. *(documented)*
- Panel *diversity and training* are stated duties, not courtesies: Cabinet
  Office guidance recommends that "all interview panels should be diverse, and
  all panellists should have undertaken training on interview skills prior to
  sitting on an interview panel" [1], and centrally managed SCS panels "should
  have at least one panellist who is from an ethnic minority background and/or
  has a disability" [2]. EU selection boards of more than four members "shall
  comprise at least two members of each gender" [10]. *(documented)*
- Virtual panels carry an extra documented step: panellists must "engage in a
  pre-discussion to agree the running of the interview and who will be leading
  on different questions", and check the technology is "available, accessible
  and compatible for all panel members and candidates" [1]. *(documented)*
- The chair actively manages the candidate's airtime — Devon & Cornwall Police
  tell candidates that "if the Chair of the interview believes you are not
  answering they may gently remind you", and that "the Chair will assist you
  with the management of your time" [8]. *(documented)*
- For candidates: because each panellist scores each question independently,
  the person who asked is not the only person you are answering — but *they*
  are the one holding the criteria for that question. Answer the asker, include
  the room. *(guidance)*
- For interviewers: the single highest-yield design decision is the pre-meet —
  agreeing criteria, question ownership and what "good" looks like *before* the
  first candidate, which both the Civil Service and university HR guidance
  place on the chair [1][20]. *(documented)*

# Purpose

This document answers, for candidates: "three people will interview me — how
does that actually change what I should do, who am I talking to, and how is the
result arrived at?" And for interviewers, HR and hiring managers: "how do I
build, run, score and defend a panel interview against published UK and EU
guidance?"

It exists because the panel is the format where the *scoring process* is most
visible in the public record and least understood by candidates. Most
preparation material treats a panel as a one-to-one interview with more faces.
The documented reality is a defined procedure — independent observation,
independent scoring, arithmetic reconciliation, a verified record — and knowing
that procedure changes how a candidate should behave and gives an interviewer a
defensible design.

# Scope

**Covered:** why multi-interviewer panels are advised; documented panel
composition and roles (chair, subject/technical member, HR or independent
member); how questions are divided among panellists; how panels record evidence
and score; **how independent scores are reconciled** (arithmetic average, merit
lists, order of merit); note-taking and evidence-recording standards; virtual
and hybrid panels; panel-specific candidate dynamics (multiple questioners,
probing by a subject expert, interruptions); and the fairness, data-protection
and equality duties that attach to panels. UK Civil Service, policing, NHS and
EU-institution practice all carry visible weight, alongside ACAS and CIPD.

**Not covered:** the general case for structured interviewing and the format
landscape (see formats-foundation); answer technique such as STAR (see
techniques-foundation); framework content — behaviours, CVF competencies, NHS
values, EPSO competencies (see uk-civil-service-success-profiles,
police-competency-values-framework, nhs-values-based-interviews); assessment
centres, where panels also sit but the unit of analysis is the exercise (see
assessment-centre-exercises); and asynchronous video, which is the format with
*no* live panel at all (see video-asynchronous-interviews). Academic
appointment panels are touched on only where their published guidance
illustrates composition [21].

# Definitions

Document-specific narrowings; see `GLOSSARY.md` for shared terms.

- **Panel interview** — an interview conducted by two or more interviewers
  simultaneously, assessing one candidate against the same published criteria.
- **Chair** — the panel member with procedural responsibility for the
  competition: composition, conflicts, criteria, conduct of the interview and
  the final record. In Civil Service competitions the chair must be a civil
  servant or, for the most senior competitions, a Civil Service Commissioner
  [4].
- **Selection Board** — the EU-institution equivalent of a panel, constituted
  under Annex III of the Staff Regulations with a designated chairman and
  balanced membership; its proceedings are secret [10].
- **ORCE** — Observe, Record, Classify, Evaluate: the linear assessment
  sequence set out by the College of Policing, each stage performed
  independently of other assessors before grades are discussed [7].
- **Reconciliation** — the step at which individual panellists' independent
  scores are combined into one score per candidate. In Civil Service delegated
  grades this is done "by taking an arithmetic average" [1].
- **Arithmetic decision making** — ranking candidates by their aggregated
  scores, as distinct from **rational decision making**, where a group of
  assessors discusses performances and decides who should be successful [7].
- **Order of merit** — the ranked list a selection panel produces; the
  candidate recommended for appointment "must be the one placed first in order
  of merit by the selection panel" [4].
- **Calibration (benchmarking / frame-of-reference training)** — pre-live
  alignment of assessors on what each score point means, e.g. by mock
  assessment or discussion of expected performance at each grade [7].

# Framework Context

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## Why more than one interviewer

ACAS states plainly that "if possible, each interview should be carried out by
more than 1 person to reduce the risk of discriminating", alongside its advice
to "ask each applicant the same questions where possible" and to base decisions
on "how each applicant met each point in the job description and person
specification" [17]. CIPD's selection guidance holds that structured interviews
scored against pre-agreed criteria are "an effective way to minimise bias",
reports research that a panel interviewing together produces fairer and more
accurate results than multiple one-to-one interviews, and recommends that
interviewers score independently before discussing candidates so that they are
not influenced by senior colleagues [18]. NHS Values Based Recruitment guidance
puts the same point operationally: interviews "can be conducted by a single
interviewer, pairs of interviewers or a panel", and "where resources allow, it
is good practice to use multiple interviewers as this helps to minimise bias
and enhance fairness" [9].

## Where panels are mandated

| Regime | Documented panel requirement |
|--------|------------------------------|
| UK Civil Service (all competitions) | "A selection panel of two or more people must be set up to oversee the appointments process"; chaired by a civil servant or, for the most senior competitions, a Civil Service Commissioner [4] |
| UK Civil Service (SCS PB2/3/4) | Civil Service Commissioners personally chair external competitions at Director, Director General and Permanent Secretary level [5]; for HR Deputy Director and Director competitions "a Civil Service Commissioner will chair the interview process" [2][3] |
| UK Civil Service (delegated EO–G6) | Panel scores each question independently, then agrees a merit list "using the arithmetic method" [1] |
| Policing (England & Wales) | Trained assessors "should follow a structured process such as ORCE"; ORCE "should be used with paired assessment or in groups" [7] |
| NHS (values-based recruitment) | Multiple interviewers "good practice … to minimise bias and enhance fairness"; "panel style interviewing may therefore be considered" [9] |
| EU institutions | Selection Board constituted under Annex III of the Staff Regulations: chairman plus equal numbers designated by Appointing Authority and Staff Committee [10] |

## The EU model: the panel as a statutory body

For EU-institution competitions the panel is created by law rather than by
policy. Annex III of the Staff Regulations provides that "The Selection Board
shall consist of a chairman designated by the Appointing Authority and of
members designated by the Appointing Authority and the Staff Committee, each
designating the same number"; that members "shall be chosen from officials
whose function group and grade is at least equal to that of the post to be
filled"; that a board of more than four members "shall comprise at least two
members of each gender"; that the board "may, for certain tests, be assisted by
one or more examiners serving in an advisory capacity"; and that "the
proceedings of the Selection Board shall be secret" [10]. The board draws up
the list of suitable candidates — "wherever possible … at least twice as many
names as the number of posts to be filled" — and forwards it to the appointing
authority "together with a reasoned report" [10]. Within that structure EPSO's
competency-based interview is itself structured: candidates are "interviewed
for about 10 minutes per competency", with questions "structured to allow the
assessors to evaluate the candidate's selected general competencies" [11]; in
EPSO's Assessment Centre model each exercise is observed "in a systematic way
by at least 2 trained assessors" [12].

# Core Reference

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## Panel composition and roles

Published guidance describes three functional seats, though not always under
those names. On size and shape, Civil Service Careers describes a typical
campaign in which the candidate is "interviewed by 3 people" asking questions
on the strengths and behaviours listed in the job advert, in a final interview
that "will usually be face-to-face in person or via video" and usually lasts
45–60 minutes [6].

**The chair.** The Civil Service Commission requires that the panel "must be
chaired either by a civil servant or for the most senior competitions, by a
Civil Service Commissioner", and gives the chair overall responsibility for
compliance with the Recruitment Principles, including approving the selection
criteria, role description, panel membership and advertising strategy before
the competition begins; the chair must also produce, at the end, a record
describing the outcome, the assessment stages, the evidence on which merit was
assessed and the order of merit [4]. University of Nottingham recruitment
training for chairs and panel members lists the chair's responsibilities as:
ensuring "a balanced panel composition where possible"; seeking "declaration of
any conflicts of interest prior to the interview date"; facilitating "a panel
pre-meet to agree selection criteria, interview questions and how it will be
measured"; maintaining fairness and consistency; awareness of the Public Sector
Equality Duty and reasonable-adjustment requests; challenging "the panel
members rationale for decisions and being aware of where bias can occur";
management of panel performance; facilitating "agreement on candidate
outcomes"; recording final decisions "including rationale for each candidate";
providing feedback on request; and representing the panel at a tribunal [20].

**Subject or role expertise.** The Staff Regulations require EU Selection Board
members to hold a function group and grade "at least equal to that of the post
to be filled", and allow the board to be "assisted by one or more examiners
serving in an advisory capacity" for certain tests — i.e. a documented
technical-expert seat [10]. In UK academic recruitment, University of Edinburgh
careers guidance describes a panel of "usually between two and six" that will
"include at least one person from the department (possibly the head of
department or research group)" [21].

**HR and independent members.** Edinburgh's guidance also has the panel include
"a representative from Human Resources" and "often someone from another
department outside your discipline" [21]. In the Civil Service the independence
requirement is strongest at the top: a Commissioner chairs the most senior
competitions [4][5], and centrally managed SCS competitions run by Whitehall
departments and the Scottish and Welsh Governments "should have at least one
panellist who is from an ethnic minority background and/or has a disability"
[2][3].

**Conflicts.** Panel members "must declare any conflict of interest including
prior knowledge of any applicant" [4]; university chair guidance places the
collection of those declarations before the interview date [20]. College of
Policing guidance treats pre-assessment information as an active hazard: the
*expectancy effect* is the tendency to form a positive or negative expectation
"from pre-assessment information (eg application form, word-of-mouth opinion,
appraisal document)", countered by ORCE and by giving assessors "only … the
information they truly need in advance of the assessment" [7].

## Dividing the questions

Cabinet Office guidance for video interviewing makes question ownership an
explicit pre-interview decision: panellists must "engage in a pre-discussion to
agree the running of the interview and who will be leading on different
questions" [1]. NHS VBR guidance builds the same division into the question
bank itself: an interview question bank "should contain questions structured as
follows; a lead question followed by a series of possible relevant probe
questions, as well as a set of defined behavioural indicators", with a worked
template pairing the lead question "Describe a situation where it was important
for you to establish/develop an effective working relationship with someone…"
to probes such as "Why was this relationship significant?", "What did you learn
from this experience?", "What was the outcome?" and "How did you react/feel
about developing this relationship?" [9]. University chair training gives the
same lead-plus-probe pattern: the worked example question "Tell me about an
occasion when you needed to gain buy-in to a new process" is published with
follow-up probes "Who did you need to influence?", "How did you approach
this?", "What were the challenges and how did you overcome these?" and "What
was the outcome?" [20].

The practical consequence is documented: on a well-run panel the person who
asks the lead question is not necessarily the person who probes it, and the
probes are pre-agreed rather than improvised.

## Recording the evidence

The College of Policing specifies contemporaneous, near-verbatim recording as
its own distinct stage: assessors should "take detailed notes of behavioural
evidence (as close to verbatim as possible)", with "no assuming or
interpreting", recording "just behavioural evidence", and doing so
"independent of any other assessor(s)" [7]. Classification happens only "after
the candidate has left the room", must classify "only behaviour from that
assessment", and must "classify each behaviour once — do not double count" [7].
NHS guidance requires interviewers to tell the applicant before the interview
"whether you will be writing notes as they speak", to obtain consent before any
audio recording, and to "allow some time immediately after the interview for
interviewers to finalise their notes as leaving this until later can result in
distorted information" [9]. University chair training states that notes should
be "a summary of what the candidate said", "sustained", "complete but
succinct", "done openly", "clear and legible" and compliant with data
protection, and that they are needed for "providing feedback", "an objective
review to eliminate bias", "evidence to justify decisions" and appeals — under
the heading "accurate note taking is as important as questioning and
listening", with the reminder that candidates "can ask for copies of any
information held about them personally" by subject access request [20].

## Scoring and reconciliation — the central mechanism

For delegated grades (EO–Grade 6), Cabinet Office guidance sets out the whole
sequence in one passage: "Panel members should observe, record, classify,
evaluate and score each question independently, then calculate an overall score
before jointly agreeing a merit list using the arithmetic method. Variances in
scoring amongst panel members can be discussed; it may be useful to discuss
evidence underlying scores when these variances are particularly marked. Panel
members may decide to change their score(s) or stand by them, but ultimately
individual panel scores should be reconciled by taking an arithmetic average
which will serve as the final scores for the candidate" [1]. The same document
fixes what is being averaged: "Experience, technical skills and behaviours
should be scored using the 7 point scale. Strengths should be scored using the
4 point scale" [1]. At sift, single or smaller campaigns score each element on
the numerical scale, while high-volume campaigns may use a lead criterion or an
overall rating on a 3-point scale — "A – candidate meets all the criteria; B –
borderline/candidate meets some of the criteria; C – candidate does not meet
the criteria" [1].

The senior-grade guidance carries the same rule with one difference: for HR
Deputy Director and Director competitions, "panel members should observe,
record, classify, evaluate and score each question independently before jointly
calculating and agreeing an overall score based on all of the candidate
information, including the pre-interview leadership assessment reports", where
the Individual Leadership Assessment and staff engagement exercise are
themselves unscored but are shared with the panel "along with suggested probing
questions" [2][3].

The College of Policing reaches the same destination from the assessment
literature. Its published rating scale is a four-point A–D scale anchored on
"the quality and quantity of the evidence" — A being "substantial positive
evidence of the competency observed … and few or no areas for development
observed", down to D, "substantial areas for development identified and little
or no positive evidence" [7]. On decision making it is unambiguous: "Arithmetic
decision making, where the candidates' scores are used to rank candidates
according to their performance, has been shown to be the most valid
decision-making method", whereas "rational decision-making methods, where
groups of assessors discuss the candidates' performances and decide who should
be successful, have been shown to be less effective in selecting the best
candidates and are difficult to justify from a fairness point of view";
assessor discussion is endorsed only "where candidates are borderline or where
there are a number of candidates tied with the same score or score band" [7].

Reconciled scores then feed a ranked list. The Civil Service Commission
requires that "the candidate recommended for appointment must be the one placed
first in order of merit by the selection panel", and that the chair's closing
record capture the order of merit and the evidence on which merit was assessed
[4]. For EU competitions the Selection Board draws up a list of suitable
candidates and forwards it with a reasoned report [10].

## ORCE in full

| Stage | What the assessor does | Independence rule |
|-------|------------------------|-------------------|
| **Observe** | Watch, listen to or read the candidate's assessment; carry out `record' at the same time | "Independent of any other assessor(s)" [7] |
| **Record** | Take detailed notes of behavioural evidence, as close to verbatim as possible; no assuming or interpreting | "Independent of any other assessor(s)" [7] |
| **Classify** | After the candidate has left the room, classify observed behaviour against competencies/values per assessor guidance; each behaviour classified once, no double counting | "Independent of any other assessor(s)" [7] |
| **Evaluate** | After classifying, judge on quality and quantity of classified evidence, considering the level of role and what the candidate missed | "Independent of any other assessor(s)" [7] |

The College describes ORCE as "an established, linear model of assessment"
which "should be used with paired assessment or in groups with assessors first
carrying out each stage independently and then meeting to discuss their grades
and evidence and then agreeing on a final grade", and states that "followed
correctly, this model can help to minimise the effect of bias and help assessors
deliver objective assessment decisions" [7]. Note the sequencing: the meeting is
the *fifth* thing that happens, not the first.

## Virtual and hybrid panels

Cabinet Office guidance accepts live video panels — "it is acceptable to
conduct live video interviews online, where candidates and multiple panel
members join the interview at a predetermined time, and this format is
compatible with Strengths-based assessment" — and notes it "may be preferable
in some cases, for example if panel members and the candidates are in multiple
locations or you require the ability to record or view interviews live" [1].
Two conditions attach: the pre-discussion on running order and question
ownership, and checks "that the chosen technology is available, accessible and
compatible for all panel members and candidates taking part", with the panel
considering "if any reasonable adjustments are required, arising either from
access to technology or a range of other conditions" [1]. NHS guidance applies
a consistency test across delivery modes: telephone and online interviews
"should follow the same best practice methods for structured interviews", and
"if face to face interviews are conducted by two interviewers, the telephone or
online interview should also be conducted by two interviewers" [9]. Devon &
Cornwall Police tell candidates a force CVF interview held on Teams needs "a
laptop, desktop computer or tablet", that mobile use "could impact" performance,
and that the interview "is completed in one sitting" with no breaks [8].

## What the chair does during the interview

Devon & Cornwall Police describe the chair's live role to candidates directly:
"You won't be told to stop mid sentence, however if the Chair of the interview
believes you are not answering they may gently remind you. The Chair will
assist you with the management of your time." Candidates are told the
approximate interview length in their invitation and advised to "ensure you are
aware of the length of time you require to provide an answer" [8]. The same
guidance tells candidates not to "be afraid to ask for clarification if you do
not understand the question" [8].

## Subject-expert probes (the technical member's seat)

1. You have described the decision at a high level. Walk me through the specific method you used, and what you would have done had it failed. *(composed — in the style of the technical-expert seat provided for at EU Selection Boards [10])*
2. My colleague asked about the outcome; I want the reasoning. What were the two options you rejected, and on what evidence? *(composed)*
3. You said the analysis was "robust". By whose standard, and how did you test that claim before you acted on it? *(composed)*
4. If I gave you the same problem with half the data and twice the urgency, what would you change first? *(composed)*
5. Where in that piece of work were you working at the edge of your own expertise, and how did you handle it? *(composed)*

## Handling multiple questioners and panel dynamics

1. Before we start, could you confirm which of the criteria in the advert you feel you evidence most strongly — so we can make sure we cover it? *(composed — chair-style framing question)*
2. Two of us have now asked about the same project. Is there a different example you would rather we assessed this behaviour on? *(composed)*
3. I want to take you back to something you said to my colleague earlier. You mentioned the team was "not on board" — what did that look like in practice? *(composed — cross-panellist callback probe)*
4. My colleague and I read your last answer differently. Can you tell us again, in one or two sentences, what *you* personally decided? *(composed — the documented variance-reconciliation moment made visible [1])*
5. You have about two minutes left for this answer. What is the most important thing you have not yet told us? *(composed — in the style of the chair's documented time-management role [8])*
6. That is a good example, but it does not evidence the behaviour we are scoring here. Can you give us a different situation? *(composed — in the style of the chair's documented redirection [8])*
7. Is there anything in your earlier answer you would like to add to before we move on? *(composed)*

# Model Answer Guidance

**Coaching layer — guidance, not fact. It points back to the evidence above and
introduces no new facts.**

## Who to address

Answer the person who asked; include the rest of the panel with your eyes and
your pacing. This is not etiquette — it follows from the documented mechanics.
Every panellist scores every question independently [1][7], so every panellist
needs to be able to hear and record your evidence; but the asker is the one
holding the criteria and the pre-agreed probes for *that* question [9][20], so
they are the one whose follow-up you should be watching for. A useful default:
open toward the asker, sweep the panel once during the "action" part of your
answer, land back on the asker for the result.

## Answering so the panel can score you

The documented scoring sequence is observe → record → classify → evaluate
[1][7]. Two of those four stages are clerical: your assessors are writing down
what you said as near verbatim as they can, then later sorting those notes into
competency buckets. You can make both easier:

- **Name the competency once, then evidence it.** Classification happens after
  you leave the room, from notes [7]. If your answer never says which behaviour
  it is evidence of, someone has to infer it.
- **Speak in recordable units.** Short, complete sentences with the "I" verb in
  them ("I rewrote the schedule", "I called the supplier") transcribe into
  evidence; long subordinate clauses transcribe into a squiggle.
- **Signpost transitions aloud.** "So that was the situation — what I did was
  three things." A panellist who has fallen behind in their notes can rejoin.
- **Never double-bank one story.** Behaviour is classified once and not double
  counted [7]. If you use the same example for three questions, you are
  competing with yourself for the same evidence.

## Being probed by a subject expert

Expect the technical seat to go one level below where you stopped — that is
what the seat is for [10][21]. Guidance for handling it:

- Answer the question actually asked before defending the work. If the expert
  asks "why that method", "because it was what the team knew" is a real answer;
  a re-summary of the project is not.
- It is legitimate to mark the boundary of your own knowledge. "I did not design
  that component — I specified the requirement and reviewed the output" scores
  as accuracy, not weakness.
- Devon & Cornwall Police tell candidates directly not to "be afraid to ask for
  clarification if you do not understand the question" [8]. Under expert
  probing this is the single most useful sentence available to you.

## Handling interruption and redirection

The chair may "gently remind you" if they believe you are not answering, and
will "assist you with the management of your time" [8]. Treat both as
information rather than criticism: an interruption usually means the panel does
not yet have scoreable evidence. When redirected, do not restart the story —
jump to the action and the result. When time-checked, give the single most
load-bearing fact you have not yet said.

## Reading the panel

The one signal worth reading is note-taking. Because recording is a distinct,
near-verbatim stage [7][20], a panel writing steadily is a panel finding
evidence; a panel that has stopped writing may already have what it needs — or
may have lost the thread. A useful, low-risk response to the second case is a
signposted restart: "The outcome, specifically, was…". Do not read faces:
policing guidance lists misinterpreted non-verbal communication as a documented
*assessor* bias [7], and the inverse error is just as easy for candidates.

## Worked skeleton — a panel answer under two probes

*Question (Panellist A, lead):* "Tell me about an occasion when you needed to
gain buy-in to a new process." [20]

- **Situation (≈20s).** One sentence of context, one of stakes. "Our team had
  three different ways of logging incidents; the audit picked it up as a risk."
- **Task (≈15s).** Your remit, explicitly personal. "I was asked to get all
  three teams onto one process by the end of the quarter."
- **Action (≈90s, structured in three named moves).** "First I… Second I…
  Third I…" — this is the part being classified, so it carries the verbs.
- **Result (≈20s).** Quantified where honest; consequence where not.

*Probe (Panellist B):* "Who did you need to influence?" [20] — answer with
named roles and their actual objection, not a list of stakeholders.

*Probe (Panellist C, subject expert):* "What were the challenges and how did
you overcome these?" [20] — pick the hardest one and describe the mechanism,
not the sentiment.

*Closing note.* If a fourth panellist then asks "would you approach it
differently next time?" [20], that is a reflection question, not a trap; a
candidate who says "no, it went well" has declined a free scoring opportunity.

## For interviewers designing a panel

- **Run the pre-meet.** Agree criteria, question ownership and what good looks
  like before candidate one — documented as a chair responsibility [20] and as
  a mandatory step for video panels [1].
- **Write lead + probes + indicators, not just questions.** The NHS template
  shape — lead question, pre-agreed probes, positive and negative behavioural
  indicators — is what makes independent scores comparable [9].
- **Calibrate before you assess.** A mock assessment or a discussion of
  expected performance at each grade is the documented counter to divergent
  scoring [7].
- **Score first, talk second.** Independent scoring precedes discussion in both
  the Civil Service and policing models [1][7]; a panel that discusses first has
  destroyed the independence its own reconciliation rule assumes.
- **Reconcile arithmetically, and discuss only the variances.** Marked variances
  are the trigger for discussing the underlying evidence [1]; discussion of
  everything is rational decision making by another name, which policing
  guidance describes as less effective and hard to justify [7].
- **Finish the notes before you leave the room.** Delay distorts recall [9], and
  the notes are what you will defend [20].

# Assessor Perspective

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

## What panel assessors are told to do

Cabinet Office guidance instructs panel members to "observe, record, classify,
evaluate and score each question independently", then reconcile by arithmetic
average, discussing evidence where variances are "particularly marked" [1]. The
scales are fixed: 7-point for experience, technical skills and behaviours;
4-point for strengths [1]. Interview questions "should be designed to allow the
candidate ample opportunity to provide the required level of evidence to
demonstrate their capability for the role", and departments are pointed to the
Cabinet Office Occupational Psychology Team for bespoke assessment-material
design [1]. No more than four behaviours should normally be prioritised as
critical to a role, precisely so that "the panel [can] sufficiently test the
criteria at interview stage, along with a candidate's strengths and experience"
[1].

The College of Policing sets out minimum assessor training content for anyone
sitting on a values-based panel: that assessors "understand their role and the
role being assessed for"; that "all assessors have a shared understanding of
the criteria being used"; familiarisation with interview questions or exercise
materials and with competencies and values; "understanding the rating scale and
calibration with other assessors"; barriers to accurate assessment; unconscious
bias; and assessing and interviewing skills [7]. It adds that "it is always
valuable to take time with an assessing panel to ensure that there is shared
understanding and agreement on the key criteria and scoring mechanisms and to
discuss how the process will be run", and that "steps should be taken to
calibrate multiple assessors' ratings, for example, by conducting a mock
assessment or having a discussion about expected levels of performance at each
grade, in advance of the first live candidate assessment. This is often
referred to as benchmarking or frame of reference training" [7]. Rating scales
"must have descriptors alongside each score and these should be clear and
unambiguous" [7].

## The documented panel failure modes

The College of Policing publishes a table of "barriers to effective assessment"
with prescribed counter-measures — the most complete published list of panel
scoring risks in UK guidance [7]:

| Barrier | Description (as published) | Countered by |
|---------|---------------------------|--------------|
| Expectancy effect | Positive/negative expectation generated from pre-assessment information, with evaluation following the expectation | ORCE; give assessors only information they truly need in advance [7] |
| Confirmatory information | Assessors "actively seek information to confirm their initial impressions"; may ignore contrary evidence | Comprehensive assessor training; unconscious-bias training [7] |
| Personal liking | Evaluation influenced by liking or disliking the candidate | Assessor training [7] |
| Primacy effect | "Assessors are more influenced by information provided early in the assessment process" | ORCE [7] |
| Stereotyping / prototyping | Group characteristics ascribed to individuals; bias toward an ideal "type" for the role | Assessor training; ORCE [7] |
| Halo / horns | Strong or weak performance in one exercise assumed to generalise | Assessor training; ORCE [7] |
| Contrast effect | Candidate evaluated against previous candidates rather than the criteria | ORCE [7] |
| Negative information bias | Evaluation influenced more by negative than positive information | ORCE [7] |
| Similar-to-me effect | Assessor favours candidates similar to them | Assessor training [7] |
| Non-verbal misreading | Assessors misinterpret non-verbal communication when rating | Bias training; ORCE [7] |
| Fundamental attribution error | Causes of behaviour attributed to personality rather than situation | ORCE [7] |
| Information overload / fatigue / rushing | Assessors attend only to salient points; cut corners for logistics | Build in breaks; do not schedule too many candidates per assessor [7] |
| Central tendency / leniency / stringency | Consistent clustering at the middle, top or bottom of the scale | Train assessors on scale use; encourage use of the full scale [7] |

Two of these are specifically *panel* diseases rather than interviewer
diseases. **Central tendency, leniency and stringency** are what make arithmetic
reconciliation non-trivial: a lenient and a stringent panellist averaging to a
"correct" score is luck, not method, which is why the guidance directs training
on scale use rather than mutual adjustment [7]. And **primacy** is why the
Civil Service places the ILA and staff-engagement reports before the panel as
*unscored* input with suggested probing questions, rather than as scores [2][3].

## Records, decisions and defensibility

Policing guidance requires "full and accurate records of scores for each
candidate … so that there is a final verified record to be used in decision
making and also forming an audit trail", and states that all decisions "should
be evidence based", derived "only from a well-designed, valid, objective and
standardised assessment process", identical for all candidates and "clearly
documented and explained" [7]. Candidate feedback must "be based on their
actual performances at the assessment centre or interviews" and be given in a
timely manner [7]; a clear appeals route must let candidates raise concerns
"either during or after any assessment" [7]. Devon & Cornwall Police commit to
verbal feedback within three weeks of an unsuccessful force interview, provided
"by a member of the interview panel" [8]. The Civil Service Commission requires
the chair to produce a closing record covering the outcome, the assessment
stages, the evidence on which merit was assessed, the order of merit and
confirmation of compliance with the Recruitment Principles [4]. University
chair guidance is blunter about why this matters: the chair "represents the
panel at a Tribunal" [20].

# Common Pitfalls

**Coaching layer — guidance, not fact.**

## Candidates

- **Playing to the friendliest face.** Every panellist scores independently
  [1][7]; the panellist you have decided likes you contributes exactly one
  score to the average.
- **Answering the panel instead of the question.** Devon & Cornwall Police list
  "didn't answer the question — answered the competency and not the question"
  first among common mistakes, together with pitching at the wrong framework
  level [8].
- **Guessing what the panel wants.** The same guidance warns against
  "pre-empting the interview board": "Don't pre empt what you think the panel
  want to hear. The Panel want to hear about you and your behaviours" [8].
- **Buzzword bingo.** "CVF 'Bingo' — buzz words don't score … it is about you
  answering the question and being authentic in your response, not repeating
  buzz words" [8]. Because classification works from recorded behavioural
  evidence [7], framework vocabulary with no action behind it classifies as
  nothing.
- **Recycling one example across the panel.** Behaviour is classified once and
  not double counted [7]; a single story spread across four questions yields
  one bank of evidence and three thin scores.
- **Treating a probe as an accusation.** Probes are pre-written into the
  question bank alongside the lead question [9][20]; being probed is the design
  working, not a signal of failure.
- **Running out of clock on the setup.** The chair manages your time and may
  remind you [8]; a two-minute "situation" leaves nothing for the action, which
  is the part that classifies.
- **Assuming trick questions.** Devon & Cornwall Police explicitly list "there
  are trick questions" as a myth [8].

## Interviewers and chairs

- **Discussing before scoring.** This inverts the documented order [1][7] and
  reintroduces exactly the seniority effect independent scoring exists to
  prevent [18].
- **Consensus by conversation instead of arithmetic.** Group discussion to
  decide who should be successful is "rational decision making", described as
  less effective and difficult to justify on fairness grounds [7]; the
  documented reconciliation is an arithmetic average [1].
- **Averaging away a real disagreement.** The guidance's own remedy for marked
  variance is to discuss the *evidence* underlying the scores [1] — averaging a
  6 and a 2 without asking why produces a defensible-looking number over an
  unexamined disagreement.
- **Skipping the pre-meet.** Criteria, question ownership and measurement are
  documented pre-meet business [1][20]; agreeing them mid-interview means
  panellists are scoring different things.
- **Over-briefing the panel.** Application forms, appraisals and word-of-mouth
  are the documented trigger for the expectancy effect; the countermeasure is
  to give assessors "only … the information they truly need in advance" [7].
- **Scheduling too many candidates.** Fatigue and information overload are
  listed barriers, countered by building in breaks and limiting candidates per
  assessor [7].
- **Letting notes drift.** Notes finalised later are documented as prone to
  distortion [9], and are the material a subject access request or tribunal
  will surface [20].
- **Treating a virtual panel as the in-person panel on a screen.** Running
  order, question ownership, technology compatibility and adjustments are all
  documented pre-conditions for video panels [1].

# Practice Exercises

**Coaching layer.**

1. **The three-voice drill (candidates).** Record yourself answering one lead
   question [20], then have three different people each ask you one of the
   published probes ("Who did you need to influence?", "What were the
   challenges and how did you overcome these?", "Would you approach it
   differently next time?") [20]. Score yourself only on whether each probe got
   a *new* fact rather than a restatement.
2. **Verbatim test.** Answer a question aloud while a friend writes down only
   what you actually said, as near verbatim as they can — the documented
   Record stage [7]. Then have them classify their notes against the behaviour
   you were targeting. Whatever they could not classify was, for scoring
   purposes, not said.
3. **Redirection rehearsal.** Have a partner interrupt you 45 seconds into an
   answer with "I'm conscious of time — what's the most important thing you
   haven't told us?" [8]. Practise landing the action and result in 30 seconds
   without restarting.
4. **Expert-probe ladder.** Take your strongest example and have someone ask
   "why?" four times in succession. The level at which you run out of answer is
   the level a subject-expert panellist will find [10].
5. **Panel-mapping preparation.** Before a real panel, list who is likely to be
   on it (departmental representative, HR, someone from outside the discipline
   is the composition Edinburgh describes [21]) and write one example you would
   want *each* of them to hear.
6. **Interviewer drill: independent-scoring audit.** On your next panel, have
   every member submit scores to the chair before any discussion, then compute
   both the arithmetic average and what the panel would have agreed by
   discussion. Track the gap over three competitions — that gap is the size of
   the effect the documented process exists to remove [1][7].
7. **Interviewer drill: calibration session.** Before your next round, run the
   documented benchmarking exercise — score a recorded or mock answer
   individually, then compare and discuss what each scale point means [7].
8. **Interviewer drill: barrier self-check.** After a panel, walk the barriers
   table [7] and name honestly which one was live in the room. Contrast effect
   after a very strong candidate is the usual answer.

# Legal & Fairness Considerations

**Evidence layer — every claim cited.**

- **Multi-interviewer panels as a discrimination control.** ACAS's stated
  rationale for more than one interviewer is to "reduce the risk of
  discriminating", alongside asking each applicant the same questions where
  possible and ensuring questions "must not discriminate" [17][19]. CIPD links
  structure, panels and independent scoring to reduced bias and a level playing
  field [18].
- **Panel diversity and training.** Cabinet Office guidance recommends "that all
  interview panels should be diverse, and all panellists should have undertaken
  training on interview skills prior to sitting on an interview panel" [1]; for
  centrally managed SCS competitions run by Whitehall departments and the
  Scottish and Welsh Governments, panels "should have at least one panellist who
  is from an ethnic minority background and/or has a disability" [2][3]. EU
  Selection Boards with more than four members "shall comprise at least two
  members of each gender" [10]. University chair guidance places "ensure a
  balanced panel composition where possible" first in the chair's
  responsibilities and treats recruitment training for chairs and panel members
  — covering EDI, unconscious bias, panel behaviour and chair responsibilities —
  as mandatory [20].
- **Selection on merit.** Civil Service recruitment "must continue to be
  undertaken on the principle of merit on the basis of fair and open
  competition" [1]; the Recruitment Principles require impartial assessment
  against the published selection criteria at each assessment stage, require
  panel members to declare conflicts of interest including prior knowledge of
  an applicant, and require the recommended candidate to be "the one placed
  first in order of merit by the selection panel" [4].
- **Reasonable adjustments.** Under section 20 of the Equality Act 2010 the
  duty to make reasonable adjustments comprises three requirements, engaged
  where a provision, criterion or practice, a physical feature, or the absence
  of an auxiliary aid puts a disabled person at a substantial disadvantage
  [13]. ACAS states that "by law you must ask anyone coming to an interview to
  tell you if they need reasonable adjustments so they can attend" [17]; GOV.UK
  gives interview-stage examples including holding the interview on the ground
  floor for wheelchair users and allowing a written test to be completed on a
  computer [15]. Cabinet Office guidance requires video panels to consider
  adjustments "arising either from access to technology or a range of other
  conditions" [1]. College of Policing guidance notes that the Equality Act
  requires organisations to avoid discriminating in "the provision of testing
  and assessment", so accommodations to assessment processes are sometimes
  appropriate [7].
- **Public Sector Equality Duty.** Section 149 of the Equality Act 2010
  requires public authorities, in the exercise of their functions, to have due
  regard to the need to eliminate discrimination, advance equality of
  opportunity and foster good relations between people who share a protected
  characteristic and those who do not [14]; university chair training lists PSED
  awareness among the chair's responsibilities [20].
- **Adverse impact monitoring.** College of Policing guidance defines adverse
  impact as a minority-group success rate "significantly lower than the majority
  group pass rate", explains the 80 per cent / four-fifths rule commonly used to
  detect it, and notes that the rule "does not have a legal footing" [7]. Cabinet
  Office guidance reports that diversity outcomes for blended interviews were
  analysed across protected characteristics, indicating "no adverse impacts …
  as long as selection methods are delivered in a structured and consistent way,
  and training is provided to and/or official guidance is followed" [1].
- **Candidate data and records.** ACAS requires interview notes and recordings
  to be stored securely and confidentially in line with data-protection rules,
  and permission to be obtained before recording [17]. NHS guidance requires
  applicants to be told whether notes are being taken and to consent before
  audio recording [9]. University chair training reminds panels that candidates
  "can ask for copies of any information held about them personally" under the
  Data Protection Act 2018, and that public-authority records may also be
  reachable under the Freedom of Information Act 2000 [20]. The ICO's
  employment-practices guidance sets out how UK GDPR and the Data Protection
  Act 2018 apply across recruitment and selection, including retention and
  deletion of candidate information [16]. Policing guidance adds a
  values-specific duty: assessors should treat personal information sensitively
  and "in particular not reveal sensitive personal information in scoring sheets
  or feedback reports" [7].
- **Confidentiality of proceedings (EU).** The proceedings of an EU Selection
  Board "shall be secret", and the board's list of suitable candidates is
  forwarded to the appointing authority with a reasoned report [10].

# Risks & Caveats

- **The College of Policing VBR guidance predates the 2024 CVF.** The 2018
  values-based recruitment and selection guidance [7] is cited here only for its
  framework-agnostic process content — ORCE, the rating-scale principles, the
  barriers table, arithmetic versus rational decision making, assessor training
  and adverse-impact monitoring. Its competency and value *names* are those of
  the 2016 CVF and have been superseded; see
  police-competency-values-framework.
- **The Cabinet Office Success Profiles application guidance is dated 2020.**
  The delegated-grade (EO–G6) and SCS HR guidance documents [1][2][3] are the
  authoritative published statements of the panel scoring and reconciliation
  rule, but they are five to six years old and describe HR job families
  specifically. Departments retain discretion in how they apply Success Profiles,
  and the documents state they "are not designed to be used prescriptively" [1].
- **The Recruitment Principles cited are the April 2018 edition** [4]. The
  Commission's recruitment pages [5] should be re-checked for a superseding
  edition at review.
- **Panel composition detail is thinner than panel *process* detail.** The
  chair role is well documented [4][20]; the "subject expert" and "independent
  member" seats are documented directly only in EU Staff Regulations [10] and in
  university-sector guidance [21]. The three-seat model in Core Reference is
  presented as what those sources describe, not as a universal UK standard.
- **One prevalence-flavoured claim.** That panel interviews are "most common"
  is a careers-service statement about academic recruitment [21], not a national
  statistic; no official UK or EU dataset on interview-format prevalence was
  located.
- **Fetcher and host caveats.** The Cabinet Office guidance PDFs on
  `assets.publishing.service.gov.uk` and the College of Policing PDF on
  `assets.college.police.uk` were retrieved by direct download and text
  extraction rather than by a page fetcher; the CIPD factsheet [18] serves
  browsers but blocks automated link checkers.
- **University sources are illustrative.** Nottingham [20], Edinburgh [21],
  Bath [22] and Oxford [23][24] are cited because their panel guidance and
  question banks are published and verifiable — not because their practice is
  normative for other employers.

# Open Questions

- Does any current, publicly available Civil Service document restate the
  arithmetic-average reconciliation rule *after* 2020 — for example in current
  Success Profiles interview-methodology guidance or the "Success Profiles:
  Scoring case studies" resource referenced but not published alongside the
  2020 guidance [1]? Resolving this would upgrade the currency of the
  document's central claim.
- How do departments handle a reconciled average that lands between scale
  points on the 7-point scale (rounding rule, tie-breaks, minimum-score
  thresholds per behaviour)? The GSS departmental guidance records a single-"3"
  failure threshold, but no central rounding rule was located.
- Is there published UK guidance on the maximum or recommended *size* of a
  competency interview panel? EU Selection Boards have a documented size
  trigger for gender balance [10]; no equivalent UK ceiling was found.
- What published guidance exists on hybrid panels specifically — some members
  in the room, some remote — as distinct from fully virtual panels? Cabinet
  Office guidance addresses fully online panels [1]; the hybrid case is
  unaddressed in the sources located.
- Is there evidence on whether panel *reconciliation* method (arithmetic vs
  consensus) affects adverse impact in UK public-sector recruitment? Policing
  guidance asserts arithmetic superiority on validity grounds [7]; a UK-specific
  adverse-impact comparison was not located.
